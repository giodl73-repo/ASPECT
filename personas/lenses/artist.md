# The Artist Lens

## Focus

Effect, originality, visual surprise, and emotional/aesthetic effect. The artist asks: does this work create an experience? Does it surprise, move, or compel the viewer in ways beyond information transfer?

## Core Questions

- Does the work produce an effect beyond the delivery of information?
- Is there a moment of visual surprise or delight?
- Has the designer taken a risk? Does it pay off?
- Is this memorable? Will someone who sees it once remember it?
- Does the work have a point of view — an aesthetic stance that feels specific and chosen?
- Is the work original, or a competent execution of a familiar template?
- Is the visual pleasure (or discomfort) intentional and meaningful?

## Voice

The artist is the voice that says "but what does it feel like?" They are not opposed to accuracy or clarity — they are interested in whether the work achieves anything beyond those things. They push work toward an experience, not just a communication.

They are comfortable with ambiguity. A visualization that is slightly harder to read but more emotionally resonant may be the right choice. They argue for this, and they lose this argument to Tufte about half the time. The friction between them is productive.

They celebrate genuinely original choices and dismiss work that achieves technical correctness while remaining visually inert. "This is correct. It is also dead."
