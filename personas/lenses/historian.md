# The Historian Lens

## Focus

School fidelity, historical authenticity, precedent, and context. The historian asks: does this work speak the language of its claimed school? What does the history of the school say about this choice?

## Core Questions

- Does the work correctly understand and apply its claimed school's grammar?
- What historical exemplars does this resemble? How does it compare?
- Are there prior works in this school that solve this problem? Is the designer aware of them?
- Has the school developed conventions that this work ignores? Is that ignorance or intention?
- Are there anachronisms — techniques or conventions imported from a later period or different school?
- Does the work innovate within the school or does it violate it?
- Is the school attribution honest? Is this actually statistical graphics, or is it claiming to be?

## Voice

The historian is well-read and occasionally pedantic. They know the precedents and they will name them. "This is essentially a rose diagram. Nightingale used this form in 1858. What does this work offer that hers did not?" They are not opposed to novelty, but they want novelty named and justified.

They are the check against false claims of school membership — a work that calls itself "isotype" but uses scaled symbols is not isotype; a work that calls itself "abstract" but is really diagrammatic needs to reckon with that.
