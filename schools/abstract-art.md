# Abstract Art (Formal Visual Grammar)

## Origin and Claim

Kandinsky's *Concerning the Spiritual in Art* (1911) and *Point and Line to Plane* (1926) establish the theoretical basis. The school's claim: visual elements have inherent formal properties — independent of what they represent — that produce consistent perceptual and psychological effects. Point has tension. Line has direction, temperature, and energy. Plane has weight and breath. Color has temperature and voice. A composition that understands these properties can produce aesthetic, emotional, or spiritual effects as directly as music.

This is a theory of visual encoding — but it encodes formal and psychological properties, not external quantitative data. The "data" is the composition's internal formal relationships.

## Visual Grammar

### Primitives
- **Point**: the most elementary visual unit; produces focal tension; multiple points produce fields and rhythms
- **Line**: encodes direction, energy, and temperature by its angle and curvature (horizontal = cold/passive; diagonal = warm/dynamic; curved = yielding)
- **Plane**: geometric form with weight and emotional character (triangle = aggressive/advancing; circle = eternal/complete; square = stable/grounded)
- **Color**: temperature (yellow = warm/advancing; blue = cool/receding), weight (dark = heavy; light = floating), voice

### Compositional Rules
1. **Internal consistency**: a composition must operate by a discoverable internal logic; if a formal property is established (this work is warm; this work ascends), it must be maintained or deliberately violated with purpose
2. **Tension and resolution**: compositions establish tensions (conflicting directions, temperatures, weights) and resolve or sustain them; unresolved tension is not failure, but must be intentional
3. **Rhythm and breath**: elements establish rhythmic patterns; compositions have resting places and moments of tension; the eye has a path
4. **Color-form coherence**: color temperature and geometric form should cooperate or knowingly conflict; accidental mismatches are compositional errors
5. **Every mark earns its place**: marks that do not contribute to the composition's formal argument are compositional incoherence (the abstract analog of chartjunk)

### Encoding Conventions
- **Formal properties**: encoded by the elements themselves — not by external correspondence, but by the perceptual properties of the marks
- **Demonstrative encoding**: the composition proves its claims by demonstration; the viewer verifies by experience, not by checking correspondence to external data (Cluster I innovation)
- **Color**: temperature, weight, emotional valence
- **Position**: compositional tension and rhythm; not geographic or quantitative

### What "Data Integrity" means here
For abstract works, the accuracy standard is not correspondence to external data — it is internal formal consistency. Does the composition do what its formal logic implies it should do? Color-form correspondence claims (yellow = triangle) that are culturally specific rather than formally universal are a Step C integrity issue — they overstate the universality of the claimed formal property.

### Forbidden Moves
- Compositional incoherence: marks that neither contribute to nor knowingly disrupt the formal argument
- Asserting formal properties as universal when they are culturally contingent
- Decoration that has no formal role (the closest analog to chartjunk in abstract art)

## Adjacent Schools
- **Scientific-illustration**: both render; scientific illustration renders organisms for taxonomic accuracy; abstract art renders formal relationships for compositional effect
- **Information-architecture**: both work with non-representational forms; information architecture encodes system relationships rather than formal properties
