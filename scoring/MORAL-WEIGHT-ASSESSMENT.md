## The Moral Weight Thread

### Origin: Innovation #106 (Work 0011, Du Bois)

Du Bois flagged the following gap: "a chart that is technically correct and informationally complete but produces no sense of stakes or urgency has failed at something important, even if the rubric scores it well." The rubric anchor identified was Resonance -- and the implication was that for works on subjects with clear human stakes (climate impact, public health, economic inequality), the panel should score whether the work's resonance is proportionate to those stakes, not just whether it achieves its stated aim.

This innovation named the **inert chart problem**: a visualization can pass all rubric dimensions (correct encoding, honest data, legible, economical) and still be morally inadequate if it treats an urgent subject as if it were merely interesting.

## Question 1: Did v2.0 Purpose Dimension capture moral weight correctly?

**Answer: Yes, substantially and correctly.**

The v2.0 Purpose dimension (15 pts) explicitly addresses moral weight proportionality in two places:

1. **The 13-15 anchor**: "For works on subjects with clear human stakes (preventable deaths, injustice, climate impact), the resonance is proportionate to those stakes -- the design does not treat urgent subjects as if they were merely interesting."

2. **The Modifiers section**: "Moral weight proportionality: for works on subjects with clear human stakes, the panel should explicitly assess whether the resonance is proportionate. This is scored here, not in Resonance."

The HOLC recalibration under v2.0 is the proof of concept. Under v1.x, HOLC scored 69.2 primarily due to Cluster N (ontological category contamination) in Integrity. Under v2.0, HOLC's Purpose score (5/15 -- the form of an objective planning tool was used to encode and amplify racial harm over 80 years; the form worked against any just purpose) contributes a new dimension of penalty that v1.x could not express. The score change reflects genuine rubric improvement, not recalibration noise.

Innovation #106's core insight -- "for works on subjects with clear human stakes, the panel should score whether the work's resonance is proportionate to those stakes" -- is now in the rubric, exactly where Du Bois placed it: as a scoring criterion, not a recommendation.

## Question 2: Is there a remaining gap that Purpose doesn't cover?

**Answer: One partial gap remains, but it is narrow and well-defined.**

The Purpose dimension correctly handles:
- Works on urgent subjects that fail to convey urgency (urban heat island, #106)
- Works that actively use their form to cause harm (HOLC, 0022)
- Works where the resonance is proportionate to the human stakes (child mortality, 0029)
- Works where no human stakes exist (Kandinsky abstract, 0015 -- correctly scores high Purpose without moral weight concern)

The one **remaining gap** is the **passive inertness case** at work-level resolution:

The Purpose dimension asks "is the resonance proportionate to the stakes?" The anchor at 5-8 reads: "An inert chart on a subject that deserved urgency." This correctly captures the failure type.

But there is a subtler case that Purpose does not fully address: **works where the design is not inert but the subject selection itself is the moral choice**. Du Bois's innovation #106 flagged this: the urban heat island chart correctly shows which cities have the worst UHI, but by choosing not to show who bears the consequences (predominantly lower-income and minority urban residents), the design makes a moral choice in its variable selection. The moral weight is not suppressed by the form; it is suppressed by what the form chose not to encode.

This is the **variable-selection-as-moral-choice** gap. It is adjacent to:
- Cluster L (variable selection as framing argument, adopted v1.9) -- which addresses whether variable exclusions create misleading framing
- Purpose dimension (13-15 anchor) -- which asks whether resonance is proportionate

But neither fully captures the case where: the variables shown are accurately encoded, the framing is honest, the resonance is appropriate for the claim made -- and yet a morally more complete version of the visualization would have included variables the designer chose to omit.

### Is this gap large enough to require a new amendment?

**No.** The existing rubric instruments address this case adequately at two levels:

1. **Integrity Question 1 (Disclosure)**: "Key variables encoded; exclusions noted where they create framing effects" -- docks for undeclared exclusions that create framing effects. If the urban heat island chart omits consequence variables and this creates a framing effect (victims appear absent), the Integrity dimension should catch it.

2. **Purpose (5-8 anchor)**: "The work is correct in its encoding but fails at its stated function" -- if the stated function includes communicating stakes, and the variable selection suppresses those stakes, this anchor applies.

The gap is not structural -- it is a calibration question. Reviewers who apply the Purpose dimension's moral weight proportionality criterion conscientiously will surface the variable-selection-as-moral-choice case. It does not require a separate amendment.
