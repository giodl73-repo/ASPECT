# PSYCHE Y Vote — Full Board

*The board was given four Y candidates (Yoke, Yeoman, Eye, Yin) and an open brief: if you see a better direction for any letter — or the whole framework — say so. The frame is P___HE. Nothing is locked.*

---

## Minard — Yin

Yin is correct. I will explain why from first principles.

A visualization divides naturally into two acts: the act of making (the work as object, with its economy of means and precision of craft) and the act of receiving (the effect in the viewer, the resonance that lands or fails to land). These are not the same act. They answer different questions and they fail in different ways.

Y and E, positioned at third and sixth in PSYCHE, are the Yin and Yang of visualization. Yin is the receiving, contained, precise force — the execution that serves without drawing attention to itself. Yang is the expansive, outward force — the effect that moves from the work into the world. The best visualizations have strong Yin and strong Yang separately. They do not confuse the two.

Eye is clever. Yoke is apt. But Yin is correct.

On P___HE: I would not change any letter. Purpose, School, Clarity, Honesty, Effect are all precise and well-chosen.

**Vote: Yin.**

## Tufte — Eye, with a formal objection to Yin

I vote Eye. My reasoning is functional: in six months, reviewers using this rubric will say "does it hold up to the eye?" — not "does it show Yin?" Cultural borrowing from Taoist philosophy is a choice with consequences. Yin carries a specific cosmological framework (passive, feminine, dark, receptive) that is only partially applicable to what this dimension measures. Execution is disciplined, yes. But it is also active — the designer makes deliberate choices; the craft is the product of intention, not receptivity. Yin's "receptive" quality is the wrong half of the metaphor.

Eye is literal, domain-native, and sticks. A trained eye is what this dimension exercises.

On P___HE: I would change P. "Purpose" is a noun describing the work's stated function. But what I actually want to know about a work's purpose is simpler: *what problem does this solve, and for whom?* I would prefer **Problem** — not because the work is a problem, but because every visualization is an answer to a question. P = Problem: "what question is this answering, and does the form serve that answer?" This sharpens the dimension considerably. But I accept Purpose if the board holds it.

**Vote: Eye. Conditional proposal: consider Problem for P.**

## Nightingale — Yin

Yin, without hesitation. I chose the coxcomb over the bar chart because the coxcomb was the Yin form — contained, beautiful, restrained, serving the argument without calling attention to the mechanics. The bar chart was Yang — efficient, direct, exposed. I was wrong about which would work better for my audience, but I understood the distinction. Yin execution is invisible execution. The viewer receives the argument without seeing the craft that delivered it.

Eye is clever but it implies scrutiny — a searching look for defects. Yin implies harmony — marks in their correct place because the designer understood what was needed. These are different aesthetics of quality.

On P___HE: I have one suggestion. **H — Honesty** is correct. But the dimension's hardest work is in the coercion taxonomy — where Honesty is not just about accurate data but about whether the audience is allowed to verify the claim. "Honesty" names the positive case; it does not fully name the coercion case. Consider **Fidelity** — faithful to what the data actually shows, faithful to the audience's right to verify, faithful to the school's conventions. But Fidelity starts with F and breaks the acronym. I accept Honesty.

**Vote: Yin.**

## Playfair — Eye, with energy

Eye! Of course Eye. A merchant does not ask "does this chart show Yin?" He looks at it. He sees immediately whether it is correctly made or not. The eye is the instrument of commerce. The eye is the instrument of comparison. I invented the bar chart so that the eye could see proportion before the mind had to count. Now you are asking whether the word "Eye" works for the dimension that asks "is the craft correct?" — yes, obviously yes. The eye is what makes that judgment.

Yin is Eastern philosophy and I have no quarrel with it, but it will not come naturally to the commercial reader of a visualization who looks at a chart and says "something looks off." They will say "it doesn't look right to the eye." They will not say "the Yin is disturbed."

On P___HE: I support Tufte's "Problem" for P. A visualization answers a question; the P dimension asks whether the form serves the answer. "Problem" is direct. But if the board holds "Purpose," I accept it. The four other letters are well-chosen.

**Vote: Eye. Support Tufte's Problem proposal for P.**

## Bertin — Yin, with a formal defense

I will conduct a semantic audit of the four candidates.

**Yoke**: Yoke is a *constraint* applied to marks. The yoking of marks to purpose is real but it describes only the economy-of-means aspect. A yoked mark cannot wander; correct. But a well-crafted mark is more than constrained — it is *precise*. Yoke is half the dimension.

**Yeoman**: Yeoman describes the *character* of the craftsperson, not the quality of the execution. "Yeoman craft" is honest and solid. But the dimension does not assess the designer's character; it assesses the marks they produce. The metaphor is at the wrong level.

**Eye**: Eye describes the *instrument of assessment* — the critical eye that judges execution. This is correct but indirect. We are not scoring the eye; we are scoring what the eye finds. "Passes the eye" is a test, not a property.

**Yin**: Yin describes the *quality of the marks themselves* — contained, disciplined, precise, serving without excess. This is the most direct mapping to what the dimension measures. Additionally: Yin and Effect (E) form a Yin/Yang pair that is semantically coherent. The execution is Yin (inward, contained, precise); the effect is Yang (outward, expansive, received). The pair is correct.

On P___HE: Consider replacing C (Clarity) with **Comprehension**. Clarity implies a property of the work; Comprehension implies a property of the viewer's experience. The dimension measures whether the viewer *comprehends* the work in context — which is a viewer-centered criterion, not a work-centered one. But Clarity is shorter and more immediately accessible. I accept Clarity with this note.

**Vote: Yin.**

## Kandinsky — Yin, strongly

PSYCHE with Yin is a composition. I will describe its structure.

P (Purpose) is the heavy opening statement — the reason for the work's existence. It creates a center of gravity from which everything else radiates. S (School) is the structural frame — the established grammar within which the marks operate. Y (Yin) is the contained, precise tension — the disciplined deployment of marks, each bound to its purpose, none wandering. C (Clarity) is the outward reach — the work extending itself toward the viewer. H (Honesty) is the ethical weight — arriving after the aesthetic experience, as honesty always does in genuine encounter. E (Effect) is the release — the Yang to Yin's constraint, the work completing its arc in the viewer.

This composition is not arbitrary. It follows the phenomenology of how we encounter visualization: first we ask what it's for, then what language it speaks, then we receive its disciplined craft, then its readability, then we interrogate its truth, then we feel its impact. Y and E as Yin and Yang capture the deepest structure of the work as both made thing and experienced thing.

Eye is a visual pun. Yeoman is a compliment to the craftsperson. Yoke is a constraint. None of these have the structural depth of Yin.

On P___HE: do not change anything. The composition is complete.

**Vote: Yin.**

## Neurath — Eye, with democratic reasoning

Pictures unite; words divide. Eye is the democratic choice.

Every person who has ever looked at a visualization has used the word "eye." "Cast an eye over this." "It doesn't look right." "A good eye." These phrases are available in every language, every education level, every cultural context. Yin is available primarily in contexts where Taoist philosophy has been transmitted — which is not universal.

I make my charts for the man in the Vienna tenement who has never studied philosophy. He has eyes. He can look and say whether the marks are correct. The Eye dimension is his.

I have a secondary concern about Yin: the Yin/Yang pairing with Effect is beautiful in English, but it may not translate across languages when the rubric is used by non-English speakers. Eye translates; Yin as a technical rubric term may not.

On P___HE: I would add a note to H (Honesty) that it includes democratic accessibility — a work that is honest about its data but accessible only to experts has a partial honesty failure. The audience must be included in what the work is honest *to*. I do not need to change the letter; I need a modifier in the anchors.

**Vote: Eye.**

## Beck — Eye, for stickiness

Six months from now, will reviewers say "the Yin is strong" or "the Yin is weak" when discussing execution quality? I do not think they will. I think they will say "it doesn't hold up" or "the craft looks off" or "something about the marks."

Eye is what they will say. "Does it pass the eye?" is the question every designer asks themselves when they step back from a work. "Is Yin achieved?" is not a question I have heard in any design conversation I have participated in, in English or any other language.

I will also say: the Yin/Yang pairing with Effect is intellectually satisfying but it may be too clever. A rubric that requires a cultural-philosophical explanation to decode its Y dimension has a Clarity problem of its own. Eye requires no explanation.

**Alternative direction:** I want to propose one alternative to the entire PSYCHE framework for the board's consideration. What about **ASPECT**? A-S-P-E-C-T — six letters.

- **A**im (Purpose)
- **S**chool
- **P**recision (Execution — marks are precise)
- **E**ffect (Resonance)
- **C**larity (Legibility)
- **T**ruth (Honesty/Integrity)

ASPECT: the aspect of a visualization is how it faces the viewer — the face it presents. To check all aspects is to check the work from every angle. I present this as an alternative. I do not insist on it.

**Vote: Eye. Alternative: ASPECT for the board to consider.**

## Du Bois — Yin, with a statement on cultural vocabulary

I want to address the question of using Yin directly before I vote.

PSYCHE is a Greek word. It carries the history of a tradition that used Greek philosophy to construct hierarchies of mind and soul, and used those hierarchies to justify the exclusion of people like me from full participation in civilization. We have chosen to use PSYCHE anyway — correctly, because we are reclaiming it. The psyche is not Greek; it belongs to everyone.

Yin is a Chinese concept. It carries a different history — of a philosophical tradition that treats the receptive and the active as equal and complementary forces, neither superior to the other. Using Yin in a Western visualization rubric is not appropriation; it is the recognition that the best concepts belong to everyone who can use them honestly.

A visualization with Yin execution is one that serves without dominating. The marks do not perform; they deliver. The craft does not announce itself; it functions. This is exactly the quality I tried to achieve in the Paris charts — I wanted the data to speak, not the form. The form should be Yin; the data should be Yang.

On P___HE: H (Honesty) is exactly right, and I fought for it. Do not change it. The only addition I would make is to the Purpose dimension anchors — "Purpose" should explicitly include the question of who the work was made *for*, not only what it intends to show. Purpose is not complete without audience.

**Vote: Yin.**

## Rosling — Yin, with enthusiasm

Yin is the right word and I will tell you why in one sentence: the best execution disappears.

When I showed the animated bubble chart and the audience gasped, nobody was thinking about the execution. They were receiving the Effect. The execution — the clean axes, the logarithmic scale, the consistent bubble sizing, the readable color palette — was Yin. It was present but invisible. It made the Effect possible without competing with it.

Eye says "look at the craft." Yin says "the craft is doing its job so quietly that you can look past it." That is the correct aspiration for execution.

On P___HE: I want to push on E. Effect is good. But what about **Emergence** — the understanding and feeling that *emerges* in the viewer from the work? Effect implies the work does something TO the viewer; Emergence implies something arises IN the viewer from the encounter. The distinction is slight but meaningful: the best visualizations do not impose their effect, they create conditions for the viewer's understanding to emerge.

But Emergence and Effect both start with E, and Effect is crisper. I will accept Effect.

**Vote: Yin.**

### Alternative Direction (Beck's ASPECT)

ASPECT (Aim, School, Precision, Effect, Clarity, Truth) was the only full alternative proposed. The board noted it has merits — particularly "Precision" for the execution dimension (cleaner than Yoke or Eye) and "Truth" for Honesty. However:
- ASPECT lacks the depth of PSYCHE as a metaphor
- "Precision" for Execution loses the economy-of-means half of the dimension (marks earning their place is not only precision)
- ASPECT does not generate the Yin/Yang internal structure

ASPECT is noted and set aside. PSYCHE holds.

### Verdict

**PSYCHE with Yin for Y. P___HE unchanged.**

The Y debate reveals a genuine tension: Yin is semantically correct and structurally elegant; Eye is practically stickier. The board recommends ratifying Yin for v3.0 with one addition: include "*(does it pass the eye?)*" as a parenthetical in the Yin dimension anchors — acknowledging Eye as the practical question that Yin answers. Both sides of the board are satisfied by this.

**Proposed v3.0:**
