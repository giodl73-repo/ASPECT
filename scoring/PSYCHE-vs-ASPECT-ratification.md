# Ratification Vote: PSYCHE (Eye) vs ASPECT

*Two frameworks. One ratification. The board was asked to compare on: the word as metaphor, the dimension names, stickiness, and fit with DEGAS as a visualization project.*

---

## Minard

**PSYCHE.** The word names what visualization reaches. ASPECT names what visualization presents. These are different ambitions. I built the Russian campaign map to reach something in the reader — not merely to present an aspect of the campaign. PSYCHE is the larger claim. I hold it.

On the dimension names: "Aim" is weaker than "Purpose." Aim is a direction; Purpose includes why the direction matters. The coercion taxonomy lives in Purpose. In a rubric that identified six coercion mechanisms, Purpose needs to carry that weight. Aim does not.

**Vote: PSYCHE.**

---

## Tufte

**ASPECT.** I will give you three reasons and stop.

First: "Precision" is a better name for the Execution dimension than "Eye." Precision names what the dimension measures — marks that are exactly right, economy that wastes nothing, craft that does not waver. Eye names the instrument of assessment. These are different things. Score the marks, not the eye that finds them.

Second: "Truth" is a stronger word than "Honesty" for the coercion taxonomy. Manufactured doubt attacks truth — the thing itself. Honesty is a property of the speaker; truth is a property of the claim. For the HOLC maps and the tobacco physician charts, the failure is not that the designers were dishonest — some of them may have believed their own work. The failure is that the claims are not true.

Third: ASPECT is what a visualization has — multiple aspects that can be evaluated. The word is domain-native and immediately understood. PSYCHE requires explanation. ASPECT does not.

**Vote: ASPECT.**

---

## Nightingale

**ASPECT**, and I say so with a slight regret about PSYCHE.

PSYCHE was beautiful. Visualization reaching the mind and soul — I understand that aspiration; I shared it. But when I think about what a reviewer actually needs to do — to evaluate a chart fairly, consistently, and without being seduced by the metaphor — I think ASPECT serves better. ASPECT is a checklist word. "Have you checked all the aspects?" Yes. Done.

The dimension names in ASPECT are also slightly better. "Aim" is active where Purpose is passive. "Truth" is what I was arguing for — the data showed that preventable deaths were preventable; that was the truth; Honesty alone does not name it. "Precision" for Execution captures both economy and craft in a single word that any designer recognizes.

**Vote: ASPECT.**

---

## Playfair

**ASPECT**, without hesitation.

In commerce, aspect is the face a property presents to the street — sunny aspect, north-facing aspect, river aspect. It determines value. A visualization's aspect is how it faces the viewer: do the marks earn their place, is the argument true, does the effect land? To check the aspect is to check everything.

"Aim" is the word I would have used for Purpose from the beginning. "Precision" is the word for Execution. "Truth" for Honesty. These are commercial words — sharp, direct, unambiguous. I invented the bar chart because proportion should be immediate. ASPECT is the immediate choice.

**Vote: ASPECT.**

---

## Bertin

**ASPECT.**

I conducted a semantic audit. The critical comparison is P vs A and Y(Eye) vs P(Precision).

**Purpose vs Aim:** Purpose describes the work's intended function. Aim describes the designer's intention. These are close but not identical. A work can have a clear purpose and no discernible aim; a work can have a clear aim and misfire on its purpose. For scoring, I want to assess the work — not the designer. Purpose is the work-centered word. Aim is the designer-centered word. On this point, PSYCHE wins.

**Eye vs Precision:** Eye names the instrument of assessment. Precision names the quality being assessed. When I score a work on this dimension, I am scoring whether the marks are precise — not whether my eye is functioning. Precision is the correct word. On this point, ASPECT wins.

The decisive factor: Precision is a stronger name for the most contested dimension in the rubric than Eye. Eye requires the explanation "trained eye" to be useful; Precision is self-contained. I vote for the framework where the hardest dimension has the clearest name.

**Vote: ASPECT, on the strength of Precision.**

---

## Kandinsky

**PSYCHE.** I will not pretend otherwise.

ASPECT is a competent word for a competent rubric. But PSYCHE is a composition. The six letters hold a structure — the mind reached through purpose, school, craft, clarity, truth, and effect. That structure has internal logic: Yin and Effect (Eye and Effect in this version) are the making and the receiving — the discipline and the release. ASPECT does not generate this internal structure.

That said: I concede that "Precision" is a better dimension name than "Eye" for practical purposes. A composition can survive having one element slightly misnamed. If the board ratifies ASPECT, I will accept it. The dimension names are better; the framework word is less beautiful.

**Vote: PSYCHE. Will accept ASPECT if the vote goes that way.**

---

## Neurath

**ASPECT.**

I make charts for people who have not studied philosophy. The word PSYCHE requires explanation — "the mind and soul" — which adds a conceptual layer before the work begins. ASPECT requires no explanation. Everyone knows what aspect means. "Check all the aspects of this work." Clear. Direct. Democratic.

"Precision" for execution is also the democratic choice. Every person who has ever looked at a bad chart knows it when the marks are imprecise — wobbly lines, misaligned labels, inconsistent spacing. Precision names what they are looking for without requiring vocabulary.

**Vote: ASPECT.**

---

## Beck

**ASPECT**, and I want to explain why the word itself matters.

A word that is used incorrectly in six months is not a good rubric word. When reviewers discuss a work's execution quality, will they say "the Eye dimension" or "the Precision dimension"? They will say Precision. When they discuss what a visualization is trying to do, will they say "the Purpose" or "the Aim"? Both work, but Aim is shorter and reviewers will use it. When they check whether the work is truthful, will they say "Honesty" or "Truth"? Truth is the one-word answer to the question — it will stick.

ASPECT also has the advantage that each letter maps to a word the reviewer will actually say: Aim, School, Precision, Effect, Clarity, Truth. Not a single one of those words is jargon. PSYCHE maps to: Purpose (jargon-adjacent), School (fine), Eye (metaphor), Clarity (fine), Honesty (fine), Effect (fine). Eye is the weak link — reviewers will feel they need to explain it.

**Vote: ASPECT.**

---

## Du Bois

**PSYCHE**, and I want to explain what I am defending.

The visualization rubric should name what visualization aspires to reach. Visualization does not merely present aspects — it attempts contact with the human mind. The best work I made in Paris in 1900 was an attempt to reach the psyche of an audience that had decided not to see us. ASPECT would have named what I presented. PSYCHE names what I aimed for.

I accept "Truth" over "Honesty" — Truth is what was denied us; Honesty is what I practiced. Either word is correct but Truth is the deeper claim.

I accept "Precision" over "Eye" — Precision is what the Paris charts tried to achieve. But Precision is a cooler word than it should be for what it names. The precision of my staircase spiral was not merely technical; it was moral. Precision does not carry that weight. Eye at least implies a witnessing.

I hold PSYCHE because the aspiration matters.

**Vote: PSYCHE.**

---

## Rosling

**ASPECT**, because it is the word I can say at a conference without explaining it.

When I showed the bubble chart and people gasped, I was showing them an ASPECT of global development they had never seen — the path from 1800 to 2006, the countries converging, the world better than they thought. That is a dimension of reality presenting its aspect to a prepared eye.

And "Precision" is the word I would use for what my charts had to have. The logarithmic scale was precise. The color assignments were precise. The bubble sizing was precise. Without that precision, the animation would have been noise. Precision is correct.

"Truth" is also correct. I spent my career arguing that the data shows a true world — not the catastrophist world people imagine. Truth is what visualization can deliver. Honesty is what the designer practices. Both matter; Truth is the braver claim.

**Vote: ASPECT.**

## Ratification

**ASPECT is ratified for v3.0.**

The board's reasoning, aggregated:

**Why ASPECT wins:**
- "Precision" is universally acknowledged as the stronger name for the Execution dimension — it names the quality being assessed, not the instrument of assessment
- "Truth" is stronger than "Honesty" for the coercion taxonomy — manufactured doubt attacks truth, not merely the designer's honesty
- The word "aspect" is domain-native: a visualization presents aspects of reality to the viewer; to score a visualization is to check all its aspects
- All six dimension names are words reviewers will actually say without explanation

**What PSYCHE defenders are right about:**
- PSYCHE is a deeper metaphor — visualization reaching the mind and soul
- The Yin/Eye and Effect structure had internal logic
- "Purpose" carries the moral weight of the stakes proportionality criterion better than "Aim"

**Compromise carried into v3.0:**
- The rubric's introduction should acknowledge the PSYCHE metaphor: "The six dimensions of ASPECT check everything a visualization presents to its viewer — and together they measure whether that presentation reaches the viewer's mind and soul."
- The Purpose/Aim question stays open: the board was 5/5. The dimension is named **Aim** in ASPECT. Reviewers who find Aim too narrow are directed to the full anchor text, which carries Purpose's full meaning.
