# DEGAS Rubric v1.12

**Forward-only. Amendments ratified from clustered innovations (2+ across 2+ works).**  
**Advisory threshold: < 60. Binding threshold: 60+.**

---

## Dimensions (100 points total)

### 1. School Fidelity (20 pts)

Does the work speak the grammar of its claimed school? Does it use the correct visual vocabulary, respect the school's constraints, and embody its principles?

**Step 0 — Classify the work's relationship to its school(s) before scoring:**

| Type | Definition | Scoring basis |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **A — School Member** | Work operates within an established, documented school; uses its conventions deliberately | Score on fidelity to that school's grammar (anchors below) |
| **B — School Founder** | Work predates or establishes its associated school; the school did not exist when the work was made | Score on internal coherence of the principles deployed and whether they form a learnable grammar |
| **C — Deliberate Synthesis** | Work explicitly operates in two or more established schools simultaneously; synthesis is declared or clearly intentional | Score on fidelity to each participating school averaged, +2 if the synthesis is named and the cross-school tensions are resolved |
| **D — Accidental Mix** | Work mixes school vocabularies without declaration, intention, or coherent resolution | Score on fidelity to no school — treat mixing as a grammar failure at the points of collision |

**Step 0c — School-story match (Cluster K amendment, v1.9):**

Before scoring, assess: is the chosen school appropriate for this story?

| Match level | Description | Adjustment |
|-------------|-------------|------------|
| **Well-matched** | The school's primary encoding grammar is the most efficient and honest way to communicate this story | None |
| **Partial mismatch** | The school can tell the story but a different school would tell it more honestly or more effectively | Note in review; dock 0–2 from School Fidelity |
| **Significant mismatch** | The school's grammar obscures or distorts the story's core argument | Dock 2–4 from School Fidelity; recommend alternate school |

School-story mismatch is distinct from incorrect school application (Type A fidelity failure). A work can apply its school's grammar perfectly and still have chosen the wrong school for the story.

**Anchors (apply after classifying):**

*For Type A (School Member):*

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 18–20 | Genuine instance of the school. An expert would recognize it immediately; nothing violates the grammar. Where conventions are broken, the break is named and justified. |
| 13–17 | Strong fidelity with minor lapses — a technique borrowed elsewhere without declaration, or a convention imperfectly applied. Core grammar respected. |
| 8–12 | Partial fidelity. Claims a school but imports significant elements from elsewhere, or applies school techniques superficially without understanding their rationale. |
| 3–7 | Nominal school claim. Resembles the school only in surface appearance; underlying grammar absent or violated. |
| 0–2 | No demonstrable fidelity. The claim is false or the execution is incompatible with the named school. |

*For Type B (School Founder):*

**Step 0b — Assess grammar declaration (Cluster J amendment):**

Before applying anchors, assess how the founding grammar is transmitted:

| Declaration level | Description | Score modifier |
|------------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Declared in-work** | Grammar explicitly stated in the work itself or in text on the same plate/page (e.g., Playfair's preface, Kandinsky's diagrams-as-demonstration) | +0 to +1 bonus on top of anchor score |
| **Declared in adjacent documentation** | Grammar stated in the immediately adjacent publication, report, or theoretical text (same author, same period) | No modifier — standard anchor applies |
| **Derivable but undeclared** | Grammar recoverable by expert analysis of the work; not stated anywhere by the founder | No modifier — standard anchor applies |
| **Requires reconstruction** | Grammar requires expert multi-work analysis to recover | −1 to −2 below anchor |

A declared grammar is a stronger founding act: it is teachable, not just observable.

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 18–20 | Establishes a coherent set of visual principles that can be learned and repeated. A future practitioner could derive — or has been told — the school's grammar from this work alone. Declared grammars reach this band more readily. |
| 13–17 | Strong founding principles with gaps, or a grammar that is derivable but requires sustained expert analysis. Adjacent-documentation declaration places a work here at its upper end. |
| 8–12 | Some founding principles present but embedded in idiosyncrasy; distinguishing the replicable grammar from personal invention requires expert analysis. |
| 3–7 | Isolated techniques without a coherent underlying system; more accident than grammar. |
| 0–2 | No principles recoverable; the work is inimitable. |

*For Type C (Deliberate Synthesis):*

Score each participating school independently on the Type A anchors, then average. Add up to +2 if the synthesis is named (in the work itself or in authorial documentation) and the cross-school tensions are legibly resolved. Maximum 20.

*For Type D (Accidental Mix):*

Score as Type A, using the school whose grammar is most violated, as the measure of how badly the mixing has broken the design. Cap at 12 regardless of partial fidelities.

### 2. Data/Story Integrity (20 pts)

Is what the visualization claims true? Are encodings accurate? Does the representation honor the underlying data or narrative without distortion?

**Before applying the main anchors, assess three sub-steps. Each sub-step informs the score; they do not replace the anchors.**

**Step A — Source quality declaration:**

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Uncertainty declared (error ranges, provenance notes, explicit caveats) | None |
| Designer unaware of data limitations | −1 per significant undeclared gap |
| Designer knew precision was insufficient but did not declare it (confidence suppression) | −2 to −4 based on severity and how much the implied precision misleads |

**Variable selection disclosure (Cluster L amendment, v1.9):** What variables were encoded, and what was excluded? Is the exclusion disclosed?

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| All relevant variables encoded or exclusions explicitly noted | None |
| Key variables excluded without declaration; exclusion creates a framing effect | Dock −1 to −2 |
| Exclusion actively misleads (e.g., title claims "high-income cities" but income not encoded) | Dock −2 to −3 |

**Step B — Accuracy tradeoff** *(only if a less precise form was chosen over a more accurate alternative):*

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Tradeoff declared and proportionate to advocacy/communication goal | −0 to −1 |
| Tradeoff undeclared but defensible (audience or context makes the choice understandable) | −1 to −2 |
| Tradeoff undeclared and disproportionate | −3 to −4 (treat as design failure) |
| No accuracy tradeoff exists | No adjustment |

**Step C — Causation claim integrity:**

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Causal claim supported by the data as presented | None |
| Causation implied by spatial proximity or structural framing; plausible but not evidenced | −1 to −2 |
| Causal claim significantly overstated relative to available evidence | −3 to −4 |
| Causal claim directly contradicted by data | −5 to −6 |

**Step D — Analytical framework / data model declaration:**

Some works embed a novel data model or analytical framework that is the real intellectual argument — but leave it undeclared, requiring the viewer to supply it.

*Does the work's argument depend on a non-standard data model or analytical framework?*

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Standard, established data model; no novel framework | None |
| **Level 1 — Declared in-work**: novel framework explicitly demonstrated or stated within the visualization itself | −0 to −1 |
| **Level 2 — Declared in adjacent documentation**: novel framework stated in the same publication or author's immediately adjacent text, but not on the plate/image itself | −1 to −2 |
| **Level 3 — Undeclared but inferable**: novel framework recoverable by careful attention to encoding choices; not stated anywhere | −2 to −3 |
| **Level 4 — Undeclared and non-obvious**: framework is the intellectual innovation itself; viewer must supply a premise they are unlikely to hold without instruction | −3 (compound with Step C if wrong framework produces false causal reading) |

*Examples:* Beck's topology-as-data-model (the relevant data is network connection, not geographic position) — novel, undeclared, inferable: −1. Snow's spatial-clustering-as-causal-evidence — novel, undeclared, non-obvious in 1854 (no established epidemiological methodology existed): −2 to −3.

**Step F — Ontological Category Integrity (Cluster N amendment):**

Some visualizations embed moral, political, or social judgments within categories that are presented as empirically neutral. When a category label mixes empirical description with value judgment — presenting the judgment as if it were measured fact — the category is ontologically contaminated.

*Before applying main anchors, assess:*

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Categories are empirically grounded and value-neutral | None |
| Categories embed mild value connotations that are conventional and not actively misleading (e.g., "prime" vs. "subprime" in financial data) | None to −1 |
| Categories embed significant moral or social judgment presented as empirical classification; the contamination shapes the argument without declaration | −3 to −5 |
| Categories present politically constructed classifications as natural kinds (e.g., race as risk category; poverty as moral failure); the contamination produces actively harmful arguments | −5 to −8 |

*Declaration as partial mitigation:* if the ontological contamination is declared ("this category reflects the HOLC assessors' racial assumptions, not independent risk measurement"), dock is reduced by half.

*Examples:*
- Booth poverty maps (0017): "Lowest class, vicious, semi-criminal" — moral judgment presented as social classification. Dock −3 to −4.
- HOLC redlining maps (0022): Race as investment risk proxy — political classification presented as financial measurement. Dock −5 to −7.
- Standard income quartiles: value-neutral economic classification. No dock.

**Main anchors (applied after all sub-steps A–F):**

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 18–20 | Every encoding faithful. Scales honest. Comparisons fair. Source quality declared. No unjustified causal claims. Analytical framework declared. Categories empirically grounded and value-neutral. Sub-steps produce no significant adjustment. |
| 13–17 | High integrity with minor issues. Modest suppression, mild causal implication, minor undeclared framework, or mild categorical connotation. Nothing that materially misleads. |
| 8–12 | Detectable integrity issues. Significant suppression, moderate causal overreach, undeclared novel framework, or moderate ontological category contamination. A careful reader would notice. |
| 3–7 | Significant distortions. Significant category contamination or multiple integrity failures combining to mislead a typical viewer. |
| 0–2 | The visualization is materially false, deceptive, or built on categories that invert or deny the underlying empirical reality. |

**Step E — Demonstrative encoding pathway (Cluster I amendment):**

Some works do not report data — they demonstrate claims by construction. Mathematical proofs, abstract compositional grammars, and logical diagrams prove their claims by letting the viewer verify through participation in the argument, not through correspondence checking.

*Before applying Steps A–D, assess the encoding mode:*

| Mode | Definition | Integrity standard |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Reportive** | The visualization represents external data; accuracy is assessed by correspondence between marks and the data they represent. Apply Steps A–D normally. | Standard Steps A–D |
| **Demonstrative** | The diagram IS the argument; the viewer verifies by following the construction or composition. No external data to correspond to. | Apply Steps E1–E4 instead of A–D |

*For demonstrative works, apply Steps E1–E4:*

**Step E1 — Logical/formal foundation:** Are the premises the demonstration builds from stated or clearly established? Undeclared premises dock −1 to −2.

**Step E2 — Demonstration completeness:** Does the diagram show all steps necessary to follow the argument to its conclusion, or are steps left for the viewer to supply? Missing steps dock −1 to −3 depending on how non-obvious they are.

**Step E3 — Logical validity:** Does the construction actually prove what it claims? A diagram that demonstrates something other than its stated claim, or that has a logical gap the viewer cannot recover from, docks −3 to −5.

**Step E4 — Diagrammatic framework declaration:** Does the diagram declare the rules under which it operates (which marks are construction aids vs. which are the proved figure; which moves are permitted)? Undeclared diagrammatic conventions dock −1 to −2.

*Examples:*
- Euclid's Elements diagrams: demonstrative. Apply E1–E4. Score: 17–19/20 for the classical propositions (premises established by prior propositions; demonstrations complete; constructions valid; conventions of the tradition).
- Kandinsky's Point and Line to Plane diagrams: demonstrative. Apply E1–E4. Score on whether the claimed perceptual properties are demonstrated convincingly (E3 most relevant: color-form correspondence claims dock under E3 when they exceed what the demonstration can show).
- Snow's cholera map: reportive. Apply A–D normally.

### 3. Legibility (15 pts)

Can the intended audience, in the intended context, read and understand it?

**Step 0 — Context specification (before scoring):**

State the following before applying anchors. Reviewers should score against the *primary* context; significant contemporary divergence is noted but does not dominate the score.

| Field | What to specify |
|-------|----------------|
| **Primary audience** | Who the work was designed for (demographic, expertise, cultural background) |
| **Primary reading context** | How they encounter it: *wayfinding* (task under time pressure), *political presentation* (accompanied by the maker), *contemplative study* (solo, extended time), *public display* (glanced, passing) |
| **Contemporary context** | How the work is now primarily encountered, if different from primary |
| **Context decay** | Low / Moderate / High — how much has the original reading context eroded? |

For canonical imports, also note:
- `version_reviewed` — which version of the work is assessed
- `institutional_divergence` — if the work is a living artifact modified after the founder's death, how much does the reviewed version diverge from the contemporary version?

**Reading context sub-types (name one as primary):**

| Sub-type | Definition | What legibility means |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Wayfinding** | Active navigation under time pressure; the viewer has a specific task (find route, choose direction) | Task completion speed and reliability; error rate for the navigation question |
| **Political presentation** | Accompanied by the maker or a mediator who can explain; a specific decision-making audience | Whether the core argument reaches the decision-maker in the time available |
| **Contemplative study** | Solo, unaccompanied, extended time; viewer reads for full comprehension | Completeness of information available without external help |
| **Public display** | Glanced in passing; no guarantee of sustained attention | Whether the gestalt and headline are legible in under 5 seconds |

**Main anchors (applied to primary context):**

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 13–15 | In the primary reading context, the intended audience extracts the necessary information without friction. The design serves the context's specific legibility demands precisely. |
| 9–12 | Good legibility in the primary context with minor friction — one element requires more effort than the context demands. Core message accessible. |
| 5–8 | Partial legibility in the primary context. The audience can understand with effort or guidance beyond what the context provides. |
| 2–4 | Significant legibility failure in the primary context. The audience cannot reliably extract the necessary information even with effort. |
| 0–1 | Inaccessible in the primary context. |

**Narration mode (v1.8 addition):**

Some works are designed for narrated deployment — the maker or a mediator speaks while the visualization is displayed. In these cases, add a narration-mode note to the context specification:

- `narration_mode: standalone` — the work is designed to function without accompanying narration; score legibility against standalone comprehension
- `narration_mode: accompanied` — the work is designed with narration as part of the encoding; score legibility against what a viewer with the standard accompanying narration can understand; note standalone legibility in brackets as `[standalone: X/15]`

For Resonance, narration-mode accompanied works may produce aha moments through the narration-visualization combination that neither delivers alone; score the combination as the primary resonance experience.

**Contemporary context note (append to score, do not replace it):**

If the contemporary context differs significantly from the primary context, append: `[contemporary: X/15]` after the primary score, with a brief explanation. This captures context decay without distorting the primary assessment.

### 4. Visual Economy (15 pts)

Are visual elements doing necessary work? Score on excess above what the content and context require — not on total complexity.

**Step 0a — Intrinsic complexity assessment (Cluster H + W amendment):**

Before scoring, assess whether visual complexity is intrinsic or additive:

| Type | Definition | Scoring basis |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Intrinsic** | The subject matter is genuinely multi-variable, structurally complex, or requires high mark density to represent accurately (biological morphology, multi-dimensional ecological systems, dense networks) | Score on excess *above* the intrinsic necessity. A work with intrinsic complexity at high fidelity scores 12–14 regardless of total mark count. |
| **Ecology-as-composition** (Cluster W) | When the scientific subject IS an ecosystem, the spatial distribution of species creates a compositional rhythm that is simultaneously data and aesthetic structure — the density gradient of organisms across zones is the ecological fact. This is not additive complexity; the arrangement IS the data. | Score as intrinsic. The density gradient, species layering, and zone transitions are all required by the subject. Only ornamental framing above-and-beyond the ecological content is additive. Works 0031 + 0033 established this. |
| **Additive** | Complexity added by the designer beyond what the data or subject requires — decorative borders, ornamental framing, repeated motifs that serve aesthetic rather than communicative purposes | Score on the full excess; these marks compete with the data without earning their place. |
| **Structural-attentional** | Marks that serve a display or compositional function (framing, visual hierarchy cues, attention-directing borders) without encoding data — neither pure data nor pure decoration | Score lightly (−1 to −2); these marks may justify themselves by serving the work's reading context even without encoding data directly. |

**Step 0b — Audience and context assessment (Cluster G amendment):**

For works designed for low-literacy or non-specialist audiences in public-display contexts, the economy criterion shifts from *data-ink efficiency* to *cognitive economy* — the number of mental steps required for the intended audience to reach accurate comprehension.

| Audience + Context | Economy criterion |
|-------------------|------------------|
| Expert / contemplative-study | Data-ink ratio: minimize marks per datum |
| General public / contemplative-study | Data-ink ratio with accessibility allowance |
| Low-literacy or non-specialist / public-display | Cognitive economy: minimize cognitive steps to comprehension for the specific audience. Repeated pictographic symbols may be more economical than abstract encodings despite higher mark count. |

**Mark categories (apply after Steps 0a and 0b):**

| Category | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|
| Data marks | Required; do not penalize |
| Intrinsic-complexity marks | Required by the subject; do not penalize beyond 12–14 floor |
| Structural-attentional marks | Lightly penalize (−1 to −2 total) unless clearly functional |
| Cognitive-accessibility marks | In low-literacy public-display contexts: do not penalize if they demonstrably reduce cognitive steps |
| Decorative marks | Penalize; these are chartjunk |

**Main anchors (applied after Steps 0a and 0b):**

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 13–15 | Every mark earns its place by data, intrinsic necessity, structural function, or cognitive accessibility. The design achieves maximum comprehension for its audience with minimum unjustified complexity. |
| 9–12 | High economy with minor excess above intrinsic/contextual necessity. Nothing that significantly burdens reading. |
| 5–8 | Detectable decorative or additive excess that taxes the viewer without serving data, subject, or audience. |
| 2–4 | Significant visual noise. The data fights its surroundings. |
| 0–1 | The design is dominated by unjustified elements. The data is barely visible. |

### 5. Resonance (15 pts)

Does the visualization land as intended? Does it create the right effect for the right reason?

**Step 0 — Classify primary resonance type(s) before scoring:**

| Type | Definition | Score on |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **I — Aesthetic-Intentional** | Designer deliberately constructed an emotional, aesthetic, or intellectual effect | Whether the intended effect is fully achieved; whether design choices are consistent in serving it |
| **II — Emergent-Data** | Affect arises from encoding fidelity to human-scale subject matter, not from aesthetic choice | Whether the encoding is faithful enough to allow emergence; whether any choices suppress it |
| **III — Advocacy-Efficacy** | Work designed to move a specific audience toward a specific action or belief | Whether the audience is correctly identified and the form appropriate to them; whether the work achieved or plausibly could achieve its goal |
| **IV — Gestalt** | Core argument available from visual shape alone, before any label is parsed | Whether a 3-second visual impression carries the essential message; whether the gestalt is correct |
| **V — Domain-Gated** | Full resonance available immediately to domain-initiated viewers (practitioners, regular users); partial or absent for viewers without domain knowledge | Whether the domain-initiated viewer receives immediate, complete recognition; whether the domain-gatedness is appropriate to the work's purpose (functional and scientific works are legitimately not designed for general-public resonance) |
| **VI — Delayed** | Work achieves its strongest resonance after the maker's lifetime — through posthumous recognition, delayed adoption, or accumulating cultural significance | Assessed on two axes: (a) speed of recognition (contemporaneous / delayed / very-delayed) and (b) agency of canonization (see matrix below). Use to supplement primary type score, not replace it. |

**Type VI — Delayed Resonance: the mechanism matrix (Cluster E2 amendment):**

The content-based sub-types (epistemic, political-testimonial, scholarly canonization, etc.) did not scale. Replace with a mechanism-based assessment on two dimensions:

*Dimension 1 — Speed of recognition:*
- **Contemporaneous**: recognized and resonant at the time of creation; no delay
- **Delayed**: recognized significantly after the maker's lifetime (decades); within the same discipline
- **Very delayed**: recognized across disciplines or eras; construction of significance by later actors

*Dimension 2 — Agency of canonization:*
- **Self-evident**: the resonance accumulates naturally from the work's qualities; no single actor constructed it
- **Scholarly constructed**: historians, critics, or educators elevated the work to canonical status; the elevation was an act of curation
- **Politically constructed**: the work was suppressed, ignored, or delayed due to identity-based or political discrimination; later recognition corrects this
- **Institutionally transmitted**: the work was transmitted through pedagogical or professional lineages (textbooks, curricula, professional training) that propagated its significance

*Scoring Type VI (use in addition to primary type):*
For delayed works, note the delay mechanism as `[speed × agency]` and apply:
- Contemporaneous / self-evident: no Type VI adjustment (already captured in primary type)
- Delayed / scholarly constructed: +0 bonus (recognition is real but constructed)
- Delayed / politically constructed: +1 bonus (the delay itself is evidence of the work's threat to power structures — a form of resonance)
- Very delayed / institutionally transmitted: +0 bonus (institutionally canonical; the construction is visible and accepted)

*Examples from the project:*
- Snow 0005: Delayed / scholarly constructed (epidemiology historians elevated the map; germ theory arrived posthumously)
- Du Bois 0004: Delayed / politically constructed (+1 bonus — the delay was caused by institutional racism; the 2018 rediscovery is itself evidence of the work's power)
- Haeckel 0006: Delayed / institutionally transmitted (Art Nouveau designers appropriated the forms; designers, not scientists, constructed the canonical status)
- Playfair 0009: Contemporaneous / self-evident (the bar chart was adopted rapidly; no delay)

**Dynamic variable note (Cluster O amendment — v1.11):**

Works that use a dynamic variable (time-as-animation, interaction-as-viewer-authorship, accumulation-over-real-time) encode their primary argument in a dimension inaccessible to static reproduction. Before scoring Resonance for dynamic works:

| Dynamic variable type | Definition | Resonance scoring |
|----------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Time-as-animation** | The visualization runs forward through time; the viewer watches the argument unfold (Rosling Gapminder) | Score the animated presentation experience as primary; note static-reproduction resonance in brackets |
| **Interaction-as-viewer-authorship** | The viewer makes choices that reveal different paths through the data; the aha moment comes from exploring (NYT 512 Paths) | Score the interactive exploration experience as primary; note single-state resonance in brackets |
| **Accumulation** | Data builds up over real time or repeated events; the texture that emerges IS the argument (Koblin flight patterns) | Score the accumulated final state plus the accumulation process as primary |

For static reproduction of dynamic works: append `[static: X/15]` after the primary dynamic score. Static reproduction can reduce Resonance by 20–30% for time-animation works.

A work can have multiple active resonance types. Identify the primary type and apply the anchors to it. Each additional type that is also achieved contributes +0 to +1 bonus (maximum 15 total).

**Main anchors (applied to primary type):**

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 13–15 | The work fully achieves its primary resonance type. Type-specific criteria are met. The effect is clear, consistent, and persists after the reading. |
| 9–12 | Solid resonance with minor misfires — the emotional register / advocacy impact / gestalt clarity is right but slightly muted, or one element undercuts the overall effect. |
| 5–8 | Partial resonance. The work communicates correctly but the primary resonance type fails or only partially fires. |
| 2–4 | Wrong effect for the type: confusion where there should be affect, inertia where there should be urgency, a gestalt that contradicts the intended message. |
| 0–1 | No detectable resonance of any type. |

### 6. Craft (15 pts)

Technical execution quality. Is the rendering precise, consistent, and polished?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 13–15 | The work is precisely executed. Typography is legible and consistent. Color relationships are controlled. Similar elements look similar. The rendering would not embarrass a professional. |
| 9–12 | Good craft with minor inconsistencies — a slightly misaligned element, an uneven spacing, a color relationship that is almost right. Nothing that damages the reading. |
| 5–8 | Detectable craft failures that undermine trust — inconsistent labeling, clashing colors, ragged alignment. |
| 2–4 | Significant execution failures. The work looks unfinished or careless. |
| 0–1 | The execution is incompetent. The work cannot be taken seriously as a rendered artifact. |

## Amendment Protocol

Amendments to the rubric follow the same logic as sister repos:

1. **Log innovations** via `degas-innovation` during or after panel sessions
2. When 2+ innovations across 2+ works form a cluster, **propose amendment** via `degas-amendment`
3. Amendment ratified if the cluster is coherent and the rubric text would improve
4. New rubric version applies **forward only** — no retroactive rescoring
