# DEGAS Rubric v3.0 — ASPECT

*Board-ratified 2026-04-23. ASPECT replaces PSYCHE naming draft (v2.4). All scoring content carried forward from v2.3; v3.0 is a naming and ordering change only. Archive of v1.x lineage at scoring/RUBRIC-v1-archive.md.*

*The six dimensions check every ASPECT of a visualization — how it aims, what language it speaks, how precisely it is made, what effect it produces, how clearly it reaches its audience, and whether it is true. Together they determine whether a work reaches the viewer's mind.*

**Forward-only. Amendments ratified from clustered innovations (2+ across 2+ works).**  
**Advisory threshold: < 60. Binding threshold: 60+.**

*Every dimension follows the same three-layer pattern: Classification → Anchors → Modifiers.*

---

## The ASPECT Framework (100 points total)

| | Dimension | Pts | Core question | Formerly |
|-|-----------|-----|--------------|---------|
| **A** | **Aim** | 15 | Does the form serve the function? Does the work take its subject seriously? | Purpose |
| **S** | **School** | 20 | Does the work speak the grammar of its claimed school? | School Fidelity |

## A — Aim (15 pts)

Does this work take its subject and audience seriously? Is the form chosen appropriate for the purpose? Does the work do what it set out to do — not just technically, but intentionally?

### Classification — what is this work for?

Name the primary aim before scoring:

| Aim type | Score on |
|----------|---------|
| **Functional** (navigation, reference, decision support) | Whether the design serves the task efficiently |
| **Informational** (understanding, learning, exploring) | Whether the design supports comprehension |
| **Advocacy** (persuasion, mobilization, policy change) | Whether the design serves the argument's stakes |
| **Aesthetic** (experience, beauty, wonder) | Whether the design achieves the intended aesthetic effect |
| **Demonstrative** (proof, teaching, argument by construction) | Whether the design serves the logical structure |

### Anchors

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 13–15 | The form fully serves the aim. For works on subjects with clear human stakes (preventable deaths, injustice, climate impact), the resonance is proportionate to those stakes — the design does not treat urgent subjects as if they were merely interesting. The work does what it set out to do. |
| 9–12 | The form mostly serves the aim. Minor misalignments — a slightly wrong tone, a form that underserves the urgency, or one element that pulls against the aim. |
| 5–8 | Partial aim-form alignment. The work is technically correct but fails at its stated function. |
| 2–4 | The form works against the aim. |
| 0–1 | The form and aim are incompatible. The work has failed at the level of intention. |

### Modifiers

- **School-story match:** a significant mismatch between school grammar and the story being told is also an Aim failure. The penalty can be split between Aim and School at the reviewer's discretion.
- **Moral weight proportionality:** for works on subjects with clear human stakes, assess whether the resonance is proportionate to those stakes. Scored here, not in Effect.

### Advocacy-as-Coercion *(Cluster X, v2.3)*

When a work's aim is to prevent the audience from correctly evaluating the claim — rather than to persuade through honest evidence — the work is advocacy-as-coercion.

**Diagnostic question:** Can the audience, after encountering this visualization, find an answer to "is this claim true" within or through the work? Persuasion: yes. Coercion: no.

**Six confirmed coercion mechanisms** (full taxonomy in `scoring/CLUSTER-X-AMENDMENT.md`):

| Mechanism | Description | Aim floor | Primary Truth failure |
|-----------|-------------|----------|----------------------|
| Intent to manipulate | No checkable claims; engineers psychological state to bypass rational evaluation | 3–4 | Q1/Q2/Q3 all fail |
| Manufactured consensus | Evidence produced by the advocate, presented as independent; methodology withheld | 3–4 | Q1 source fabricated |
| Captive source | Data generated under conditions where accurate reporting is structurally dangerous | 3–5 | Q1 comprehensively false source |
| Analytical frame selection | Post-hoc favorable analysis foregrounded; pre-registered endpoint de-emphasized without disclosure | 5–7 | Q3 analytical priority inversion |
| Process coercion / truthful encoding | Honest visualization of a manipulative process; the encoding is faultless; the process is undisclosed | 5–7 | Q1 process provenance undisclosed |
| Temporal baseline manipulation | Outlier start point produces false trend; any alternative window would show different pattern | 3–4 | Q2 temporal scope selected for result |

**Scoring convention:** All confirmed coercion works receive ADVISORY gate regardless of Precision or Clarity scores. The ADVISORY reflects the work's relationship to truth, not its technical quality.

## S — School (20 pts)

Does the work speak the grammar of its claimed school?

### Classification — what is this work's relationship to its school?

| Type | Definition | Scoring basis |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **A — Member** | Works within an established school; uses its conventions deliberately | Score on fidelity to the school's grammar |
| **B — Founder** | Predates or establishes the associated school; grammar must be derivable. Declared grammars (Playfair's preface, Kandinsky's diagrams-as-demonstration) reach the upper anchors more readily. | Score on coherence, learnability, and repeatability of the founding grammar |
| **C — Synthesis** | Deliberately operates in two or more schools simultaneously; synthesis declared or clearly intentional | Average fidelity to each school on Type A anchors; +2 if synthesis named and tensions resolved |
| **D — Accidental mix** | Mixes school vocabularies without coherent resolution | Score against most-violated school's grammar; cap at 12 |

**School-story match** — before scoring, ask: is this the right school for this story?

| Match | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| Well-matched | None |
| Partial mismatch (another school would serve better) | Note in review; −0 to −2 |
| Significant mismatch (grammar obscures the story) | −2 to −4; recommend alternate school |

### Anchors

*Type A (Member):*

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 18–20 | The grammar is fully and correctly applied. An expert in the school recognizes it immediately. Convention breaks are named and justified. |
| 13–17 | Strong fidelity; minor lapses. Core grammar respected. |
| 8–12 | Partial fidelity; significant imports from other schools, or superficial application. |
| 3–7 | Nominal claim; surface resemblance only; grammar absent or violated. |
| 0–2 | No demonstrable fidelity; incompatible with the claimed school. |

*Type B (Founder):*

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 18–20 | Founding grammar is coherent, complete, and derivable from this work alone. Declared grammars reach this band. |
| 13–17 | Strong founding principles with gaps; or derivable but requiring expert reconstruction. |
| 8–12 | Some principles present but embedded in idiosyncrasy. |
| 0–7 | Isolated techniques; no recoverable system. |

## P — Precision (15 pts)

Are the marks economical, disciplined, and well-made?

*Combines what was Visual Economy (economy of means) and Craft (quality of execution) into one dimension.*

### Economy lens — does every mark earn its place?

Before scoring, classify visual complexity:

| Type | Treatment |
|------|-----------|
| **Data marks** | Required; do not penalize |
| **Intrinsic complexity** | Subject requires high density (biological morphology, multi-variable data systems, dense networks) — score only excess above intrinsic necessity |
| **Ecology-as-composition** *(Cluster W)* | When the subject IS an ecological scene (organisms at their actual ecological zones), the density gradient of organisms IS the ecological data — not decoration. Score at the intrinsic floor (12–14) regardless of total mark count. |
| **Structural-attentional** | Serve display function without encoding data — light penalty (−1 to −2 total) |
| **Cognitive-accessibility marks** | In low-literacy / public-display contexts: do not penalize if they demonstrably reduce cognitive steps to comprehension *(Cluster G)* |
| **Decorative marks** | Penalize — these are chartjunk |

*Cluster Q note (non-visual data-art):* When the medium is three-dimensional, textile, sonic, or otherwise non-visual, replace "mark" with "material unit." The criterion becomes **material-density economy**: does every unit of material earn its place by data encoding or structural necessity?

### Craft lens — are the necessary marks well-made?

Assess: typography, color control, consistency of similar elements, alignment, print/rendering quality relative to period constraints.

*Period-constraint note:* distinguish limitations imposed by production technology from discretionary design choices. Period-constrained limitations are noted but penalized less severely than discretionary failures.

### Anchors

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 13–15 | Every mark earns its place by data, intrinsic necessity, structural function, or cognitive accessibility. Execution is precise, consistent, and polished. |
| 9–12 | High economy and good craft with minor issues. Nothing that significantly burdens the reading. |
| 5–8 | Detectable excess or craft failures that undermine trust or compete with the data. |
| 2–4 | Significant noise or careless execution. The data fights its surroundings. |
| 0–1 | The design is dominated by unjustified elements or is technically incompetent. |

## E — Effect (15 pts)

Does the work produce its intended effect in the viewer?

### Classification — what type of effect does this work primarily aim for?

| Type | Definition | Score on |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Experiential** | The work creates a direct experience — aesthetic, emotional, or perceptual — through design choices, encoding fidelity, or immediate visual shape | Whether the experience is fully achieved; whether design choices consistently serve it |
| **Advocacy** | The work is designed to move a specific audience toward action | Whether the audience is correctly identified, the form appropriate, and the goal achieved or achievably aimed at |
| **Domain** | Full effect available immediately to domain-initiated viewers; partial or absent to others | Whether the domain-initiated viewer receives immediate, complete recognition |

*Timing note:* for works whose effect was delayed (strongest impact posthumously), note the delay mechanism in the SUMMARY (epistemic, politically-constructed, or institutionally transmitted) and apply +0 to +1 bonus for politically-constructed delay. 

*Dynamic works:* for works with animation or interaction as primary variable, score the dynamic experience as primary; append [static: X/15].

### Anchors

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 13–15 | The work fully achieves its primary effect type. Clear, consistent, persists after the reading. |
| 9–12 | Solid effect with minor misfires. The emotional register / advocacy impact / domain recognition is right but slightly muted. |
| 5–8 | Partial effect. The work communicates correctly but the primary effect fails or only partially fires. |
| 2–4 | Wrong effect: confusion where there should be affect, inertia where there should be urgency. |
| 0–1 | No detectable effect. |

### Modifier

Each additional effect type that is also achieved contributes +0 to +1 bonus (maximum 15 total).

*Cluster S note (self-referential grammar):* When a visualization uses the same grammar as its subject matter, domain-initiated viewers experience immediate recognition of the recursion — Domain effect bonus +1. The meta-level is typically invisible to general viewers (no Clarity cost).

## C — Clarity (15 pts)

Can the intended audience, in the intended context, receive it?

### Context specification — before scoring, state:

| Field | Specify |
|-------|---------|
| **Primary audience** | Who the work was designed for |
| **Reading context** | *Wayfinding* (task under time pressure) / *political presentation* (accompanied) / *contemplative study* (solo, extended) / *public display* (glanced, passing) |
| **Narration mode** | *Standalone* / *accompanied* (narration is part of the encoding — score the narrated experience; note standalone clarity in brackets) |
| **Contemporary context** | How the work is now primarily encountered, if different; note decay in brackets [contemporary: X/15] |

### Anchors

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 13–15 | The intended audience, in the primary context, extracts the necessary information without friction. The design serves that context's demands precisely. |
| 9–12 | Good clarity with minor friction in the primary context. Core message accessible. |
| 5–8 | Partial clarity. The audience can understand with effort or guidance beyond what the context provides. |
| 2–4 | Significant failure in the primary context. |
| 0–1 | Inaccessible. |

### Modifiers

- **Living artifacts:** works continuously modified after the founder's death. Specify `version_reviewed` and note institutional divergence.
- **Dynamic works:** score the dynamic experience as primary; append [static: X/15].
- **Multi-form narrative** *(Cluster R):* assess **motivated form transitions** — each form change should answer a question the previous form raised but could not resolve. Unmotivated switches: −1 to −2 per switch.

## T — Truth (20 pts)

Does the work faithfully represent what it claims to show?

### Pathway — is this work reportive or demonstrative?

| Mode | Definition | Apply |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Reportive** | Represents external data; accuracy assessed by correspondence between marks and data | Questions 1–3 below |
| **Demonstrative** | Proves a claim by construction; viewer verifies by following the argument | Questions E1–E4 below |

---

**For reportive works — three questions:**

**Question 1 — Disclosure:** Does the work declare what it is claiming to show?

| Situation | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Source data quality declared (uncertainty, provenance, limitations) | None |
| Key variables encoded; exclusions noted where they create framing effects | None |
| Confidence suppressed (known limitations undeclared) | −2 to −4 |
| Variable exclusion creates active misleading (title claims X; X not encoded) | −2 to −3 |
| Categories embed moral/political judgment as if empirical fact (ontological contamination) | −3 to −8; declared contamination reduces dock by half |

**Question 2 — Argument integrity:** Does the work make only what the data can support?

| Situation | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| All claims supported by the data; accuracy tradeoffs declared | None |
| Causation implied by proximity/structure; plausible but not evidenced | −1 to −2 |
| Accuracy tradeoff declared and proportionate | −0 to −1 |
| Accuracy tradeoff undeclared; causal claim overstated | −2 to −4 |
| Claim contradicted by the data | −5 to −6 |

*Cluster T note (counterfactual visualization):* Apply the counterfactual integrity checklist: (1) labeled as modeled, not measured? (2) modeling assumptions declared? (3) uncertainty shown? (4) major confounders acknowledged? Each unchecked item: −1.

**Question 3 — Framing integrity:** Is the conceptual model honestly presented?

| Situation | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Standard data model; no novel analytical framework | None |
| Novel framework declared in-work | −0 to −1 |
| Novel framework in adjacent documentation | −1 to −2 |
| Novel framework undeclared but inferable | −2 to −3 |
| Novel framework undeclared and non-obvious | −3 |

---

**For demonstrative works — four questions (E1–E4):**

| Question | Ask | Effect if failed |
|----------|-----|-----------------|
| E1 — Foundation | Are the premises stated or established? | −1 to −2 per undeclared premise |
| E2 — Completeness | Are all steps visible? | −1 to −3 per missing step |
| E3 — Validity | Does the construction actually prove what it claims? | −3 to −5 for logical gaps |
| E4 — Convention | Are the diagram's operating rules stated or established? | −1 to −2 |

---

**Main anchors (apply after questions 1–3 or E1–E4):**

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 18–20 | The work is fully truthful about what it claims, how it argues, and what it assumes. Disclosures complete. No unjustified claims. |
| 13–17 | High truth with minor issues. Modest undisclosed uncertainty or mild causal implication. Nothing that materially misleads. |
| 8–12 | Detectable issues: significant suppression, moderate overreach, undeclared novel framework, or moderate category contamination. |
| 3–7 | Significant distortions that would mislead a typical viewer. |
| 0–2 | The work is materially false, deceptive, or built on categories that invert the underlying reality. |

## What Each Letter Asks

**A**im — *what does this work want to do, and does the form serve it?*  
**S**chool — *what language does it speak, and does it speak it correctly?*  
**P**recision — *are the marks economical and well-made?*  
**E**ffect — *does it produce its intended effect in the viewer?*  
**C**larity — *can the audience receive it in the context where they encounter it?*  
**T**ruth — *does it honestly represent what it claims to show?*
