# Brief: Minard's Carte figurative (Napoleon's March, 1869)

*Canonical import — reviewing an existing historical work, not a DEGAS-generated design.*

## Subject

The visualization depicts the fate of the French Grande Armée during Napoleon's 1812 Russian campaign. It encodes six variables simultaneously:

1. **Army size** — the width of the band (in men); advance and retreat separately
2. **Geographic position** — plotted on a simplified map of Eastern Europe/Western Russia, with named cities and rivers
3. **Direction of movement** — color: tan/buff = advance toward Moscow; black = retreat westward
4. **Army position over time** — the horizontal axis of the band traces the route geographically
5. **Temperature during the retreat** — a separate line chart below the map, in degrees Réaumur, with dates
6. **Key dates** — labeled on the temperature chart; linked to the band position above by shared horizontal position

The army entered Russia at the Niemen River with approximately 422,000 men. It returned with approximately 10,000.

## Audience

The original audience was French educated public and officials in 1869. The work has since become a universal reference for any audience studying data visualization — it is the canonical example taught in every visualization course globally.

## School

Primarily cartographic; simultaneously statistical-graphics. The work sits at an intersection that had no name in 1869. The map base uses cartographic conventions (rivers, cities, national borders simplified). The band uses a quantitative graphic encoding (width = quantity). The temperature chart is a line graph — squarely statistical. The work does not fit cleanly into any single school documented in DEGAS.
