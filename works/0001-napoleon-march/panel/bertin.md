## Review

I will conduct a visual variable audit of this work. This is how I am most useful.

**Variable audit:**

The work deploys six encoded variables. I will assess each encoding against the retinal properties of the visual variable chosen.

*Army size → band width (size variable):* Size is ordered and quantitative. Army size is quantitative. The match is correct. Moreover, size at this scale (band width from ~10mm to near-zero) is perceptually estimable: the viewer can form an approximate ratio (this band is one-fifth the original width) before they read the numeric label. This is the most successful encoding in the work. The viewer reads the quantitative loss without counting.

*Direction → color hue (tan vs. black):* This is where I have my most significant critique. Hue is a selective variable — appropriate for nominal categorical data. The direction variable (advance vs. retreat) is indeed nominal: two categories with no intrinsic order. So the hue choice is technically correct by my grammar. However, the specific hues chosen (warm tan, cold black) introduce connotative order — the viewer reads tan as living/forward and black as dead/backward. This connotative ordering is not declared by the encoding grammar; it operates below the level of the visual variable system. I call this a **connotative variable** — a layer of encoding that uses cultural association rather than retinal property. My grammar does not account for this. It works, but it works by means I did not anticipate.

*Geographic position → X,Y on map base:* Position is the most powerful retinal variable — the eye reads it first and most reliably. Using position for geographic coordinates is the standard cartographic convention; it is correct and efficient.

*Route/sequence → continuous band path:* This is a compound encoding. The path through space implies temporal sequence without a separate time variable. The reader understands that left-to-right (in the advance band) is also earlier-to-later. This is a geographic-temporal compression that position alone does not normally achieve. It works only because the army moved in one direction. If the army had doubled back frequently, the encoding would fail.

*Temperature → vertical position in lower register:* Position (vertical) for quantitative data is correct by my grammar — position is ordered and quantitative. The Réaumur scale is labeled; the axis is legible.

*Date → horizontal position in lower register:* Here the encoding collision occurs. Horizontal position is simultaneously encoding geographic longitude (in the upper register) and temporal date (in the lower register). Both are ordered, quantitative variables, and both are correctly matched to position. But they are different data, mapped to the same axis. The axis is overloaded. It works because geography and time coincide in this particular historical event, but the encoding does not declare that coincidence — it assumes it.

**On School Fidelity:** I score this 14 because the work operates in no single school. My semiology applies to both cartography and statistical graphics; this work uses both simultaneously. Where the two schools agree (position for quantitative data), the work is excellent. Where they diverge (the overloaded horizontal axis), the work creates a collision that is not resolved at the level of the encoding grammar.

**On Legibility:** The overloaded horizontal axis is my primary legibility concern. A reader who understands the geographic encoding will not immediately understand that the same horizontal position means something different in the lower register. The reader must discover the two interpretations and then reconcile them. This takes time and effort that could be reduced by declaration.

**On Visual Economy:** One deduction: the geographic base map includes some terrain suggestion (the light gray shading) that does not encode data. It is contextual. I understand the argument for it — it places the army in a landscape — but it is not a data encoding. It is decoration that happens to be tasteful.

**On Resonance:** I am not primarily a theorist of emotional effect. But I note that the work's most powerful moment — the narrowing of the retreat band to near-nothing — is achieved entirely through the size variable. No color change signals the crisis (black is used throughout the retreat); no position change signals it. Only width. The eye follows the width and sees the catastrophe in the geometry of the path itself. This is a retinal effect, not a connotative one. It is, I think, the purest form of resonance I know how to name.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Connotative variable layer** — encodings that use cultural association (color temperature: warm = life, cold = death) in addition to retinal variable properties. The hue choice (tan/black) encodes directionality twice: once categorically (hue = nominal category), once connotatively (warm/cold = forward/backward, alive/dead). My grammar addresses retinal properties; it does not address connotative layers. This is a genuine gap. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity / Data Integrity. Implication: the rubric should recognize connotative encoding as a distinct layer from retinal encoding, and score whether it reinforces or contradicts the retinal message.*

2. **Geographic-temporal compression via monotonic path** — using geographic path to imply temporal sequence, which works only when the subject moves monotonically through space and time. The encoding is efficient and powerful, but it is fragile: it would fail if the army had retreated and re-advanced. The rubric has no way to score the structural precondition on which an encoding depends. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity. Implication: the rubric should assess whether an encoding's success depends on structural preconditions in the data — and whether those preconditions are declared or assumed.*
