## Review

I will try to be honest about this, though it is a peculiar position to be asked to review one's own work.

**On School Fidelity:** I must score this below perfect because the work belongs to no school that existed when I made it. I was trained as a civil engineer. I made maps for railway and canal commissions — infrastructure maps, route surveys. What I did with the figurative maps was something different, something I had to invent as I went. The cartographic tradition gave me the base map. The statistical tradition gave me the number. I gave the number a body. There was no school for that. If the panel requires a school, I offer: the work founds something rather than belongs to something. I do not know how the rubric scores a founder. That is a problem for the rubric, not for the work.

**On Data/Story Integrity:** The troop figures are not census figures. They are estimates assembled from French military records, many of them destroyed or falsified. I believe they are substantially correct; I do not believe they are precise. The temperature figures are from Captain Louis-Joseph de Caulaincourt's journal, corroborated by other sources. The Réaumur scale requires conversion for modern readers, but it was standard in 1812. I lose two points because I cannot certify the precision of the source data, and because I give no indication of the data's provenance in the work itself. A citation would not have fit; that is not an excuse.

**On Legibility:** The six-variable encoding is not simple. A reader encountering this work without instruction will spend several minutes before they understand the temperature chart's relationship to the map above it. The title block explains the encoding in prose — I list all six variables — but prose embedded in a graphic is a weak legend. The reader must translate words back into the image. I accept this penalty.

**On Visual Economy:** I removed everything I could. The base map carries only what the band needs: rivers (because they caused the worst losses at the crossings), cities (because they mark the dates), and nothing else. No decorative border. No cartouche. No compass rose. The only elements present are the ones that answer a question. I would not change this.

**On Resonance:** This I will not be modest about. The band begins wide and ends thin as a thread. The reader knows 422,000 men. The reader sees 10,000 men. The reader does not need me to tell them what happened. The encoding is the mourning. I have thought for a long time about why this is — why showing numbers works where describing them fails — and I believe it is because the eye sees the whole loss at once, as a shape, before the mind has assembled the count. The grief is spatial before it is arithmetic.

**On Craft:** The registration between the geographic band and the temperature chart is not perfect. At the Berezina River crossing — the worst moment of the retreat, where thousands drowned or froze — the alignment between the band above and the temperature data below is slightly off. A reader following the thread carefully will notice. This is an execution failure I regret.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Multi-school synthesis as founding act** — the work does not belong to a school; it founds an intersection. The rubric's School Fidelity dimension assumes a school exists to be faithful to. Works that precede their schools — or that synthesize multiple schools — require a different scoring framework. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity. Implication: the rubric should distinguish between school-member works and school-founding works.*

2. **Spatial mourning** — resonance achieved through quantitative encoding alone, without aesthetic manipulation. The band is not designed to be beautiful or tragic; it is designed to be accurate. The tragedy is a product of the data, not of the design choices above the data. *Rubric anchor: Resonance. Implication: the rubric should distinguish between aesthetic-intentional resonance and emergent-data resonance.*
