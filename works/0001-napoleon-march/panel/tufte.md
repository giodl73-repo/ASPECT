## Review

I called this the best statistical graphic ever drawn. I stand by that. I also intend to score it honestly, which I suspect will produce a number lower than the reputation warrants. Reputation is not a rubric.

**On School Fidelity:** I have to accept a middling score here for reasons that I think expose a rubric problem rather than a work problem. The work does not operate in a single school. It is a map and it is a chart and it is neither. My instinct is that the School Fidelity dimension does not apply well to works of this synthetic type, and that scoring it here produces a distortion — a penalty for invention rather than for failure. I will note this for the panel. I score 15 to indicate strong fidelity to the principles of both participating schools, minus the penalty for the ambiguity.

**On Data/Story Integrity:** This is where I am genuinely critical. The troop figures are assembled from fragmentary military records, as Minard himself acknowledged. The temperature data comes from a single officer's journal, Caulaincourt. There is no error indication on either dataset. The visualization presents estimates with the confidence of census figures. In 1869 this was standard practice — data provenance was not a convention of the form. In contemporary standards, presenting data of this quality without uncertainty indicators is a significant integrity issue. I deduct three points. The data is probably substantially correct; it is not certified as such, and the form does not declare the uncertainty.

**On Legibility:** The work requires time. Specifically, it requires the reader to understand the relationship between the two registers — to realize that the horizontal axis is doing double duty: geography above, time below. This is a non-trivial encoding. Once understood, it is clear and powerful. Before it is understood, the work is confusing. I do not penalize complexity per se, but I do penalize designs that withhold their logic from the first-time reader without instruction. The prose legend (the title block) is doing work that the visual structure should do. Twelve points.

**On Visual Economy:** Fifteen out of fifteen. I have nothing to remove from this image. Not one line. Not one label. The base map carries only what serves the band. The band carries only what serves the data. The temperature chart adds variables without adding clutter. The data-ink ratio is not perfect — it is approximately 90% — but the remaining 10% (base map, gray terrain) is doing work: it is the geographic scaffold without which the band has no position. I would not call that chartjunk. I call it necessary context and I score it accordingly.

**On Resonance:** This is interesting territory for me, because I am more interested in clarity than in emotional effect. But the work achieves both simultaneously, and I have to credit that. The visual argument is devastating: the band goes out and almost nothing returns. The reader does not compute this; they see it. That is a genuine achievement. I deduct one point because the temperature chart's emotional weight — which is considerable; it is −30°R at the Berezina — is somewhat disconnected from the band. The reader has to actively make the link between cold and loss. The work provides the materials but not the final connection.

**On Craft:** Two deductions. First: the temperature registration problem Minard flagged. The alignment between the geographic band and the temperature chart, while approximately correct, is not geometrically precise. The two registers share a horizontal axis but that axis means different things above and below. This is a fundamental design tension that the execution does not fully resolve. Second: the troop count labels are placed at irregular intervals, creating visual rhythm that is inconsistent. Some key inflection points are unlabeled; the reader must infer the count by band width at those moments.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Dual-axis horizontal bridge** — the horizontal position variable is doing two different jobs: geographic longitude in the upper register, temporal position in the lower register. These two interpretations of the same axis coincide because the army's movement is roughly monotonic in space and time simultaneously. This is not a standard technique; it is a structural invention that the rubric has no name for. *Rubric anchor: Visual Economy / School Fidelity. Implication: the rubric should recognize — and score — cases where a single visual variable carries legitimately multiple readings, as distinct from cases where a variable is ambiguously or accidentally overloaded.*

2. **Confidence suppression** — presenting data of uncertain provenance with the visual confidence of precise measurement. The visual form (continuous band, precise numeric labels) implies a data quality that the underlying sources cannot certify. This is not specific to Minard — it is endemic to the history of visualization — but it is a failure mode the rubric does not currently score. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity. Implication: the rubric's Data Integrity anchors should explicitly address whether uncertainty in the source data is declared or suppressed.*
