# Brief: Nightingale's Diagram of Causes of Mortality (1858)

*Canonical import — reviewing an existing historical work, not a DEGAS-generated design.*

## Subject

Two polar area diagrams (coxcombs / rose diagrams) showing causes of death among British soldiers during the Crimean War, April 1854 – March 1856. The diagrams are published in her private report to Parliament and the War Office.

Each diagram covers one year of data. Together they span 24 months. Each month is a wedge (sector) radiating from the center. The wedge area encodes total deaths that month. The area is subdivided into three concentric layers by color:

- **Blue/grey**: deaths from "Preventable or Mitigable Zymotic Diseases" (infectious diseases — cholera, typhus, dysentery — preventable by sanitary improvement)
- **Red**: deaths from wounds received in battle
- **Black**: deaths from all other causes

The argument is political: the blue sections (preventable disease) dwarf the red sections (battle wounds) in almost every month. More soldiers were being killed by conditions in the hospitals and camps than by the enemy.

## Audience

**Original:** senior British government officials, army administrators, Members of Parliament, Queen Victoria. Nightingale distributed the report privately to decision-makers who had power to reform military medicine. They were not statisticians. They were men accustomed to receiving narrative reports, not tables of figures.

**Review audience:** the DEGAS panel, assessing what the work does that our rubric does not yet adequately name.

## School

**School type: B (Founder)**. The polar area diagram existed in rudimentary form before Nightingale (André-Michel Guerry, 1829; Georg von Mayr). Nightingale's use is the first documented case of the form deployed explicitly as political advocacy addressed to a non-statistical audience. She did not use it because it was a standard form; she used it because she believed it would work where tables had not. The school of advocacy visualization — design that carries an argument to those who hold power — begins here.

The rubric v1.1 Type B classification applies. We score on whether the work establishes a coherent founding grammar for advocacy visualization, not on fidelity to a pre-existing school.

## Constraints

- The original diagrams are in color (blue/red/black); color is critical to the encoding
- Published in a private report, not publicly distributed; estimated ~1,000 copies reached decision-makers
- The mortality statistics come from Army medical records that Nightingale herself helped compile; the data quality is better than Minard's troop counts but still subject to classification disputes (what counts as "zymotic"?)
- We work from the 1858 printed version; Nightingale produced multiple versions with slight variations
