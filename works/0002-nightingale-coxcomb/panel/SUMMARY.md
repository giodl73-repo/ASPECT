## Key Agreements

**Type B (Founder) classification holds.** All reviewers accept the founding classification. No dissent on this — the v1.1 amendment is already doing work. Without it, three reviewers would have applied Type A anchors and produced a distorted score for a work that predates its school.

**Data/Story Integrity is the weakest dimension.** Nightingale, Minard, and Tufte all dock significant points here, converging around the same two issues: the implied causation (Sanitary Commission → mortality decline) is overstated; and the data classification ("Preventable Zymotic") is constructed to serve the argument. The panel agrees the data is substantially correct; the panel agrees the implied causal claim exceeds what the data supports.

**Resonance is the strongest dimension.** Every reviewer acknowledges the work's political efficacy, though they differ on what kind of resonance this represents. Nightingale gives 15/15; Tufte gives 13/15 specifically because he distinguishes political efficacy from aesthetic resonance and finds the aesthetic resonance moderate.

## Key Tensions

**Does advocacy efficacy belong in the Resonance score?**  
Nightingale scores Resonance at 15/15 on the grounds that the work changed British military medicine — it did what it was designed to do. Tufte scores 13/15, holding that political impact and aesthetic/intellectual resonance are different things and that the diagram's aesthetic resonance is real but not exceptional. The Audience Lens scores 14/15, finding the gestalt argument compelling regardless of political outcome. These three positions are all defensible and they are incompatible. *The Resonance dimension currently cannot hold all three simultaneously.* This is the strongest signal yet for an amendment.

**The diptych problem.**  
Nightingale and Minard both flag that the work's argument lives in the pairing — neither diagram alone makes the causal case. The rubric treats every work as a single artifact. For the diptych, scoring the individual diagrams separately would give a different (lower) result than scoring the pair as a unit. We have been scoring the pair; the rubric does not say to do this. This is an unresolved ambiguity in the scoring framework.

**Statistical suboptimality as choice vs. failure.**  
Tufte and Minard both note that the polar area form is less statistically accurate than a bar chart. Their positions diverge: Tufte treats this as a genuine integrity and economy problem (score it as a cost). Minard accepts it as a purposeful tradeoff (acknowledge it but don't penalize it as failure). The rubric currently has no way to distinguish these two framings. This is a direct target for Cluster B's data integrity amendment.

## Cluster Confirmations

### Cluster A — Resonance sub-types: **AMENDMENT THRESHOLD MET**
**Works: 0001 + 0002. Innovations: #2 (spatial mourning), #8 (data anthropomorphism), #10 (advocacy efficacy), #13 (argument-first design)**

Four innovations across two works, all pointing at the same gap: the Resonance dimension cannot distinguish between:
- **Aesthetic-intentional resonance** — the designer crafted an emotional effect
- **Emergent-data resonance** — the encoding of human quantities produces affect as a by-product
- **Advocacy efficacy** — the work moved the audience to act; the political goal was achieved
- **Gestalt resonance** — the core argument is available in the visual shape before any detail is parsed

The panel produced three incompatible scores for Resonance in work 0002 precisely because each reviewer was assessing a different one of these. *Amendment required.*

### Cluster B — Data Integrity uncertainty/tradeoff: **AMENDMENT THRESHOLD MET**
**Works: 0001 + 0002. Innovations: #4 (confidence suppression), #7 (implied causation from juxtaposition), #11 (intentional accuracy-for-impact tradeoff), #15 (statistical suboptimality as rhetorical strategy)**

Four innovations across two works, all pointing at the same gap: Data/Story Integrity treats all accuracy departures as failures. It cannot distinguish:
- **Data quality failure** — the designer did not know the precision was insufficient
- **Confidence suppression** — the designer knew but did not declare it
- **Intentional accuracy tradeoff** — the designer knowingly chose less precision for rhetorical gain, for a defensible reason

The current anchors penalize all three equally. They should not. *Amendment required.*

### New cluster forming — Legibility context-dependence: **WATCH**
**Work: 0002. Innovations: #17 (context-dependent legibility), #20 (campaign artifact vs. standalone), #14 (circular reading disadvantage)**

Three innovations pointing at a Legibility gap: the rubric scores legibility as if the audience and reading context are fixed. They are not. Works designed for accompanied presentation, specific political contexts, or campaign deployment have very different legibility profiles than standalone gallery or textbook works. *Watch for work 0003.*

## Design Recommendations

*(Canonical import — recommendations are for rubric and pipeline, not for revision of historical work.)*

1. **Amend Resonance** to distinguish four sub-types: aesthetic-intentional, emergent-data, advocacy-efficacy, gestalt. Cluster A confirmed across 2 works.

2. **Amend Data/Story Integrity** to distinguish data quality failure, confidence suppression, and intentional accuracy tradeoff. Cluster B confirmed across 2 works.

3. **Add diptych/multi-part work handling** to the rubric — when a work's argument lives in the relationship between multiple panels, the rubric should score the pair as a unit and note whether the relational argument is legible.

4. **Add gestalt legibility as a named criterion** to Legibility anchors — can the core argument be extracted from the visual shape alone in under 3 seconds?

5. **Add advocacy work-type field** to frontmatter: `advocacy_type: directed | public | campaign-artifact`. Scoring of legibility and resonance should reflect this.
