## Review

I approach this from two angles: the original audience (British government officials in 1858) and the contemporary audience (anyone encountering this diagram now in a visualization textbook or exhibition).

**For the original audience (1858):**

The form would have been largely unfamiliar. The polar area diagram had few precedents visible to British officials in 1858. This unfamiliarity is part of the design — it commands attention precisely because it does not look like anything they have seen before. An official who has already ignored twelve tables of mortality statistics will look at this diagram because it is visually strange.

Once they look, the core argument is available within seconds without instruction: the blue dominates. Blue means preventable. The blue is enormous in Year 1 and small in Year 2. Whatever the viewer makes of the detail, the headline — preventable deaths dwarf battle deaths, and the decline is dramatic — is visible before they have read a label. This is excellent design for the original audience.

The legibility cost is the sector overlap in Year 1's worst months. For the original audience, receiving the diagram with Nightingale or an aide present to explain it, this cost is manageable. The diagram was not designed for unaccompanied reading; it was designed for presentation in a specific political context. By that measure: 13 points.

**For the contemporary audience:**

The contemporary viewer encounters this diagram stripped of context — in a textbook, on a screen, in a museum. Without explanation, the form is confusing. The circular time axis is non-obvious. The area encoding is not self-evident. The three-color legend is clear, but "Zymotic Diseases" requires medical vocabulary that many contemporary readers lack. A significant percentage of contemporary viewers will extract only the visual gestalt (blue big in Year 1, small in Year 2) and not the specific data. The gestalt is correct. The specifics are inaccessible without instruction.

This is a form legibility problem, not a data problem: the diagram was designed for a 1858 presentation context and is now encountered in a 2026 reading context. Its legibility has degraded over time as the form has become unfamiliar (replaced by bar charts) and the vocabulary has shifted (Zymotic is archaic). The Audience Lens notes this not as a design failure but as a time-decay of legibility that no 1858 designer could have anticipated.

**On Resonance:**

For both audiences: the contrast between Year 1 and Year 2, communicated by the gross visual difference in scale, is immediately affecting. The contemporary viewer who grasps only the gestalt still grasps something true and important: an enormous preventable catastrophe, then its correction. That emotional landing — waste, then repair — is available even to a viewer who cannot read the detail. Score 14.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Context-dependent legibility** — a work's legibility is not fixed at design time; it depends on the reading context, including whether explanation is present, whether the form is familiar to the viewer, and whether the vocabulary has shifted. Works designed for accompanied presentation (explained to a specific audience in a specific political moment) may be highly legible in their original context and far less legible in later, unaccompanied reading contexts. The rubric's Legibility dimension currently assumes a fixed audience and context. *Rubric anchor: Legibility. Implication: canonical-import reviews should score legibility both for the original context (as designed) and for the contemporary reading context, noting where time-decay has degraded accessibility.*

2. **Gestalt argument** — a work that communicates its core argument through visual shape before the viewer has parsed any detail, relying on the gestalt (overall visual impression) to carry the headline. The rubric scores legibility in terms of comprehension of the full encoding. It does not separately score gestalt legibility — whether the viewer can extract the one-sentence argument from the visual shape alone, without reading any labels or parsing any encoding. For advocacy work, gestalt legibility may be the primary measure of success. *Rubric anchor: Legibility / Resonance. Implication: add gestalt legibility as a named criterion — the ability to extract the core argument from the visual shape alone within 3 seconds.*
