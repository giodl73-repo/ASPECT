## Review

My function here is to assess school fidelity — whether this work correctly understands and inhabits its claimed school — and to bring historical precedent to bear.

**School classification and precedent:**

The brief correctly classifies this as Type B (Founder). The polar area diagram had precedents. André-Michel Guerry used polar forms in his moral statistics work (1829). Georg von Mayr used them in agricultural data. Nightingale knew Guerry's work through William Farr, her statistical collaborator.

This matters: Nightingale is not inventing the form ex nihilo; she is adapting it from quantitative social science to advocacy. The founding act is not the form but the purpose: using a statistical form deliberately chosen for maximum impact on a non-statistical decision-making audience, with a specific political outcome as the design criterion.

This is genuinely novel in 1858. Nothing before this work uses statistical graphics as a political intervention targeting a specific power-holding audience with documented intent to change legislation. Playfair used charts for commercial and political argument, but his audience was the reading public, not specific legislators. Minard's figurative maps are public communication, not targeted advocacy. Nightingale's work is the first documented case of statistical visualization used as directed lobbying material.

**On the founding grammar (Type B scoring basis):**

The founding grammar Nightingale establishes has these recoverable principles:

1. Know which decision-maker you need to move; design for them specifically
2. The form should command the attention of an audience that has ignored tabular data
3. The moral conclusion should be embedded in the vocabulary (label choices, color choices)
4. The before/after argument should be visible before the viewer has read a label
5. The work is not standalone — it is part of a campaign (report, letters, personal meetings, follow-up)

Principle 5 is the one that subsequent practitioners have most often missed. The coxcomb was distributed with a 800+ page report that contextualized it. Nightingale met personally with many of the men who received it. The diagram was one instrument in a sustained campaign, not a standalone artifact. Works that cite this as a model for standalone visualization advocacy are misreading the founding act. Score 17 — the grammar is mostly recoverable but principle 5 is implicit, not explicit.

**Historical comparisons the panel should hold:**

The coxcomb predates by a generation the work of Charles Booth (poverty maps of London, 1889) and by two generations the work of W.E.B. Du Bois (American Negro charts, 1900 Paris Exposition). Both Booth and Du Bois were clearly in the advocacy visualization tradition; both likely knew Nightingale's work. Assessing the coxcomb's innovations should acknowledge that the tradition it founded produced these successor works.

Du Bois's work is particularly relevant: he adapted statistical graphics for an advocacy purpose (demonstrating African American economic progress to a hostile European audience) with even more deliberate formal choices than Nightingale, including decorative and aesthetic elements that Tufte would consider chartjunk but which functioned as rhetorical amplification for Du Bois's specific political moment.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Directed advocacy vs. public advocacy** — a distinction the school history requires: directed advocacy (targeting a specific small audience of decision-makers with the power to act) differs fundamentally from public advocacy (persuading a broad public). The design logic, the distribution strategy, and the definition of "success" are different. The rubric should distinguish these two variants of advocacy visualization when classifying Type B works. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type B). Implication: the founding grammar assessment for advocacy works should ask whether the work is directed (to power-holders) or public (to a broad audience), since the design principles for each are different.*

2. **Campaign artifact vs. standalone work** — the coxcomb was part of a multi-instrument campaign (report, diagram, personal meetings, letters). The diagram was not designed to function autonomously; it was designed to function in conjunction with Nightingale's personal presence and with the 800-page accompanying report. Reviewing a campaign artifact as a standalone visualization misassesses its legibility and its resonance. The rubric should recognize campaign artifacts and score them as elements of a larger system rather than as standalone works. *Rubric anchor: Legibility / Resonance. Implication: add a work-type field distinguishing standalone works from campaign artifacts; the scoring of legibility and resonance should reflect the intended deployment context.*
