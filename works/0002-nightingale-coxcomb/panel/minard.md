## Review

I recognize the problem Nightingale is solving. I have solved a version of it: how do you make numbers that describe death reach a reader who will otherwise look through them?

Her solution and mine are different enough that I learn something from the comparison.

**On School Fidelity (Type B):**

The founding grammar she establishes is for what I would call *argument-first visualization* — design that begins with a political conclusion and works backward to a form that will deliver that conclusion to a specific audience. This is different from what I do. My work begins with the data and finds a form that reveals what is there. Nightingale begins with what she needs the viewer to believe and finds data that proves it.

This is not dishonest — the data genuinely supports the argument. But the design logic is reversed. I built the Russian campaign map to show what happened. She built the coxcomb to show what must change.

As a founding act, the grammar she establishes is: (1) know your audience's power before you choose your form, (2) make the argument visible before the reader has parsed the encoding, (3) embed the conclusion in the title. These three principles are learnable and replicable. She scores 16 rather than 18 because the multi-part diptych structure is not clearly established as a principle — a practitioner learning from this work might miss that the pair is the argument.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

I have my own experience with imprecise source data presented as precise. My troop counts are estimates; Nightingale's mortality classifications are contestable. The difference is that her classification choices actively serve her argument — "Preventable" is a word that determines cause and apportions blame. I labeled things numerically. She labeled them morally. This is more powerful and more prone to distortion.

The implied causation — Sanitary Commission intervention → mortality decline — is the work's central vulnerability. The decline coincides with the intervention but may not be caused primarily by it. She presents the before/after pairing as if the causal reading is obvious and uncontested. It is not. I deduct seven points.

**On Legibility:**

The polar area form requires learning. My flow band is intuitive — wider means more. The coxcomb is not: the viewer must understand that area (not radius, not angular width alone) encodes quantity. This is not a self-evident encoding. However, for her audience — educated men who would have had someone explain the diagram to them — this barrier is probably manageable. Thirteen points.

**On Visual Economy:**

The sector overlaps in Year 1 bother me. In January and February 1855 the sectors are so large they cross the area of other months. I understand the argument for leaving this: the overlap is itself a visual signal of scale. But it violates a principle I hold: the viewer should not have to disentangle marks to read them. When two sectors occupy the same space, the viewer must resolve the visual collision before they can read either. That resolution is cognitive work that the design is imposing without need. Eleven points.

**On Resonance:**

The shape of Year 1 — that bloated, asymmetric explosion in the winter months — is genuinely affecting. Not in the same way my Russian campaign map affects: the band shrinks and the viewer feels the loss. Here the sectors bloat and the viewer feels the waste — the unnecessary scale of preventable death. This is a different emotional register, and it works. But the form does not guide the eye through a sequence; the viewer can enter the diagram anywhere and the argument is available in fragments rather than as a narrative arc. Fourteen points.

**On Craft:**

The printing quality issues are real. Beyond that: the angular division of the year into 30° sectors creates unequal visual emphasis on months depending on their position in the circular layout. Months near the top of the circle (close to 12 o'clock) are read more naturally; months near the bottom feel reversed or disorienting. This is a structural problem with the circular form that Nightingale did not solve. Eleven points.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Argument-first design** — a design logic that begins with a political conclusion and selects form, data, and encoding to deliver that conclusion to a specific power-holding audience. This is distinct from data-first design (form follows the data's shape) and from both of the Resonance sub-types identified in work 0001. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type B), Resonance. Implication: the founding grammar for advocacy visualization is argument-first; the rubric should score founding works on whether the argument-first logic is coherent and whether the data genuinely supports the predetermined conclusion.*

2. **Circular reading disadvantage** — in circular (polar) layouts, sectors near the bottom of the circle are read in a reversed or disorienting direction by viewers habituated to left-to-right reading. This is a systematic legibility penalty for polar forms that the rubric's Legibility dimension does not name. *Rubric anchor: Legibility. Implication: the rubric should explicitly account for directional reading penalties in circular and radial layouts.*
