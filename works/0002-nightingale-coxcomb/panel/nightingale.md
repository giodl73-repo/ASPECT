## Review

I will be direct. I made this because everything else had failed.

I had given Parliament tables. I had given them narrative reports. I had given them the testimony of surgeons. The mortality from preventable disease in the Crimea was higher than battlefield mortality by a factor of seven in the worst months of the winter of 1854-55, and the people who had the power to fix it had read the numbers and done nothing. So I made the diagram.

**On School Fidelity (Type B — Founding):**

I did not know I was founding a school. I knew I was making something that did not yet exist in the form I needed. The founding grammar, such as it is, rests on three principles that I think can be learned from this work:

First: the audience is the governor, not the statistician. Every design choice — form, color, scale, title — should be made with respect to what will move a man of authority who distrusts numbers to act on them.

Second: the form should be unusual enough to arrest attention. The tables they had already read without acting. A diagram that looks nothing like a table may be read by someone who has already skipped the tables.

Third: the argument should be in the form, not only in the text. The viewer should be able to grasp the political claim before they have read a word.

I score 18 rather than 20 because the diptych structure — which I consider essential to the argument — is not sufficiently coherent as a founding principle. The pairing creates the before/after argument, but it does so through placement, which is not a principle I can state as a rule. Future practitioners could miss the pairing and produce single diagrams without the causal structure. The grammar is incomplete.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

This is where I must be honest. The data classification is mine. "Preventable or Mitigable Zymotic Diseases" is a category I defined, with help from William Farr. Other physicians would have drawn the line differently. The Army medical records from which these figures come were not compiled with statistical rigor; they were field records, often incomplete, often contradictory. I worked with Farr to make them as consistent as possible.

The deeper problem: the decline in Year 2 is attributed implicitly to the Sanitary Commission's intervention in March 1855. But the intervention coincided with the end of winter (when infection rates naturally decline), the arrival of resupply ships that improved nutrition, and the end of several of the siege operations that had created the worst conditions. I cannot isolate the Sanitary Commission's contribution from these other factors. The diagram presents a before/after that implies a causal story I cannot fully prove. I believe the story is substantially correct; I cannot certify it. I deduct six points because the implied causation significantly exceeds what the data alone supports.

**On Legibility:**

The form is unfamiliar. A first-time reader of the polar area diagram does not know to read area rather than radius. A wide-angle wedge that is short may represent more deaths than a narrow wedge that is long, and the eye does not know this without instruction. I addressed this with a verbal explanation in the accompanying text of the report. A reader who encounters only the diagram without the text is partially adrift.

I do not think this was a design failure — I think it was the price of choosing a form that was visually arresting. The price was moderate; most of my intended audience were shown the diagrams with an explanation present. Fourteen points.

**On Visual Economy:**

The color system has a logic, but the wedge areas in months with very high mortality become so large that they overlap the adjacent months' sectors in Year 1. This is not a data error — the overlap represents the scale of the crisis — but it does create a visual tangle in the worst months of winter 1854-55 where the sectors interfere with each other. I could have addressed this by using a larger plate, by switching to log scale (which would have understated the crisis), or by leaving the overlap as a visual indicator of catastrophe. I chose the third. Twelve points — the overlap is defensible but not ideal.

**On Resonance:**

Fifteen out of fifteen. The work accomplished what it was for. It reached Lord Panmure and the Army Council. The Sanitary Commission went to Scutari. Whether the diagram caused the Commission's dispatch or merely contributed to a political climate that made it possible — I cannot separate the causes any more than I can in the mortality data — but the diagram was part of a campaign that produced action. I will not score a visualization's resonance at less than perfect when it changed something that needed to change.

**On Craft:**

The color printing of this period was imprecise. Several copies I have seen have the blue and grey poorly distinguished, which affects the reading of the "other causes" category. The typography on the sector labels is consistent but small — legible at reading distance, crowded at Year 1's outer sectors. Twelve points.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Advocacy efficacy as a resonance criterion** — the Resonance dimension currently asks whether the work "lands as intended." For advocacy visualization, the measure of landing is political: did the work change something? A work that is aesthetically resonant but politically inert has failed in its primary purpose. The rubric has no way to ask whether a work succeeded at its actual goal. *Rubric anchor: Resonance. Implication: Resonance should have an advocacy sub-type — assessed not on emotional effect but on whether the work moved the audience it was designed to move.*

2. **Intentional accuracy-for-impact tradeoff** — choosing a less statistically precise form (polar area) over a more precise one (bar chart) because the less precise form has greater rhetorical impact on the target audience. This is not a data integrity failure; it is a deliberate design choice. The rubric currently scores Data/Story Integrity and Visual Economy as if precision is always the goal. It does not account for cases where precision was sacrificed knowingly, for legible reasons. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity / Visual Economy. Implication: the rubric should score whether a precision tradeoff is declared and whether it is proportionate to the advocacy goal.*

3. **Diptych causation** — a multi-part work in which the argument is not located in either part alone but in the relationship between them. The "before" diagram and the "after" diagram are each incomplete arguments; the causal claim lives in the pairing. The rubric currently assesses individual works; it has no vocabulary for works whose meaning is constituted in the relationship between two panels. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity / Resonance. Implication: the rubric should address multi-part works and score whether the relational argument (across parts) is legible and honest.*
