## Review

I am going to be careful to distinguish three audiences for this map, because they have three completely different legibility profiles. This is Cluster D.

**Audience 1 — The 1933 London Underground passenger (original design target):**

This person knows London geography from living in it. They can orient themselves by the Thames. They know roughly which direction their destination is. They have probably seen earlier versions of Underground maps. They are standing on a platform or sitting in a carriage; they have seconds, not minutes, to make a route decision.

For this person: legibility is near-perfect. The map answers every question they have — which line, where to change, which direction — faster than any alternative. The color coding is legible at the size of a pocket fold-out in carriage lighting. The interchange rings are immediately visible. 14/15 for this audience (the King's Cross label collision is a minor friction even here).

**Audience 2 — A contemporary non-London tourist (common contemporary user):**

This person has no geographic intuitions about London. They do not know where the Thames is in relation to where they are. They may be consulting the map on a phone (a large version) or from a poster on a platform wall. They are in the right context (underground, navigating), but they lack the geographic background knowledge that would allow the Thames to function as an orientation anchor.

For this person: legibility is moderate. They can identify line colors and trace routes correctly once they have found their origin and destination stations. The difficulty is the "where am I on the map?" problem: the map provides no geographic cross-references except the Thames, which they cannot read. The result is that the tourist can route correctly but cannot sanity-check their progress against surface reality. 10/15 for this audience.

**Audience 3 — A design student or museum visitor encountering the map as an artifact (common contemporary context):**

This person is not navigating. They are studying. They have unlimited time. They are probably looking at a large reproduction, not a pocket fold-out. They have no journey context.

For this person: legibility of the design logic is high — the angular discipline, the color system, the interchange markers are all visible and learnable. But the *data content* of the map is partly inaccessible: without knowing London, they cannot verify whether the route information is correct, and the topological distortion makes it impossible to relate the map to geographic reality they might have. They can appreciate the design; they cannot use the map. 9/15 for this audience.

**Averaged Legibility: 12/15** — strongly legible for the original audience, moderately legible for contemporary navigation users, low for contextless reading.

**On Resonance:**

For Audience 1 (1933 passenger): the map just works. There is satisfaction in that — the experience of a tool that does exactly what it promises. This is not aesthetic resonance; it is functional satisfaction.

For Audience 3 (design student): the map is aesthetically striking. The angular discipline produces a kind of crystalline order. The color system is bright and confident. The network shape — especially the Northern line's Y-fork, the Circle line's enclosure, the radial spoke pattern — is visually pleasing in a way that reveals the network's character. This is close to Tufte's "network gestalt," and it lands for the informed viewer.

Score 12 — resonance is real for audiences 1 and 3; it is absent for audience 2 (the contemporary tourist consulting the map as a navigation tool without geographic background, for whom the map works but produces no particular feeling).

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Three-audience legibility divergence** — this work has the most pronounced multi-audience legibility split we have encountered. Its Legibility score varies from approximately 14/15 (original use context) to 10/15 (contemporary navigation without geographic background) to 9/15 (contextless design appreciation). The rubric asks for a single Legibility score, which requires choosing which audience to assess. The choice is consequential: a 14 and a 9 represent different verdicts on the design. *Rubric anchor: Legibility. Implication: for works with multiple distinct use contexts, the rubric should require specifying the primary scored audience and noting secondary audience scores. Works designed for a specific original context should be scored primarily against that context, with secondary contemporary context scores noted but not dominating.*

2. **Context-decay of legibility** — confirming innovation #17 from work 0002: the 1933 Underground map was designed for an audience that brought substantial geographic and cultural context to the map. As that context erodes (for contemporary tourists, for non-London users, for viewers outside any navigation context), the map's legibility decays — not because the design degraded, but because the audience's brought-context changed. The rubric should distinguish between *design legibility* (how well the encoding communicates, given the intended context) and *context-sufficient legibility* (whether the intended context is still reliably available to the contemporary audience). *This directly confirms innovation #17 — Cluster D threshold consideration.*

3. **Functional resonance** — a form of resonance specific to tools: the satisfaction of a tool that fits its task precisely. Not aesthetic pleasure; not advocacy impact; not gestalt recognition. The feeling the 1933 passenger had when they realized they could now navigate clearly. This is different from all four current Resonance types (I: aesthetic-intentional, II: emergent-data, III: advocacy-efficacy, IV: gestalt). *Rubric anchor: Resonance. Implication: consider a Resonance type for functional works — tools, navigation systems, interfaces — where the primary resonance is task-completion satisfaction rather than aesthetic, data-driven, advocacy, or gestalt effect.*
