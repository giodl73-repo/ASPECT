# Brief: Du Bois's "American Negro" Charts, Paris Exposition 1900

*Canonical import — reviewing a suite of historical works as a unified series.*

## Subject

In 1900, W.E.B. Du Bois, then a professor at Atlanta University, designed approximately 60 hand-drawn charts, graphs, and maps for the "Exhibit of American Negroes" at the Paris Exposition Universelle. The exhibit was organized by Thomas Junius Calloway. Du Bois's statistical section was titled "The Georgia Negro: A Social Study" and a companion series, "A Series of Statistical Charts Illuminating the Condition of the Descendants of Former African Slaves Now Resident in the United States of America."

The charts show: population growth, geographic distribution, land ownership, income, occupations, illiteracy rates, and property valuation of African Americans — with particular focus on Georgia — from 1790 to 1900. They were produced at Atlanta University by Du Bois and his students using india ink and watercolor on poster board, approximately 28 × 22 inches each.

**Key charts in the series:**
- Population growth spirals (African Americans, 1790–1900)
- Occupation bar charts (comparative, by decade)
  Land ownership maps (Georgia counties, acreage)
- Income/property valuation spirals and bar charts
- Illiteracy rates (comparative: Black Americans vs. other nations)
- "The Georgia Negro" city population diagrams
- Comparative bar charts: "Conjugal Condition" (marriage rates)
- The famous "Proportion of Freemen and Slaves" timeline (uses a flowing form unlike any standard chart)

The series is formally inventive. Du Bois did not rely on standard chart forms. He adapted, bent, and invented forms — spirals, irregular polygons, flowing time-lines — to suit each dataset. The decoration in these charts is not chartjunk; it is rhetorical amplification, calibrated for an international audience that was expected to associate African Americans with backwardness.

## Audience

**Original:** International audience at the Paris Exposition Universelle, 1900. Primarily European visitors and officials; the exhibit competed with others for attention. The audience had no reason to expect sophisticated statistical work from this exhibit and may have arrived with racist assumptions Du Bois was specifically countering. They were also the educated global public that mattered for American international reputation at the turn of the century.

**Review audience:** DEGAS panel under rubric v1.3.

## School

**School type: C (Deliberate Synthesis).**

Du Bois synthesizes:
- **Statistical graphics** (bar charts, time series, geographic data): inherited from Playfair and the tradition
- **Advocacy visualization** (Type B, founded by Nightingale): the argument-first logic, the targeting of a specific audience with political stakes
- **Aesthetic amplification**: Du Bois adds formal inventiveness, decorative curves, bold color, and invented forms that go beyond what either parent school would endorse — but that serve the rhetorical goal of commanding attention and asserting dignity

The synthesis is deliberate: Du Bois was academically trained in statistics (PhD, Harvard + Berlin) and understood standard statistical forms. He chose not to use them exclusively. His formal inventiveness is a political act.

## Core Questions (Review Mode)

1. **Tufte's dilemma**: the charts contain decorative elements — curves, spirals, bold fills, invented forms — that Tufte would call chartjunk. Are they? Or does the decorative excess serve a rhetorical function that makes it legitimate? How does the v1.3 rubric handle decoration-as-argument?

2. **Cluster E confirmation candidate**: Do these charts exhibit domain-gated gestalt (#25) or functional resonance (#31)? Or something else — a form of resonance rooted in the political stakes of the work's moment?

3. **Cluster F confirmation candidate**: Du Bois's data model is social and political — he is not just showing statistics; he is showing *humanity* and *progress*. Does the data model question (#22) arise here in a new form?

4. **The suite question**: this is a series of ~60 charts, not a single work. The Nightingale diptych (innovation #12) raised the question of how we score multi-part works. Du Bois's suite is far larger. What is "the work"?

## Constraints

- Original charts are hand-drawn on poster board; they are not printed reproductions
- Colors vary across the series but a consistent palette (red, black, tan, green, gold) runs through most charts
- We focus on approximately 10 of the most formally significant charts; the full series of 60 is too large to analyze at that resolution
- The charts were rediscovered and widely reproduced after Battle-Baptiste & Rusert's 2018 compilation; before 2018, many were unknown to visualization scholars

## Open Questions

- Is decoration-as-argument a data integrity failure, a visual economy failure, or a legitimate new category?
- How does the suite-as-unit question resolve? Are the ~60 charts one work or many?
