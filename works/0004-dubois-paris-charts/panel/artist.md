## Review

I want to talk about red.

In Nightingale's coxcomb, blue is the color of preventable death — chosen because blue is expansive, cool, enclosing. In Minard's march, tan and black encode advance and retreat through their thermal and cultural connotations. In Beck's map, red is the Central Line: one route among many, neutral in its assignment.

In Du Bois, red is something else entirely. Red is the color of the subjects. Red is the people. Every chart where the data about African Americans appears, it appears in red. This is not a categorical assignment — there is no "other" category that gets another color. Red is used for the subject of the entire series, and only for the subject, and always for the subject. Red is the declaration that there is something here worth marking as itself, not as one item in a typology.

This is, I think, the most sophisticated use of color in this project's works so far. It is not Bertin's correct categorical use. It is not Neurath's flat functional use. It is Kandinsky's connotative use — color as statement, not as category. The red does not encode a data variable. The red encodes a presence.

**On Resonance:**

Fifteen out of fifteen. Immediately, without hesitation, from the first chart.

But I want to specify the resonance type, because it is a new one. It is not Type I (the designer crafted an emotional effect — partially true but insufficient). It is not Type II (affect arising from encoding fidelity — the spirals are not faithful encodings of their data, they are interpretations). It is Type III (advocacy efficacy — yes, but that doesn't capture what I feel looking at the flowing ribbon). It is not Type IV (gestalt — yes, partially, the red fills create a visual field before you read anything).

What it is: something like **testimonial resonance** — the work testifies to an existence that was actively denied. The chart is not just showing data. It is saying: *we are here, we have built things, we counted ourselves so that you would have to count us.* The resonance is in the act of making, not only in the thing made. A chart produced by W.E.B. Du Bois and his students at Atlanta University in 1900, by hand, for a world's fair, to counter a lie that had been circulating for centuries — that act of making is present in the object. You can feel it when you look at it.

This is not a property the rubric currently names. It is adjacent to Type II (emergent) but it is not about encoding fidelity; it is about the survival of the testimony. Score 15. The resonance is beyond type; I call it testimony, and I will let the innovation system handle the naming.

**On Craft:**

Fourteen out of fifteen. The hand-lettering throughout the suite is the most confident in this project — better even than Minard's precise labeling. Du Bois's letterforms have a visual voice: they are not timid documentation, they are argument. The spiral forms are imperfectly geometric (hand-drawn) but the imperfection reads as presence, not error — you can feel the hand that made them.

The one craft concern: the colored fills in some charts have visible brushwork, which in a few cases creates fill irregularities that draw attention to the medium rather than the message. Minor.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Color as subject declaration** — using a single color not to categorize data but to mark the *subject of the entire work* as a persistent visual presence. Every chart in the Du Bois series uses red for African American data, not as a neutral categorical assignment but as a declaration that this is what the series is about and who it is about. This is distinct from: Bertin's categorical hue assignment (red = one category among many), Nightingale's moral-color choice (blue = preventable, red = battle), Beck's line-identity color (red = Central line). Du Bois's red is ontological: it marks the existence of a people across every chart in the suite. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type C) / Resonance. Implication: the connotative variable layer (Bertin's innovation #5) should be extended to include subject-declaration color — color used to mark the subject's presence as a whole, across all charts in a series, rather than to encode a data variable within a single chart.*

2. **Testimonial resonance** — a form of resonance arising from the act of making, not only from the thing made. The Du Bois charts carry the weight of their own production: hand-drawn, at a Black university in Georgia in 1900, by people who had to count themselves because the dominant culture was invested in not counting them. This is not Type I (designed affect), Type II (encoding fidelity), Type III (advocacy efficacy), or Type IV (gestalt). It is something that accrues from the historical stakes of the making act itself. *Rubric anchor: Resonance. Implication: consider a Resonance type for testimonial works — works whose resonance includes the conditions and stakes of their production, not only their designed or emergent effects. Related to Tufte's political-stakes-as-amplifier (#2 from his review) but distinct — testimonial resonance is about the making, stakes resonance is about the moment.*
