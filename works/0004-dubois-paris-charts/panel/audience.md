## Review

Three audiences again, though the boundaries are sharper this time.

**Audience 1 — 1900 Paris Exposition visitor (original design target):**

European, educated, arriving with an assumption that the "American Negro" exhibit is a curiosity or a charitable display. No expectation of statistical rigor. Has been walking through an exposition all day; attention budget is limited.

For this person: the red fills and spiral forms stop them. They stop. They look. The conventional charts (conjugal condition, illiteracy comparison) are immediately legible — the comparison format is familiar from the European statistical tradition; the argument (we compare favorably) is available within seconds. The spiral forms require more investment, which some visitors will make and some will not.

Legibility for this audience: 14/15. The design succeeds at its primary task (stopping and then serving the attention of the passing visitor) with minor friction at the invented forms.

**Audience 2 — Contemporary visualization scholar or student (now the dominant audience):**

This person encounters the charts in the Battle-Baptiste/Rusert 2018 book or as digital reproductions. They have unlimited time. They know something about data visualization history. They are approaching the charts with the question "what is this doing?"

For this person: the conventional charts are immediately legible. The spiral forms are partially legible — the expanding-spiral metaphor is not standard in contemporary data viz education; most students will require the book's explanatory text to fully decode the spiral's encoding logic. The flowing ribbon (Chart 4) is the most challenging: its form is genuinely novel even to visualization-literate contemporary readers.

Legibility for this audience: 12/15. High for conventional charts, moderate for invented forms.

**Audience 3 — 1900 Paris visitor hostile to the exhibit's argument:**

This is the hardest audience case, and the most important one. Some visitors at the Paris Exposition in 1900 were there specifically to see the American exhibits and were committed to the racist narratives the exhibit was countering. For this person, the legibility question is not "can I decode the charts?" but "will I let these charts reach me?"

Du Bois's precision-as-defiance strategy (showing the unflattering numbers honestly alongside the flattering ones) is designed for this audience. A skeptical visitor who expects selective presentation will find unexpected honesty. The illiteracy chart, which shows the high illiteracy rate without concealment, is the one that might reach the hostile viewer precisely because it is not what they expected.

This is a legibility question the rubric has never had to address: legibility to a resistant audience. For this audience, honest data is more legible (in the sense of being able to penetrate resistance) than idealized data.

**Averaged Legibility: 13/15**

[Contemporary context: 12/15 — visualization-literate but spiral-unfamiliar contemporary reader, reading with time but without explanatory context]

**On Resonance:**

For every audience, including the hostile one: 15/15.

The charts exist. 120 years later they are in major museum collections, reproduced in a critically praised book, cited in every serious history of data visualization. They reached the record. They changed what the record contains. No subsequent viewer of the history of visualization can encounter it without encountering these charts as a moment when data was used as an act of dignity.

Tufte scores 14 because he holds a harder standard for Type III resonance (measuring ideological impact rather than record-entry). I score 15 because I think entering the record — making something that cannot be unremembered — is the highest form of advocacy efficacy available to a visualization. The charts are now part of the permanent argument. Score 15.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Hostile audience legibility** — a legibility dimension not previously considered: can the work reach a viewer who has motivation to resist its argument? Du Bois's precision-as-defiance strategy (honest about the unflattering numbers) is specifically designed to penetrate resistance by denying the hostile viewer the opportunity to dismiss the work as selective. This is a form of legibility — *reaching the resisting viewer* — that the rubric does not currently assess. *Rubric anchor: Legibility. Implication: for advocacy works, consider adding a hostile-audience legibility note: what does the design do to reach viewers who are motivated to discount it?*

2. **Resonance that accumulates** — the Du Bois charts were filed away for decades and rediscovered in 2018, after which their resonance expanded dramatically. Type III resonance as currently defined asks whether the advocacy goal was achieved — implying a contemporaneous assessment. Some works achieve their resonance on a long delay: they enter a record that is only read much later. This is different from contemporaneous resonance and different from both aesthetic resonance (which can also accumulate over time) and gestalt resonance. *Rubric anchor: Resonance (Type III). Implication: advocacy resonance should distinguish contemporaneous efficacy (the work achieved its goal at the moment of presentation) from delayed record-entry efficacy (the work's existence in the archive matters across time). These may score differently.*
