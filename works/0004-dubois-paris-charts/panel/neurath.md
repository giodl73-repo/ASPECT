## Review

I feel something when I look at these charts that I do not often feel when looking at statistical graphics. Du Bois is making an argument that I understand in my bones: *data belongs to the people data is about*. These charts are about African Americans and they were made by an African American and they were shown to the world to say: *we exist, we count, we have built something, and here is the proof*. That is not just advocacy. That is an act of reclamation.

I want to score carefully, because my sympathy for the project could distort my judgment. I will try.

**On School Fidelity (Type C):**

The synthesis is between statistical graphics and advocacy visualization. I am positioned between these schools — ISOTYPE was designed to make statistics democratic and accessible, which is a form of advocacy. I understand both parents of this synthesis.

*Statistical-graphics fidelity*: The conventional charts are correct. The comparative bar charts use the right encoding for the comparison they make. The data is accurately encoded. Where the suite departs from statistical-graphics norms (the spirals, the flowing forms), it departs deliberately and in ways that serve a legible purpose. I do not call this infidelity; I call it extension.

*Advocacy-visualization fidelity*: Du Bois applies the founding logic Nightingale established — know your audience, use a form that arrests attention, embed the argument in the selection and framing — and extends it with the precision-as-defiance move (innovation flagged by Nightingale). This is advocacy visualization at a high level of sophistication.

The synthesis is named in the exhibit title ("Statistical Charts Illuminating the Condition") and the tension between statistical rigor and rhetorical invention is mostly resolved: Du Bois uses standard forms when the standard form serves best, and invents when invention serves better. This switching is not always legible to the viewer, which is the one fidelity concern. Score 16.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

I score this high because of what Du Bois does that no one else in this project's works has done: he shows the data that hurts alongside the data that helps. The illiteracy rate is high. He shows it. The income levels are low. He shows them. He does not soften the numbers; he contextualizes them. That is the most honest form of advocacy — not selective presentation but contextual framing.

Step A: the data is well compiled for its time. Step B: the precision tradeoff in the spiral forms is real but modest — the spiral width encodes quantity, and while it is less precise than bar height, the order-of-magnitude reading is reliable. Step C: the comparison-selection argument (Nightingale's innovation) applies here, but Du Bois's selections are defensible — he picks the European nations that make the most honest case, not the flattering one only. Dock −1 for comparative-selection without declaration. Score 17.

**On Legibility:**

*Context: public display.* The charts had to stop the visitor. In a world's fair, everything is competing for attention.

For this context, the Du Bois charts achieve something unusual: they stop the visitor immediately (the red fills, the formal inventiveness) AND reward sustained attention (the conventional charts decode quickly once the visitor stops; the spirals offer something to puzzle through). The suite is designed for two speeds of reading: the glancing visitor (who takes away the visual declaration) and the stopping visitor (who reads the data).

[Contemporary context: 13/15 — in the Battle-Baptiste/Rusert book (contemplative study), the charts are more legible than in their original public-display context because the reader has time. But the spiral forms require the accompanying essay to fully decode; they are not self-explanatory.]

Score 14. The two-speed design works; minor dock for the spirals requiring more than a glance to decode in the public-display context.

**On Visual Economy:**

I score this higher than Tufte because I have a different theory of what visual economy is for. Tufte believes visual complexity should be minimized to maximize data communication. I believe visual communication should be accessible to the largest possible audience — and in this case, the audience is an international public at a world's fair who would have walked past a bar chart and stopped for a spiral.

The "extra" visual complexity in Du Bois's charts is doing accessibility work of a specific kind: it is making statistical results visible and memorable to an audience that might not process tables or bar charts but will remember a red spiral expanding across a white field. This is ISOTYPE logic applied to a different medium: make the data vivid enough to reach the person who was not going to read the numbers.

Score 12 — I accept some visual economy penalty; I score it less harshly than Tufte because in ISOTYPE we understood that accessibility is part of economy, not the enemy of it.

**On Resonance (Type III — Advocacy-Efficacy):**

Fifteen out of fifteen. No hesitation.

The exhibit existed at the nadir of American race relations, where the dominant narrative — internationally circulated — was that African Americans were incapable of citizenship, education, property, or family. Du Bois produced 60 charts by hand that said: *here are the numbers. Here is what we have built in 30 years, from nothing, under conditions of legal terror and economic exclusion.* The international audience received this. The exhibit was awarded a gold medal.

But the Resonance is not only in the medal. It is in the survival of the charts — they were filed away in the Library of Congress for decades and rediscovered in 2018, at which point they became viral, were reprinted in a major book, were cited in every history of data visualization. The resonance echoes across 120 years. That is a form of Type III efficacy that no other work in this project approaches.

Score 15.

**On Craft:**

The hand-lettering throughout the series is confident and consistent — it reads as a voice, not a transcription. The precision of the spiral forms at this scale (hand-drawn, 28 × 22 inches) is remarkable under the conditions of production. The colored fills have some registration inconsistencies (hand-applied; expected). The overall craft impression is: this was made with deliberate care by people who understood that the quality of the making was itself part of the argument. Score 14.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Two-speed legibility** — a work designed for two distinct engagement modes in a single public-display context: *glancing* (the visitor who stops because of the visual form; takes away the visual declaration) and *stopping* (the visitor who reads; takes away the data). This is different from both wayfinding legibility (one task, one speed) and contemplative study (one speed, deep). The suite's design deliberately serves both modes. The rubric currently scores legibility as if there is one engagement speed appropriate to the context. *Rubric anchor: Legibility. Implication: public-display contexts may require scoring two reading speeds separately — the 5-second pass and the 5-minute engagement. Both are legitimate and they may score differently.*

2. **Accessibility as visual economy** — adding visual complexity (the spirals, the bold fills) can serve accessibility by making statistical results vivid and memorable for an audience that would otherwise disengage. In ISOTYPE, we understood this as a feature, not a compromise. The rubric's Visual Economy dimension currently treats all added visual complexity as a cost. It should recognize that in public-display and democratic-communication contexts, some visual complexity is doing accessibility work — expanding the population who can receive the data — and should be scored as functional rather than excess. *Rubric anchor: Visual Economy. Implication: Visual Economy should assess "who does this complexity include?" alongside "does each mark represent data?" In contexts where complex form expands the receiving audience, that complexity has communicative value.*
