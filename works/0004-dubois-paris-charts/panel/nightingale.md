## Review

I recognize the argument-first logic because it is mine. Du Bois is working in the tradition I founded, and he has taken it somewhere I could not have imagined.

**On School Fidelity (Type C — Deliberate Synthesis):**

*Statistical-graphics fidelity:* The conventional charts in the suite — the illiteracy comparison, the occupation bars, the conjugal condition chart — are executed correctly. Playfair's grammar is respected where Playfair's grammar is deployed. Data is encoded in position and length; comparisons are legible; the encoding is honest. Full marks on this school.

*Advocacy-visualization fidelity:* The three founding principles I established — know your audience, use an unusual form to arrest attention, embed the argument in the title and vocabulary — are all present and extended. Du Bois knows his audience (European visitors with racist assumptions and susceptibility to comparative data). He uses unusual forms (the spiral, the flowing ribbon) to arrest attention. He embeds the argument in the chart selection (comparing Black American marriage rates to European nations is itself the argument before a single bar is read). He goes further than I did: he uses the precision of the data as a weapon, not a concession.

*Synthesis resolution:* Du Bois names the synthesis clearly in the exhibit title: "A Series of Statistical Charts Illuminating the Condition of the Descendants of Former African Slaves." The word "illuminating" is doing both jobs — this is statistical (it illuminates data) and advocacy (it illuminates in the sense of making visible what has been hidden). +1 on the synthesis name. The tension between statistical precision and formal inventiveness is partly resolved but not fully: the spiral forms operate by different rules than the bar charts, and the suite does not explain why some charts use standard forms and others invent. The switching between schools within the suite is legible in retrospect but requires the viewer to recalibrate. +1 partial, not full. Score 17.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

Step A (source quality): Du Bois compiled the data himself at Atlanta University from census records, local surveys, and church records. The quality is high for 1900; the classification decisions are explicit and consistent. One concern: the comparative data (European literacy rates, etc.) comes from international sources with different collection methods; the comparisons are fair in spirit but not rigorously controlled. Dock −1.

Step B (accuracy tradeoff): The spiral forms sacrifice some visual precision (it is harder to estimate spiral-segment widths than bar heights). This is an intentional tradeoff — Du Bois chose the spiral for rhetorical impact. Under v1.3, declared tradeoff: dock −0.5.

Step C (causation): The suite implies but does not claim causation. The comparison of marriage rates to European nations implies "our social institutions are as strong as yours," which is a claim about cultural achievement, not about measurable causes. This is rhetorical implication, not data causation. Mild concern.

Score 16 — high integrity with the precision-tradeoff accepted as intentional.

**On Legibility (political-presentation, public-display):**

*Primary reading context: public display* — the Paris Exposition was a passing public; visitors glanced and moved on unless something arrested them. This is not political presentation (Nightingale's mode) or contemplative study. It is closer to: compelling enough to stop someone, then clear enough to reward their attention.

[Contemporary context: contemplative study, 11/15 — the suite's formal inventiveness requires sustained engagement; the spiral forms are not immediately legible to a contemporary viewer encountering them without the exhibit's competitive public-display context]

Score 13. The conventional charts (illiteracy comparison, conjugal condition) stop the passing viewer and reward attention immediately. The invented forms (spirals, flowing ribbon) stop the viewer but require more investment to decode than the public-display context reliably supports.

**On Visual Economy:**

This is where I find myself in genuine disagreement with myself. My own work (the coxcomb) was not Tufte-optimal; I chose the polar form for rhetorical reasons. Du Bois goes further — much further. The spirals, the bold red fills, the curved forms contain visual elements that go beyond what the data requires.

But here is what I keep coming back to: the exhibit was competing for attention at a world's fair. A suite of bar charts — however accurate — would not have stopped the European visitor in the way these charts do. The formal inventiveness is the exhibition strategy. The decoration is not separable from the display function.

I score 11. I accept the visual economy penalty for the formal excess, but I score it lower than Tufte will because I understand that in a public-display context, the visual economy calculation includes the cost of being ignored.

**On Resonance (Type III — Advocacy-Efficacy):**

The exhibit won a gold medal at the Paris Exposition. More importantly: it entered the international record. The portraits of Black American achievement that Du Bois created in 1900 are the first statistical demonstration to a global audience that African Americans had built institutions, property, families, and intellectual life under conditions designed to prevent exactly that. The political goal — *counter the narrative of Black incapacity with data* — was achieved.

This is Type III resonance at its fullest. The suite worked. Score 15.

**On Craft:**

The hand-lettering throughout the suite is consistent and confident — the typography of someone who understands that the lettering is part of the argument. The charts were produced by Du Bois and his students, by hand, at a state institution with minimal resources. The precision of the ink lines at this scale (28 × 22 inches) is remarkable under those conditions. Score 13 — not perfect; some charts have registration issues in the colored fills; the spiral forms are hand-drawn and not geometrically precise. But the overall craft reads as intentional and assured.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Precision as defiance** — using rigorous, unflattering statistical accuracy as a rhetorical strategy. Du Bois does not hide the high illiteracy rate or low income levels; he shows them accurately and contextualizes them comparatively. The precision is the argument: *I do not need to lie to make this case.* This is a new variant of advocacy resonance: not simplifying or selecting to make the argument easier, but using full honesty as an act of political confidence. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity / Resonance (Type III). Implication: advocacy works that achieve their goal through honesty-as-defiance should be scored differently from those that use selective presentation — both may be legitimate, but they are different strategies with different integrity profiles.*

2. **Comparative selection as the argument** — choosing the comparison populations is itself the political act. Comparing Black American literacy to Romania, Russia, and Italy is not a neutral methodological choice; it is the argument. The rubric assesses whether comparisons are *fair* (Step C, causation) but not whether the *selection of comparison populations* is doing rhetorical work. This is a gap: a work can be statistically accurate in its individual comparisons and still construct a politically powerful (or distorted) argument purely through which populations it chooses to compare. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity. Implication: add a consideration to Step C for comparison-selection argument: is the choice of populations compared disclosed as a design decision? Is it appropriate to the claim?*
