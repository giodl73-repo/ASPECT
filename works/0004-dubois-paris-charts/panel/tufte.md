## Review

I need to be honest about something before I score this work, because my honesty has consequences for how the rubric is used.

I find the spirals and decorative curves in Du Bois's charts objectionable on my standard grounds: they add visual complexity that does not represent data. I have said this publicly. I maintain it. And I also recognize that applying my standard grounds to this work without acknowledging what the work is doing — and what it had to do, in 1900, in Paris, for that audience — would be a failure of critical intelligence, not an exercise of it.

So I will score it by my rubric and tell you where I think my rubric is wrong for this work.

**On School Fidelity (Type C):**

The synthesis spans statistical graphics and advocacy visualization. On statistical-graphics fidelity: the conventional charts are executed correctly; the invented forms (spirals, flowing ribbons) depart from the statistical-graphics tradition in ways that reduce encoding precision. On advocacy-visualization fidelity: the argument-first logic, the audience identification, the comparative framing are all well executed. The synthesis is partially named in the exhibit title.

I score 15 because the synthesis creates productive tension that is mostly resolved. The unresolved element: the suite switches between standard and invented forms without explicitly signaling why. A viewer who sees the conjugal-condition bar chart and then the population spiral is recalibrating between two encoding logics without instruction. Score 15.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

Step A: Du Bois compiled the data rigorously. The Atlanta University studies he drew from were, for their time, methodologically serious. The comparative data from European sources is less controlled but the comparison is honest in spirit. Step B: the spiral forms sacrifice quantitative precision. This is declared — implicitly, by the choice of the form. I dock 1 for the implicit rather than explicit declaration, and 1 for the degree of precision loss in the spiral encodings. Step C: the comparative selection (choosing nations with high illiteracy to compare against) is an argumentative move. It is not causation, but it is selection. Dock 1.

Score 15.

**On Legibility:**

*Context: public display* — 1900 Paris Exposition, visitors moving through an exhibition hall.

[Contemporary context: 12/15 — the suite is now encountered primarily in the 2018 Battle-Baptiste/Rusert book, which is contemplative study. In that context the conventional charts read immediately; the spiral forms require a paragraph of explanation to decode properly.]

For the primary public-display context: the conventional charts stop attention and deliver a legible argument within seconds. The spiral forms stop attention but do not deliver a legible argument within seconds — the viewer must engage more deeply to understand what the expanding spiral represents. This is a legibility cost that is not fully repaid by the public-display context, where deep engagement cannot be guaranteed. Score 12.

**On Visual Economy:**

This is where I score lowest and where I need to explain myself.

The spirals contain visual elements — the curvature of the form, the expanding radius, the gradated fill — that represent the same data that a bar chart would represent with less visual complexity. The flowing ribbon (Chart 4, freedom/slavery proportions) contains curves that represent a proportion changing over time — what a stacked bar chart would represent with greater precision and less decoration.

I understand the counterargument: the exhibition context demanded visual invention; the audience would not have stopped for bar charts; decoration is functional in a competitive display environment. I accept this argument. It changes my Visual Economy score from the 5 it would receive under strict data-ink analysis to an 8, which acknowledges the functional argument while maintaining that significant visual complexity exists that serves exhibition purpose rather than data representation.

What I will not do is pretend the spirals are equally economical to bar charts. They are not. The extra visual complexity is deliberate, purposeful, and in this context arguably justified. It is still extra visual complexity. Score 8.

**On Resonance (Type III — Advocacy-Efficacy):**

The exhibit won a gold medal. The charts entered the international record as the first statistical demonstration of African American achievement to a global audience. The Type III criterion — did the work achieve its advocacy goal? — must be answered: yes. I score 14 rather than 15 because the reach was significant but bounded. The exhibit changed the record; it is less clear it changed the minds of the visitors who most needed to be changed. The gold medal is an institutional recognition, not a measure of ideological impact. This is a harder standard than Nightingale's (which produced measurable policy change). Score 14.

**On Craft:**

Hand-drawn at 28 × 22 inches. The line quality is high. The lettering is consistent. The hand-drawn spirals are not geometrically perfect — they are freehand renderings of what would ideally be mathematically precise forms. Under other circumstances I would dock more; under the conditions of production (students at a Black university in Georgia, 1900, with limited resources), the craft level is extraordinary. Score 13.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Decoration as exhibition strategy** — the distinction between decoration-as-chartjunk (adding visual complexity that serves no communicative purpose) and decoration-as-exhibition (adding visual complexity specifically to compete for attention in a public-display context). These are formally identical — both add marks beyond what the data requires — but they have different purposes and different justifications. My rubric currently cannot distinguish them. A work designed for public display in a competitive environment has a different Visual Economy calculation than a work designed for contemplative study. *Rubric anchor: Visual Economy. Implication: Visual Economy should ask "is the visual complexity appropriate to the reading context?" not just "does each mark represent data?" In public-display contexts, some complexity that would be chartjunk in a report is functional as attention-arrest.*

2. **Political stakes as resonance amplifier** — Du Bois's charts were produced at the lowest point in American race relations since the Civil War. The Plessy v. Ferguson decision had just (1896) affirmed "separate but equal." Lynching was at its peak. The political stakes of demonstrating Black achievement to an international audience in 1900 were existential, not rhetorical. The Type III Resonance score asks whether the advocacy goal was achieved, but it does not account for the magnitude of the stakes — a work that successfully advocates under extreme political pressure, at great personal and institutional risk, for a goal that matters at the level of human dignity, is not equivalent to a work that successfully advocates for a minor policy reform. *Rubric anchor: Resonance (Type III). Implication: advocacy efficacy scores should consider the stakes of the advocacy goal, not just the achievement of it.*
