# Brief: Snow's Broad Street Cholera Map (1855)

*Canonical import.*

## Subject

A map of the streets around Broad Street, Golden Square, Soho, London, showing the location of deaths during the cholera outbreak of August–September 1854. John Snow, a physician, compiled the death data door-to-door from local records and plotted them himself.

**What the map shows:**
- Street grid of approximately 10 × 12 blocks of central Soho
- Black stacked bars (one bar = one death) placed perpendicular to the street at each address where a death occurred, stacked outward from the street
- Eleven water pump locations marked with a distinct symbol (circle or cross)
- No legend stating the causal argument; the viewer is meant to see it

**What the map argues** (by spatial evidence):
- Deaths cluster densely around the Broad Street pump
- Deaths are sparse or absent near the Lion Brewery (workers drank beer, not water)
- Deaths are sparse near the Poland Street Workhouse (had its own well)
- Deaths occur along the path people would walk to reach the Broad Street pump
- One death far from the cluster is explained: a woman had water from the Broad Street pump sent to her because she preferred its taste

The spatial clustering is the argument. Snow persuaded the local Board of Guardians to remove the Broad Street pump handle in September 1854. The outbreak was already declining; the removal's causal contribution is debated. The pump handle was reinstalled after the outbreak subsided without official acceptance of Snow's theory.

## Audience

**Original:** Medical community, local government officials, public health administrators. Second edition published 1855 — a scientific/medical readership. Not a world's fair public; not a parliamentary audience. Experts who could evaluate spatial evidence for a disease source.

**Review audience:** DEGAS panel, v1.3.

## School

**School type: B (Founder).** The founding act of epidemiological spatial analysis — using spatial clustering of point events as methodological evidence for source identification. Thematic cartography existed before Snow (disease maps had been made); the specific method — placing one mark per event at its geographic origin and reading the clustering pattern as causal evidence — is Snow's founding contribution. No prior work established this as a method.

School not yet documented in `schools/`. This design description serves as the initial grammar statement.

## Core Questions (Review Mode)

**E1/E2 split test (primary purpose of this review):**
- Does this work exhibit **domain-gated resonance** (E1): the clustering around the Broad Street pump is immediately legible to epidemiologists as a founding moment; is it legible to general viewers in the same way?
- Does this work exhibit **delayed resonance** (E2): Snow died in 1858, before germ theory was established, without widespread recognition. The map became canonical decades later. Is this the same phenomenon as Du Bois's delayed record-entry (#40), or is it different?
- Is **testimonial resonance** (Du Bois's #42) present here? Is Snow's making act politically urgent in the way Du Bois's was?

If E1 and E2 fire independently — domain-gated without testimonial; delayed without political urgency — the split is confirmed.

**Cluster F test:** Snow's data model is that spatial clustering IS causal evidence. Is this the same claim as Beck's (topology IS the faithful encoding of network data)? Does F apply here?

**Cluster G test:** The cholera map has no visual complexity beyond what the data requires. Is Cluster G (Visual Economy context-dependent) relevant when the audience is scientific, not public-display? If not — if the scientific audience makes decoration-as-accessibility irrelevant — this non-confirmation is itself useful for defining when G applies.

## Constraints

- The map is published in Snow's 1855 book; we review that printed version
- Snow drew the map himself; it was engraved for publication
- Black-and-white only; color not used (unlike Nightingale and Du Bois)
- Small scale (approximately 12 × 14 cm in the book); designed for close reading in a scientific text
- Snow died in 1858, aged 45, before his theory was widely accepted
- Germ theory (Pasteur, Koch) was established 1860s–1880s, after the map's publication

## Open Questions

- Is spatial clustering sufficient causal evidence without knowing the mechanism? (The statistician will have a position.)
- The outbreak was declining when the pump handle was removed — did the map achieve its advocacy goal or did it merely coincide with natural decline?
- Does the absence of deaths (brewery, workhouse) count as part of the map's data, or as supplementary narrative?
