## Review

**Precedents and the founding act:**

Disease mapping existed before Snow. Thomas Shapter mapped cholera deaths in Exeter in 1849. Robert Baker mapped typhus in Leeds in 1833. The German physician Leonhard Fincke produced thematic mortality maps in the 1790s. What Snow adds is not the idea of mapping disease deaths geographically — it is the specific method of using spatial clustering as causal evidence to identify a source, with systematic attention to negative cases.

The founding act is methodological, not cartographic. The map is the argument's vehicle, not its innovation. The innovation is: if you plot every event at its source location, and the events cluster near a candidate cause, and events are sparse where that cause is absent, you have evidence for causation. This is the birth of modern epidemiology as a science of source-tracing through spatial distribution.

Snow's medical context is important: in 1854, the dominant theory of cholera transmission was miasma (bad air). Snow was not just proposing an alternative mechanism; he was attacking the conceptual framework that most physicians and the General Board of Health were using. His spatial argument threatened the dominant paradigm. He was right; the paradigm resisted.

**On School Fidelity (Type B — Founding):**

Score 18. The founding grammar is clearly recoverable. What docks two points: the method Snow is founding requires the map AND the text to express fully. A practitioner learning epidemiological source-tracing from the map alone would miss the negative-evidence methodology — the systematic attention to where events did NOT occur. Snow's most important methodological contribution (triangulation through negative cases) is in the prose, not the image.

This is a structural limitation of the map medium for this particular method. The map cannot easily show "absence of events in regions where events would be expected if the proposed mechanism were operating globally." Snow solved this in text; a stronger founding grammar would need to solve it visually too. Subsequent epidemiologists have developed methods for this (dot-density maps with null zones, choropleth with statistical significance shading), but those came later.

**The delayed resonance question (E2):**

Snow died on June 16, 1858, aged 45. He never saw germ theory established. He never saw his method validated by mechanism. He never received wide recognition for either his cholera work or his pioneering work on anesthesia.

The map became canonical in the 20th century. The first edition of his book (1849) was largely ignored; the second (1855, containing the map) was read in medical circles but not widely celebrated. The epidemiology textbooks that made his work foundational were written decades after his death.

This delayed recognition differs from Du Bois's in a crucial respect: Snow's work was not suppressed or discounted due to identity-based discrimination. It was ahead of its scientific framework — the analytical tools (germ theory, bacterial culture, statistical causal inference methods) needed to validate his method did not exist during his lifetime. The medical establishment was not refusing to see what was there; it genuinely could not verify the mechanism by available methods.

This is the Nightingale innovation (#1 from her panel review) confirmed: **epistemic delay** (framework unavailable) is distinct from **political delay** (framework withheld). The E2 cluster must split:

- **E2a — Epistemic delay**: work recognized posthumously because the validating framework didn't yet exist (Snow, possibly others)
- **E2b — Political delay/testimonial**: work suppressed or delayed because of identity-based discrimination or political pressure against the work being credited (Du Bois)

Both have "delayed resonance" as a surface feature. Their causes, and therefore their implications for assessment, are different.

**Comparison to successor works:**

The first work to use Snow's method explicitly is probably John Simon's work in the 1860s-70s. William Farr, who initially disagreed with Snow, later came to accept the waterborne theory after further data. The method Snow founded — spatial clustering as source identification — is now standard in outbreak investigation globally, taught in every epidemiology program.

The Snow map's late emergence as a canonical example is partly a construction of science history in the 20th century; it was not a natural emergence. Science historians like John Eyler and Tom Koch have analyzed how Snow's work was selectively elevated to founding status in the epidemiology origin story. The canonization is real; it was also constructed.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **E2 cluster split: epistemic vs. political delay** — confirming Nightingale's innovation and formalizing the split. Delayed resonance (E2) must distinguish: (a) epistemic delay — framework to validate the work arrived posthumously; (b) political-testimonial delay — framework existed but was withheld due to identity, power, or political pressure. Both produce posthumous recognition; they have different moral valences and different implications for how we assess the work's relationship to its moment. *Rubric anchor: Resonance (E2). Implication: the E2 cluster, when amended, should include this distinction explicitly. Canonical import reviews should specify which type of delay applies.*

2. **Canonization as a separate event** — some works achieve their resonance not through their initial reception but through a later act of canonization (science historians, textbook authors, discipline-founders selecting a particular work as the founding document). Snow's map was not celebrated in 1855; it was elevated to founding status by 20th-century epidemiology historians. This canonization is a form of delayed resonance that is neither the epistemic variety (the framework came later) nor the political-testimonial variety — it is an institutional construction of founding narrative. Works can be "made canonical" by later actors; this is different from naturally accumulating resonance. *Rubric anchor: Resonance (E2). Implication: the E2 distinction should include a third sub-type: constructed canonization — works elevated to founding status by later institutional actors, which may or may not reflect the work's actual historical significance.*
