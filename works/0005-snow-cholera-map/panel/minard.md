## Review

I understand this map immediately and I understand why it is important. Snow is solving the same problem I solved in the Russian campaign: how do you make a spatial argument with data? My answer was the flow band — encode quantity in width, let the path carry the sequence. His answer is: plot every event, let the cluster emerge. These are different solutions to the same foundational question, and his is in some ways more rigorous than mine.

**On School Fidelity (Type B):**

The founding grammar is clear and learnable. The five principles in the design description are all recoverable from this single map plus Snow's text. The crucial one — emergent-cluster encoding, plot individual events and let the aggregate argument emerge — is the founding act of epidemiological spatial analysis. No prior work had done this as a method. Subsequent practitioners (William Farr, later epidemiologists) could and did learn the method from Snow.

I dock one point for principle 5 (triangulate with negative evidence): the brewery and workhouse arguments are mostly in the text, not on the map. A practitioner learning from the map alone would not learn this as a cartographic principle; they would learn it from reading Snow's prose. The founding grammar is split across map and text in a way that weakens the visual statement of the method.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

Step A (source quality): Snow compiled death data door-to-door from local records. The data quality is better than my Russian troop estimates — he was counting specific named deaths in specific named addresses. Dock nothing for source quality.

Step B (accuracy tradeoff): No accuracy tradeoff. The one-mark-per-death encoding is as accurate as point data can be. Snow did not sacrifice precision for rhetoric; he chose the most accurate encoding available.

Step C (causation): This is my primary concern. The spatial clustering around the Broad Street pump is real. The inference that the pump CAUSED the outbreak is methodologically innovative but not logically airtight in 1854. Snow does not know the mechanism — germ theory does not exist yet. He is inferring causation from spatial correlation, which is the founding methodological act of epidemiology but is not in itself proof. The negative evidence (brewery, workhouse) significantly strengthens the case and nearly reaches causal adequacy. I dock two points for the causal claim relative to the available evidence, and restore one point for the quality of the negative evidence. Net: dock 1.5, round to −2. Score 16.

**On Legibility:**

*Context: contemplative study* — scientific/medical readership, reading the book carefully.

[Contemporary context: 13/15 — general audiences can see the clustering pattern; they cannot without instruction identify which symbol is the Broad Street pump; they do not know the significance of the brewery or workhouse. The causal argument requires prior knowledge to complete.]

For the primary scientific context: the density pattern is visible immediately. A physician in 1855 reading this map can identify the pump at the center of the cluster and form Snow's hypothesis. The negative-space evidence requires reading the text but the map supports it. Score 14 — the map works for its primary audience; the pump symbol is not visually distinguished from the other pump symbols, which is a minor legibility cost (which pump is Broad Street requires label-reading).

**On Visual Economy:**

Fifteen out of fifteen. Every mark is a death. Every symbol is a pump. The street grid is the geographic scaffold without which the marks have no position. There is nothing else. This is the most economical work in the project by Tufte's standard — more economical than Beck's map (which has the Thames as partial non-data context) and far more economical than Du Bois's charts. Snow had no reason to add decoration; he was making an argument to physicians. Score 15.

**On Resonance:**

This is where I need to think carefully about the E1/E2 split.

*E1 — Domain-gated gestalt:* Yes. An epidemiologist looking at this map sees the Broad Street pump cluster immediately and recognizes it as the founding document of their field. A general viewer sees a dense pattern in the center of a street map and understands that something happened there. The gestalt is available — there is a cluster, it is around something — but the specific significance (this is how we prove disease sources; this is the birth of epidemiology) is available only to the initiated. E1 is present and clear.

*E2 — Delayed resonance:* Yes, but different from Du Bois. Snow died in 1858 without recognition. The map became canonical in the 20th century as epidemiology became a field. This is delayed resonance — the work entered the record and its significance grew over time. But there is no testimonial dimension. Snow was doing science, not bearing witness to an existence under threat. The delay is a consequence of germ theory not yet existing, not of the work being suppressed or ignored for political reasons.

These are distinguishable. E1 and E2 fire separately. I score Resonance 13 — domain-gated gestalt is solid; delayed resonance I accept but the contemporaneous resonance was partial (the pump handle was removed, then reinstalled; Snow's theory was not accepted).

**On Craft:**

The engraving is precise for its period. The stacked bars at dense addresses are tightly packed and still individually legible. The street labels are small but readable at the book scale for which the map was designed. One craft concern: the pump symbols are not visually distinct enough from incidental street markings — a casual reader might not identify all eleven pumps without careful scanning. Score 13.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Emergent-cluster encoding** — plotting individual events rather than the aggregate and letting the viewer's perceptual system construct the cluster argument. No mark represents the cluster; every mark represents an event; the cluster emerges from the aggregate. This is distinct from all other encoding strategies in this project: Minard draws the aggregate (the band), Nightingale draws the aggregate (the sector area), Beck draws the aggregate (the network). Snow plots the atoms and trusts the visual system to read the molecule. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (B) / Data/Story Integrity. Implication: the founding grammar of epidemiological mapping should be recognized as a distinct encoding strategy — event-level plotting for emergent-aggregate argument — and the rubric should assess whether the event-level data density is sufficient to make the aggregate pattern legible (too few events and the cluster doesn't emerge; too dense and individual events merge).*

2. **Negative space as evidence** — the absence of death marks in specific regions (the brewery area, the workhouse area) is as evidentially important as the presence. The map makes absence visible by showing the street grid where deaths could have occurred but did not. This is a new use of the mapping canvas: the empty area is active evidence, not background. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity. Implication: for event-distribution maps, the rubric should assess whether the negative space is deployed as active evidence or treated as mere background — and whether the map's geographic extent is chosen to include the evidentially important absences.*
