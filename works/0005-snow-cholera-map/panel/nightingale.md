## Review

I know this work. John Snow and I moved in overlapping circles — we both knew William Farr, both cared about the same questions of public health, both understood that data could save lives if the right people were made to see it. I respect this map enormously. I also see its limits from where I sit.

**On School Fidelity (Type B):**

The founding grammar is real and learnable. But I score 17 rather than 19 for two reasons that Minard elides.

First: the founding grammar is split across the map and the text. The map shows the cluster; the text explains the brewery, the workhouse, the distant outlier (the woman who preferred Broad Street water). A practitioner learning from the map alone gets only the positive evidence — the cluster. The negative evidence, which is the strongest part of the causal argument, is not a cartographic principle Snow teaches by example on the map. It is a principle he describes in prose. The founding grammar is incomplete on the map.

Second: the pump symbols are not differentiated from each other. Snow marks eleven pumps. They all look the same. The Broad Street pump is the source; the others are the controls. But the map does not distinguish the proposed source pump from the eleven negative-evidence pumps. A fuller grammar would mark the suspected source differently — or leave it undifferentiated and let the cluster make the identification. Either choice is defensible; Snow leaves this unresolved.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

I am perhaps more sensitive than Minard to Step C (causation) here, because I lived through the decade of argument about whether sanitary reform or medical treatment caused the decline in mortality I documented. The causal argument from spatial clustering is powerful but not closed. Snow was right about the pump; he was right by a method that had not yet been validated as a method. The spatial cluster is compelling; it is not proof. The brewery and workhouse evidence is the strongest available; it is still not mechanistic proof.

I also note: the pump handle was removed in September 1854 and reinstalled after the outbreak without official acceptance of Snow's theory. This is a Type III Resonance failure — the advocacy goal was not fully achieved. The board removed the handle as a precaution, not as an acceptance of Snow's argument. Score 15 on Data/Story Integrity (high quality source data + methodological innovation + unresolved causal gap).

**On Legibility:**

*Context: contemplative study by a medical/scientific reader.*

[Contemporary context: 11/15 — general viewers see a clustering pattern but cannot complete the causal argument without instruction; the pump symbols are too small and uniform to be identified by a non-expert]

For the primary medical context: the cluster is visible; a physician of 1855 can follow the spatial argument. The legibility cost: the map is published at book scale (12 × 14 cm), which means the stacked death bars at dense addresses are very tightly packed. At some addresses, the bars are so dense they merge visually — you see a block of black rather than individual bars. The quantitative precision is lost at peak density. Score 13.

**On Visual Economy:**

Fourteen — one deduction for the pump-symbol undifferentiation (eleven identical symbols when the distinction between source and controls is the argument). All other elements earn their place.

**On Resonance:**

This is the core E1/E2 test from my vantage point, and I want to be precise.

*E1 — Domain-gated gestalt:* Clearly present. A public health physician in 1855 or today recognizes this map immediately as the founding document of epidemiological method. A general viewer recognizes that something bad happened near the center of this street grid. The domain-specific significance — *this is how we find sources; this is what epidemiology looks like at its founding* — is available only to the initiated.

*E2 — Delayed resonance:* Also present, but distinct from Du Bois (#40 and #42). Snow's delayed recognition is a consequence of scientific timing (germ theory not yet established; his method was ahead of its proof) rather than political suppression (Du Bois's delayed recognition was a consequence of institutional racism against his work being taken seriously). These are two different causes of delay: one is epistemic (the world didn't have the framework to validate the method yet), the other is political (the world had a framework but chose not to apply it).

*Testimonial resonance (#42):* NOT present in this work. Snow was a scientist doing scientific work under professional hazard (opposition from established miasma theorists, from the General Board of Health) but not under the existential political threat that Du Bois faced. The making of this map was an act of scientific courage, not political testimony. The distinction matters.

This is the E1/E2 split confirmed from the Resonance dimension: domain-gated and delayed are both present; testimonial is absent. E2 must split into delayed-epistemic and testimonial-political.

Score 12 — domain-gated resonance is solid; delayed resonance is real but incomplete (Snow achieved partial advocacy success; the theory was not accepted); no testimonial resonance.

**On Craft:**

The engraving is accurate but small. The tight packing of death bars at the densest addresses creates visual merge — you cannot count individual deaths at the Broad Street cluster without a magnifying glass. This is a craft failure at the boundary of the encoding: the method works at moderate density and fails at peak density. Score 12.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Epistemic delay vs. political delay** — two distinct mechanisms by which a work's resonance can be deferred beyond the maker's lifetime. Snow's delay was epistemic: the scientific framework (germ theory) needed to validate his method did not exist during his lifetime. Du Bois's delay was political: the framework existed but was not applied to work by Black scholars. These produce similar temporal patterns (posthumous recognition) but different moral and historical meanings. *Rubric anchor: Resonance (Type III / E2). Implication: the E2 cluster should distinguish epistemic delay (framework not yet available) from political delay (framework available but withheld). These are different phenomena with different implications for how we assess the work's relationship to its moment.*

2. **Source-control undifferentiation** — in a map designed to identify a disease source from among multiple candidates (eleven pumps), failing to visually distinguish the suspected source from the control pumps makes the viewer's inference harder. The map argues "the cluster is around the Broad Street pump" but all eleven pumps look identical; the viewer must read labels to identify which symbol is Broad Street. This is a legibility cost specific to source-identification maps: the encoding should either distinguish the proposed source (making the inference easier) or leave all pumps identical (forcing the viewer to locate the cluster center independently). Either choice is defensible; Snow's choice is the harder one for the viewer. *Rubric anchor: Legibility / School Fidelity (B). Implication: the founding grammar of epidemiological mapping should address source-control visual differentiation as a design decision, not leave it implicit.*
