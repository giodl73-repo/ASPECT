## Review

**Context specification:**

- Primary audience: Educated European general public, naturalists, students; not specialist taxonomists
- Primary reading context: Contemplative study — serial installments purchased and studied at home; designed for reading-desk engagement, extended time, solo
- Contemporary context: Museum, library, and digital reproduction; often encountered as art prints before scientific documents
- Context decay: Moderate — the scientific context has eroded significantly; these are now primarily known as art objects rather than as biological reference

**[contemporary: 11/15 — as art objects, the plates succeed; as biological reference, the lack of scale and systematic labeling fails the contemporary scientific reader]**

**On legibility for the original audience:**

The intended reader — an educated European of 1900 with general scientific interest — would have had several of the following: familiarity with the concept of the microscope, some knowledge of natural history taxonomy, and access to Haeckel's accompanying text volumes. For this reader, the plates function well. The taxonomic group is declared per plate. The organisms are rendered with sufficient detail to communicate their characteristic forms. The overall message — "look at the extraordinary diversity and beauty of microscopic life" — is immediately available.

Where the legibility fails even this sympathetic reader: relative scale. A reader looking at radiolaria plates and bat plates in the same volume cannot determine whether they are looking at organisms of similar or vastly different sizes. This is not a small omission. Size is one of the most fundamental biological facts about an organism, and it is consistently absent from the encoding.

The text volumes are doing work the plates should do themselves. The plates are not fully self-sufficient as scientific documents; they require the accompanying essay to provide the taxonomic context, the scale information, and the interpretive framework. For a contemplative-study context this is acceptable — the reader has access to the text. For any faster or more casual context it would be a serious failure.

**On the Art Nouveau secondary audience:**

The plates were also encountered by a secondary audience for whom they functioned as design references. For this audience — architects, textile designers, ornamentalists — the plates are perfectly legible in a different way: as a vocabulary of biological forms available for formal appropriation. The radial symmetry of radiolaria, the branching geometry of siphonophores — these are visually extractable without any biological knowledge. This audience reads the plates through a completely different frame and succeeds.

The existence of this secondary audience, and its success in reading the plates for design purposes, tells us something important: the plates contain a formal vocabulary that is visually independent of the scientific content. The forms communicate even without the taxonomic labels. This is a legibility success in a domain Haeckel did not design for. Worth noting, though it does not raise the primary score.

**On first-impression legibility:**

Opening a plate, a general viewer immediately understands: these are biological organisms, rendered carefully, arranged for display. The variety and intricacy are immediately legible as *natural complexity.* The headline is available: "this is what microscopic life looks like." That is a legibility success for the work's primary purpose.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Secondary-audience legibility** — scientific illustration plates can achieve legibility for an unintended secondary audience (designers, artists) through their formal vocabulary, independent of their scientific content. This secondary legibility is not the same as primary legibility but it has contributed significantly to these works' canonical status. The rubric's Legibility dimension does not currently assess multi-audience legibility. *Rubric anchor: Legibility / Resonance.*
