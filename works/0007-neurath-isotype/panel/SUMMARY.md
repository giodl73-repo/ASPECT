## Key Agreements

**School Fidelity is the strongest dimension -- scores 18-19.** All reviewers agree the founding grammar is complete, learnable, and consistently applied across the three reviewed charts. This is the clearest founding grammar the project has reviewed: explicit rules, consistent application, documented theory. Neurath's *International Picture Language* (1936) makes him the only founding-school persona in the project who published his own grammar explicitly.

**Legibility in the primary context is excellent -- scores 13-15.** The public-display reading context is demanding (walking viewers, 2-3 meter distance, no statistical training), and all reviewers credit the charts with succeeding in it. The comparison is available before approach; the headline is legible at distance; the counting gesture is intuitive. The one shared failure: the unit-declaration legend requires closer approach to read.

**Data Integrity is contested -- scores 13-16.** The spread between Neurath (16) and the Statistician (13) is the sharpest disagreement in this panel. The Statistician's normalization failure concern (absolute counts vs. per-capita rates in Chart A) is the primary source. All reviewers dock for something; none dock heavily. The system is honest about its precision limits, less honest about its normalization choices.

## Key Tensions

**The Neurath-Tufte disagreement -- the central tension, as specified:**

Neurath: repetition of symbols IS visual economy for the intended audience. The measure of economy is cognitive steps from viewer to understanding, not marks per datum. For a viewer with no statistical training, repeated countable symbols require fewer cognitive steps than a bar chart.

Tufte: visual economy is a property of the display, not a post-hoc rationalization of what any particular audience prefers. More ink per datum is lower economy, period. The audience argument proves too much -- it licenses any decoration that any audience finds comfortable.

The Audience lens provides the decisive evidence: a representative of the intended audience finds the ISOTYPE charts more immediately legible than a bar chart would be, in 15 seconds, without instruction. This is not a preference; it is a measured cognitive outcome.

**The panel does not resolve this disagreement.** But it clarifies the terms: Tufte's metric (marks per datum) and Neurath's metric (cognitive steps for the specific audience) are both valid measures of different things. The rubric currently uses Tufte's metric exclusively. Cluster G, if ratified, would add Neurath's metric as a context-sensitive alternative.

**Normalization: the Statistician's challenge:**

The Statistician identifies a systematic ISOTYPE flaw: absolute-count default produces misleading cross-country comparisons when population sizes differ significantly. Neurath and Nightingale both defend the choice as audience-appropriate (rates require understanding "per capita"). The Statistician insists that even if the audience cannot compute rates, the chart should not imply a structural comparison the data does not support.

No resolution; genuine methodological disagreement. The Step B rubric (accuracy tradeoff) scores this as "undeclared but defensible" -- dock 1-2, not 3-4.
