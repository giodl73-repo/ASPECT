## Review

**Context specification:**

- Primary audience: Working-class Viennese museum visitors, 1926-1934; limited formal education; limited or no statistical training; literate but not numerically sophisticated
- Primary reading context: Public display -- museum wall installation; walking visitors; glancing at 2-3 meters; no extended reading expected
- Contemporary context: Design history collections, digital archives; often studied as design objects rather than as communication tools
- Context decay: Moderate to high -- the original display context (the Vienna museum) no longer exists; contemporary encounter is primarily scholarly or aesthetic
- Cluster G test: This work is the primary test case for Cluster G (Visual Economy context-dependence in public-display contexts)

**[contemporary: 12/15 -- as design objects they communicate their formal qualities; as statistical displays they are studied rather than used, which inflates their apparent legibility for a contemporary viewer who brings statistical literacy to them]**

**On primary-context legibility -- the Cluster G test:**

I am the audience that this work is designed for (or rather, I am representing them). I will try to be that audience.

I walk into the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum. I see a large chart on the wall. There are figures -- human figures, silhouettes -- arranged in rows. Germany: one row of ten figures. USA: three rows plus four more. The sign says: each figure equals one million workers. I can count. I count ten. I count thirty-four. Germany has ten million; the USA has thirty-four million.

I understood that in fifteen seconds. Without training. Without anyone explaining it to me. I would not have understood a bar chart as quickly -- I would have needed to find the axis, read the scale, convert the number. I might have found the chart, shrugged, and walked on.

This is the Cluster G confirmation: for this audience and this reading context, the repeated symbol is more legible, faster, and more reliable than the more data-ink-efficient bar chart would be. Visual economy, measured in cognitive steps from viewer to understanding for this specific audience, is higher in the ISOTYPE chart. Score 15 of 15 for primary context.

**On the unit declaration as a legibility risk:**

One failure. The unit declaration (1 figure = 1 million workers) is in small text below the chart. At 2-3 meters reading distance, it is not readable without approaching. A viewer who does not approach will not know the unit -- they will know that Germany has fewer than the USA, but not by how much in absolute terms. The ratio is available at a glance; the absolute count requires the unit declaration; the unit declaration requires approach. This is a minor legibility failure in the primary context. I subtract zero from the score because the ratio comparison is the primary message; the absolute count is secondary. But the unit declaration placement should be noted.

**On the Cluster G question for the rubric:**

This work directly confirms that Visual Economy, as currently anchored, does not score correctly for low-literacy public-display audiences. The current anchors assume a viewer who can decode statistical abstractions efficiently. For a viewer who cannot, more marks (repeated symbols) reduce cognitive friction. The rubric should add a context step to Visual Economy: specify the audience's statistical literacy and the display context before applying the anchors. The anchors for a public-display, low-literacy context should weight differently than anchors for a contemplative-study, expert context.
