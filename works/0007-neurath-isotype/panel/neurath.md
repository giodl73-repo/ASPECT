## Review

I will say something that I rarely say: this is a work that does what it sets out to do, almost exactly.

The grammar is complete. Repeat the unit symbol; never scale it. One figure equals one million workers, and the eye of the factory worker in Vienna can count the figures and know immediately: Germany has ten million manufacturing workers; the United States has thirty-four. He does not need to know what a million is. He does not need to be able to read a numerical table. He can count. Everyone can count. That is the point.

**On the founding grammar (Type B assessment):**

What can a future practitioner learn from these charts alone? Everything essential. Repeat, do not scale. Standardized symbols per referent -- one symbol type per thing depicted; Arntz's figure for a worker is the same in every chart. Flat color, no shadow, no gradient. No numbers inside the graphic. Unit declared outside. Comparison by array adjacency. This is a complete grammar. A student who studied only these three charts would know how to make ISOTYPE charts, and they would make them correctly. Score 19 of 20 -- I withhold one point because the unit declaration (1 symbol = X units) is small and easily missed by a viewer approaching the display from a distance; the founding grammar does not fully specify how to make the unit declaration legible at the display scale.

**On the Visual Economy question -- the central contested point:**

I understand what Tufte will say. He will say that a bar chart communicates the same comparison with less ink. He is correct that it uses less ink. He is wrong that this means it communicates more efficiently.

For the working-class visitor to the Vienna museum in 1930, a bar chart requires: understanding that bar length represents quantity, that the scale on the axis provides the reference, that you must read the axis number and mentally convert it. These are not obvious steps. For a viewer who has never seen a chart, a bar chart is not simpler than a symbol array -- it is abstract in a different way. The symbol array requires: recognizing that the figure is a worker (self-evident from the image), counting the figures (a universally learned skill), comparing the two rows. These steps are easier for a viewer with no statistical training.

The repetition of symbols is not visual waste. The repetition IS the visual economy, correctly measured for the intended audience. Tufte measures economy in ink. I measure economy in cognitive steps from viewer to understanding. These are different measurements, and for different audiences, they produce different answers. Visual Economy must be context-sensitive. This is why Cluster G must eventually be ratified.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

I will not pretend the system is perfect. The precision available from counting figures is limited by the granularity of the unit. One figure equals one million workers. What if the true count is 34.7 million? The chart shows 34 figures plus... what? A partial figure? I used the convention of showing whole figures only. This rounds the data to the nearest unit. For social and economic statistics of this period, rounding to the nearest million workers is not a distortion -- the source data was not more precise than that. But I must acknowledge that the system sacrifices sub-unit precision, and when it is applied to data with false precision at the unit level, the rounding can mislead. Step B: -2 for the declared precision tradeoff, which I declare but the viewer must accept.

**On advocacy and democratic resonance:**

This work changed what working-class Viennese people knew about their own economic situation. They could see, on the museum wall, that they were in housing conditions that were worse than their neighbors in other European cities. They could count this. They could argue about it. That is political agency produced by visualization. I know of no bar chart that did the same thing.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Audience-relative visual economy** -- visual economy (minimum marks for maximum communication) is not context-independent. For an audience with limited statistical training, more marks (repeated symbols) can mean less cognitive work than fewer marks (bar chart). The rubric's Visual Economy anchors assume a literate reader; they do not account for the audience's statistical training as a variable in what constitutes economy. This directly confirms Cluster G. *Rubric anchor: Visual Economy.*

2. **Unit-granularity precision tradeoff** -- the ISOTYPE system's rounding to whole units is a known and declared accuracy tradeoff, not a data quality failure. The rubric's Data/Story Integrity Step B should explicitly recognize this type of declared-and-proportionate tradeoff as distinct from undeclared distortion. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step B).*
