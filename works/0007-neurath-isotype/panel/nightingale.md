## Review

Neurath and I share a mission that the others on this panel do not fully share: we are not making charts for people who already understand statistics. We are making charts to reach people who have been kept from understanding statistics -- because understanding statistics is a form of power, and power has historically been rationed.

That shared mission gives me both the sympathy to appreciate what ISOTYPE achieves and the critical distance to identify where it falls short.

**On advocacy and democratic accessibility:**

The housing-conditions chart (Chart B) is the most powerful of the three reviewed. The shift from dark houses to light houses across 1900-1927 is immediately legible as improvement. The viewer does not need to know what overcrowding means statistically; the visual proportion of dark to light is self-evident. This is precisely the kind of encoding I tried to achieve with the coxcomb: make the proportion available before the number is parsed.

Where ISOTYPE exceeds my own approach: it works for viewers with lower literacy than the politicians and administrators I targeted. My coxcomb required an educated viewer who could engage with a somewhat unfamiliar chart form. ISOTYPE works for a viewer who has never seen a statistical chart. Neurath's audience is genuinely broader than mine.

**On advocacy efficacy (Resonance Type III):**

The charts from the Vienna museum are not simply beautiful or informative. They are political instruments. They communicate to working-class visitors the conditions of their own lives in terms they can understand and act on. The infant mortality chart (Chart C) -- baby-cradle pictograms, sorted by nation, Romania at the top -- is not a neutral observation. It is a claim that your infant is more likely to die here than there, and it is addressed to the people whose infants are dying. This is advocacy visualization in the fullest sense.

The question I always ask: did it work? Did anyone act differently because of these charts? The historical evidence is partial but positive. The Vienna museum attracted working-class visitors in significant numbers; the charts were cited in social reform debates; the ISOTYPE method was adopted by international organizations and educators. This is a plausible causal chain from visualization to action. Resonance Type III: score 14.

**On the data integrity concern -- normalization:**

Tufte will raise the normalization problem (absolute counts vs. per-capita rates). He is correct that Chart A (Germany vs. USA workers) presents raw counts rather than rates. I want to note something he may not: Neurath was aware of this. His choice to show absolute counts rather than rates was deliberate -- absolute counts communicate more immediately to a viewer without statistical training than per-capita rates (which require knowing what "per capita" means). The accuracy tradeoff is real. It is also, for this audience, arguably the right tradeoff. Step B: -1 for undeclared tradeoff that is defensible given audience and context.

**On legibility in the primary context:**

Public display: museum wall, walking viewer, 2-3 meters reading distance. The charts are designed for this. The symbols are large, bold, and legible at distance. The comparison is available without approaching the chart. Score 14 -- one point withheld because the unit declaration legend is small and requires closer approach to read.

**On what ISOTYPE cannot do:**

Neurath's method is excellent for comparison at the population level. It is less suited to individual variation, uncertainty representation, or multi-variable relationships. These are not failures of the works reviewed; they are the appropriate limits of the school. The rubric should score the work against what the school attempts, not against what it does not attempt.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Advocacy-proportionate accuracy tradeoff** -- when a visualization deliberately sacrifices quantitative precision (absolute vs. per-capita rates) to serve advocacy legibility for a specific audience, this tradeoff is defensible if declared and proportionate. The rubric's Step B should distinguish: (a) undeclared and indefensible, (b) undeclared but defensible given audience, (c) declared and proportionate. ISOTYPE's tradeoffs fall into category (b), not (a). *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step B).*

2. **Audience bandwidth as advocacy design variable** -- the intended audience's cognitive baseline (statistical literacy, reading ability, formal education) should be a declared variable in advocacy visualization design. A visualization that succeeds with a lower-literacy audience is achieving a harder communication task than one that succeeds with a higher-literacy audience, and the rubric should recognize this. *Rubric anchor: Legibility / Resonance (Type III).*
