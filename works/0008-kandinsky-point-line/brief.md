# Brief: Kandinsky's Point and Line to Plane (1926)

*Canonical import — the analytical diagrams within the theoretical text.*

## Subject

The diagrams in Kandinsky's Bauhaus textbook *Punkt und Linie zu Fläche* (1926). Not his paintings — the analytical diagrams he draws to illustrate his theory of abstract visual elements: how points generate tension, how lines carry direction and temperature (warm/cold, active/passive), how planes have weight and breath.

The diagrams are: dot patterns demonstrating compositional tension; line-type illustrations showing different energies (horizontal, diagonal, curved); angle analysis; compositional rhythm studies. They are neither data charts nor artworks in the painting sense — they are **visual arguments** about the formal properties of abstract elements.

**Why these, not the paintings**: the diagrams are Kandinsky making claims about visualization itself — about how visual elements work. They are the most analytical, and therefore the most relevant to a visualization study.

## School

**Type B (Founder) — abstract visual grammar as visualization.** The claim: visual elements have formal, learnable properties independent of what they represent. This is a theory of visual encoding — it just encodes psychological/formal properties rather than external data.

## Core Questions

**Rubric stress test**: This is the first work in the project with no external data. The rubric was designed for information visualization. What happens when we apply it to abstract composition?

1. **Data/Story Integrity for abstract works**: what is the "data"? Kandinsky claims each element has specific formal properties (yellow is advancing; the angle of a line encodes energy; a curve is warmer than a diagonal). Are these claims falsifiable? If the work claims formal properties that can be verified perceptually, does Data/Story Integrity apply?

2. **Legibility without information extraction**: the reader of an abstract composition is not extracting facts. What does it mean to "read" this correctly?

3. **Will the rubric need new dimensions, or will it fail gracefully?**
