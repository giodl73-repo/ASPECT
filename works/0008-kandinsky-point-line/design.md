# Design Description: Kandinsky's Point and Line to Plane Diagrams

## What the Diagrams Claim to Show

*Point and Line to Plane* (1926) is a theoretical text arguing that abstract visual elements have formal, learnable properties — analogous to the grammar of music. The diagrams illustrate this theory:

**Diagram Type 1 — Tension fields**: A single point placed in different positions within a square. The claim: position relative to the frame changes the point's "tension" — its felt weight and direction. Center position: stable. Upper right: energized, rising. Lower left: heavy, sinking. The diagram shows the same mark in multiple positions and asserts that the viewer feels these differences.

**Diagram Type 2 — Line character**: Side-by-side illustrations of: horizontal line (cold, calm, passive), vertical line (warm, active, ascending), diagonal line (most dynamic, hottest or coldest depending on direction), curved line (warm, yielding, slow). Kandinsky illustrates each type with its visual character name labeled.

**Diagram Type 3 — Compositional rhythm**: A dense composition of points and lines analyzed to show rhythmic structure — where tensions cluster, where the composition "breathes," where the eye is directed.

**Diagram Type 4 — Color-form correspondence**: Color swatches paired with geometric forms. Yellow → triangle (aggressive, advancing, sharp). Blue → circle (deep, receding, smooth). Red → square (heavy, stable, grounded).

## The Encoding — or What Serves as Encoding

This is the core analytical difficulty. Kandinsky's diagrams do not encode external data in Bertin's sense. They encode claims about perceptual properties. The diagram of a point in the upper-right corner is not a data point; it is an example of a claim — the claim being that the upper-right position in a frame creates tension that the viewer experiences.

**What the diagrams claim:**
- Formal properties (position, direction, curvature) are not neutral — they produce consistent psychological/emotional responses in viewers
- These responses are systematic, learnable, and reproducible
- A composition is "correct" or "incorrect" to the extent that its formal elements cooperate to produce a consistent emotional register

**Are these claims falsifiable?** Partially. Perceptual research (including Arnheim's later work in visual psychology) has confirmed some of Kandinsky's intuitions about compositional tension. Others remain contested. The claims are empirical about perception, not arbitrary.

## The Abstract School Grammar

1. Every mark has inherent formal properties (position, direction, weight, temperature) independent of what it represents
2. Formal properties produce consistent perceptual responses across viewers
3. Composition is the arrangement of formal properties to produce a coherent emotional/aesthetic effect
4. Color has temperature (warm/cold), weight, and direction — independent of symbolic meaning
5. A composition is internally consistent when its formal properties cooperate; inconsistent when they conflict without resolution

## Color System

The color-form correspondence diagrams use flat swatches of primary colors, paired with pure geometric forms (triangle, circle, square). No gradient, no mixing. Color is presented as a formal property, not as decoration.

## Rendering Notes

- Black-and-white diagrams primarily; some color plates
- Designed as pedagogical illustrations for a Bauhaus textbook
- Small format (book size, approximately 15 × 22 cm)
- The diagrams are spare, not decorative — they are the argument stripped to essentials
