## Review

I approach Kandinsky's diagrams with a kind of professional respect for a parallel project. We are both trying to answer the same question: what are the formal properties of visual marks, and how do those properties constrain what the marks can communicate? We arrived at very different vocabularies for the same inquiry.

**On the visual grammar comparison:**

My seven visual variables (position, size, shape, value, color, orientation, texture) describe the retinal properties of marks and their capacity to communicate nominal, ordered, or quantitative information. Kandinsky's elements (point, line, plane, color temperature) describe the psychological and emotional properties of the same marks and their capacity to produce perceptual experience.

The overlap is substantial. My "orientation" variable -- the direction a mark runs -- is Kandinsky's "line character" (warm, cold, active, passive). My "value" variable -- lightness -- is adjacent to Kandinsky's "weight" concept. My "color hue" variable overlaps with Kandinsky's "color temperature."

The difference: my variables are defined by what the visual system can decode reliably and efficiently. Kandinsky's elements are defined by what the viewer experiences. These are not incompatible descriptions; they are descriptions at different levels of the same perceptual system.

The grammar comparison: Kandinsky's abstract grammar is internally coherent and learnable. It is different from mine but not incompatible. A practitioner who learned both would have a richer vocabulary for thinking about marks. Score on School Fidelity 17 -- strong founding grammar, with the gap that the compositional grammar (how elements combine to form a whole) is less fully specified than the element grammar (how individual elements work).

**On Data/Story Integrity -- the hardest question:**

The claims in these diagrams are empirical claims about perception. I will audit them using my rubric:

*Position-as-tension (Diagram Type 1):* The claim that the same point in different frame positions produces different tensions is testable. It corresponds to Gestalt perceptual psychology's findings on figure-ground relationships and positional salience. I accept this as a supported claim. No dock.

*Line character (Diagram Type 2):* The claim that horizontal lines feel cold and diagonal lines feel dynamic is partially supported by cross-cultural research on line orientation and affect. The thermal metaphor (warm/cold) is less secure -- it is a common synaesthetic mapping in European cultures but not universal. Dock -1 for undeclared cultural specificity.

*Color-form correspondence (Diagram Type 4):* Yellow = triangle, blue = circle, red = square. This is the weakest claim in the work. The correspondence is asserted as formal and universal; subsequent research has found it to be partially cultural. The specific yellow-triangle correspondence has not been robustly replicated cross-culturally. Step C: a causal claim (these correspondences are formal properties, not cultural associations) that significantly overreaches the available evidence. Dock -3.

Net: 17 - 1 - 3 = 13.

**On Visual Economy:**

The diagrams are spare. Black-and-white predominantly; minimal marks; each diagram makes one claim and then stops. The color-form diagrams are slightly more complex (color swatches + geometric forms + labels), but still economical. This is the economy of pedagogical argumentation: nothing is here that does not serve the claim being demonstrated. Score 13 -- one point withheld for the label text in some diagrams that could be reduced without loss.

**On Legibility -- "reading" an abstract composition:**

What does legibility mean for these diagrams? The viewer is not extracting a fact; they are experiencing a claimed effect and judging whether the claim is correct. Legibility here is: can the viewer experience what the diagram claims to demonstrate, and does the diagram make the claimed experience available?

For Diagram Types 1 and 2 (tension fields, line character): yes. The viewer experiences the claimed tension and temperature. Legibility high. For Diagram Type 4 (color-form correspondence): the correspondence is asserted but not obviously experienced -- the viewer is told that yellow goes with triangle, not shown a demonstration that makes this self-evident. Legibility lower.

Score 10 -- the demonstrations work for position-tension and line-character; the color-form correspondences are asserted more than demonstrated.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Perceptual grammar vs. retinal grammar** -- Kandinsky's visual grammar and Bertin's visual grammar are parallel descriptions of the same perceptual material at different levels: experiential (Kandinsky) vs. retinal-operational (Bertin). Neither is complete without the other. The rubric's School Fidelity anchors for abstract-art schools should recognize that the relevant grammar is perceptual-experiential rather than retinal-operational. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity.*

2. **Asserted vs. demonstrated claim in abstract argument** -- some abstract diagrams demonstrate their claims (the viewer experiences the claimed effect), while others assert them (the viewer is told the correspondence). These are different epistemic structures within the same work. The rubric's Data/Story Integrity anchors should distinguish between claims that are demonstrable in the work and claims that must be taken on authority. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity.*
