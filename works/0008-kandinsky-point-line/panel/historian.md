## Review

**On the Bauhaus context:**

*Point and Line to Plane* was published as Bauhaus-Bucher 9 in 1926 -- part of the series of pedagogical texts the Bauhaus was producing to document and transmit its methods. This context matters. The diagrams are not artworks in the exhibition sense; they are course materials for Kandinsky's Basic Course at the Bauhaus Dessau. The audience was Bauhaus students who were already immersed in formalist theory.

This shapes how the founding grammar should be assessed. The diagrams assume a reader who accepts Kandinsky's theoretical framework -- who grants that visual elements have formal properties -- and who is studying how to apply that framework. A reader who does not accept the framework will not find the diagrams convincing. The founding grammar is internally coherent for the initiated; it requires prior commitment from the reader.

This is a different founding situation from, say, Playfair's bar chart, which demonstrates its encoding rules to any viewer by simply showing bars of different lengths. Playfair's founding grammar is immediately accessible; Kandinsky's requires prior theoretical orientation. This distinction matters for how School Fidelity Type B is scored for different kinds of founding acts.

**On school fidelity and precedents:**

The abstract visual grammar tradition that Kandinsky was working in had significant predecessors. Adolf Holzel's color theory lectures (pre-1914 at Stuttgart); Paul Klee's own Bauhaus course materials (1921-1931, partly concurrent with Kandinsky); Johannes Itten's preliminary course. Kandinsky was not working in isolation -- he was contributing to a school that was forming through multiple founders simultaneously.

This means the Type B classification requires nuance: Kandinsky is a co-founder of abstract visual grammar, not a sole founder. The specific vocabulary of *Point and Line to Plane* -- point/line/plane as primary elements; tension; temperature -- is distinctly his. But the broader project of abstract formalist analysis was shared. Score 17 rather than 19: strong founding contribution within a shared founding act.

**On the data integrity concern -- Gestalt psychology:**

Kandinsky's claims about compositional tension draw on Gestalt psychology, which was a serious scientific project in the 1920s (Wertheimer, Kohler, Koffka). The tension diagrams have some empirical grounding. But Kandinsky cites Gestalt psychology selectively and does not acknowledge the significant ongoing debates within the field. He presents the Gestalt findings as settled where they were contested. Step A: -1 for confidence suppression in citing scientific context.

The color-form correspondences have no strong empirical grounding even in 1926. Kandinsky's synesthetic intuitions about yellow-triangle and blue-circle are presented as data when they are better described as hypotheses. Step C: -2.

**On the E2 question -- the delayed resonance of Bauhaus abstraction:**

The Bauhaus was closed by the Nazis in 1933. Its faculty dispersed to the USA, UK, and elsewhere, and the school's ideas spread through the diaspora. Kandinsky's formalist theory, including the vocabulary of *Point and Line to Plane*, entered graphic design, typography, and visual communication education globally through this diaspora. The work's canonical status in design education was established decades after the Bauhaus's closure, through institutional transmission rather than direct reception.

This is a case of E2 -- delayed resonance -- but the mechanism is neither epistemic delay (E2a) nor political-testimonial delay (E2b) nor productive-appropriation canonization (E2d, Haeckel). It is something closer to: diaspora transmission leading to posthumous canonical status through educational institutions. A possible E2e -- diaspora-institutional canonization. The sub-type proliferation is getting unwieldy; I will flag this and leave the full E2 taxonomy to the panel summary.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Shared founding act** -- some schools are founded by multiple contributors simultaneously rather than by a single founding work. Type B scoring for such cases should recognize that the "founding grammar" may be distributed across multiple founders, and should score each work's distinctive contribution rather than requiring the full grammar to be derivable from the work alone. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type B).*

2. **Initiated-audience founding grammar** -- some founding works require the reader's prior acceptance of a theoretical framework to access the grammar. This is distinct from founding works whose grammar is immediately accessible. The Type B anchors should distinguish: (a) immediately-accessible founding grammar (anyone can derive it from the work), (b) framework-dependent founding grammar (initiated readers can derive it; general readers need the framework first). *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type B).*
