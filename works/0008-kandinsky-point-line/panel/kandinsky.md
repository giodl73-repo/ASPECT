## Review

It is unusual to review one's own work, but the panel requires it, and I will try to be honest.

**On the founding grammar (School Fidelity, Type B):**

The diagrams in *Point and Line to Plane* are not paintings. They are arguments. An argument about how visual elements work -- about what a point is, what a line does, how temperature and direction are not metaphors but formal properties of marks. A future practitioner studying these diagrams can extract the rules: the point creates tension through its position in the frame; the line carries energy in its angle and curvature; the plane carries weight and breath. These rules are demonstrated, not merely asserted.

I withhold 2 points from the maximum because the grammar, while strong, has a gap: the relationship between the element properties (point-tension, line-temperature) and the construction of a complete composition is demonstrated but not fully systematized. A student would know how to use a point. They would need additional work -- my paintings, not just these diagrams -- to understand how to build a composition from points, lines, and planes. The diagram grammar is a component grammar, not a complete compositional grammar.

**On Data/Story Integrity -- the rubric stress test:**

This is the hardest question for this work, and I want to answer it carefully because I believe the rubric is capable of handling it, with thought.

What is the "data" in these diagrams? The data is my claims about perceptual properties of formal elements. I claim that a point placed in the upper-right corner of a frame creates upward tension. I claim that a diagonal line feels warmer than a horizontal line. I claim that yellow corresponds to the triangle form, blue to the circle, red to the square.

These are empirical claims about perception. Some have been confirmed by subsequent perceptual research (compositional tension from position -- Arnheim's work validates this; color temperature as a cross-modal phenomenon -- confirmed in experimental aesthetics). Others remain contested (the specific color-form correspondences are culturally variable; my yellow-triangle correspondence is not universal).

So: Data/Story Integrity for abstract work means: are the claims made by the diagrams consistent with what is actually perceivable? And the answer is: mostly yes, with important exceptions.

The tension diagrams (a point in different positions) work. The viewer does experience different tensions from the same point in different positions. This is demonstrable and the diagram correctly shows it. Score this component highly.

The color-form correspondences (yellow = triangle, blue = circle, red = square) are less secure. I believed them fully; I was less certain that they are universal than I claimed. The cultural variability of color symbolism means that what feels self-evident to a European viewer of 1926 is not universal. Step C: the claim that these correspondences are formal rather than cultural is a causal claim that overreaches the evidence. Dock -2.

The line-character claims (horizontal = cold/calm, diagonal = dynamic) are moderately supported by cross-cultural perceptual research. Minor overreach. Dock -1.

Net Data/Story Integrity: 15.

**On compositional integrity as data integrity:**

I want to name something the rubric does not yet have a name for. In these diagrams, the "data" is the composition itself. When I place a point in the upper-right corner to demonstrate upward tension, the diagram is both the claim AND the evidence. The viewer verifies the claim by experiencing the tension in the diagram. This is a different epistemic structure from a statistical chart, where the claim and the data are separate. Here they are the same thing.

I call this compositional integrity as data integrity: the diagram demonstrates rather than reports. The standard of accuracy is not correspondence to external measurements but internal coherence -- does the composition produce the claimed effect in a viewer? This is a valid and important distinction that the rubric should recognize.

**On Resonance:**

The resonance of these diagrams is Type I (Aesthetic-Intentional) -- I constructed them to produce specific perceptual effects, and they largely succeed. When the viewer looks at the tension diagrams, they experience the tension. When they look at the line-character diagrams, they feel the temperature difference. The intended effect is achieved.

Score 15 -- the full primary resonance is achieved. I withhold nothing here.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Compositional integrity as data integrity** -- in abstract visual argument, the diagram demonstrates rather than reports. The claim and the evidence are the same composition; the viewer verifies by experiencing the claimed effect. This is a distinct epistemic structure from statistical or cartographic visualization, and the rubric's Data/Story Integrity anchors should recognize it. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity.*

2. **Perceptual claim as falsifiable data** -- visual-formal claims (this composition creates tension; this line feels warm) are empirical claims about perception that can be tested, partially confirmed, and partially refuted. They are not arbitrary assertions. The rubric should not treat all abstract-art claims as outside the scope of Data/Story Integrity; it should assess their falsifiability and the current evidence. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Steps A-D).*
