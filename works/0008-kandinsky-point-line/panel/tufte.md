## Review

This is the work in the project I find most difficult to score, and I want to be transparent about why: I am not sure what I am supposed to be evaluating.

Kandinsky claims to be making arguments about visual properties. Fine. Arguments can be evaluated. But the argument-structure is unusual: the diagrams both state the claim and are the evidence for it. When I look at a point in the upper-right corner of a frame and am told this creates "upward tension," I experience... something. A directional pull. Is that the tension Kandinsky means? Is my experience confirmation of his claim? This is a different epistemic situation from a bar chart, where I can check the bar height against the data table.

**On Data/Story Integrity -- the rubric stress test:**

I will apply the rubric's steps and see what they produce.

Step A: Source quality. What is the data? Kandinsky's claims about perceptual properties. Are these well-sourced? He draws on his own perceptual experience and artistic practice, and on some Gestalt psychology that was available in the 1920s. He does not cite sources. He asserts his claims with the confidence of a scientist but the documentation of an artist. Step A dock: -1 for unsourced empirical claims about perception.

Step B: Accuracy tradeoff. Is a less precise form chosen over a more accurate alternative? The diagrams are the most direct demonstration available for perceptual claims -- you cannot convey "this position feels tense" more directly than by making a mark in that position. No accuracy tradeoff here. No dock.

Step C: Causation claim integrity. The color-form correspondences (yellow = triangle, blue = circle, red = square) are presented as formal and universal. The evidence does not support this. Cross-cultural studies show these correspondences are culturally specific. The causal claim -- that these are properties of color and form rather than cultural associations -- is significantly overstated. Dock -3.

Step D: Analytical framework declaration. Kandinsky's analytical framework -- that visual elements have formal properties independent of what they represent, and that these properties produce consistent perceptual effects across viewers -- IS declared. It is the central stated thesis of the book. However, this framework includes the empirical claim that the effects are consistent and cross-cultural, which is the problematic part. Score -0 for framework declaration (declared), but the Step C dock already handles the overreach.

Net: 17 - 1 - 3 = 13. But I push this down to 12 because the overall epistemic status of the work is unusual -- I am not confident that my experience of the diagrams is a valid test of Kandinsky's claims, and he does not explain how to test them.

**On chartjunk in abstract art -- the central challenge posed:**

"What is chartjunk in abstract art?" The brief asks this directly. I will answer it.

Chartjunk, in my usage, is marks that carry no information. In a statistical chart, a decorative border carries no data -- it is chartjunk. In abstract art, every mark is potentially information (it has position, direction, color, weight). There is no obvious criterion for "this mark carries no information" because the information IS the formal properties of the marks, and every mark has formal properties.

So: chartjunk as I normally apply it does not transfer to abstract art. But there is an analog. In abstract composition, there can be marks that are incoherent with the composition's internal logic -- marks that do not contribute to the compositional tension/rhythm/weight that the work is constructing. These are not chartjunk in the ink sense; they are grammar violations in the abstract sense. Kandinsky's diagrams are remarkably free of these. Each diagram makes one claim and shows it. The marks are economical. Score 14 on Visual Economy.

**On what the rubric does and does not handle:**

The rubric does not fail on this work. It produces a score in the low-to-mid 70s range when I apply it honestly, and I believe that score is roughly correct -- this is a strong, innovative, imperfect work whose primary weakness is the cultural overreach of the color-form claims. The rubric handled it.

What the rubric lacks is a vocabulary for the distinct epistemic structure of demonstrative abstract argument (the claim is demonstrated by being experienced, not reported by encoding external data). This is what Kandinsky's innovation represents, and it is worth naming.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Demonstrative vs. reportive encoding** -- statistical and cartographic visualizations report external data; the claim and the evidence are separate. Abstract visual arguments demonstrate their claims; the composition is both the claim and the evidence. The viewer verifies by experiencing, not by checking against a separate data source. This is a fundamentally different epistemic structure that Data/Story Integrity should handle with a distinct set of anchors. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity.*

2. **Chartjunk analog in abstract art** -- in abstract composition, the equivalent of chartjunk is compositional incoherence: marks that do not serve the work's internal formal logic. The rubric's Visual Economy anchors, applied to abstract works, should ask "does this mark serve the compositional argument?" rather than "does this mark represent data?" *Rubric anchor: Visual Economy.*
