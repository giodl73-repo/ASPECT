# Brief: Playfair's First Bar Chart (1786)

*Canonical import — a single chart from the Commercial and Political Atlas.*

## Subject

The bar chart of Scottish trade (1780–1781) from Playfair's *Commercial and Political Atlas* (1786, with the bar chart appearing in the 3rd edition 1801 in its clearest form). Horizontal bars of different lengths show exports and imports between Scotland and 17 trading partners. The length of each bar encodes the total trade value; bars are divided to show exports (one shade) vs. imports (another shade).

Playfair invented this chart because the trade data for Scotland in one year could not be shown as a time series (there was only one year of data). He needed a different form for cross-sectional comparison. He invented the bar chart.

## School

**Type B (Founder) — statistical-graphics.** Playfair invented the bar chart (this chart), the line graph (1786, earlier in the same book), and the pie chart (1801). No prior work used these forms. The school of statistical graphics — quantitative comparison through visual proportion — begins here.

## Core Questions

1. **Is the first bar chart also a good bar chart?** The form Playfair invented has been refined over 240 years. By contemporary standards, how does the original measure up?

2. **Step D for the bar chart**: the data model "bar length proportional to quantity" was not established before Playfair. Did he declare this? (He did, in his introduction — unusually for the works in this project, Playfair explains the encoding explicitly.)

3. **Founder vs. practitioner scores**: Nightingale and Du Bois, who worked in the tradition Playfair founded, score in the low 80s. Does the founder score higher or lower than his successors?
