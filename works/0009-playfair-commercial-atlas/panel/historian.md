## Review

**On the founding of statistical graphics:**

Playfair's claim to priority on the bar chart is well-established. The first bar chart appears in the 1786 first edition of the *Commercial and Political Atlas*. No prior work used this form. The line graph in the same volume (showing trade over time) is equally a founding act. The pie chart, in *Statistical Breviary* (1801), completes the triptych.

What distinguishes Playfair's founding acts from other inventors of graphical forms (there are some precursors in scientific diagrams) is the systematic quality of his grammar: the same principles -- proportional encoding, axis as reference, explicit legend -- apply consistently across all three forms he invented. He was not generating isolated inventions; he was developing a coherent school.

The precedent question: was anyone making statistical charts before Playfair? Some yes: Johann Heinrich Lambert used line graphs for temperature data (1765) and J.H. de Magellan used graphical displays of scientific measurements. But these were isolated instances in scientific literature, not systematic methods for communicating economic and political data to non-specialist audiences. Playfair's distinctive contribution is applying the graphical principle to the communication of social and economic data to legislative and commercial audiences. The school he founded is as much about audience as about method.

**On the school's founding clarity:**

The founding grammar of statistical graphics is the clearest in the project. It can be stated as five rules (proportional encoding, axis as scale, categorical perpendicular axis, visual comparison before numerical reading, explicit legend). These rules have persisted for 240 years without fundamental revision. No other school reviewed in this project has a founding grammar so durable.

Why? I think the answer is data-type constraint (as Bertin notes): quantitative comparison on a categorical axis has a small number of grammatically correct encoding options, and Playfair chose them correctly. The grammar is clear partly because the data type does not permit many wrong answers.

**On the E2 question:**

Playfair died impoverished and relatively obscure in 1823. The bar chart was used throughout the 19th century but Playfair himself was not well known as its inventor. His canonical status was established retrospectively through the history of statistics and data visualization scholarship in the 20th century -- particularly through the work of Michael Friendly, Howard Wainer, and the history-of-statistics community.

This is an E2 case -- delayed resonance -- but which sub-type? It is not E2a (epistemic delay -- the encoding rule was not contested, just unused). It is not E2b (political-testimonial -- no political suppression). It is not E2d (productive-appropriation -- the canonization was scholarly, not practical). It is most similar to E2c (scholarly canonization), where a later academic community retroactively elevated the work to founding status.

The distinction from Snow (E2c, scholarly canonization): Snow's canonization was in service of a narrative about epidemiology's founding. Playfair's canonization was more straightforwardly credit assignment -- scholars realized who had invented the bar chart and gave him credit. Less narrative construction, more archival recovery. Perhaps E2c applies but with lighter narrative construction.

I leave the sub-type question open and recommend the panel hold E2 for structural taxonomy.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Founding clarity as data-type function** -- the clearest founding grammars are those where the data type constrains the available encodings to a small set. Playfair's bar chart is clear because quantitative comparison on a categorical axis has few grammatically correct options. The rubric should note this relationship when assessing Type B works: founding clarity is partly data-type-determined, not only designer-skill-determined. (Parallel to Bertin's innovation 81, from a historical rather than semiological perspective.) *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type B).*

2. **Archival-recovery canonization** -- a sub-type of E2c where the canonical status is established by scholarly archival recovery (who invented this form?) rather than by narrative construction (this work founded this field). Lighter narrative investment than Snow's E2c; more factual credit-assignment than interpretive canon-building. May not need a separate sub-type; may be a variant of E2c. Flag and hold. *Rubric anchor: Resonance (Type V, E2).*
