## Review

I will review my own work as honestly as I can, which means I will try to separate what I know I intended from what the chart actually achieves.

**On the founding grammar (School Fidelity, Type B):**

When I made this chart -- the Scottish trade chart, Christmas 1780 to Christmas 1781 -- I had a problem. Every other chart in the Atlas showed time series: trade over a span of years. For Scotland, I had only one year of data. I needed a form that showed comparisons across categories without time. I invented one.

The rules, as I stated them in the preface: bar length shows amount. Horizontal axis measures monetary value. Vertical axis lists categories. The key shows which pattern is imports, which is exports. That is the complete grammar. It fits in four sentences. Any reader could make a bar chart from those instructions.

I score 19 of 20. I withhold one point because the chart has a minor flaw: the bars are not sorted with maximum clarity. I sorted approximately by trade volume, which is better than alphabetical, but a strict sort from largest to smallest on total trade would have made the ranking comparison more immediate.

**On the explicit encoding declaration (Step D) -- why this score is unusual:**

I explained my encoding. I wrote: "The advantage proposed by this method is not that of giving a more accurate statement than by figures, but it is to give a more simple and permanent idea of the gradual progress and comparative amounts, at different periods, by presenting to the eye a figure, the proportions of which correspond with the amount of the sums intended to be expressed."

I said what the chart is, what it is for, and what it is not for (more precise than a table -- I disclaim this explicitly). I told the reader that bar length corresponds to amount. I gave them the grammar. They did not have to figure it out.

Why did I do this? Partly because the form was genuinely new and I could not assume familiarity. Partly because I understood that if the reader did not know how to read it, the chart was useless. The declaration was not a virtue -- it was a practical necessity for a new form. But the effect is that Step D dock = 0, and the Data/Story Integrity score reflects that.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

The Scottish trade data came from official Customs records, which were the most reliable commercial data available in 1786. I cannot claim they were perfect -- the customs system had significant evasion and recording inconsistencies -- but they were the standard source and I had no better alternative. Step A: -1 for inherent source limitations that I could not fully declare without undermining the chart's authority with its intended audience (Parliament, merchants).

Step B: No accuracy tradeoff. Bar length proportional to monetary value is the most accurate available encoding for this data type.

Step C: No causal claims. The chart is purely comparative.

Step D: Framework explicitly declared. Dock 0.

Net: 17 - 1 + 1 (explicit declaration) = I score 18, accepting the Step A dock and crediting the declaration fully.

**On the political-presentation reading context:**

My Atlas was addressed to politicians and merchants. They would encounter this chart in the context of a briefing or a reading of the Atlas. The primary context is political-presentation: the chart must make its argument reach a decision-maker in the time available, accompanied by my explanations if necessary.

For this context: the chart succeeds. A member of Parliament can see immediately that Scotland trades most with Ireland and the British colonies, and that the trade is more balanced with some partners than others. The comparison is visually available before any numbers are read. The legend is adequate for the context.

Score 13 of 15. One point withheld for the hand-lettered typography, which is somewhat difficult to read at the trading-partner name labels. One point withheld for the color-coding of imports vs. exports, which relies on the viewer reading the key before interpreting the bar segments.

**On Resonance:**

This chart is 240 years old and the form it invented is still the most used statistical chart in the world. I did not know that when I made it. The resonance I intended was practical: help politicians understand Scottish trade. What the chart actually achieved was founding an entire school of data visualization. That is emergent resonance beyond my intention.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Explicit encoding declaration as founding virtue** -- the explicit declaration of encoding rules in the accompanying text is not merely good documentation; it is the act of founding. A grammar that is only practiced is a grammar that cannot be transmitted. Playfair's preface declaration transforms a visual invention into a teachable form. The rubric should recognize explicit declaration of encoding logic as evidence of founding quality in Type B works. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type B) / Data/Story Integrity (Step D).*

2. **Practical necessity as declarative discipline** -- Playfair declared his encoding because he had no choice (the form was new and the audience was unfamiliar). This suggests that founders who address unfamiliar audiences with new forms are forced into declarative discipline that later practitioners, working in established forms, can avoid. The rubric may need to adjust Step D expectations based on whether the form was new to the audience. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step D).*
