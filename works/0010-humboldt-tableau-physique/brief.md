# Brief: Humboldt's Tableau Physique des Andes (1807)

*Canonical import — the large-format plate accompanying the Essay on the Geography of Plants.*

## Subject

A large-format diagram (approximately 60 × 50 cm) showing the cross-section of Mount Chimborazo (Ecuador), the highest peak in the Andes, with surrounding landscape. On and around this cross-section, Humboldt encodes approximately 9 variables simultaneously across altitude bands:

- Plant communities (which species grow at which altitudes, named in columns alongside)
- Temperature at each altitude zone
- Atmospheric pressure (barometric readings)
- Atmospheric humidity
- Atmospheric composition (oxygen content, cyanometry)
- Gravitational intensity
- Animal communities at altitude
- Geological features
- Cultural/human settlements

The diagram founded the science of biogeography — the study of how species distribute themselves across ecological zones. Humboldt's key insight: altitude and latitude organize life in parallel ways; a climb up Chimborazo replicates a journey from the equator to the poles in ecological terms.

## School

**Type B (Founder) — ecological visualization.** The multi-variable cross-section encoding (altitude as primary axis; multiple data columns flanking the central image) is Humboldt's invention. No prior scientific illustration had encoded ecological, meteorological, geological, and cultural data simultaneously in a single diagram.

## Core Questions

1. **How does the rubric handle 9+ variables?** Data/Story Integrity, Visual Economy, and Legibility are all tested by extreme data density. Is the complexity intrinsic (the ecological system IS multi-variable) or decorative?

2. **Step D**: Humboldt's data model is "altitude zones organize ecological communities" — this was the scientific founding insight, not an established framework. How severely does the rubric penalize a founding idea that was undeclared because it couldn't yet have been declared (the field didn't exist)?

3. **Natural beauty as scientific evidence**: like Haeckel, Humboldt finds natural form beautiful. Does the aesthetic quality of the mountain cross-section enhance or impede the scientific encoding?
