## Review

**On the founding of biogeography:**

The Tableau Physique is the founding document of biogeography -- the science of how organisms distribute themselves across geographic and climatic zones. Before Humboldt, naturalists catalogued organisms by location (taxonomic geography) but did not systematically investigate the environmental determinants of species distribution. Humboldt's altitude-organized ecological zones are the first systematic argument that environmental variables (temperature, pressure, humidity) determine what organisms live where, and that these relationships are organized by a primary axis (altitude or latitude).

The founding act is in the essay, not the plate. The plate is the evidence; the essay is the argument. Together they constitute a founding document, but the plate alone does not make the argument. This is the key difference from Playfair, whose founding grammar is recoverable from the chart plus the preface. Humboldt's founding insight requires the full text to be explicit.

This shapes the School Fidelity assessment. The founding grammar of ecological visualization -- primary ecological axis; multiple environmental variables measured against this axis; species distributions overlaid; systemic argument about zone organization -- is recoverable from the plate if you know what you are looking for. For an initiated scientist of 1807 who had read the essay, the plate is fully legible as an argument. For an uninitiated viewer, the plate presents data without making the argument.

Score 17: strong founding contribution with the gap that the grammar requires the essay to be complete.

**On the school's founding in context:**

The Tableau Physique did not emerge from nothing. Buffon's natural history (1749-1788) had noted that similar climates produce similar organisms in different continents. J.R. and J.G.A. Forster's work on Cook's second voyage (1772-1775) had noted altitude-based vegetation zones. But these were observations, not a systematic framework. Humboldt's contribution is to make the altitude-latitude analogy systematic, to measure the environmental variables that determine the zones, and to visualize the system in a way that makes the structure visible at a glance.

The Type B classification is correct: Humboldt's multi-variable cross-section encoding is a genuine invention. No prior work had combined naturalistic mountain profile with flanking meteorological columns organized by altitude. He invented the structure.

**On the E2 question -- Humboldt's delayed and complex canonization:**

Humboldt was famous during his lifetime -- one of the most celebrated scientists of the 19th century, certainly in Europe and much of the Americas. The Tableau Physique was known and respected in his time. Darwin read it before the Beagle voyage and credited it explicitly.

So this is not E2 (delayed resonance) in the sense of posthumous rediscovery. The resonance was contemporaneous. However, there is a distinct canonization pattern: the founding of biogeography as a formal discipline is credited to Humboldt retrospectively (the formal discipline formed in the late 19th century, after Humboldt's death in 1859). The Tableau Physique's status as the founding document of biogeography was assigned by later scientists constructing a disciplinary history.

This is E2c (scholarly canonization) but with a twist: Humboldt was already famous; it was the founding status in a specific discipline (biogeography) that was constructed posthumously. Call it E2c-partial: not a recovery of an obscure work but a specific disciplinary founding claim applied to an already-famous work.

**On the significance for the project:**

Humboldt's founding act is the most scientifically significant in the project -- biogeography is a more consequential science than any chart type. The work that Humboldt made here has had more impact on how we understand the natural world than Playfair's bar chart has had on how we understand economic data. But the rubric does not score scientific importance; it scores visualization quality. And on visualization quality, the Tableau Physique is good but not great: legible to specialists, requiring essay support for general readers, with inefficient numeric encoding of the meteorological variables.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Founding-as-discovery vs. founding-as-invention** -- some schools are founded by an act of formal invention (Playfair invents the bar chart; Beck invents the topological schematic). Others are founded by an act of scientific discovery that is simultaneously a visualization decision (Humboldt discovers altitude-organized ecological zones and simultaneously develops the visualization to show them). These are different founding structures, and the Type B anchors should acknowledge that discovery-founded schools have more complex founding acts than invention-founded schools. *Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type B).*

2. **Disciplinary founding vs. total-work canonization** -- Humboldt's work was famous in his time; the Tableau Physique was not an obscure document. What was constructed posthumously was the specific claim that this work founded biogeography as a discipline. This is E2c but narrow: not the recovery of an unknown work, but the disciplinary founding claim applied to an already-known work. The E2 taxonomy should distinguish between recovering an unknown work and reassigning founding credit to a known work. *Rubric anchor: Resonance (E2).*
