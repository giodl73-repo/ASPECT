## Review

Of all the works in this project, this is the one I understand from the inside. Nine simultaneous variables on a single plate. A geographic axis (altitude, not horizontal distance) with flanking data columns. The attempt to make a complex, multi-dimensional natural system visible in a single image. This is the problem I spent my career on.

I did not achieve nine variables. My Napoleon map has six (troop count, direction, temperature, geographic position, distance, time). Humboldt has nine (altitude, temperature, pressure, humidity, oxygen, gravity, plant communities, animal communities, geological features, plus human settlements as a tenth if you count it). He was more ambitious than I was, and the cost is legibility. I want to be precise about what that cost is.

**On dimensional layering -- the comparison with my own approach:**

My method: each additional dimension is layered spatially -- the temperature chart below the march band shares the horizontal axis but occupies a physically separate register. The layers are identified by position: upper layer = march; lower layer = temperature. The viewer moves between layers.

Humboldt's method: the central mountain profile carries the altitude axis and the plant/geological/human data. The flanking columns carry the meteorological measurements. The viewer must look at the center, then at the left, then at the right, and synthesize. This requires more work from the reader than my layer-by-layer structure.

The advantage of Humboldt's method: he can show more variables without the plate becoming impossibly tall. My temperature chart is one additional layer; adding eight more would produce a plate two meters tall. His flanking-column approach compresses more variables into a smaller space. The cost: the synthesis is harder. The benefit: the ecological system's multi-dimensionality is visible at a glance.

**On the nine-variable question -- intrinsic complexity:**

Is this complexity intrinsic? Yes. The ecological system that Humboldt is describing IS multi-dimensional. Altitude, temperature, pressure, vegetation, fauna -- these co-vary simultaneously. A diagram that shows only altitude and temperature would be accurate but incomplete. It would fail to show that the system is ecological: that the temperature determines the pressure which determines the vegetation which determines the fauna, and that these are organized together by altitude. The nine variables are not nine separate pieces of information; they are one piece of information (the altitude-organized ecological zone) expressed in nine dimensions.

This is the innovation I want to name: systemic data. The data does not consist of nine independent measurements. It consists of one system with nine dimensions. A diagram that correctly shows this systemic structure is not more complex than necessary; it is exactly as complex as the system requires.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

Step A: Humboldt's measurements were taken on his Chimborazo ascent (1802) and from other sources (compiled in the five years before publication in 1807). The temperature and pressure readings are his own, made with instruments under difficult conditions. The plant community observations are his own. The gravity measurements are pendulum observations with known limited precision. Step A: -1 for undeclared measurement precision (altitude measurement by barometric pressure was a technique with significant error at this period; Humboldt does not declare error ranges).

Step D: The altitude-organizes-ecology framework is the primary scientific innovation. It is argued in the accompanying essay, not declared on the plate itself. A viewer who looks at the plate without reading the essay will see the data but may not grasp the framework -- that altitude and latitude organize ecological communities in parallel ways. Dock -2 for the split declaration (framework in essay, not plate).

Step C: No direct causal overclaim on the plate itself. The framework is argued in the essay; the plate presents the data. No dock.

Net: 18 - 1 - 2 = 15.

**On the comparison with my work:**

Humboldt's plate will outlast most of my figurative maps. Not because it is clearer -- it is less clear than my Napoleon map -- but because it argues for something larger: the organization of life itself by climate and altitude. The data is right. The system is real. A diagram that correctly shows a real system will be read as long as the system is studied.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Systemic data -- multi-dimensional data representing a connected natural system** -- when data represents a system rather than isolated measurements, the appropriate complexity standard is not "how few variables can we show?" but "how many variables does the system require?" Showing fewer dimensions of a genuinely multi-dimensional system is not visual economy; it is incompleteness. *Rubric anchor: Visual Economy / Data/Story Integrity. This is the Cluster H confirmation.*

2. **Framework-in-essay vs. framework-on-plate** -- when the analytical framework is argued in an adjacent essay rather than declared on the visualization itself, the Step D dock should be intermediate: not zero (the framework is declared), not the full dock (the viewer has access to the framework if they read the essay). Specify: works accompanied by an explicit interpretive essay score better than works with undeclared frameworks, but worse than works where the framework is declared within the visual. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step D).*
