## Review

I want to begin by naming what makes this plate different from everything else in the project, because the difference is significant. Every other work we have reviewed encodes data that is logically independent of the visualization: Minard's troop counts, Snow's deaths, Playfair's trade values. The data exists in some form before the visualization; the visualization encodes it.

Humboldt's Tableau Physique encodes data that is, in part, constituted by the observation. The plant communities at specific altitudes exist only as Humboldt described them -- no one had catalogued them this way before. The altitude-organized ecological zones are not a pre-existing dataset that Humboldt visualized; they are a scientific discovery that the visualization both records and claims. This makes the Data/Story Integrity assessment genuinely complex.

**On data density:**

Nine variables on one plate. Let me calculate the information density. The central mountain image carries qualitative spatial data (geological zones, vegetation, settlements) that cannot be easily quantified. The flanking columns carry approximately 15-20 altitude-matched data points per variable, across four to six meteorological variables. Call it roughly 75-100 data points in the columns, plus the spatial qualitative data on the profile.

By Minard's standards (my Napoleon map has perhaps 200 precise data points in the temperature register alone), this is not exceptional data density in the numeric sense. But in terms of the number of simultaneously represented variables, it is the highest in the project. I call this **variable density** rather than data density: the number of distinct data dimensions simultaneously represented. Nine variables. Nothing in the project exceeds this.

Is variable density high a virtue? When the variables co-vary in a system (altitude organizes them all), yes. Showing them separately would miss the point. Showing them together -- imperfectly, at some legibility cost -- is the right choice. I accept the Cluster H argument here: the complexity is intrinsic. The ecological system is genuinely nine-dimensional.

**On chartjunk in the Tableau Physique:**

Less than I expected. The mountain profile is naturalistic but not decorative -- it is the primary data structure. The geological cross-section rendering serves a scientific purpose. The flanking columns are spare. The main non-data elements are the elaborate title block and the ornamental border, which are period-standard production conventions. Less chartjunk than Haeckel; less than I initially feared.

**On what should have been done differently:**

The meteorological variables (temperature, pressure, humidity, oxygen, gravity) are encoded as tabular numbers. A graphical encoding -- a curve showing temperature vs. altitude -- would be more legible and require less area. Humboldt had Playfair's line graph available to him (published 1786, 21 years before the Tableau Physique). He chose not to use it. This is either unfamiliarity with the graphical tradition or a deliberate choice to preserve numeric precision. Either way, it is a detectable encoding inefficiency.

If the meteorological variables had been graphed as curves parallel to the mountain profile, the visual comparison between temperature curve and vegetation zone would be immediately available: the viewer would see that the vegetation transition corresponds to a specific temperature threshold. As tabular numbers, this correspondence requires mental computation. The missed opportunity is real, and it is the reason the legibility score is not higher.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

Step D: the altitude-organizes-ecology framework is the scientific innovation of the work. It is in the essay, not on the plate. The plate presents the data; the essay presents the interpretation. This split is workable (the essay and plate are in the same publication) but it means the plate cannot stand alone as an argument. Score: -2 for framework in essay rather than plate.

Step A: measurement precision undeclared for altitude (barometric method), temperature, and plant distribution (qualitative field observations made under difficult conditions). -1.

Net: 17 - 2 - 1 = 14.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Variable density vs. data density** -- data density (information per unit area) and variable density (number of simultaneous data dimensions) are distinct measures that a visualization can optimize for independently. High variable density is appropriate for genuinely systemic data; high data density is appropriate for precision-maximizing statistical work. The rubric's Visual Economy dimension should recognize this distinction. *Rubric anchor: Visual Economy / Data/Story Integrity.*

2. **Adjacent essay as framework declaration** -- when an analytical framework is declared in an adjacent essay (not within the visualization), the Step D dock should be intermediate: the framework IS declared, but requires the viewer to read the essay to access it. Score better than undeclared; worse than in-visualization declared. The rubric should specify this gradation explicitly. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step D).*
