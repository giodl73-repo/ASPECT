## What the Revision Reveals

**The panel's recommendations translated directly to score improvement.** Each of the four recommended changes maps to a measurable score improvement:

1. Remove region color → School Fidelity +2.0 (K amendment dock eliminated entirely)
2. Qualify title → Data/Story Integrity +2.0 (L amendment dock halved; Step A improved)
3. Add methodological note → Data/Story Integrity included in +2.0 above (Statistician +3.0 is the strongest signal here)
4. Add trend indicator → Visual Economy +1.5 (intrinsic data marks replace additive decorative complexity)

The rubric functioned as a design tool. The panel identified specific weaknesses; specific fixes recovered specific points. This is the rubric working correctly as a productive instrument for original design improvement, not just as a criticism apparatus.

**School Fidelity is the most elastic dimension under revision.** The full +2.0 improvement comes entirely from eliminating the sort-color conflict. Amendment K's dock is appropriate and recoverable: fix the mismatch, recover the dock. The amendment is not punitive; it diagnoses a solvable problem.

**The Du Bois gap persists.** His justice-framing critique (Innovation #105: variable selection as framing argument; the chart should ask about heat burden, not ranking) is not addressed by any of the four revisions. His score improves by only 1 point overall. The deeper revision he recommends — reconceiving the story's variable selection — would require a v3 design that changes the school and possibly the brief. The revision cycle has limits: it improves the existing form without reconceiving it.

**Trend data is the most productive single addition.** The trend indicator pays dividends across multiple dimensions: Visual Economy (intrinsic data marks), Legibility (minimal decoding cost), Data/Story Integrity (addresses Statistician's trend-suppression critique), and Resonance (urgency context). A single design decision contributing to four dimensions is a well-targeted revision.

**Title qualification (v2 title hedge) generalizes as a design principle.** Innovation R2 (below): when adding the missing variable is not feasible (no income data in this chart), hedging the title from assertion to tendency is the minimal Amendment-L-compliant fix. "Tend to have" is weaker advocacy than "have" — but it is honest advocacy, which is what the rubric rewards.

## Innovations from the Revision

| # | Name | Rubric Anchor | Implication |
|---|------|---------------|-------------|
| R1 | Sort-color conflict resolution: removing the weaker variable | Visual Economy / School Fidelity | When two organizational logics conflict, choose the one the chart's primary story requires and remove the other. The revision confirms this produces score recovery across multiple dimensions. |
| R2 | Title qualification as minimal Amendment-L fix | Data/Story Integrity (Amendment L) | When the missing variable cannot be added, hedging the title from assertion to tendency reduces the misleading framing effect. Generalizes: "tend to" is an honest hedge; "have" without the data is an assertion the chart cannot support. |
| R3 | Trend indicator as compatible second variable in ranked-comparison charts | Visual Economy / Data Integrity | A symbol column (not color, not sort order) can add a second data variable to a ranked bar chart without creating an organizational conflict. The key is choosing a visual variable that does not compete with the primary organizational logic. |
| R4 | Panel-as-design-tool: rubric produces recoverable prescriptions | Meta-observation | The revision demonstrates that the rubric's amendment docks are not merely punitive — they identify specific, addressable problems. A score under v1.9 is a design specification: fix the identified problems, recover the docked points. |
