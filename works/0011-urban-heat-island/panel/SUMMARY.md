## This is the first original design in the project. What the panel found:

The chart is competent and correctly applies the school grammar. It is not yet the best version of this story. The panel has a clear prescription for improvement, which is valuable: we now know what the rubric sees in original work, and it sees genuinely useful things.

---

## Key Agreements

**The sort-color conflict is the single biggest weakness.** Playfair, Tufte, and Bertin all independently identify it. The region color encoding creates groupings that the sort order immediately fragments. All three recommend the same fix: choose one organizational logic, not two. Either sort by region (group the colors) or remove the color (sort by UHI cleanly).

**The error bars are right and unusual.** The Statistician notes this is better than most historical canonical imports on uncertainty disclosure. The panel credits the designer for including them; historical masters didn't. This is a positive finding: original design under the rubric is producing better data integrity practice than historical founders.

**The title-data gap is real.** The title claims "high-income temperate cities" but the chart encodes no income data. All reviewers flag this, at varying severity.

---

## Key Tensions

**Tufte vs. Du Bois on what the chart should be:**

Tufte: the chart should show the two-variable relationship (UHI vs. income or energy consumption) as a scatter plot — that is the honest story. The bar chart is technically correct but chooses the wrong form for the story.

Du Bois: the chart should ask a different question entirely — not which cities have the highest UHI (a quantity story) but which cities have the worst unaddressed heat burden (a justice story). The form is not the problem; the variable selection is.

These are incompatible recommendations from two reviewers who are both making valid arguments. The designer cannot do both simultaneously. The tension is productive: it reveals that the brief's "core message" ("high-income temperate cities are worse") is itself a framing choice that privileges a different story than the justice framing.

**Playfair vs. Tufte on the right fix:**

Playfair would keep the bar chart but remove the region color. Tufte would replace the bar chart with a scatter plot. These produce different charts with different stories. The designer must choose.

---

## Design Revision Recommendations (priority order)

1. **Remove the region color or sort by region** — the sort-color conflict is the most fixable problem and the most consistently flagged. If region matters to the story, group by region and sort within groups. If UHI ranking matters most, drop the color.

2. **Fix the title or add income data** — either qualify the title ("tend to have") or add a second variable (per-capita energy consumption or income) to the chart to demonstrate the claim. A small annotation could note the income tier of the top five cities without requiring a full second axis.

3. **Add a trend indicator** — even a simple up/down arrow beside each city name (indicating whether UHI has increased or decreased in the past decade) would add critical context with minimal visual cost.

4. **Consider the alternate form** — Tufte's scatter plot suggestion has merit. If the designer's story is really about the UHI-income relationship, a scatter plot encodes that more honestly. This is a deeper revision that changes the school from "ranked comparison" to "relational analysis" — worth considering before the second draft.

5. **Add methodological note** — declare that UHI measurements across cities may use slightly different methodologies; point to data notes.

## What original design revealed that canonical review did not

**The panel recommended specific revisions.** In canonical review, the panel could only observe. In original design, the panel tells the designer what to do next. This is the rubric functioning as a design tool, not just a criticism tool.

**Du Bois's voice is new and productive.** His variable-selection-as-argument innovation (which variable to encode IS the political act) is something the canonical reviews never surfaced, because historical works were reviewed as completed objects. Original design exposed the variable selection as a choice — and therefore as something the panel can weigh in on.

**School-story mismatch is a new failure category.** Tufte's "wrong form for this story" critique (innovation #103) cannot appear in canonical review, because canonical works are already committed to their form. Original design reveals the decision point that canonical review obscures: why did the designer choose this school for this story?
