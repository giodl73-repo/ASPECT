## Review

The chart is honest and well-formed. I have a different objection than my colleagues, and it concerns not the form but the story the form tells and the story the form leaves untold.

**On School Fidelity:**

The grammar is applied correctly but timidly. Playfair and Tufte have addressed the technical failures (title-data gap, sort-color conflict). My concern is different: the chart sorts cities by UHI differential — a climate measurement. This framing presents urban heat islands as a climate phenomenon. But urban heat islands are also a justice phenomenon. The cities at the top of this chart — Phoenix, Beijing, Chicago, Seoul, Tokyo — have populations with money, access to air conditioning, and political power to demand action. The cities at the bottom — Nairobi, Manila, Lima, Bogotá, Johannesburg — have smaller heat islands but fewer resources to cope with any heat at all. The sorted bar chart obscures this entirely. It makes Phoenix look like the victim.

A chart that ranked cities by heat island effect × percentage of population without air conditioning access would tell a completely different story about where the real heat burden lies. The data choice is the argument. The current data choice is not wrong, but it is partial.

I dock five points for school fidelity because the designer chose a form that presents one variable without asking whether that variable tells the right story. The statistical-graphics school does not require the designer to tell the whole truth — but a designer working in the tradition of precision-as-argument should ask which variables were not encoded and why.

**On Data/Story Integrity:**

The error bars are present and appropriate. The zero baseline is correct. But the title claims "high-income temperate cities have the worst urban heat islands" — this invites the reader to conclude that high-income cities are the victims of their own development. The chart does not include data on who bears the consequences of urban heat. That omission is not a falsification; it is a framing choice that changes the moral weight of the argument. Score 15 — honest within its stated scope, but the scope is narrow.

**On Legibility:**

The chart is legible for the stated audience (urban planners, climate policy researchers). Thirty cities, one quantitative variable, sorted — this is exactly what the school can handle. The color regions add some cognitive load but are within the audience's capability. Score 14.

**On Resonance:**

Nine. The chart does not move me, and I think it should. The urban heat island is not just a climate measurement — it is a record of what cities have done to their air and land in pursuit of economic growth. Phoenix is 4.8°C hotter than its surroundings because it replaced desert vegetation with concrete, because it chose sprawl over density, because it runs air conditioning at industrial scale and pumps that waste heat into the streets. That is a moral story. The bar chart tells a quantity story instead. The quantity story is correct. The moral story is absent.

Rosling would say: show me the trend. Has Phoenix's heat island grown? Shrunk? Is it getting better or worse as the city responds to climate pressure? The static chart hides all of this.

## Innovations Flagged

1. **Variable selection as framing argument** — the choice of which variable to encode is itself an argument about what matters. A chart of UHI differential frames the question as "which cities have the most climate modification?" A chart of UHI differential weighted by population cooling access frames the question as "which cities have the most urgent climate justice problem?" These are different arguments from the same underlying phenomenon. The rubric should assess whether the variable selection is disclosed and whether alternative variable choices are acknowledged. *Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step A/C). Implication: for original designs, add a variable-selection disclosure criterion: what was the designer's reason for encoding this variable rather than an alternative? Is the choice disclosed?*

2. **Moral weight and the inert chart** — a chart that is technically correct and informationally complete but produces no sense of stakes or urgency has failed at something important, even if the rubric scores it well. The Resonance dimension currently asks "does the visualization land as intended?" — but what if the designer's intention is too narrow? The panel should be able to flag works where the subject has moral weight that the design has suppressed. *Rubric anchor: Resonance. Implication: for works on subjects with clear human stakes (climate impact, public health, economic inequality), the panel should score whether the work's resonance is proportionate to those stakes — not just whether it achieves its stated aim.*
