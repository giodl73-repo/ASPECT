# Brief — 0012 ISOTYPE Plastic Pollution

## Subject

Global plastic pollution — ocean plastic accumulation by source region (2023). Which world regions contribute most plastic to the oceans, and how does that contribution compare to what educated audiences in wealthy countries assume?

## Core Data

Eight regions by estimated annual ocean plastic input (millions of tonnes/year, 2023 estimates):

| Region | Mt/year |
|--------|---------|
| East Asia & Pacific | 8.1 |
| South Asia | 3.4 |
| Sub-Saharan Africa | 2.2 |
| Latin America & Caribbean | 1.9 |
| Middle East & North Africa | 1.1 |
| Europe | 0.5 |
| North America | 0.3 |
| Central Asia | 0.1 |

**Unit for display**: 1 symbol = 1 million tonnes/year. Maximum array = 8 symbols (East Asia). Minimum = 0 or 1 (Central Asia: round up, declare).

## The Story

Asia contributes the most plastic to the oceans in absolute terms. Europe and North America contribute far less absolutely — and the naive conclusion is that they are not the problem. The more honest story requires context: Europe and North America consume vastly more plastic per capita but export much of their plastic waste to Asian nations, many of which lack the waste infrastructure to process it. The comparison is complicated by the waste-export system. The ISOTYPE form, however, can show only what can be counted in symbols. The moral complexity must be named in annotations, not encoded in the symbols themselves.

## The Core Question

Does ISOTYPE's repetition grammar change the moral weight of the comparison compared to a bar chart? When a viewer sees 8 ocean-plastic symbols for East Asia and 0-1 for North America, do they reach the correct conclusion, the wrong conclusion, or a productively incomplete one that the annotation system must complete?

## Audience

General public, specifically: museum visitors or exhibition attendees in Europe or North America who have some awareness of plastic pollution. Low-to-medium statistical literacy. High moral engagement with the topic. Not specialists in waste management or trade economics.

## School

ISOTYPE (Type A). All core ISOTYPE rules apply:
- Repetition-not-scaling
- Flat-color standardized symbols
- Unit declaration required (1 symbol = 1 million tonnes/year)
- No numbers in the image
- Array composition

## Constraints

- Must show all 8 regions with honest symbol counts
- Must declare the per-capita problem in an annotation zone (not in the image itself)
- Must include a source note
- Must be legible at public-display scale (museum-wall sized, glanced at 2m distance)

## Open Questions

1. What symbol represents "ocean plastic"? An ocean wave with plastic fragments? A stylized plastic bottle in water? Must be universally readable and not culture-specific.
2. How to handle the decimal rounding (8.1 rounds to 8, 0.3 rounds to 0 — do we show 1 symbol minimum for any non-zero entry)?
3. Should the per-capita annotation be part of the ISOTYPE plate or a separate explanatory panel? ISOTYPE purists argue for separation.
4. Should the regions be sorted by quantity (advocacy order) or by geography (legibility for global audiences)?
