*Primary audience: museum visitors in Europe/North America, general public, moderate environmental awareness, low statistical literacy. Reading context: public-display (glanced, 2m).*

## Voiced Review

Walking past this at a museum, I stop. Eight ocean-wave symbols in the top row, almost nothing at the bottom for "North America." I count them — I actually count them, the way you don't count bars in a bar chart. That is the power of this form. I have understood something before I have read a word.

Then I read the title: "Plastic to the Ocean by Region." I read the unit: "Each symbol = 1 million tonnes per year." Now I understand what I counted. Eight million tonnes from East Asia. Almost nothing from North America. My first reaction: we're fine; it's their problem.

Then I try to read the gray panel at the bottom. From two meters, I cannot. The text is too small. I would have to walk up and stand close to read it. I do not do that — I am in a museum, there is another exhibit to my left. I move on having understood the wrong thing.

The design's failure is not its legibility — the array is beautifully legible. The failure is that the most important sentence in the entire work (that Europe and North America export their waste burden) is placed where the intended audience cannot read it without changing their behavior in a way museum visitors typically do not change.

If I could read the annotation panel, I would feel the intended double-punch: first the surprise at who tops the list, then the reorientation when I understand the waste export system. That two-step is a powerful rhetorical design. But it only works if both steps actually reach me.

What does work: the counting. You genuinely count the symbols. And when you count, you cannot be confused about the unit — one symbol, one unit, four symbols, four units. No axis tricks, no scale manipulation. I trust what I'm seeing.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #114 — Symbol-counting engagement advantage**: ISOTYPE repetition triggers an active counting behavior in viewers that passive bar-chart reading does not — the eye follows each symbol individually, producing a more deliberate comprehension process. This higher engagement is distinct from faster comprehension; the viewer spends more time per datum but trusts it more. This may be measurable as a legibility advantage for high-moral-stakes data. Rubric anchor: Legibility / Resonance (Type III).
