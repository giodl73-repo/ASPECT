## Voiced Review

The question at the heart of this design is the question I have faced myself: when the honest data can be misread to support a false conclusion, what is the designer's obligation?

In Scutari I had data showing that soldiers died of preventable disease at rates that should have shamed the War Office. But the raw numbers could be read to say simply "war is dangerous" — an obvious truth that would prompt no action. I chose a form (the coxcomb) that made the preventable fraction impossible to ignore. I made a visual argument, not just a visual representation. This work faces the same choice and I think it makes the wrong one.

The ISOTYPE array correctly shows that East Asia contributes more ocean plastic in absolute tonnes than Europe and North America combined. That is true. But the intended audience — museum visitors in Europe or North America — will likely respond with a version of relief: "We are not the problem." That response is false, and the design seems to know it (hence the annotation panel). But the advocacy failure is precisely that the corrective information does not reach the glancing visitor.

I would have designed this differently. The advocacy goal — to shift behavior and policy in Europe and North America — requires that the per-capita message land. That means either: (a) a second ISOTYPE chart on per-capita figures placed at equal visual weight to the absolute chart; or (b) a completely different form — perhaps a coxcomb comparing regions on both axes simultaneously — that makes the normalization visible in the gestalt, not in the footnotes.

The current design advocates for a conclusion it does not quite reach. It is technically honest about what it shows; it is not honest about what it does not show. For advocacy visualization, that is the most important distinction.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #113 — Gestalt-annotation asymmetry in advocacy design**: When an advocacy visualization places the primary visual argument (the gestalt) in a high-attention zone and the corrective context in a low-attention zone, the design produces a structural advocacy failure: the wrong conclusion is available to the majority of viewers (glancing visitors), while the correct conclusion requires sustained reading. This is distinct from simple omission — the correction IS present, but placed where it cannot reach the target audience. Rubric anchor: Resonance (Type III advocacy-efficacy) / Legibility (context-zone mismatch).
