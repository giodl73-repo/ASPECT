## Voiced Review

ISOTYPE is a school I have always respected more than I have recommended — it is lower data-density than a bar chart, but it is honest about what it is doing and why. This work correctly executes the grammar. The repetition is real repetition; the unit is declared; there is no scaling masquerading as repetition. That is more than most practitioners manage.

My concern is with the annotation panel, and it is not a small concern. The design deploys the annotation panel to carry the most important contextual information — that Europe and North America produce more plastic per person, that they export waste to the countries shown with the most symbols. This is not marginal context. This is the information that determines what moral conclusion the viewer should draw. And the designer has placed it in 10-point italic text on a gray panel at the bottom of a museum display that viewers will encounter at two meters' distance.

The annotation fails its reading context. Public display at two meters means that text smaller than approximately 14 points is not reliably legible to a standing viewer. The designer has effectively made the corrective context invisible to everyone who does not walk up and read carefully. This is not a craft failure — it is a design strategy failure. The visual argument (East Asia: 8 symbols; North America: trace) lands perfectly at 2m; the correction does not reach the viewer at all.

The honest solution is what Neurath suggests: a paired ISOTYPE chart for per-capita figures. Or — and this is my suggestion — remove the annotation panel and instead change the title to accurately reflect what the chart shows: "Where plastic enters the ocean: absolute tonnes, 2023." Let the chart be the chart. Let viewers draw their own conclusions or read separate explanatory materials. What is not acceptable is a corrective panel that cannot be read in the context for which the chart was designed.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #112 — Context-legibility gap in annotation panels**: When a visualization designed for a public-display context (2m glanced) includes correction text at font sizes legible only in a contemplative-study context (arm's length, sustained attention), the annotation fails the reading context test. The visual argument and the corrective annotation operate at different effective reading distances, producing a design that appears complete but is functionally incomplete for its actual context. Rubric anchor: Legibility (Step 0 — context specification mismatch between main image and annotation zones).
