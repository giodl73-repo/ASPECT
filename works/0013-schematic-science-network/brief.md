# Brief — 0013 Schematic Science Network

## Subject

A schematic map of global scientific collaboration — the 20 most-connected research cities by co-authorship links (2022 Web of Science data). Cities are treated as stations; co-authorship frequency is encoded as line weight; research domains are encoded as line colors. The story: a surprisingly small number of city-pairs dominate global science, and that dominance follows wealth and historical ties more than geography.

## Core Data

Top 20 cities by total co-authorship links (2022):
Beijing, Shanghai, London, New York, Boston, Paris, Tokyo, Seoul, Singapore, Sydney, Toronto, Berlin, Zurich, Cambridge (UK), San Francisco, Los Angeles, Amsterdam, Chicago, Vienna, Bangalore

Top city-pairs by co-authorship frequency (selective list for line weighting):
- London–New York (highest)
- Boston–London
- Beijing–Shanghai (domestic but high-volume)
- Paris–London
- Zurich–London
- Cambridge(UK)–London
- Beijing–London
- New York–Boston (same metro but co-authorship counted separately)
- Singapore–London
- Seoul–Beijing

Research domain color categories (5 lines):
- Life sciences/medicine: red
- Physical sciences/engineering: blue
- Social sciences: green
- Mathematics/computer science: orange
- Earth/environmental: teal

## The Story

The 20 cities are not evenly connected. A handful of city-pairs — primarily London and a cluster of US East Coast cities — dominate co-authorship volume. The network is not a web; it is a hub-and-spoke system centered on London and the Boston-New York corridor. East Asian cities are heavily connected internally but less connected to European and American hubs than their total paper volume would predict. This is the structure of global science: historically accumulated, not geographically natural.

## The Core Question

Can Beck's grammar apply to a non-transit network? What breaks when there is no geographic anchor (no "Thames") to provide orientation? Without a river or coastline retained as a geographic reference, the schematic map loses its orienting anchor. What replaces it? Does the grammar require a geographic anchor, or can a conceptual anchor (e.g., a "scientific gravity" center of mass) substitute?

## Audience

Science policy researchers, research administrators, academic bibliometricians. Expert audience with spatial literacy but not necessarily cartographic literacy. Likely reading context: professional conference presentation or policy report (political-presentation context with brief for the audience).

## School

Schematic-cartography (Type A). Apply Beck's grammar:
- Topology over geography (co-authorship connections, not geographic proximity)
- Angular discipline (0°, 45°, 90° only)
- Color as line identity (research domain)
- Interchange priority (cities that appear on multiple domain-lines get interchange markers)
- One geographic anchor: PROBLEM — what serves as the geographic anchor when there is no terrain?

## Constraints

- Maximum 20 "stations" (cities)
- Lines weighted by co-authorship frequency (three weights: heavy, medium, light)
- City labels horizontal where possible
- No geographic coastlines or national borders in the image proper

## Open Questions

1. What replaces the Thames? Options: (a) an implicit "gravity center" with no visual marker; (b) a conceptual "weight" indicator at the dominant hub city (London); (c) nothing — accept that schematic science maps have no geographic anchor; (d) retain a continent-outline ghost in very light gray as orientation aid without encoding data.
2. Is the line-weight encoding (co-authorship frequency) within Beck's grammar? Beck uses line weight for transfer priority, not for frequency. This is a grammar extension.
3. How to handle domestic city-pairs (Beijing-Shanghai) that are geographically close but schematically should be equidistant from their topology?
4. Does the hub-and-spoke finding survive the schematization, or does the angular discipline obscure it?
