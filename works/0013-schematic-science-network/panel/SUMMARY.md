## Key Agreements

**The geographic anchor problem is real and unresolved.** Beck, Bertin, Tufte, and the Historian all identify it from different angles. Beck says the ghost continent is a crutch, not an anchor. The Historian identifies this as genuinely new territory — Beck never faced a network without a shared geographic anchor. The Historian's Innovation #121 names the problem precisely: the correct substitute for the Thames in a non-transit schematic is not a ghost geography but an internal structural anchor (the visually dominant hub). This is the design's most important unresolved problem and its most important theoretical contribution.

**Five domain-colors is too many for complex interchange nodes.** Bertin (Innovation #118) establishes a saturation limit: approximately 4 hues for networks with 3+ lines sharing major interchange nodes. Tufte recommends small multiples as an alternative. Beck notes the two-navigation-variable problem (color + weight simultaneously). The panel agrees the color system is at or beyond its retinal limit.

**The hub-and-spoke gestalt does land.** All reviewers note that London's complexity reads in the gestalt. The resonance scores (11-12) are consistent across the panel. The design's single strongest achievement — making the hub-and-spoke structure visible in the schematic form — is achieved.

## Key Tensions

**Beck vs. Tufte on the geographic anchor:**

Beck's prescription: remove the ghost continent; let the London node serve as internal anchor through its visual complexity alone. Accept that the schematic has no geographic anchor.

Tufte's prescription: use small multiples (one panel per domain-line), which reduces per-panel complexity and eliminates the anchor problem entirely — each panel has fewer lines and the user can trace any one domain without the intersection confusion.

These are structurally different remedies. Beck's solution preserves the single-diagram format; Tufte's abandons it. The single-diagram format is the Beck grammar's core mode; small multiples are a statistical graphics solution being imported from a different school. The tension is: do you fix the Beck diagram, or do you replace it with a better tool from a different school?

**Beck vs. Historian on what this work contributes:**

Beck views the ghost continent as a failure of grammar fidelity. The Historian views the same design choice as an interesting and productive first attempt at solving a genuinely new problem — the non-transit Beck application with no natural geographic anchor. These are not incompatible — both can be right — but they produce different assessments of the design's value.

## Design Revision Recommendations

1. **Replace ghost continent with internal structural anchor** — remove the ghost continent outlines; make London's node visually dominant through interchange complexity alone (multiple colored rings at the node); add a note that London is the network's highest-connectivity node. This is the Beck-grammar-faithful solution to the anchor problem (Beck's prescription + Historian's formulation).

2. **Reduce domain categories from 5 to 4** — merge the two weakest or most similar domain-lines (e.g., fold social sciences into one of the others, or create a "humanities and social sciences" combined category). Bertin's saturation limit suggests 4 maximum for this network complexity.

3. **Consider small multiples as a companion view** — five small panels (one per domain) do not replace the main diagram but can accompany it in the report version. Main diagram shows total network; small multiples support domain-specific queries. Both are present; viewer chooses the appropriate view for their question.

4. **Reduce line weight to two classes** — heavy and light; the three-class system adds a navigation variable (Beck's objection) and the middle class is visually ambiguous at small sizes.

5. **Revise title to state the finding** — "London Anchors Global Science: Co-authorship Networks by Domain (2022)" is more advocacy-complete and tells the viewer what to look for.

## Cluster Updates

**Cluster N (new) — Non-transit Beck grammar**: Innovations #116, #117, #121 all address the same structural problem: Beck's grammar was designed for transit navigation and some of its rules (geographic anchor, single navigation variable) do not translate directly to non-transit networks. This is a productive cluster for future Beck applications — science networks, organization charts, ecological networks. Three innovations in one work names the cluster. Watch for confirmation in any future schematic-cartography application to non-transit domains.

**Cluster K (confirmation) — School-story mismatch**: Tufte's small-multiples prescription (Innovation #119) imports a solution from a different school (statistical-graphics) because the Beck grammar cannot solve the multi-domain legibility problem within its own constraints. This is the third confirmation of Cluster K (school-story mismatch as a failure category). Cluster K amendment is now strongly indicated.
