## Voiced Review

I am a science policy researcher sitting in a conference room. Someone puts this diagram on the screen and begins talking. My first impression: this looks like a metro map. I know metro maps — I use the London Underground regularly. The visual language is familiar. I look for London. I find it — it is the most complex node, where the most lines converge. That orients me immediately.

Now I try to trace a line. I want to follow the red line — life sciences. At London, there are five colored lines converging. I try to follow the red one north — is that north? east? The angular lines mean I cannot use the ghost continent directly. I follow the red line by color, not by direction. I lose it twice where it crosses blue and orange lines at tight intersections.

The presenter says: "Notice how London connects to both the US East Coast and East Asia through separate line systems." Now I see it. The presenter is doing necessary work that the diagram cannot do alone. That is what political-presentation context means — the presenter completes the diagram.

I appreciate the line weight. Heavy lines feel important; light lines feel peripheral. I do not know exactly how many co-authored papers constitute a "heavy" connection, but I feel the hierarchy.

What I do not feel, without the presenter, is the East-West asymmetry story — that East Asian cities are highly connected internally but weakly connected to European hubs. I see the diagram. I do not read the argument from it. That requires narration.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #120 — Presenter-dependency as legibility sub-type**: When a visualization is designed for political-presentation context but the presenter completes arguments that the diagram cannot make alone, the design has shifted from "political-presentation" to "presenter-dependent" — a sub-type where the diagram is documentation for the talk rather than a self-contained argument. This is a legibility failure in a specific mode: the design is competent as a speaker aid but incompetent as a standalone reference. The distinction matters for how the work is distributed and archived. Rubric anchor: Legibility (Step 0 — context specification; primary vs. standalone legibility).
