## Voiced Review

My grammar was designed for one thing: answering the question the traveler actually has. Which line? Where to change? How many stops? Every decision followed from that question. The question determined what information mattered and what was noise.

Your question is different: which cities are most connected, through what fields, with what intensity? That is three questions embedded in one diagram, and my grammar was never designed to answer three questions simultaneously. I answer one question very well. You are attempting three.

The angular discipline is correct. The interchange markers are correct. The color-as-identity convention is correctly applied to research domains. I have no complaint on those points — the grammar is present.

My objections are two. First: the ghost continent solution. I retained the Thames not for beauty but because every traveler who used the map knew the Thames. It was a shared cognitive anchor. Ghost continent outlines serve a different purpose — they impose geographic familiarity on a network that the design is trying to free from geography. The viewer sees the ghost and immediately tries to locate cities geographically. The schematic fights the ghost. If there is no natural anchor, the correct solution is not an artificial one — it is to accept that the schematic must build its own orientation through internal structure. The London node, through its visual complexity, can serve as anchor. Let it.

Second: line weight as frequency. In my map, all lines are equivalent — the difference between them is their destination, not their importance. You have introduced a frequency hierarchy that my grammar does not include. That is an extension, and you have declared it, which I respect. But it creates a new problem: the viewer must now track line color (domain) AND line weight (frequency) simultaneously. That is two navigation variables where I used one.

The work is competent. It is not fully mine.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #116 — Shared cognitive anchor vs. imposed geographic anchor**: Beck's geographic anchor (the Thames) works because it references a feature the user already knows from lived experience. In non-transit schematic networks, no equivalent shared anchor exists — ghost geographic features impose familiarity that the viewer may or may not possess. The alternative is an internal anchor: a visually dominant hub node that serves as the organizational center of the schematic by its own visual complexity, not by geographic reference. Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Beck grammar / geographic anchor rule) / Legibility.

**Innovation #117 — Multi-variable Beck extension (the two-navigation-variable problem)**: Beck's grammar achieves legibility partly by holding navigation variables to a minimum: color = line identity; that is the primary variable. Adding a second navigation variable (line weight = frequency) increases decoding load and may undermine the primary legibility goal. This is a Beck-grammar-specific constraint: any extension that adds a navigation variable must justify itself against the question "does this help the traveler answer their actual question?" Rubric anchor: Visual Economy / School Fidelity.
