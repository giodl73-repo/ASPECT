## Voiced Review

Let me name the visual variables in use: position (topological), color hue (domain — 5 values), line weight (frequency — 3 classes), node shape (interchange/non-interchange — 2 values). That is four simultaneous visual variables. In *Sémiologie Graphique* I established that the eye processes position automatically and color selectively — which means that for color to work as an identity variable, the viewer must perform an active search rather than a passive registration.

For a transit map with 5-6 line colors, this works because the traveler already knows which color they want — they look for it. In your diagram, the viewer does not arrive knowing which domain they want to trace. They are exploring the whole network simultaneously. Five hues in a complex intersection network will create what I call "selective failure": the eye cannot isolate any one domain line at a complex interchange without sustained effort. At the London node — the highest-interchange node — all five domain colors converge. The viewer will experience this as confusion, not as information.

My prescription: reduce domain categories or use position more aggressively to separate them. Four maximum hues for a complex network; three is better. Or use small multiples — five panels, one per domain, showing only that domain's connections. Each panel is simple; the viewer compares across panels. This is more information at less cognitive cost.

The ghost continent outlines I find acceptable. They are structural-attentional marks; I note the −1 to −2 penalty but apply only −1 because they genuinely serve orientation without competing with the data at the viewing distance specified.

The position variable is the most powerful and it is being used correctly — topological relationships are preserved, distances are distorted to serve legibility. That is the fundamental Beck insight and it is correctly implemented here.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #118 — Domain-color saturation limit in complex network schematics**: In Beck-grammar network diagrams, the number of simultaneous hue-identity variables is constrained by the complexity of the network's interchange structure. At high-interchange nodes, the simultaneous discrimination of 5+ hues under selective (not automatic) processing exceeds reliable retinal capacity. The saturation limit appears to be approximately 4 hues for networks with 3+ lines sharing major interchange nodes. Rubric anchor: Visual Economy / Legibility (retinal efficiency).
