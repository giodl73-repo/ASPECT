*School fidelity: Beck grammar Type A.*

## Voiced Review

Beck designed for a specific problem: the London Underground traveler who needed to navigate a dense, complex network that was geographically illegible because it ran underground. Every element of his grammar addressed that specific problem. The angular discipline reduced intersection ambiguity. The interchange markers told you where to change. The geographic anchor (the Thames) connected the schematic to the traveler's mental map. The color system made lines individually traceable in a complex web.

This work applies Beck's grammar to a fundamentally different problem — understanding a global academic network — and discovers, appropriately, that some elements translate and others do not. The angular discipline translates. The interchange markers translate. The color-as-identity convention translates with caveats. The geographic anchor does not.

What is historically interesting here is that Beck himself never had to solve this problem. His network had a clear physical anchor (the Thames) that was already part of his users' mental landscape. He did not need to invent a solution because the solution existed geographically. This work is the first application of Beck's grammar to a network with no pre-existing shared geographic anchor among its users — science policy researchers in different countries do not share a common geographic reference point.

The ghost continent solution is defensible but incomplete. It is essentially a compromise between Beck's grammar (retain one geographic anchor) and the acknowledgment that no natural anchor exists. The compromise is: retain a ghost of all geographic features simultaneously, at sub-threshold visibility. The problem is that "sub-threshold" ghost outlines are never truly invisible — viewers of different visual acuity and screen calibrations will see different amounts of them.

The prior art for non-transit Beck applications is limited: airline route schematics have been attempted (Mijksenaar's work in the 1990s); some organizational hierarchy diagrams apply Beck conventions. This work is the most explicit grammar application to a scholarly network, and the geographic anchor problem is its genuine novel contribution.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #121 — The geographic anchor substitution problem in non-transit Beck schematics**: Beck's grammar requires one retained geographic anchor because transit users navigate by connection to a shared geographic mental model (their city). In non-transit networks, no shared geographic mental model exists among users from different regions. The geometric solution (ghost continent outlines) introduces a geographic presence that the grammar is designed to suppress. The correct substitute may be an internal structural anchor — the visually dominant hub — rather than any geographic feature. This is the first explicit formulation of this grammar problem in the DEGAS project. Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Beck grammar / geographic anchor rule).
