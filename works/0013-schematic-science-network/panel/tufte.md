## Voiced Review

The question this design must answer is: what does the intended viewer need to know? If the audience is science policy researchers at a conference, they need to know: which cities are most connected; which domains drive those connections; whether the network is hub-and-spoke or distributed. The schematic grammar can answer the first and third questions efficiently. It struggles with the second.

My specific objection is to the small multiples alternative that has not been chosen. Five domain-color lines on a complex 20-node network creates the kind of layered complexity that makes individual lines difficult to trace. A viewer who wants to know "what is the life-sciences connection between Singapore and Boston" must visually filter out the blue, green, orange, and teal lines from the red one across the entire diagram. This is active work, not passive reading.

Small multiples — five panels, one per domain, each showing only that domain's connections — would answer the same question with less cognitive effort. The panels would be smaller individually but the targeted question would resolve faster. I have argued for small multiples in many contexts; this is one where the argument is particularly strong.

The ghost continent decision I understand but do not endorse. Ghost outlines that have no data function but serve orientation are structural-attentional marks — under the rubric, lightly penalized. My concern is that "lightly penalized" may undercount the actual cognitive interference. The ghost continent invites the viewer to check city positions against the outline, which is exactly the geographic thinking the schematic is designed to override.

The hub-and-spoke story is visible in the gestalt. London's interchange complexity reads. That is the design's strongest single achievement.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #119 — Small multiples as Beck grammar alternative**: For schematic networks with 4+ domain-color lines, small multiples (one panel per domain-line, each showing the full node set but only that domain's connections) may achieve higher domain-level legibility than a single multi-color diagram. This is a Beck-grammar extension that reduces per-panel complexity at the cost of total-network gestalt. The tradeoff: you cannot see all domains simultaneously, but you can trace any single domain instantly. Rubric anchor: Legibility / Visual Economy.
