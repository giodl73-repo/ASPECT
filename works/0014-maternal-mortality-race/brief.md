# Brief — 0014 Maternal Mortality Race

## Subject

Maternal mortality rates by race in the United States, 2016–2022. The primary finding: Black American women die in childbirth at 2.5–3× the rate of white American women, and this gap has widened over time. The secondary finding (contested design choice): overall US maternal mortality is high by OECD standards — 23.8 per 100,000 live births in 2020, compared to OECD averages of 7-8.

## The Core Question (Du Bois vs. Nightingale)

**Du Bois's position (precision-as-defiance)**: Show the unflattering full context. US overall maternal mortality is high by OECD standards. Show that both white and Black American women die at rates that would shame other wealthy nations. The racial gap is the core injustice, but the international comparison shows that the racial gap sits inside a national-level failure. Being honest about the full scale of the problem — including the parts that reflect badly on the US healthcare system at every level — is the political and intellectual obligation. The white US rate (19.1) is already worse than the UK (9.7). That is part of the data.

**Nightingale's position (targeted advocacy)**: The decision-maker this work must reach is the US legislator or hospital administrator who must act on racial disparities in maternal care. For that audience, the international comparison is a distraction that dilutes the urgency. "The US is worse than Europe" is a different political fight than "Black women die at 3× the rate of white women." Mixing them produces a work that cannot close on either argument. Focus the racial gap; give decision-makers one thing to act on.

## Audience

US healthcare policy researchers, hospital administrators, and legislators. Expert audience with some statistical literacy. Likely reading context: policy report or advocacy presentation (political-presentation context). A secondary audience: educated general public via journalism.

## School

Advocacy-visualization (Type A). Du Bois and Nightingale are both founding figures. The school permits:
- Form chosen for audience impact over statistical precision
- Comparison selection as argument
- Color as moral category
- Title as argument

## Constraints

- Data must be accurate and sourced
- The racial gap must be the primary visual finding
- The design must reach a US policy audience — not just those already convinced
- Both Du Bois and Nightingale should recognize this as their school

## Open Questions

1. Should the OECD comparison be in the same panel or a separate companion panel?
2. How is "maternal death" defined consistently across years? (CDC changed ICD coding in 2018, affecting comparability — must be declared)
3. Should COVID-19 years (2020-2021) be flagged as anomalous?
4. Should the design show three racial groups (Black, White, Hispanic) or focus on the Black/White comparison as the most dramatic gap?
