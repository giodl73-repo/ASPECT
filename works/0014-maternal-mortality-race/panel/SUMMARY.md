## Key Agreements

**The shared y-axis diptych is the correct form for a two-level argument.** Nightingale (Innovation #122), Du Bois, Tufte, and Audience all affirm that the shared y-axis is the critical design element — without it, the two panels are two separate arguments; with it, they are one argument with two dimensions.

**The data sourcing is exemplary.** The Statistician gives the highest data praise seen in any panel to date. ICD coding change declared. COVID period flagged. Both primary sources (CDC WONDER, OECD) cited. This matches or exceeds the data integrity of canonical works reviewed in this project.

**The Du Bois precision-as-defiance principle applies with full force.** All reviewers recognize that showing white US rates above European norms is not a distraction but a structural argument. Du Bois (Innovation #124) names this "international comparison as anti-exceptionalism device."

## Key Tensions

**Nightingale vs. Du Bois on single-argument focus vs. multi-argument completeness:**

This is the panel's central productive tension, specified in the brief. Nightingale (Innovation #123) argues the two-argument diptych diffuses the direct emotional urgency needed to move a US legislator in thirty seconds. Du Bois argues that a single-argument version allows the racial gap to be filed as "a minority health problem" rather than a systemwide failure. Both are correct analyses of different advocacy risks. The tension does not resolve — it is a genuine strategic disagreement that the designer must own.

**The design's choice (Du Bois position) is declared.** The design specification states it explicitly; the panel has evaluated it on its own terms. Score disagreement (Du Bois 88, Nightingale 81) reflects strategic disagreement, not execution disagreement. The execution is consistently praised by all five reviewers.

**Tufte on the COVID shading band, Audience on its value:**

Tufte would remove the COVID band; Audience would keep it. This is a smaller tension within Visual Economy — both positions are defensible, and the resolution depends on which audience segment the designer treats as primary (those who know about COVID/maternal mortality interactions vs. those who do not).

## Design Revision Recommendations

1. **Add COVID comorbidity quantification** — per the Statistician (Innovation #127): add a note to the COVID shading band quantifying estimated COVID contribution to the 2020–2021 peak. "Approximately 40-50% of excess deaths in 2020–2021 attributed to COVID-19 as contributing cause (Thoma et al., 2022)." This resolves the risk that the peak is read as a secular trend rather than a COVID artifact.

2. **Add OECD comparability caveat** — different countries use different postpartum windows for maternal death classification. One sentence in the source notes. The Statistician is correct that this is a Step A gap.

3. **Consider a single-panel journalism version** — the diptych is correct for its primary audience (policy presentation). For the secondary audience (journalism/general public), a single-panel version with the international comparison as a reference line may be more publishable. Design both; declare which is for which audience.

4. **Annotation of the COVID band** — if the band is retained (Audience position prevails), add the COVID mortality annotation text directly to the band rather than in a separate note.

5. **Test the 30-second read** — Nightingale's concern about the 30-second legislator window is a real usability question. The design should be shown to a policy professional and timed; if the two-argument integration does not happen in under 45 seconds, Nightingale's strategic position is empirically supported.
