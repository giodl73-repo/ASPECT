*Primary audience: US health policy researchers and legislators. Secondary: educated general public via journalism.*

## Voiced Review

I am a US health policy researcher. I have seen charts about racial disparities in maternal mortality before. My response to yet another one has some risk of fatigue — I have been shown this problem and I know it is not fixed; what I need is not another demonstration but a new argument.

This design gives me a new argument. The shared y-axis diptych shows me something I have not seen clearly before: that the white American rate — a rate I might unconsciously treat as the "normal" baseline — already exceeds the UK's rate. That is not something I knew in felt terms, even if I knew it statistically. The shared scale makes it bodily: I can see that the white American rate sits in the bottom third of the bars, not at the bottom. The design moved me from "the racial gap is the problem" to "the racial gap is the most severe symptom of a systemically broken system."

For my secondary audience — a journalist writing about this for a general reader — the design is perhaps one panel too many. A general-audience version might use only Panel 1 (the racial gap over time) and include the international comparison as a reference line on the same chart rather than a full second panel. That would be more compact and more likely to be published.

The COVID shading band is helpful: I was going to ask why the rates spike so sharply in 2020. It answers my question before I can ask it. Tufte wants to remove it; I would keep it.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #126 — Diptych vs. compound chart for dual-argument advocacy**: When an advocacy work makes two simultaneous arguments (racial gap + international context), a diptych (two linked panels) and a compound chart (both comparisons on a single panel) represent different audience tradeoffs. The diptych separates the arguments spatially, allowing each to be processed individually before integration; the compound chart forces simultaneous processing. For expert audiences in presentation context: diptych is preferable (presenter can sequence the arguments). For general public via journalism: compound chart is preferable (single image, single load). The choice should be declared as audience-specific. Rubric anchor: Legibility (Step 0 — audience and context specification) / Resonance (Type III).
