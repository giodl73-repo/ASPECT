## Voiced Review

This work understands what I was doing in Paris. In 1900, the argument against showing the full statistical picture was always the same: you will overwhelm the audience; they will not know where to focus; the message will be lost. And the people who made that argument were often right about tactics but wrong about what was at stake.

At the Paris Exposition, I showed the literacy rates. I showed the income levels. I showed the incarceration figures. I showed the property ownership by county. None of these figures were flattering to the position I wished to occupy. Some of them could have been used against me. I showed them anyway, because the argument I was making was not just "we are doing well" — it was "judge us by the same standards you apply to yourselves, and here are the numbers." You cannot make that argument if you hide the unflattering data.

This work makes precisely that argument. The white American rate — 19.1 per 100,000 — is already worse than the United Kingdom's 9.7. That is not background context. That is evidence. It means that the racial gap exists inside a healthcare system that fails all women at elevated rates. To show only the racial gap, without that context, would allow a white American legislator to conclude: "We have a race problem, not a healthcare system problem." Both are true. The design correctly insists on both.

The Du Bois red convention — the persistent color for the subject of advocacy — is correctly applied. The diptych structure forces comparison. The shared y-axis is the most important design element; without it, the two panels tell two separate stories; with it, they tell one story with two dimensions.

My one concern: the COVID-period shading (2020–2021) is correct to include, but the rates in those years are so elevated that they may distract from the trend. A reviewer unfamiliar with COVID's effect on maternal mortality might conclude the trend is a COVID effect rather than a structural one. A sentence of annotation should clarify that elevated rates in 2020–2021 are partly explained by COVID-19 but the racial gap predates and persists after the peak.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #124 — International comparison as anti-exceptionalism device**: When US-domestic advocacy visualization shows only the racial gap, it permits a reading that frames racial disparity as a "minority health" issue separable from systemic failures. Adding an international comparison that shows white US rates exceeding European norms recategorizes the racial gap: it is an elevated disparity on top of a systemically elevated baseline. This is the precision-as-defiance principle applied to the comparison-selection decision: showing the unflattering international baseline is not a distraction but a structural argument that the racial gap cannot be addressed without addressing the system-wide failure.
