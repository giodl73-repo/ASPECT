## Voiced Review

I disagree with this design's central decision, and I will say so plainly — but I recognize the argument for it.

When I took data to Parliament about soldiers dying of preventable disease, I made a choice about what to show. I did not show everything I knew. I showed the preventable fraction, in a form that made the preventable fraction impossible to miss. Had I added three other charts showing how British hospitals compared to French ones, or how military medicine compared to civilian medicine, I would have given my audience three arguments to deliberate among. They would have left the room having understood none of them with sufficient urgency to act.

This design makes the opposite choice. It shows: the racial gap over time; and the US position in international rankings; simultaneously, in a diptych. The subtitle carries both arguments. The two panels require the viewer to integrate two comparisons.

My concern is audience attention. A US legislator looking at this has approximately thirty seconds before their aide says it is time for the next meeting. In thirty seconds, can they read both panels, integrate the comparison, and leave with a clear action agenda? I am not certain they can. The gestalt of the single-panel version — three lines diverging, Black dramatically higher — is available in under five seconds. The diptych requires more.

The counterargument, and Du Bois makes it better than I could: if the work reaches only the racial gap, white US legislators can file it as "a minority health problem" and move on. The international comparison makes it impossible for any US-born viewer to feel comfortable. That is a real strategic advantage.

I find the diptych well-executed. I find the shared y-axis inspired — it is the critical element that makes the two panels comparable. I disagree with the strategy, but I respect the execution.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #122 — The shared y-axis as diptych argument device**: In advocacy diptychs comparing two datasets (temporal change + international context), a shared y-axis is not merely a design choice but the argument itself — it forces the viewer to see that the same quantity unit applies to both panels simultaneously. Without the shared scale, the diptych shows two things; with it, the diptych shows one thing from two angles. Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step C — structural framing as causal implication) / Resonance (Type III + IV).

**Innovation #123 — Single-argument focus vs. multi-argument completeness in advocacy design**: Advocacy designs face a structural tradeoff between comprehensiveness (showing all morally relevant context) and focus (giving the target audience one unmistakable action agenda). More context increases intellectual completeness and reduces the risk of motivated misreading; it also increases cognitive load and may dilute the primary urgency. This tradeoff is undeclared in the advocacy school literature (the school prescribes "comparison selection as argument" without addressing when multiple simultaneous arguments conflict). Rubric anchor: Resonance (Type III) / School Fidelity.
