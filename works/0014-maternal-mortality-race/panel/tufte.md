## Voiced Review

The diptych is well-constructed. The shared y-axis is the key decision and it is correct — without it the two panels are unrelated; with it they are the same argument from two angles. The data is honest. The ICD note is present. The title states the argument. I can find very little to object to on data integrity grounds.

My objection is on visual economy, and it is specific. The design carries three encoding layers simultaneously on Panel 1: line color (three racial groups), line weight (bold for Black women), and the COVID shading band. On Panel 2: bar color (US racial groups vs. European countries), the reference line at the US White rate, and the y-axis that also serves Panel 1. That is six active visual elements in a diptych.

The COVID shading band is the one element I would remove or simplify. It is structurally attentional — it marks a time period, not a data point — and it draws attention to the 2020–2021 spike that Du Bois correctly notes may be misread as a COVID effect rather than a structural trend. The spike is visible in the lines themselves; the band makes it more visible but at a visual cost. A simpler solution: a small annotation note ("2020–2021: rates elevated by COVID-19 comorbidities") attached to the peak without the band.

I note that Nightingale and Du Bois disagree on strategy. I decline to adjudicate advocacy strategy — that is not what the rubric asks. What I will say: the two-argument design is internally consistent and well-executed. If the advocacy strategy is correct (Du Bois's position), the execution earns it. If the advocacy strategy is incorrect (Nightingale's position), the execution cannot save it.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #125 — Structural-attentional marks as distraction risk in advocacy time-series**: In time-series advocacy charts where an anomalous period (COVID, recession, policy change) explains a spike, shading the anomalous period structurally draws the viewer's eye to the spike and may encourage the wrong reading (spike is the story). A targeted annotation label achieves the same acknowledgment at lower visual cost and without creating a visually dominant non-data zone. This is a Visual Economy prescription specific to advocacy time-series with known anomalous periods. Rubric anchor: Visual Economy / Resonance (Type III).
