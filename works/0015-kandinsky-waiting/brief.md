# Brief — 0015 Kandinsky Waiting

## Subject

The sensation of waiting — specifically, the formal properties of anticipatory tension (waiting for news, waiting for an outcome). No quantitative data. A composition using Kandinsky's point-line-plane grammar to represent the formal properties of waiting: the gathering of attention toward a single point; the suspended line; the unresolved tension between approach and arrival.

## What is "waiting" formally?

Waiting has identifiable formal properties that can be mapped to Kandinsky's grammar:

- **The gathering point**: attention collects toward a locus — a door, a phone, a clock face. Formally: a point of maximal focal tension, surrounded by fields of secondary attention.
- **The suspended line**: the action that has not yet completed; the line that extends toward a terminus it has not yet reached. Formally: a line in motion that does not arrive; horizontal tendency with interrupted terminus.
- **The unresolved tension**: the compositional logic of waiting is held tension — not the release of resolution, not the chaos of crisis, but sustained, organized, gathering pressure. Formally: multiple forces directed toward a single center without achieving it.
- **The ambient field**: waiting happens in a field of ordinary time that has been charged by the expected event. Formally: a background plane that is active but not urgent — it hums, it does not shout.

## No Data

There is no quantitative data in this work. The "data" is the phenomenology of waiting as a psychological state. Under the abstract-art school, this is legitimate: the composition encodes formal and psychological properties, not external quantities. Data integrity here means: does the composition accurately represent the phenomenology it claims to represent?

## Core Question

Can the Kandinsky grammar successfully represent a psychological state? What does "data integrity" mean for a work about a felt experience rather than a measured phenomenon?

The critical sub-question: Kandinsky claims that certain formal properties (cold horizontal line = passive; concentrated point = focal tension; diagonal = dynamic) are perceptually universal — not culturally contingent. If those claims are culturally contingent, the composition's "accuracy" cannot be verified the way a chart's accuracy can. How does the panel evaluate this?

## Audience

Any viewer who has experienced waiting. The design is not domain-gated. Primary reading context: contemplative study in a gallery or exhibition context.

## School

Abstract-art (Type A). Kandinsky's point-line-plane grammar. Internal consistency is the accuracy standard, not external correspondence.

## Constraints

- No representational elements (no clocks, no doors, no literal figures)
- All formal properties must be justified within Kandinsky's grammar
- Color must serve the composition's formal logic, not decoration
- The composition must be self-contained — no title explanation required for basic comprehension (though a title is permitted as minimal orientation)

## Open Questions

1. How is "data integrity" evaluated when the claimed referent is a psychological state rather than a measured phenomenon?
2. Can Kandinsky's universality claims be taken at face value here, or must the composition acknowledge their cultural contingency?
3. What is the difference between "the composition works" (formal internal consistency) and "the composition represents waiting" (correspondence to a phenomenological reality)? Does the rubric distinguish these?
