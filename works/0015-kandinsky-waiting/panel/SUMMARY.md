## Key Agreements

**The truncated-approach device is a genuine compositional discovery.** Kandinsky (Innovation #128), Artist (Innovation #131), and Historian (Innovation #133) all affirm that the approaching-but-not-arriving lines are an original formal finding within the school's grammar. Kandinsky: "The incompletion IS the data." Artist: "When you look at it you feel the suspension before you understand why. That is the criterion."

**The color restriction is formally justified and bold.** All five reviewers recognize the chromatic suppression as a positive formal argument, not a failure of the school's color vocabulary. The palette restriction encodes the phenomenology of waiting's chromatic impoverishment.

**The grammar is correctly and thoroughly applied.** School Fidelity scores (16-18) are the highest of any original design work reviewed to date. The point-line-plane vocabulary is precise; every element has a declared formal function; nothing is decorative.

## Key Tensions (as specified in the brief)

**Tufte vs. Kandinsky and Artist on data integrity:**

Tufte (Innovation #132): the composition claims to represent waiting specifically, but multiple psychological states (longing, sustained grief, chronic fatigue) share the same formal properties. The composition's "accuracy" is unverifiable because formal properties underdetermine the specific psychological state. He scores Data/Story Integrity at 11.

Kandinsky: the composition is accurate at the level it claims — it encodes the formal properties of anticipatory tension (gathering, suspension, approach-without-arrival). Whether a viewer names this "waiting" or "longing" is secondary to whether the formal properties are correctly deployed.

Artist: the composition works if you sit with it. The question of whether it represents "waiting" exactly rather than adjacent states is a philosopher's objection to a painter's achievement.

This tension is productive and unresolved. It surfaces Innovation #132 (perceptual claim verification failure for psychological states) as a genuine rubric gap.

**Bertin's universality objection:**

Bertin (Innovation #130) distinguishes between structural formal claims (point = focal tension, approaching line = approach — broadly universal) and color/temperature claims (blue = deep/receding/gravitational — culturally contingent). He scores Data/Story Integrity at 13, below Kandinsky's 16, for this reason.

Kandinsky acknowledges the universality question and notes that the structural claims are more secure than the color claims. He does not fully concede but narrows the disagreement.

## Design Revision Recommendations

1. **Recalibrate the focal point position toward near-center** — Kandinsky's note that a slightly more centered placement (52%/50% vs. 55%/48%) would create more excruciating suspension is worth testing. The composition currently makes the asymmetry perceptible; making it sub-perceptible (felt but not seen) would be more formally precise for waiting's character.

2. **Differentiate line quality as well as line weight** — the Artist's Innovation #131 is the most actionable revision: the heaviest approaching line should carry driven hand-energy in the rendering; the lightest should feel exhausted. Add a rendering note specifying this.

3. **Acknowledge the universality limit in adjacent documentation** — Bertin's objection cannot be addressed within the composition itself (representational solutions are forbidden). But a minimal adjacent note — "This composition deploys Kandinsky's formal grammar. Some formal-property claims are structurally universal; color conventions derive from European visual culture." — would satisfy Step C by declaring the cultural scope of the claim.

4. **Address the underdetermination problem in the title zone** — Tufte's objection (multiple states share these formal properties) is structural. A subtitle — "Waiting, No. 1: on anticipatory tension" — narrows the claimed state from "waiting" (general) to "anticipatory tension" (the specific formal property being encoded). This may partially satisfy the underdetermination objection.

## Cluster Updates

**Cluster P (new) — Formal underdetermination in abstract-art subjects**: Innovations #128, #130, #132 all address the same structural problem: when the subject is a psychological state rather than a measured phenomenon, multiple formal configurations can represent it, and multiple psychological states can share the same formal configuration. The rubric's abstract-art integrity standard ("internal formal consistency") is insufficient for psychological-state subjects. Cluster P watches for further cases where formal encoding is accurate but underdetermined relative to the claimed subject. Three innovations in one work is sufficient to name the cluster. Proposed rubric amendment: Data/Story Integrity should include a sub-step E for abstract-art works: assess whether the formal encoding uniquely determines the claimed subject (structural formal properties) vs. is shared by multiple possible subjects (psychological-state claims).

**Cluster K (fourth confirmation) — School-story mismatch in reverse**: Bertin and Tufte raise whether the Kandinsky grammar can represent psychological states "accurately" enough to satisfy the rubric. This is Cluster K in reverse: not "wrong school for this story" but "this school's accuracy standard may be insufficient for this story." The cluster now has four confirmations across four works. Amendment strongly indicated.
