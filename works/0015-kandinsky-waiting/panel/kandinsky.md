## Voiced Review

A composition is working when you find yourself in front of it and the formal properties have already begun their effect before you have articulated what you are feeling. This composition works.

The near-miss of the focal point is exact. In *Point and Line to Plane* I described the point as a concentrated tension — not because of its position in the world but because of its formal property of concentration. When you place four lines approaching a point and stop them short, you have made the formal structure of anticipation. The tension is not in any single element; it is in the relationship between the approaching lines and the unreached point. The composition holds this relationship across the entire surface. That is the hard thing.

The color decision is correct and bold. Waiting is monochromatic — it bleaches the world. The composition removes color to encode that bleaching. This is a formal argument made through restriction rather than addition. It takes confidence to remove color in a school where color is a primary element. The confidence is earned here.

I want to push on one element. The asymmetry of the focal point placement (55% horizontal, 48% vertical) is described as creating "slight unease without the dynamism of a strongly off-center position." I agree with the principle but question whether 55%/48% is the right calibration. A placement at 52%/50% — even more nearly centered, but not quite — would create a more excruciating suspension, because the eye would keep correcting toward the center and failing. The slight asymmetry as designed is perceptible; a near-invisible asymmetry would be felt without being noticed, which is more like waiting.

The universality question raised in the brief: I stand behind the claim that these formal properties have consistent perceptual effects. But I acknowledge that the claim is stronger for the structural elements (point creates focal tension; approaching-not-arriving line creates suspension) than for the color conventions (blue = deep/receding). The blue focal point is not universally perceived as gravitational in the way the approaching lines are universally perceived as approaching.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #128 — Compositional incompletion as formal encoding**: The truncated line — a line that approaches a point but stops short — encodes the formal property of suspension more precisely than any complete figure could. The incompletion IS the data. This is distinct from "unfinished work"; it is deliberate formal truncation as an encoding choice. The rubric currently assesses compositional elements as either contributing or failing to contribute to the formal argument; this is a case where an element's incompleteness IS its contribution. Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (abstract-art definition) / Visual Economy.

**Innovation #129 — Color restriction as positive formal argument**: When a composition in a color-active school (abstract art, advocacy visualization) deliberately restricts its palette to near-achromatic tones, the restriction encodes a formal property of the subject (here: the chromatic impoverishment of the waiting state). This is not a failure of color deployment but a formal argument through absence. It should be assessed as a positive School Fidelity choice, not a neutral one. Rubric anchor: School Fidelity / Resonance (Type I).
