## Voiced Review

I am going to be honest about my position rather than simply critical. I am not the right reviewer for this work, and I know it. My framework is built on verifiable correspondence: a chart represents numbers; we check the numbers; the chart is accurate or it is not. In this work, the "numbers" are a psychological state that is unverifiable by external inspection.

The composition is internally consistent. I can see that the approaching lines approach the focal point. I can see that they stop short. I understand, having been told, that this is meant to represent suspension — the formal property of waiting. I cannot verify from the composition alone that I am experiencing waiting rather than longing, or anticipation, or incomplete attention. The formal properties are real; their correspondence to a specific psychological state is asserted, not demonstrable.

This is not a criticism of the school — it is a statement about what the school's accuracy standard can and cannot guarantee. Kandinsky's grammar describes formal properties that have perceptual effects. But psychological states are underdetermined by formal properties: more than one psychological state can share the same formal properties. Waiting and longing are not formally distinguishable in this composition.

I score Data/Story Integrity at 11 — above the "significant issues" band, not in the "high integrity" band — because the composition is honest about what it claims (it does not overclaim certainty) but the accuracy claim cannot be verified by the viewer. That is a genuine limitation, not a moral failure.

The economy is good. The craft is good. The School Fidelity is good. My quarrel is with what "data integrity" means in a school where the data is unverifiable. I am not satisfied with the answer the school gives, and that is my problem as much as the school's.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #132 — Perceptual claim verification failure for psychological states**: In abstract-art compositions, the Data/Story Integrity standard is "internal formal consistency" — the composition must do what its formal logic implies it should do. But when the subject is a specific psychological state rather than a formal property (the subject is "waiting," not "suspension"), the composition must establish correspondence between formal properties and that specific state. This correspondence is underdetermined: multiple distinct psychological states may share the same formal properties. The rubric's abstract-art integrity standard ("internal formal consistency") does not address this underdetermination problem. This is a genuine gap in the rubric's treatment of psychological-state subjects in abstract work. Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (abstract-art definition) — proposed rubric amendment target.
