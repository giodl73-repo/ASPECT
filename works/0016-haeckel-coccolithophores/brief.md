# Brief — 0016 Haeckel Coccolithophores

## Subject

The structural morphology of five coccolithophore species — microscopic marine algae whose calcium carbonate plates (coccoliths) form a crucial part of the global carbon cycle. These organisms absorb CO2 as they calcify and, when they die, sink their calcium carbonate to the ocean floor, sequestering carbon for millions of years. Each species has distinctive plate geometry; the illustration should show enough morphological detail to distinguish them to species level, while conveying the structural variety that makes coccolithophores scientifically significant.

## Five Species

| Species | Plate type | Diagnostic feature |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| *Emiliania huxleyi* | Muroliths (tube cycle + shield cycle) | Slotted distal shield with radiating elements; most abundant coccolithophore on Earth |
| *Coccolithus pelagicus* | Heterococcoliths (placolith) | Large, robust interlocking shields; prominent central opening |
| *Calcidiscus leptoporus* | Placoliths | Smooth, solid shields; no central process; highly symmetric |
| *Gephyrocapsa oceanica* | Muroliths | Prominent bridge element crossing central opening; distinguishes from *E. huxleyi* |
| *Umbilicosphaera sibogae* | Muroliths | Open central area; perforated shields; elegant radial symmetry |

## Scientific Significance

The CO2-absorption function of coccolithophores is scale-dependent: their contribution to the global carbon cycle is a product of their abundance × their calcification rate × their coccolith mass. The calcification rate and coccolith mass are functions of plate morphology — which is precisely what the illustration shows. The illustration IS the scientific argument about why coccolithophore structural morphology matters to climate science.

## Audience

Marine biologists, paleoceanographers, and earth scientists who study the carbon cycle. Expert audience. Reading context: contemplative study (scientific journal plate or monograph). The illustrations will also appear in a public-facing exhibit on ocean carbon cycling, creating a secondary general-public audience at lower visual literacy.

## School

Scientific-illustration (Type A). All core rules apply:
- Accuracy as foundation (taxonomic identification to species level)
- Composition serves communication (multiple views and scales to establish morphological identity)
- Population not individual (each species shown from multiple viewing angles)
- Scale must be declared

## Constraints

- Must show all five species at a scale that shows diagnostic morphological features
- Must include scale bar
- Must show at least two views of each species (top-down + oblique)
- Color: morphologically accurate pigmentation; coccolithophores are white/cream (calcium carbonate); subtle structural coloring is permissible where it serves diagnostic function
- Background: dark (SEM-style imaging convention for coccolithophores)

## Open Questions

1. How to handle the scale problem: *E. huxleyi* plates are ~3-4µm; *C. pelagicus* plates are ~10-15µm. At a uniform scale, *E. huxleyi* becomes too small to show diagnostic features. Options: individual scale bars per species (honoring actual size difference); schematic equalization (same display size, different scale bars — the standard scientific illustration convention for taxonomic comparison).
2. Dark vs. white background: SEM convention uses dark background; traditional botanical/natural history illustration uses white. The SEM convention is more familiar to the scientific audience; the white background is more familiar to the general-public audience.
3. Should the illustration include the whole coccosphere (the spherical cell covered in plates) or focus on individual coccoliths (plates alone)?
