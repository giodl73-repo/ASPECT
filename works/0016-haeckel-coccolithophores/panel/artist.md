## Voiced Review

What these organisms look like is extraordinary. The Umbilicosphaera sibogae — with its open central area and perforated, lace-like shield — is among the most formally beautiful structures I have encountered in scientific illustration, and I have looked at many. The radial symmetry, the negative space of the umbilicus, the perforation pattern: a designer who introduced these elements decoratively would be accused of ornamental excess. But the organism IS this way. The complexity is the form.

This is the accuracy-generated beauty principle (from the scientific illustration school's rubric notes, Innovation #57 from Work 0006) at its clearest. The beauty is generated by fidelity; you cannot separate the aesthetic effect from the scientific accuracy because the accuracy IS the source of the effect. Haeckel understood this. This work understands it.

The dark background is the right choice. On white ground, the cream-white calcium carbonate structures would merge into the background at small features. The dark navy gives them luminosity — they glow rather than lying flat. This is not a stylistic choice; it is a perceptual choice that serves accuracy.

My one request: the Umbilicosphaera sibogae column should be slightly wider than the others, or the views of this species should be slightly larger. Its open, lattice structure has more visual air than the other species; its diagnostic beauty is harder to read at the same visual size as the denser Emiliania huxleyi. This would require declaring an exception to the schematic equalization principle, which creates a tension with the scientific illustration grammar. I flag it as a recommendation without insisting on it.

The pale gold accent is exactly right. Maximum two features per species, on the features that distinguish each species from its nearest relative. It is used so sparingly that it does not feel like added color — it feels like a lit match in a dark room, pointing to the exact feature that matters.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #137 — Differential visual allocation for morphological complexity in comparative plates**: When species in a comparative plate differ substantially in morphological complexity (dense structure vs. open lattice), equal visual allocation (same column width, same display size) may underserve the more complex or more visually distinctive specimen. A declared variation in column width or display size — in proportion to morphological complexity — would give the most structurally distinctive species the space its form requires. This is an extension of schematic equalization (Innovation #136) toward complexity-weighted equalization. Rubric anchor: Visual Economy (H amendment extension) / Legibility.
