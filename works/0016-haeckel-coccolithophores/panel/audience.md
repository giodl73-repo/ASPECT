*Primary audience: marine biologists, paleoceanographers. Secondary audience: general public via museum exhibit.*

## Voiced Review

I am going to speak for two audiences, because the brief specifies both.

As a marine biologist: this plate is immediately useful. I can identify all five species from the top-down views before I read the labels. The pale gold accent on the bridge element in Gephyrocapsa oceanica is exactly the feature I check to distinguish it from Emiliania huxleyi in field samples. The three-view structure (top, oblique, coccosphere) gives me all the angles I need. The scale bars are correct. I would print this and pin it to my lab wall.

As a general-public museum visitor with no knowledge of coccolithophores: I see five columns of glowing white circular objects on a dark background. They are beautiful — the radial patterns are immediately striking. I do not know what I am looking at. The title says "Five Coccolithophore Species: Structural Morphology and Diagnostic Features" and I do not know what a coccolithophore is or why I should care about diagnostic features. The scale bars say "3 microns = 3mm displayed" and I do not have a feeling for what 3 microns means.

The brief mentions a public-facing exhibit. For that context, this plate in its current form would require substantial accompanying text. The caption zone needs: (1) what are coccolithophores; (2) why do they matter for climate; (3) why are there five species rather than one. These are not design failures — they are content gaps that the caption zone should fill, and the design specifies a caption zone but not its content. For the museum audience, the caption zone content is as important as the illustration.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #139 — Caption zone content as legibility determinant for dual-audience plates**: When a scientific illustration plate serves both an expert audience (who can read morphological detail directly) and a general-public audience (who cannot), the legibility for the general audience depends almost entirely on the caption zone content — not the illustration itself. The illustration is domain-gated for the general audience; the caption zone is the access point. This means that in dual-audience scientific illustration, the caption zone should be specified with the same rigor as the illustration, including: subject definition, scientific significance, and reading instruction.
