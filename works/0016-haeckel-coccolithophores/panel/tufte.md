## Voiced Review

This is where scientific illustration and my own principles of visual economy converge rather than conflict. I have criticized decorative excess throughout my career — the chartjunk that substitutes ornament for information. This work contains none. Every mark represents something scientifically necessary. The 30-35 radiating elements in the Emiliania huxleyi top-down view are not decoration; they are the diagnostic information. I cannot remove them without removing the science.

The dark background convention deserves analysis. In my usual framework, background is a structural-attentional element that should earn its place. The dark navy here does earn its place: calcium carbonate plates, which are cream-white, achieve maximum structural contrast against dark ground rather than white. The SEM imaging convention is familiar to the scientific audience and signals the imaging tradition this illustration references. The functional justification is complete; I do not penalize it.

The pale gold diagnostic accent is the one element I would scrutinize. It introduces a color variable into an otherwise near-achromatic plate. Bertin has already evaluated it by his retinal efficiency criterion — the accent is selective and directs attention to the distinguishing feature. I agree with the functional justification. My concern is narrower: the accent must not make any feature appear more prominent than its actual structural importance. If the bridge element in Gephyrocapsa oceanica is accented in pale gold while the equally important shield structure is not, the accent has distorted the visual hierarchy. The design specifies "one or two features per species, not decorative" — this is the right constraint, but the execution must be precise.

The schematic equalization choice — all five species shown at comparable visual size with individual scale bars — is the correct scientific illustration convention for taxonomic comparison. A viewer who sees all five species at the same visual scale can compare morphological complexity directly; the scale bars then tell them the actual size relationship. This is more informative than showing all species at true relative scale (where the tiny Emiliania huxleyi would be invisible beside the much larger Coccolithus pelagicus).

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #136 — Schematic equalization as taxonomic comparison tool**: Displaying multiple organisms at schematic equal visual size (with individual scale bars declaring actual size) enables direct morphological comparison that true-scale display cannot achieve when organisms differ substantially in size. This is the scientific-illustration parallel to Beck's central expansion: distort scale to achieve legibility while declaring the distortion. Under rubric H, schematic equalization is a form of intrinsic-complexity management — it is required for this subject and should not be penalized.
