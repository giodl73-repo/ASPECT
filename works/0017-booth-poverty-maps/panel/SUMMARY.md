## Key Agreements

**The gestalt argument is unambiguously strong.** All five reviewers agree: the east/west poverty gradient is immediately visible without labels. The map makes its core argument spatially before any text is read. This is among the strongest gestalt resonances in the project.

**Visual economy is excellent.** All reviewers score 13-14/15. Every street segment is a data mark. No decorative excess. The data-geographic synthesis achieves near-perfect economy through the shared visual element (color on street = both geographic identifier and social data).

**The category label problem is the panel's primary concern.** All reviewers flag it under Data/Story Integrity. Bertin identifies variable overloading; Tufte identifies categorical confidence suppression; Du Bois identifies causal inversion; the Historian identifies analytical framework decay. Consensus: the category labels mix empirical economic description with moral judgment, presented with the visual authority of objective classification.

---

## Key Tensions

**How much does the category label problem cost the score?**

Reviewers disagree on magnitude. The Historian (13/20) treats the labels as period-appropriate framework — flawed but not an error by Booth's standards. Tufte and Bertin (12/20) treat it as a serious analytical framework failure regardless of period. Du Bois (11/20) adds that the spatial distribution of moralized categories creates a causal inversion: the map implies structural moral failure where the research argues structural economic causation. Panel consensus lands closer to the Tufte/Bertin position.

**Is this a Type C synthesis that earns the bonus?**

The synthesis is real and functional: cartography and social demography share the color variable without conflict, declared in adjacent documentation. However, the cross-school tension — geographic precision vs. categorical imprecision — is unresolved within the map. Synthesis bonus: +1 (not +2).

**Du Bois vs. the rest on Resonance:**

Du Bois scores resonance 11/15 where others score 12-13/15. His position: the advocacy resonance is double-edged — the map supported reform while spatially legitimating moral condemnation of the poor. His reading adds analytical precision: the resonance worked differently for reformers vs. the mapped population.

## Final Assessment

The Booth Poverty Maps are a genuine landmark: the first systematic street-level social survey of a major city, rendered spatially with cartographic precision. The spatial argument — poverty is structurally geographic, not randomly distributed — is one of the most important arguments visualization has ever made.

The work's primary failure is embedded in its success: the category labels that make the spatial argument legible simultaneously assert a moral taxonomy that the research itself contradicts. The maps prove structural poverty causation while labeling the poor as "vicious, semi-criminal." This is the most significant Data/Story Integrity compound failure in the project — not because the data is wrong, but because the categories are ontologically contaminated.
