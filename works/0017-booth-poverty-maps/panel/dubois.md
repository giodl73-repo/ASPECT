# Panel Review — Du Bois on 0017: Booth Poverty Maps

## Opening

I am the appropriate person to examine this map, and not only because I am a sociologist who has worked with poverty data. I am the appropriate person because I have been on the other side of this kind of classification.

In 1900, I brought charts to Paris to show what Black Americans had achieved in the face of conditions not unlike those Booth mapped in London. I was classified, as my community was classified, by observers who applied their own frameworks to our lives. I know what it costs to be encoded by someone else's categories.

Let me be precise: Booth's maps are the most rigorous poverty survey of the Victorian period. The data collection was extraordinary. The spatial argument is genuine and important. And the category "Lowest class, vicious, semi-criminal" is one of the most consequential misrepresentations in the history of social cartography.

These are both true. The rubric must handle both.

## 2. Data/Story Integrity /20 — Score: 11

I want to say something precise about the category label problem that my colleagues have raised.

"Vicious, semi-criminal" is not an economic measurement. It is not an income range. It is a characterization of moral character applied to an entire population — applied, moreover, by informants (police constables, clergy) who had structural reasons to characterize the poor as criminal. Police constables encounter the poor at their worst moments. Clergy encounter them as supplicants. Neither population is neutral.

The visualization encodes this judgment as if it were equivalent in kind to "18s to 21s a week for a moderate family." It is not. One is an economic measurement; the other is a moral verdict.

But here is what I want to add to what Bertin and Tufte have said: the consequence of this encoding is that the map makes the spatial distribution of moral failure visible. A reader sees that black (vicious, criminal) is concentrated in the East End. The map argues — unintentionally, through the grammar of spatial distribution — that moral failure is spatially clustered. This is a causal implication from juxtaposition (Innovation #7): if criminality is concentrated here, the implicit inference is that there is something about this place that produces criminality.

Booth's argument, in his text, is the reverse: poverty is concentrated here because of structural forces, not moral failure. The map, with its labels, implies the opposite. The text and the map make opposite arguments.

This is a Data/Story Integrity failure of the first order: the story the visualization tells is the inverse of the story the underlying research supports.

**Step C adjustment:** −2 (causal implication from spatial distribution contradicts the work's own analytical conclusions — the most serious form of this failure, though it operates through the category labels rather than through the encoding directly)

**Combined adjustments:** −1 (A) −1 (B) −2 (C) −3 (D — category ontological mixing, Level 4) = −7 from base of 18.

**Score: 11/20**

## 3. Legibility /15 — Score: 11

For a 2026 audience encountering this map without context, "vicious, semi-criminal" does not read as a social science category. It reads as an accusation. The legibility of the category system has undergone semantic decay that is not recoverable without historical explanation.

**Score: 11/15** [contemporary: 8/15 — the category labels now require substantial contextualization]

## 5. Resonance /15 — Score: 11

The gestalt resonance of the spatial argument is genuine — the east/west divide is visible at a glance and it is a true argument. I respect that.

But the resonance for the populations encoded in black is something else entirely. Those streets, those communities, are permanently marked on a published official-looking map as "vicious, semi-criminal." That marking had real consequences. The map was used in reform campaigns, but reform campaigns that categorized the poor as semi-criminal did not necessarily reform in the direction the poor would have chosen.

I give this work credit for the spatial argument (Type IV gestalt is achieved) and I note that the advocacy resonance (Type III) is historically demonstrated. But I must also note that the advocacy was double-edged: the same map that argued for housing reform also argued, through its labels, for the moral inferiority of those who needed housing.

**Score: 11/15**

## Innovation Flagged

**Innovation: Visual inversion of the text-map argument** — Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step C). When a visualization's category labels imply a causal argument through spatial distribution that contradicts the analytical conclusions of the accompanying research, the visualization and the text make opposite arguments. This is distinct from standard Step C failures (causal claim overclaim or contradiction): the visualization's labels do not claim causation directly — they produce causal implication through spatial distribution combined with moralized category names. The rubric should recognize this as a compound failure: Step C (causal implication from spatial distribution) compounded with Step D (ontologically contaminated category labels) producing an argument that inverts the researcher's own conclusions.
