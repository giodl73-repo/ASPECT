# Panel Review — Historian on 0017: Booth Poverty Maps

## School Fidelity and Historical Context

### Cartographic tradition

Booth's maps emerge from a specific Victorian tradition: the thematic map, which had been developing since the 1820s. The key predecessors are:

- **André Michel Guerry's** crime maps of France (1829) — the first systematic spatial mapping of social phenomena
- **Adolphe Quetelet's** moral statistics work (1835) — establishing that crime and poverty have statistical regularities
- **Henry Mayhew's** London Labour and the London Poor (1849-1862) — precursor survey, though without systematic cartography

Booth knew this tradition. His maps are the most rigorous execution of the social mapping program that Guerry initiated. From the cartographic school's perspective, the work is a genuine and important instance of the thematic cartography tradition applied at street level.

### The category system in historical context

The panel has spent considerable energy on the category labels, particularly "Lowest class, vicious, semi-criminal." I want to provide historical context without excusing the problem.

Victorian social science operated with a taxonomy of the poor that was essentially moral. The distinction between the "deserving poor" (honest workers who had fallen on hard times) and the "undeserving poor" (habitual criminals, drunkards, the congenitally feckless) was not fringe Victorian thought — it was the mainstream analytical framework of the entire social reform tradition, from the Charity Organization Society to the Settlement House movement. Booth was working within this framework, not inventing it.

This context does not make the framework correct. Du Bois is right that the label encodes a moral judgment as a factual classification. But it means that Booth's failure is a failure of the period's analytical framework, not an idiosyncratic choice. Every social survey of the Victorian period used variants of this taxonomy. The Booth maps are the clearest spatial rendering of what that taxonomy meant when applied to actual streets.

The historical assessment: Booth's maps correctly represent the Victorian social science tradition's analytical framework. They also perfectly illustrate why that framework was wrong — and the evidence is in the maps themselves, which show that "vicious, semi-criminal" populations cluster in areas of greatest economic deprivation, suggesting structural causes that the moral taxonomy cannot accommodate.

### School Fidelity assessment

**Score: 16/20** — Strong instance of the thematic cartography tradition; the category labels are period-appropriate but analytically contested. School fidelity should not penalize Booth for working within his period's analytical framework, even when that framework is now understood as flawed.

## Innovation Flagged

**Innovation: Period-analytical-framework decay** — Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Step D) / Legibility (context decay). The Booth category labels are analytically contested in 2026 but were analytically mainstream in 1889. This creates a specific form of context decay that affects not just the reading context (how audiences encounter the work) but the analytical framework itself (whether the categories are legitimate). The rubric currently handles context decay in Legibility but has no vocabulary for analytical-framework decay — when the intellectual framework the visualization embeds was valid in the primary period but is now understood as flawed or contested. This is distinct from errors of the period (Snow's pre-germ-theory causal claim) because there the framework was right but unvalidated; here the framework is wrong in ways that could have been contested even then.
