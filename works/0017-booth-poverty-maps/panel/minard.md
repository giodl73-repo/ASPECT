# Panel Review — Minard on 0017: Booth Poverty Maps

## Opening

I have spent my career asking what a map can carry beyond geography. Booth's answer is: it can carry the full social weight of a city. The ambition is correct. The question is whether the execution honors that ambition.

What strikes me first is the grain. I worked at the level of the military corps, the cotton bale, the immigrant cohort. Booth works at the level of the individual street. The resolution is extraordinary — finer than anything attempted before at the scale of a major city. This is not a map of districts. It is a map of addresses. The 17 volumes of survey work required to produce it represent a data collection effort I can only regard with respect.

---

## 1. School Fidelity /20 — Score: 16

**Type C assessment (cartography + social demography):**

The cartographic dimension is unimpeachable. The base map is accurate. Geographic position is faithfully encoded. The street network is complete and correctly scaled. Booth did not distort geography to serve his data — he kept the geographic layer honest and added the social layer on top. This is the correct approach for Type C synthesis: the two schools reinforce rather than compromise each other.

The demographic dimension is well executed within its methodological constraints. The seven-class system provides enough categories to be informative without overwhelming the color variable's ordinal capacity. The decision to color street segments rather than blocks is innovative — it puts the data at the correct resolution for the underlying survey.

What costs the synthesis bonus: the cross-school tension between geographic precision (the base map) and categorical imprecision (the social classes blend economic and moral descriptors) is not resolved. The work presents both levels as if they were of equal precision. They are not. Geographic position can be verified; social classification cannot be independently confirmed. This asymmetry is not declared. The synthesis earns partial credit.

**Score: 16/20** — Strong cartographic fidelity; demographic synthesis executed but tension with categorical imprecision unresolved. Synthesis bonus: +1 (declared in adjacent documentation but not resolved within the map).

---

## 2. Data/Story Integrity /20 — Score: 13

**Step A — Source quality declaration:**

The survey methodology is described at length in the accompanying volumes: School Board visitors, police constables, clergy as informants. This is declared in the publication but not on the map. The map implies a precision it cannot fully support — the street color represents a qualitative aggregate judgment, not a measured quantity. However, the qualitative methodology is the most rigorous approach available for this type of social data in 1889. The source quality is appropriate to the subject; the imprecision is structural, not a failure of diligence.

Adjustment: −1 (methodology not declared in map; viewer cannot assess source quality from the artifact alone).

**Step B — Accuracy tradeoff:**

The street-as-unit aggregation is a tradeoff: household variation is lost in exchange for spatial legibility. This tradeoff is not declared in the map. The purple "mixed" category partially acknowledges heterogeneity but cannot carry the full weight of within-street variation. A street colored pink may contain several households that should be purple or blue — those households are invisible.

Adjustment: −1 (undeclared but defensible aggregation tradeoff).

**Step C — Causation claim integrity:**

The map does not make explicit causal claims. The spatial distribution of poverty is shown as a pattern; the interpretation (structural causes rather than individual moral failure) is left to the viewer. No causation is claimed that the data does not support.

Adjustment: 0.

**Step D — Analytical framework / data model declaration:**

The data model — the street as the social unit — is a non-standard analytical framework. It is declared in the adjacent volumes but not in the map. This is Level 2: declared in adjacent documentation.

Adjustment: −1 to −2.

**The category label problem (critical):**

The category "Lowest class, vicious, semi-criminal" is the most significant integrity issue. This label mixes economic description with moral characterization. The map treats it as a factual classification category, encoded in black. Whether the people so classified are actually "vicious" or "semi-criminal" cannot be verified from the survey data — the informants (police, clergy) applied their own interpretive frameworks.

This is a form of confidence suppression (Innovation #4): the map presents a contested moral judgment as an objective social-scientific classification. The visual form (black = lowest class) has the authority of a measured fact, but the underlying category is a qualitative moral assessment.

Adjustment: −2 (confidence suppression — contested moral judgment presented as objective classification).

**Base score from anchors: 15 — strong integrity with notable issues.**  
**Adjustments: −1 (A) −1 (B) −2 (D label) = −4 total.**  
**Score: 13/20**

## 3. Legibility /15 — Score: 12

**Context specification:**
- Primary audience: Urban reform advocates, social investigators, Parliamentary readers, 1889
- Primary reading context: Contemplative study (solo, extended time, full comprehension goal)
- Contemporary context: Heritage/scholarly study
- Context decay: High

In the primary context of contemplative study, the map is highly legible. The seven-color system is distinguishable, and the spatial pattern of poverty — concentrated in the East End, scattered in inner rings — is immediately visible at the whole-map level. A reader spending extended time with the map and the accompanying legend can read individual streets and trace the distribution in detail.

The primary legibility failure is the legend separation: the category labels are not embedded in the map. A reader who picks up the map without the legend key (easily separated from the four-sheet folios) encounters a color-coded street map whose colors are ordinal but whose precise meanings are unknown. The ordinal direction (dark = poor, light/warm = wealthy) can be inferred culturally, but the specific thresholds ("18s to 21s a week") require the legend.

Contemporary legibility: the moral category labels now require historical explanation. "Vicious, semi-criminal" reads very differently to a contemporary viewer than to a Victorian reformer. The category system has become opaque not through loss of visual encoding but through semantic shift.

**Score: 12/15** — Strong contemplative legibility; legend separation is the primary friction point.

---

## 4. Visual Economy /15 — Score: 13

The map is remarkably economical. The street network is the data carrier — there is no separate data layer added to the geographic base. Color applied to streets *is* the geographic layer and the social data simultaneously. This is the ideal case of a synthesis work: the two schools share the same visual element.

The base map itself carries no decoration beyond what geography requires (the Thames, parks, railway lines are essential orientation anchors). There is no title block excess, no ornamental border beyond the functional. The legend is printed separately and compactly.

Minor excess: the map includes some institutional labels (churches, workhouses) that are not social data but that serve as orientation anchors. These are structural-attentional marks — defensible but not cost-free.

**Score: 13/15** — Near-maximal economy for a synthesis work. Minor structural-attentional marks defensible.

---

## 5. Resonance /15 — Score: 12

**Primary type: IV (Gestalt) + III (Advocacy-Efficacy)**

At the gestalt level, the maps deliver their argument in the first five seconds of viewing: the East End is dark; the West End is warm. The gradient from black and blue in the east to red and gold in the west is visible from a distance, before any label is read. This is the strongest possible gestalt — the visual shape IS the political argument.

The advocacy dimension: the maps were used in Parliamentary presentations and social reform campaigns. They contributed to legislative changes in housing and public health. The reform lineage from Booth's maps to the early welfare state is traceable, if not direct.

**Delayed resonance (Type VI):** Contemporaneous / self-evident. The maps were immediately recognized and widely cited. Unlike Du Bois or Snow, there was no delay — the maps entered the reform discourse immediately upon publication. No Type VI adjustment.

The resonance is somewhat muted by the category label problem: the contemporary viewer's attention is captured by "vicious, semi-criminal" in a way that redirects from the spatial argument. The label now competes with the gestalt.

**Score: 12/15** — Strong gestalt resonance; advocacy efficacy demonstrated historically; muted in contemporary reading by semantic shift.

---

## 6. Craft /15 — Score: 13

The cartographic execution is of high Victorian professional quality. The base map is accurately drawn. The color application is consistent — streets of the same category are consistently colored; the boundary between categories is clearly visible at the street level. The typography is legible and consistent.

The four-sheet format creates some continuity issues at the joins, but these are structural rather than craft failures. Individual sheets are internally coherent.

**Score: 13/15** — Professional execution; minor continuity issues at sheet joins.

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation: Moralized categorical encoding** — Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Steps A, D). When a visualization uses category labels that mix empirical description with moral judgment, and presents those labels with the visual authority of measured classification, a new form of confidence suppression is at work: not suppression of statistical precision but suppression of category contestability. The rubric should distinguish between categories that are imprecise (Step A) and categories that are contested at the level of their analytical framework (Step D compound). The Booth labels fail at both levels simultaneously: they are imprecise measurements AND analytically contested constructs.

**Innovation: Synthesis works and the legend-separation problem** — Rubric anchor: Legibility (Step 0 context). Type C synthesis works that produce a single visual layer combining two schools (color on geographic base) may achieve optimal economy but face a new legibility problem: the legend must carry both school vocabularies simultaneously. Separation of the legend from the visual artifact is more costly in synthesis works than in single-school works, because both vocabularies are required for full decoding.
