# Panel Review — Tufte on 0017: Booth Poverty Maps

## Opening

Let me say what this work gets right before I say what it gets wrong. What it gets right is fundamental: it is a map in which every mark does work. The street colored black is a data mark. The street colored gold is a data mark. There is no decoration. There is no chartjunk. There is no border that does not border something. The data density — the quantity of social information per square inch of printed surface — is extraordinary. Booth surveyed every street in inner London and put every street on the map. That is the correct decision.

Now the problem.

---

## 1. School Fidelity /20 — Score: 16

The cartographic tradition is correctly deployed. The geographic base is accurate and subordinated to the data layer. The synthesis with social classification is ambitious and mostly executed. The street-level grain is the right call — coarser aggregation would have produced a district map that hides the fine structure of poverty's distribution.

Type C synthesis: the two schools do not fight each other in this map. That is an accomplishment.

**Score: 16/20**

---

## 2. Data/Story Integrity /20 — Score: 12

Here is the problem that most critics of this map miss. The problem is not the color scheme. The problem is the category labels, and specifically what those labels claim to measure.

"Lowest class, vicious, semi-criminal."

This label claims to encode a social-scientific classification. It does not. It encodes a Victorian moral taxonomy applied to poor people by middle-class informants — police constables and clergy — who had their own interests in characterizing the poor as criminal. The visualization presents this taxonomy with the visual authority of measured fact. This is confidence suppression (Innovation #4) applied not to imprecise numbers but to a contested analytical framework.

I am also troubled by the accuracy tradeoff of street-as-unit aggregation. A street that contains households ranging from destitution to comfort is colored as a single category. The household variation is invisible. This is a form of oversmoothing that conceals the fine structure the survey was designed to reveal.

That said: Booth was doing something genuinely difficult. Measuring poverty at the household level in 1889 London was a methodological challenge with no solved answers. The street-level aggregation was the best available approach, and it was the most rigorous attempt made to that point. The integrity problems are real but they are not failures of diligence — they are failures of methodological declaration.

**Step A:** −1 (source quality declared in adjacent volumes, not in map)  
**Step B:** −1 (street aggregation undeclared but defensible)  
**Step C:** 0 (no explicit causal claim)  
**Step D:** −2 (category labels mix empirical and moral constructs; non-obvious without Victorian context; Level 3-4)  
**Category label moral/empirical mix:** Additional −2 (confidence suppression)

**Score: 12/20**

---

## 3. Legibility /15 — Score: 13

For the contemplative-study audience — urban reformers, investigators, parliamentarians reading carefully with the legend available — this is a highly legible work. The spatial pattern is clear. The seven colors are distinguishable. The fine street-level grain rewards sustained reading.

The primary legibility friction is the legend separation. The map's meaning depends entirely on the legend, which is a separate document. This is a structural problem.

Contemporary legibility: the category labels now require historical unpacking. A contemporary reader encountering "vicious, semi-criminal" will spend cognitive effort on that label rather than on the spatial argument. The semantic shift is context decay — not a design failure by Booth — but it is real and worth noting.

**Score: 13/15** [contemporary: 10/15]

---

## 4. Visual Economy /15 — Score: 14

Near-perfect data-ink ratio. Every colored street segment is a data mark. The base map provides necessary geographic orientation. Nothing decorative is present. This is the work's strongest dimension from my standpoint.

Minor deduction: the map includes some non-data geographic features (the Thames is rendered as a white band — technically a non-data element, though necessary for orientation) and institutional building labels that are structural rather than data marks.

**Score: 14/15**

---

## 5. Resonance /15 — Score: 12

The gestalt resonance is genuine and powerful. The dark east, the warm west — this is one of the clearest gestalt arguments in cartographic history. A viewer seeing the full four-sheet map from a distance, before reading any label, receives the core argument: London is divided, and the division is spatial.

The advocacy resonance is historically demonstrated. The maps contributed to reform discourse and were cited repeatedly in Parliamentary debates.

**Score: 12/15**

---

## 6. Craft /15 — Score: 13

Professional Victorian cartographic execution. Consistent color application, accurate base, legible typography. The four-sheet format is well managed.

**Score: 13/15**

## Innovation Flagged

**Innovation: Confidence suppression of categorical validity** — Rubric anchor: Data/Story Integrity (Steps A, D). Innovation #4 (confidence suppression) was defined as presenting uncertain numeric data with the visual confidence of precise measurement. The Booth maps introduce a related but distinct failure: presenting a contested analytical category — one that mixes empirical description with moral judgment — with the visual authority of an objective classification. This is not numeric imprecision; it is ontological imprecision at the category level. The rubric should recognize "categorical confidence suppression" as distinct from "measurement confidence suppression."
