# Brief — 0018: Euclid's Elements Geometric Diagrams (~300 BCE)

## Work Identity

**Title:** Geometric diagrams for Propositions I.1–I.5 of the Elements  
**Author:** Euclid of Alexandria (~325–265 BCE)  
**Date:** ~300 BCE  
**Form:** Geometric diagrams accompanying formal proof propositions  
**School:** mathematical-proof-visualization (Type B, School Founder — NEW SCHOOL)  
**Publication context:** *Stoicheia* (Elements), the foundational text of Greek mathematics; transmitted through manuscript copies, then print from 1482 (Ratdolt edition)

## What It Shows

The five diagrams reviewed are:

| Proposition | Claim | Diagram |
|------------|-------|---------|
| I.1 | On a given finite straight line, construct an equilateral triangle | Two intersecting circles with the intersection point marking the triangle apex |
| I.2 | To place at a given point (as an extremity) a straight line equal to a given straight line | Complex multi-step construction showing auxiliary circles and transferred lengths |
| I.3 | To cut off from the greater of two given unequal straight lines a straight line equal to the less | Compass arc construction for equal-length transfer |
| I.4 | If two triangles have two sides equal to two sides respectively, and have the angles contained by the equal sides equal, they will also have the base equal, and the triangle will equal the triangle | Two triangles shown side by side, labels marking equal elements |
| I.5 | In isosceles triangles the angles at the base are equal to one another | Extended isosceles triangle with auxiliary construction lines |

## Critical Distinction: Demonstrative Encoding

These diagrams do not report measured data. They do not depict an observed object. They demonstrate proofs by geometric construction.

The claim of Proposition I.1 is not "this triangle exists and is equilateral." The claim is: "given any finite straight line, a procedure exists by which an equilateral triangle can be constructed." The diagram shows the procedure. The viewer verifies the claim by following the construction and confirming that each step is valid by geometric principle.

This is **demonstrative encoding** — a fundamentally different epistemic relationship between diagram and claim than the data-reporting relationship assumed by all other works in this project. In demonstrative encoding:

- The diagram does not represent a measured reality
- The diagram IS the argument (not evidence for the argument)
- "Accuracy" means logical validity, not correspondence to measured fact
- The viewer does not check the diagram against external reality — the viewer checks the reasoning

This is **Cluster I (demonstrative encoding)** — the primary theoretical motivation for reviewing this work. Works 0008 (Kandinsky) and 0018 (Euclid) together should confirm whether the rubric's assumptions about what "accuracy" means require structural amendment for non-data-reporting visualizations.

## What "School Fidelity" Means for a 2,300-Year-Old School

Euclid's diagrams did not merely found a school — they founded one that has been practiced continuously for 2,300 years. The grammar is:

1. **Proposition** — a claim stated in natural language
2. **Construction** — a step-by-step procedure using only compass and straight-edge
3. **Diagram** — a visual representation of the completed construction, with labeled elements
4. **Proof** — logical derivation from axioms and prior propositions, referring to the diagram's labeled elements

The diagram's role is specific: it provides spatial reference for the proof's logical steps. The letters (A, B, C, Γ, Δ, Ε) label points, lines, and angles that the proof text refers to. The diagram does not prove anything by itself — it anchors the proof to visible space.

School fidelity for work 0018 means: does the diagram correctly serve the proof? Do the labeled elements correspond to what the proof text claims? Is the construction shown accurately (each step correct)?
