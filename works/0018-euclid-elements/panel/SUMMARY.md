## Key Agreements

**Perfect visual economy, by structural necessity.** All five reviewers score Visual Economy 15/15 — the maximum. This is not a close call. The Euclidean proof diagram grammar structurally prohibits excess marks. Every mark is logically necessary. If it is not logically necessary, it is a proof error. This is the strongest economy score in the project, and the only case where the score is not the result of the designer's discipline but of the form's logical requirements.

**Demonstrative encoding is real and requires rubric amendment.** All five reviewers independently reach the same conclusion through different analytical approaches: Kandinsky through compositional grammar, Bertin through variable analysis, Tufte through the data-ink ratio generalization, the Historian through Greek epistemic terminology (apodeixis vs. ekthesis), and the Artist through the aesthetic of necessity. The convergence is remarkable.

**School Fidelity at the high end.** Scores range 17-19/20. All reviewers agree this is the most coherent and stable founding grammar in the project. The 2,300-year continuous transmission is itself evidence of the grammar's robustness.

---

## Key Tensions

**Legibility is the primary weakness.** All reviewers score 11-12/15 for primary context. The core problem: no visual distinction between construction elements (tools) and figure elements (the proved result). This is a structural limitation of the grammar, not an execution failure. The grammar does not provide a convention for distinguishing tool-marks from result-marks. Later pedagogical traditions added such conventions (dashed construction lines, bold final figure), but these are not canonical Euclidean.

**Tufte's I.4 superposition deduction vs. others' smaller deduction:**
Tufte scores Data/Story Integrity 15/20 (noting both I.1 circle-intersection diagram-dependence and I.4 superposition gap). Others score 16-17/20 (noting only I.1). The Historian's score of 16 acknowledges the superposition issue. Kandinsky's 17 does not explicitly engage it. Panel consensus: the superposition issue in I.4 is a real logical gap that reduces the perfect-integrity claim, but does not dramatically change the score given the work's demonstrative-encoding context.

**Historian's elevated School Fidelity (19/20):**
The Historian argues that assessing a 2,300-year-old school against its own mature form justifies a higher school fidelity score — the founding act is confirmed by the mature tradition it enabled. Other reviewers score 17-18/20. This represents a genuine interpretive difference about what "school fidelity" means for founding works assessed against their mature form.

## Cluster I — RATIFIED

**Status: THRESHOLD MET AND CONFIRMED**

Works 0008 (Kandinsky, Point and Line to Plane) + 0018 (Euclid, Elements I.1-I.5) confirm demonstrative encoding across two distinct domains. All five reviewers in 0018 confirm explicitly. The amendment to Data/Story Integrity should be drafted as follows:

**Proposed amendment text:**

Before applying standard sub-steps A-D, assess whether the work uses demonstrative encoding:

> *Demonstrative encoding:* the visualization's primary epistemic function is demonstration — the diagram instantiates a principle or procedure that the viewer validates by perception or reasoning, with no external data reality to which the diagram corresponds. The diagram IS the argument, not evidence for the argument.

If demonstrative encoding applies, replace Steps A-D with:

| Step | Demonstrative equivalent | Criterion |
|------|--------------------------|-----------|
| A-demo | Logical foundation quality | Are the axioms/principles the demonstration rests on sound and declared? |
| B-demo | Demonstration completeness | Are all necessary steps of the construction/demonstration present? |
| C-demo | Logical validity | Is each step logically valid? Does the demonstration assume any conclusion from the diagram that the logic should establish? |
| D-demo | Diagrammatic framework | Does the diagram correctly instantiate the geometric/logical framework it claims? |

Works in which demonstrative encoding applies: 0008 (Kandinsky diagrams in Point and Line to Plane), 0018 (Euclid's Elements). Future works should be assessed for this property before applying standard sub-steps.

## Final Assessment

Euclid's Elements geometric diagrams are the oldest work in this project and, in several dimensions, the strongest. They establish the most minimal and logically rigorous visual grammar in visualization history. They are the primary evidence for Cluster I — demonstrative encoding — which has now been confirmed and should be ratified as a rubric amendment.

The work's weakness is precisely its historical context: the accompanied-by-teacher reading context that made the diagrams fully legible to their primary audience is gone. Without that context, the grammar's failure to distinguish construction tools from proved results becomes a significant legibility barrier.

**Weighted score: 86.8 / 100** — Above the 79-84 target range, driven by near-perfect economy and school fidelity, and by the Historian's elevated assessments.
