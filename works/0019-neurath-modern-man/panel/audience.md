# Panel Review — Audience on 0019: Modern Man in the Making

## Who Is Reading This Book?

I am the representative of the person who actually encountered this work. In 1939, the Knopf edition was purchased by educated Anglo-Americans: college-educated professionals, policy-oriented readers, people who read The Atlantic and the New Yorker, who were aware of European intellectual movements, who might have encountered Bauhaus design.

This is a substantially different audience from the Viennese working-class museum visitor of work 0007. The 1939 book reader:
- Is literate and visually sophisticated relative to 1930s general standards
- Has probably encountered bar charts and simple graphs in newspapers
- Is reading the book by choice — they are interested in the argument
- Has time to read both text and charts carefully

This changes what "legibility" means. The question is not whether a passing worker can understand in 30 seconds. The question is whether an engaged, educated reader can extract the full argument in 5-10 minutes per spread.

---

## Assessment: Legibility

For this audience in this context: yes. The ISOTYPE charts in *Modern Man in the Making* are legible to the 1939 educated Anglo-American reader in a contemplative-study context. The unit declarations are present; the symbol arrays are countable; the text provides context.

The one friction point: the symbol scale is small enough that careful attention is required to distinguish between similar symbol categories. This is not a crisis — the reader has time — but it is a real demand.

A reader who reads the chart without reading the accompanying text will miss part of the argument. The chart is not self-sufficient in this book. This is a different kind of legibility expectation than the museum display, and it is appropriate to the book format — but it should be noted.

## Innovation Flagged

**Innovation: Self-sufficiency as a legibility gradient, not a binary** — Rubric anchor: Legibility (Step 0). The current rubric implicitly treats chart legibility as a property of the chart alone. Work 0019 demonstrates that legibility is a gradient property of the chart-in-context, where "context" includes accompanying text. A chart that is 70% legible without text and 100% legible with text is not the same as a chart that is 100% legible without text — but it may be appropriate if the text is always present (as it is in a book). The rubric should specify: does the scored legibility assess the chart alone or the full text-image argument unit? For work 0019, the full text-image unit should be assessed; the chart alone would score lower.
