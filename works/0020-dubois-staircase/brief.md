# Brief — 0020: Du Bois "Staircase" Spiral Chart (1900)

## Work Identity

**Title:** "Negro landholders of Georgia" — staircase-spiral property ownership chart  
**Author:** W.E.B. Du Bois (1868-1963) and Atlanta University students  
**Date:** 1900  
**Form:** Single invented staircase-spiral form; hand-drawn and hand-lettered; two-color  
**School:** advocacy-visualization (Type B — contributing to founding)  
**Exhibition context:** Paris Exposition Universelle, 1900, "American Negro" exhibit

## What It Shows

A single chart from the Paris Exposition suite showing the increase in African American property ownership in Georgia from 1875 to 1899. This chart is specifically the "staircase spiral" — Du Bois's most formally inventive work in the suite.

**The form:** A staircase-spiral where each step represents a five-year period (1875, 1880, 1885, 1890, 1895, 1899) and the width of the step represents the value of property owned by Black Georgians. The staircase ascends from the inner lower left to the outer upper right, with each wider step indicating greater total property value.

**The data:**
| Period | Property Value |
|--------|---------------|
| 1875 | $1,173,624 |
| 1880 | $1,410,683 |
| 1885 | $1,979,560 |
| 1890 | $3,346,380 |
| 1895 | $3,822,950 |
| 1899 | $4,165,560 |

The argument: a consistent increase in property ownership over 24 years, despite conditions of systematic legal, social, and economic discrimination. The property value in 1899 represents a 3.5x increase from 1875.

## Why This Work is Distinct from 0004

Work 0004 reviewed the Paris Exposition suite as a whole — 60 charts representing the full range of Du Bois's statistical argument. That review assessed the suite's collective approach: the grammar, the advocacy strategy, the mix of conventional and invented forms.

Work 0020 isolates the staircase-spiral as a single invented form for individual analysis. This is important because:

1. **Invented form vs. suite grammar:** The suite (0004) includes conventional bar charts, line graphs, and maps alongside the invented forms. The staircase-spiral is the most formally unusual piece in the suite. Reviewing it alone removes the conventional anchor.

2. **Legibility without context:** The suite as a whole provides visual context — the viewer sees that Du Bois uses conventional forms for some arguments and invented forms for others. Isolated, the staircase-spiral has no such context. Its legibility as a standalone artifact is likely worse than its legibility within the suite.

3. **The invented form question:** The staircase-spiral does not exist in any prior visualization tradition. Du Bois invented it for this data and this argument. What does School Fidelity mean for a Type B founding work where the specific form is novel even within the founder's own school?

## The Staircase-Spiral Form

**What makes it a staircase, not just a spiral:**
A conventional spiral encodes quantity through radial distance — the spiral arm grows thicker or the radius increases. Du Bois's form is specifically a staircase: each step has a flat top (the step tread) and a vertical riser. The step tread represents the time period; the vertical riser represents the increase in property value from the previous period.

**Why this matters:**
The staircase form encodes:
- **Temporal sequence:** the steps proceed left-to-right and inward-to-outward; temporal direction is clear
- **Cumulative magnitude:** the width of each step represents total property value (not increase from previous period)
- **Growth rate:** the height of each riser represents the increase in value — larger risers indicate faster growth

The staircase encodes three variables simultaneously: time (step sequence), cumulative value (step width), and growth rate (riser height). This is dimensional layering in the Minard tradition applied to a non-geographic form.
