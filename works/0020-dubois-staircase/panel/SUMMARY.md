## Key Agreements

**Data integrity is the unanimous strongest dimension.** All five reviewers score 17/20. The property values are sourced from Georgia state records; the precision-as-defiance strategy requires absolute data integrity; Du Bois could not afford errors. The nominal-dollar presentation (which understates real growth) is conservative rather than misleading.

**Craft is exceptional.** 14-15/15 unanimously. Hand-drawn geometric precision in 1900 under constrained conditions is remarkable. The Artist gives 15/15; others give 14/15 (allowing for the hand-drawn nature of the work and acknowledging the extraordinary achievement within that constraint).

**Legibility is the primary weakness.** 9-11/15 range. The novel form imposes a form-learning tax on the viewer. The gestalt argument (property grew) is available in five seconds; the full encoding (width = cumulative value, riser = period increase) requires sustained attention and inference. In a public-display context, this is a significant limitation.

**The delayed resonance is the dominant resonance story.** All reviewers credit the Type VI Delayed/Politically-constructed resonance. The 118-year delay, the 2018 rediscovery, and the immediate canonical recognition form the strongest Type VI case in the project.

---

## Key Tensions

**Legibility scoring: Du Bois/Bertin (10/15) vs. Tufte (9/15) vs. Nightingale/Artist (11/15):**

Tufte is hardest on legibility because he compares the staircase-spiral to the bar chart alternative and finds the form-learning tax unjustified when a simpler encoding exists. Du Bois and Bertin score 10/15, crediting the gestalt effectiveness while penalizing the full-encoding barrier. Nightingale and the Artist credit the attention-arrest function as a partial offset to the legibility cost — a visitor who stops at this chart and spends 3 minutes has achieved more than a visitor who glances at six conventional bar charts.

The panel's working position: Du Bois's innovation (attention-arrest legibility tradeoff) is a real phenomenon that the rubric does not yet score. The legibility cost is real; the attention-arrest benefit is real; the current rubric captures only the cost.

**Resonance: Artist (15/15) vs. Du Bois/Tufte/Bertin (13/15) vs. Nightingale (14/15):**

The Artist gives maximum resonance credit for the suppression-resistant resonance phenomenon. Other reviewers score 13-14/15, crediting the Type VI bonus but not reaching maximum. The Artist's position — that the act of suppression itself is evidence of the work's resonance potential, and that immediate recognition upon recovery confirms the maximum-strength Type VI claim — is analytically compelling. The panel does not reject it; it registers as a minority position pending further confirmation.
