# Panel Review — Du Bois on 0020: The Staircase-Spiral

## Opening

This is the chart I made specifically not to be ignored.

The Paris Exposition had thousands of exhibitors. In the "American Negro" exhibit, I had one wall. I could have put sixty bar charts on the wall. I could have put tables. I could have put the data in a report that no visitor would have read. I chose to make something that would stop people — that they would look at and wonder "what is this?" before they read what it showed.

The staircase-spiral was designed to arrest attention. Let me explain what I was doing and let the panel decide whether I succeeded.

---

## The Advocacy Rationale for the Form

In 1900, the narrative about Black Americans was: they cannot sustain civilized society, they cannot accumulate wealth, they cannot maintain economic progress. This was not fringe opinion — it was the official position of mainstream American sociology, law, and politics. It was what the Paris visitors had been told, and many believed.

I needed to show them that $4,165,560 in property was owned by Black Georgians in 1899 — up from $1,173,624 in 1875. And I needed them to feel the growth, not just read the number.

A bar chart shows the growth as a comparison between bars. You read the bars, you compute the difference, you understand that there was growth. This is correct but it is intellectual, not visceral.

The staircase-spiral shows the growth as an accumulation. The viewer's eye follows the staircase outward: each step is wider than the last, each step is further from the center, the form expands. The expansion IS the growth. You do not compute it; you see it.

The staircase adds one dimension the pure spiral does not have: the risers. Each riser represents the increase in a specific period. The viewer can see not just that property grew but where it grew fastest (the riser from 1885 to 1890 is the largest — the period of the most dramatic increase). This is the dimensional layering that Minard would recognize: time, cumulative value, and growth rate in a single form.

## 1. School Fidelity /20 — Score: 16

**Type B assessment (School Founder — novel form):**

The advocacy-visualization grammar demands: begin with the audience and the political conclusion; choose form and data to deliver it to that audience; formal invention is justified if it serves the argument without falsifying the data.

This chart follows that grammar exactly. The audience (Paris Exposition visitors, skeptical of Black American capability) is identified. The political conclusion (24 years of consistent economic progress) is the argument. The form (staircase-spiral) was chosen to make that argument visceral rather than intellectual. The data is accurate.

The grammar declaration is undeclared — I wrote about my methods in *The Philadelphia Negro* and elsewhere, but not in the chart itself. The grammar is derivable from the full suite (60 charts, all following the same advocacy-first logic). For this single chart, the grammar is present but requires the full suite context to be fully visible.

**Score: 16/20** — Strong founding grammar; undeclared and requires suite context for full derivation.

---

## 2. Data/Story Integrity /20 — Score: 17

The property values are drawn from Georgia state tax records. The source is as reliable as 1900 state records allow — imperfect, but the best available. The values are stated directly on the chart. The five-year periods correspond to actual data collection points.

The one integrity question: the chart shows property value in current dollars without inflation adjustment. From 1875 to 1899, there was significant deflation in the US (prices fell by roughly 20%). So the real increase in purchasing power was larger than the nominal increase. I did not show this — in 1900, constant-dollar adjustments were not standard practice, and showing the inflation-adjusted growth would have made my argument stronger, not weaker.

This is a case where the undeclared accuracy tradeoff (nominal vs. real dollars) goes against me — the chart understates the growth. Step B adjustment: 0 (the tradeoff is undeclared but directionally honest; the argument would be stronger, not weaker, with adjustment).

**Score: 17/20**

## 3. Legibility /15 — Score: 10

**Context specification:**
- Primary audience: Paris Exposition visitors, 1900; international, educated, but unfamiliar with the specific form
- Primary reading context: Public display (glancing, with time to stop for interesting works)
- Contemporary context: Design history scholarship, advocacy visualization study
- Context decay: Very High

I am being honest: this chart is not immediately legible. The staircase-spiral is a novel form. A viewer who has never seen it must work out the encoding before extracting the data. The title helps: "Increase of the Negro population in Georgia, 1790-1890" — wait, that is a different chart. The staircase-spiral's title is "Negro landholders of Georgia" with the years and values printed on each step.

The gestalt works: the viewer sees growth and expansion. That is available in under five seconds. What the viewer cannot extract in five seconds: the specific encoding (width = cumulative value; riser = period increase). The full encoding requires time and inference.

For the passing visitor: 5-7/15. For the stopping visitor who reads the steps: 12-13/15. As a public-display work, I must score against the passing visitor's experience.

The suite context helps: a visitor who has seen other charts in the exhibit (conventional bar charts and line graphs) is primed to look for encoding rules. But the staircase-spiral is sufficiently different from the other forms that this priming helps only partially.

**Score: 10/15** — Gestalt works; full encoding requires inference; novel form imposes legibility cost.

---

## 4. Visual Economy /15 — Score: 12

The form carries three variables: time, cumulative value, and growth rate. Each is encoded. The numeric labels are necessary because the spiral's visual encoding alone is insufficient for precise values. The red color is consistent with the suite's color convention (red marks the subject). The title and date labels are essential.

No decorative excess. The form is the data.

Minor deduction: the spiral's spatial expansion creates some visual bulk that a bar chart would not — the outer steps are physically much larger than the inner steps, which is correct but creates a visual impression that the later periods have "more" presence than the data warrants. A bar chart would present each period at the same visual weight. The spiral's cumulative expansion slightly over-emphasizes the later periods as visual objects.

**Score: 12/15**

---

## 5. Resonance /15 — Score: 13

**Primary type: III (Advocacy-Efficacy) + VI (Delayed / Politically constructed)**

The advocacy resonance in 1900 is difficult to assess: I do not have direct evidence that this specific chart changed minds at the Paris Exposition. The exhibit as a whole received a Gold Medal. Whether any individual chart moved visitors is unrecorded.

The delayed resonance (Type VI: Delayed / Politically constructed, +1 bonus) is the primary resonance story for this chart. The 2018 rediscovery and the current scholarly and design community engagement with Du Bois's work is the strongest sustained resonance event in the project. The staircase-spiral is among the most reproduced charts from the suite in contemporary discussions precisely because it is the most formally inventive — the novel form is now read as evidence of Du Bois's visual intelligence.

**Score: 13/15**

## Innovation Flagged

**Innovation: Attention-arrest legibility tradeoff** — Rubric anchor: Legibility / Resonance. In public-display contexts, a novel or formally unusual visualization may have worse immediate legibility (encoding rules require inference) but better attention capture (the novelty arrests the passing viewer). The attention-arrest effect is a prerequisite for advocacy resonance in high-traffic, high-competition public-display contexts: if the viewer does not stop, the legibility score is irrelevant. The rubric should recognize that in public-display advocacy contexts, a moderate legibility cost for a significant attention-arrest gain may be the correct design choice. The current scoring penalizes the legibility cost without crediting the attention-arrest benefit.
