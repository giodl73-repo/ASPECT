# Panel Review — Nightingale on 0020: Du Bois Staircase-Spiral

## Opening

Du Bois and I made the same decision: choose a form that will be looked at over a form that is statistically optimal. I chose the polar area diagram not because it was the most accurate way to show the mortality data but because it was the most persuasive way to reach Parliament. He chose the staircase-spiral not because it was the most legible way to show property growth but because it was the most visually arresting way to stop a visitor at the Paris Exposition.

We both paid a legibility cost. I paid less than he did — the polar area diagram is formally unusual but its encoding rules are quickly derivable (angle = category, area = magnitude). The staircase-spiral's encoding rules are less obvious.

Whether the cost was proportionate to the advocacy benefit is the question.

## 3. Legibility /15 — Score: 11

**Public-display context, 1900 Paris Exposition visitor.**

The gestalt argument (property grew substantially) is available in 5 seconds. The precise encoding (step width = cumulative value; riser = period increase) is not available without sustained study — perhaps 2-3 minutes.

For a visitor who stops: 13/15. For a visitor who glances: 7/15. This is a public-display work and must be scored against the median visitor behavior. I estimate 40% stop, 60% glance — weighted legibility approximately 10-11/15.

The comparison to the polar area diagram: my coxcomb's encoding rules are more derivable from the visual grammar alone. A viewer who has never seen a polar area diagram can, by examining it, work out that angle = time period and area = quantity. The staircase-spiral's dual-variable step encoding (width + riser height) is less derivable without the numeric labels.

**Score: 11/15** — Gestalt strong; full encoding requires labels.

## Innovation Flagged

**Innovation: The form IS the argument (beyond the data)** — Rubric anchor: Resonance / School Fidelity. In advocacy visualization, the choice of a formally inventive, non-standard form can itself constitute part of the argument. Du Bois's staircase-spiral argues not just that Black Georgians own more property — it argues, through the form's sophistication and originality, that Du Bois and his students have the intellectual capacity to invent new visual languages. The form's inventiveness is an implicit counter-argument to the dominant narrative of Black intellectual incapacity. This "form as implicit argument" is distinct from Innovation #13 (argument-first design logic) — it is not that the argument determines the form choice, but that the form choice is itself an additional, implicit argument. The rubric has no vocabulary for implicit arguments made through formal choices.
