# Panel Review — Audience on 0021: Rosling Gapminder

## Who Is Watching This?

**Primary audience (TED, 2006):** Approximately 800 attendees at a TED Conference — disproportionately tech industry leaders, entrepreneurs, journalists, and academics. Above-average visual and statistical literacy. Self-selected for curiosity and openness to new ideas. Not specialists in global health or development economics.

**Secondary audience (online video):** >15 million viewers over 18 years. Far more diverse. Includes students, general interest viewers, educators who use it in classrooms, and specialists reviewing it for professional reasons.

**What the primary audience could follow in real time:**

In the TED context, with Rosling narrating at a pace calibrated to the audience's comprehension:
- The income/life expectancy axes: clear within 30 seconds (Rosling explains them)
- The region colors: clear (legend is visible; Rosling names the regions)
- The bubble size as population: less clear — most viewers do not spontaneously interpret bubble size as population without being told
- The log scale: often missed or initially resisted — Rosling must explain why income is on a log scale
- The animation as time passage: immediately clear
- Individual country tracking: possible for highlighted countries; impossible for unlabeled ones

**The narration does most of the legibility work.** Without Rosling, this chart is harder for a non-specialist audience than it appears. The log scale alone would confuse many viewers. The population-as-size encoding is not obvious. The narration is not supplementary — it is load-bearing.

## Innovation Flagged

**Innovation: Narration-load-bearing legibility** — Rubric anchor: Legibility (Step 0). In some visualizations, the accompanying narration (or text) carries part of the legibility burden — it explains encodings that the visual alone does not make clear. For ISOTYPE book charts (0019), we noted that the text supplements the chart. In the Gapminder presentation, the narration is more than supplementary — it is load-bearing for several encodings (log scale explanation, bubble size identification, country identification). The rubric should distinguish: (a) narrative as supplement (the chart is fully legible without it), (b) narrative as enhancement (the chart is partially legible without it, more legible with it), and (c) narrative as load-bearing (some encodings are only legible with the narration). Category (c) creates a legibility vulnerability: remove the narration and the chart fails those encodings.
