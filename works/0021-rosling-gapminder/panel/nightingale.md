# Panel Review — Nightingale on 0021: Rosling Gapminder

## Opening

He and I are doing the same thing. He has the animation and the TED stage; I had the coxcomb and the Parliamentary hearing. Both of us chose a form that was not the statistically optimal encoding because the optimal encoding would not have worked for the audience we needed to reach.

I chose the polar area diagram for Army generals who would not read a bar chart. He chose an animated movie for a 21st-century audience that had grown up watching television. The form must meet the audience.

Where I am going to push back: the argument that the narration is part of the visualization. It is — but it is also a problem. My coxcomb was designed to function without me in the room. I could hand it to a Member of Parliament who was not at my presentation and it would still make the argument. Rosling's animated chart, without Rosling, is substantially weaker. The school's grammar must eventually solve this dependency.

## Innovation Flagged

**Innovation: Presenter-dependence as school grammar fragility** — Rubric anchor: School Fidelity (Type B). When a founding work's primary effectiveness depends on the specific performance skills of the founder (Rosling's narration timing, emphasis, humor, and live interaction), the school's grammar has a performer-dependence fragility: it can only be fully demonstrated by someone with those skills. This is a grammar portability problem distinct from technical portability (moving from museum to print) — it is a human capital portability problem. A school's grammar should be assessable for how well it specifies techniques that other practitioners can learn and apply without requiring the founder's personal performance skills. Schools with high performer-dependence have more fragile grammars than schools with low performer-dependence.
