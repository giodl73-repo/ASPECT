# Brief — HOLC Residential Security Maps (1935–1940)

## Work Description

The Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) "residential security maps" were produced between 1935 and 1940 for 239 American cities. The HOLC was a New Deal agency created in 1933 to refinance home mortgages in default during the Depression. Beginning in 1935, the agency commissioned local appraisers, real estate agents, and bank representatives to produce neighborhood-by-neighborhood risk assessments for mortgage lending. The result was a city map divided into color-coded zones.

The color system is a four-class ordered categorical scheme:

| Grade | Color | Label | Meaning |
|-------|-------|-------|---------|
| A | Green | Best | New construction, in-demand, homogeneous population |
| B | Blue | Still Desirable | Slower sales, aging but stable |
| C | Yellow | Definitely Declining | Obsolescent housing, infiltration of lower-income populations |
| D | Red | Hazardous | Older housing, business or industrial encroachment |

The D-"Hazardous" category explicitly used racial composition as a primary classification criterion. HOLC appraisal sheets in D-graded neighborhoods consistently list "Negro concentration," "infiltration of Negroes," "mixed racial area," or similar language as the primary risk indicator. In many cities, any neighborhood with a significant Black or immigrant population was automatically graded D regardless of housing quality, income levels, or actual mortgage default rates.

This work is assessed using the 1940 Chicago HOLC map as the primary case — one of the largest, most thoroughly documented examples in the project.

## The Central Problem

The HOLC maps present a moral and political judgment — that Black and immigrant neighborhoods are investment risks — using the visual grammar of an objective empirical classification. The four-color choropleth is the standard cartographic vocabulary for objective geographical data. The maps look like weather maps, zoning maps, census maps. Nothing in the visual grammar signals that the D category is defined by race rather than by measured financial risk.

The category contamination is ontological: the D-red category is simultaneously an empirical claim (these neighborhoods have high default risk) and a racial designation (these are neighborhoods where Black people live). The maps cannot be read as one without reading as the other, because the HOLC defined risk using race. The category construction embeds a circular argument: Black neighborhoods are risky because they are Black; they are Black; therefore they are risky.

## Historical Impact

The HOLC maps were used by private banks and the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) to deny mortgage loans in redlined areas through the 1950s, 1960s, and in some cases the 1970s. The GI Bill (1944) nominally provided veterans with access to mortgage guarantees, but Black veterans found themselves unable to use those guarantees in most neighborhoods because banks refused to lend in D-graded areas. The maps did not cause residential segregation in American cities — segregation was already well-established through deed restrictions, violence, and market exclusion — but they formalized and perpetuated it through the apparatus of federal lending policy.

The term "redlining" derives directly from the D-grade red color on these maps. The maps are now in the National Archives and have been digitized by the Mapping Inequality project (University of Richmond, 2016).
