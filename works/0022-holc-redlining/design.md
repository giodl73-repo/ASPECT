# Design Description — HOLC Residential Security Map, Chicago 1940

## Physical Description

The Chicago HOLC map (1940) is a large-format city map, approximately 24" × 30", printed on heavy paper. The base map shows the city's street grid, major landmarks, and the Chicago River and Lake Michigan shoreline. Neighborhood boundaries are drawn in thick black lines and filled with one of four colors. Neighborhood codes (alphanumeric: A-1 through D-68) are printed in black within each zone.

## Visual Variable Inventory (Bertin analysis)

| Visual variable | Use | Data type |
|----------------|-----|-----------|
| Hue (green/blue/yellow/red) | Primary: Grade category (A/B/C/D) | Nominal (as constructed); ostensibly ordinal |
| Value/saturation | Secondary: distinguishes neighboring zones of the same grade | Structural (geographic organization) |
| Position | Geographic: neighborhood locations in Chicago | Quantitative (spatial) |
| Shape | Zone boundaries (irregular polygons) | Structural |
| Size | Zone area (not encoded independently — varies by neighborhood geography) | Incidental |

The encoding grammar is choropleth cartography: geographic areas filled with categorical color. The grammar is entirely conventional and correct for its declared purpose. A viewer approaching the map in 1940 would read it as they would read any zoning or classification map.

## The Four-Color System

**A — Green (Best):** Used for the most recently developed, most homogeneous, most "American" neighborhoods. In Chicago 1940, these are primarily the far North Shore suburbs (Rogers Park periphery, Evanston border) and the far northwest. A-grade neighborhoods are characterized on HOLC appraisal sheets by: new single-family construction, 100% "American" or northern European descent, high income, owner-occupied. The green color carries conventional connotations of safety, growth, approval.

**B — Blue (Still Desirable):** Established neighborhoods that are slightly older, with some turnover, some rental mix, but still "stable." Most of Chicago's middle-class residential neighborhoods on the North Side fall here. Blue is the color of calm, stability, water — also conventional.

**C — Yellow (Definitely Declining):** The warning category. Neighborhoods described as aging, with increasing rental density, with "infiltration" of lower-income or "foreign-born" populations. Yellow signals caution. In Chicago, this category covers much of the inner ring of settlement-era immigrant neighborhoods.

**D — Red (Hazardous):** The denial category. The South Side Black Belt (Grand Boulevard, Washington Park, Douglas, Oakland), the West Side Black neighborhoods, and several heavily Jewish or southern European immigrant neighborhoods on the near North and West Sides. Appraisal sheets for these neighborhoods describe racial composition as the primary hazard. A neighborhood could have sound housing stock, strong community institutions, active commerce — and still receive a D grade if it was predominantly Black. The red color carries the full weight of danger, prohibition, stop.

## Geographic Structure of the Chicago Map

The color pattern of the Chicago 1940 map produces a recognizable shape that corresponds almost perfectly to the contemporaneous pattern of residential segregation:

- A swath of green and blue along the North Shore and northwest
- A ring of yellow in the middle transitional areas
- A concentrated belt of red covering the South Side from 26th Street to 63rd Street, and scattered red zones on the West Side

This color geography encodes the racial geography of Chicago in 1940. The map looks like a risk assessment; it is also a map of where Black people live.

## The Accompanying Appraisal Sheets

Each coded neighborhood zone has an accompanying appraisal sheet (not on the map itself, but in the HOLC archives). The sheets include:
- Housing type and age
- Mortgage availability and terms
- "Clarifying remarks" — typically one to three sentences describing the population

For D-grade zones in Chicago, the clarifying remarks consistently include language identifying Black or immigrant population as the primary hazard. Example from Zone D-8 (Douglas neighborhood, 1940): "This is a Negro area. Property values have declined and will continue to decline. No white mortgage funds available." The population description is the explanation for the grade. Race IS the risk category.

The appraisal sheets are not shown on the map. A viewer of the map alone sees only color. But the color's meaning is constructed by the sheets.

## School Analysis

**Primary school: Advocacy-visualization** — the maps argue for a specific investment policy (do not lend in red zones) using a visual form that presents the argument as objective data.

**Secondary school: Cartography** — the base map, geographic encoding, choropleth format, and legend system are all conventional cartographic practice.

**Type C synthesis:** The map combines advocacy-visualization (the argument structure: categories designed to produce a specific lending decision) with cartography (the visual vocabulary: geographic zones, color fills, scale). The synthesis is neither named nor resolved: the cartographic grammar suppresses the advocacy character by presenting the advocacy argument as a neutral geographic classification.

This suppression is not accidental — it is the work's central rhetorical operation. Using cartographic grammar for an advocacy argument makes the argument appear to be a fact.

## Encoding Validity vs. Argument Validity

The map encodes its four categories faithfully. If you accept the categories as defined, the encoding is correct: D areas are shown in red consistently; zone boundaries correspond to the appraisers' neighborhood definitions; the legend accurately describes the system. The encoding is valid.

The argument is not valid. The categories mix empirical claims (default risk) with racial designations (neighborhood racial composition) without distinguishing between them. The D category does not measure financial risk — it designates race and then calls that designation a risk. The map is a technically correct encoding of an ontologically contaminated category system.

This is the key distinction the panel must navigate: encoding validity and argument validity are different properties.
