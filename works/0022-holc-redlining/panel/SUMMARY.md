## Key Agreements

**Cluster N is confirmed across works 0017 and 0022.** Du Bois (Innovation #175), Bertin (Innovation #178), the Historian (Innovation #179), and the Statistician (Innovations #180, #181) all independently confirm: the D-grade category mixes a racial designation with a financial risk claim, presented using the visual authority of objective cartographic measurement.

**The encoding is valid; the argument is not.** Bertin's Innovation #178 establishes this distinction clearly: encoding validity and argument validity are different properties. The rubric's Data/Story Integrity dimension assesses argument validity; the current sub-steps are not designed for cases where the categories themselves are ontologically contaminated.

**The circular measurement problem is methodologically distinct.** The Statistician's Innovation #181 (self-fulfilling risk designation) identifies a failure mode not covered by any existing rubric sub-step: the designation causes the measured outcome, which then appears to validate the designation.

## Key Tensions

**Bertin on grammar vs. politics:** Bertin insists the visual grammar is innocent; the grammar faithfully encodes the categories it was given. Du Bois insists that using cartographic grammar to encode a racial designation is itself a moral act. Both are correct; they agree on the dock location (Data/Story Integrity, not School Fidelity) while disagreeing on the underlying framing.

**Nightingale on advocacy ethics:** Nightingale's Innovation #176 (advocacy-as-harm) proposes that when advocacy visualization is deployed for an unjust conclusion, the form's strengths become amplifiers of harm. Du Bois is skeptical of rubric provisions that impose an ethics test — a political act by the rubric itself. This tension is unresolved and should be surfaced in the v1.10 amendment discussion.
