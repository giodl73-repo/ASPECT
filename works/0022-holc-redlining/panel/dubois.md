# Du Bois Review — HOLC Residential Security Maps

## Opening

I know this map. I have known it all my life without having seen it, because I have lived inside its conclusions. The people who made this map in 1940 did not know me either. They knew what I was in their category system: a hazard.

Let me be precise about what this map does. It does not misrepresent data. It does not truncate an axis or choose a misleading scale. It does not overstate a correlation as a causation. It does something more fundamental: it constructs a category that embeds a racial designation inside an economic concept, presents that hybrid category using the visual vocabulary of objective data, and then deploys the result to determine who may own a home in the United States. The contamination is not in the encoding. The contamination is in the ontology of the categories themselves.

## Category Construction as Moral-Empirical Confusion

The D-grade "Hazardous" category is defined, in the HOLC appraisal sheets, by racial composition. A neighborhood is hazardous if Black people live there. This is not a correlation used as a proxy for risk. It is a designation presented as a measurement. "This is a Negro area. Property values have declined." The first sentence defines the category; the second names a consequence of the first (because the D designation itself causes property value decline, by restricting lending, which reduces demand). The causation is circular.

I have spent my life analyzing how racial categories are constructed and deployed as objective facts. The Atlanta University studies, The Philadelphia Negro, the Paris charts — all were arguments against this precise operation: the operation of presenting racial classification as empirical description. The HOLC maps are the most consequential American example of this operation I have encountered in this project, and they are more consequential than any map I produced, because they were wielded by the federal government to deny fifty years of home equity to the people most in need of it.

## Cluster N Confirmation

What I see here is what Innovation #140 named in the Booth maps and what innovations #142, #143 confirmed: ontological category contamination. The category mixes an empirical property (financial default risk, which was never actually measured — there are no default rate data in the HOLC archive that would justify the D designation) with a racial designation (neighborhood racial composition). The visual variable — red choropleth fill — presents both properties as a single encoded fact.

This is Cluster N confirmed across a second work. The Booth maps mixed moral judgment with economic description. The HOLC maps mix racial designation with financial risk. Both use standard cartographic grammar to present the hybrid category as objective geographic fact. Both produce political and policy consequences far beyond any other failure mode in the rubric.

Innovation #175 (to be logged): **Circular risk designation** — when a risk category is defined by a property that causes risk only because of the designation itself (race designated as risk → lending denied → values decline → designation confirmed), the category constitutes a self-fulfilling prophecy presented as an objective measurement. Standard Step A (source quality) cannot address this: the measurement IS the designation.

## Scoring

**School Fidelity (Type C synthesis: advocacy + cartography): 14/20**

The cartographic grammar is correctly applied. The advocacy structure (visual argument for a specific lending decision) is effective. The synthesis type — cartographic form used to suppress the advocacy character — is formally coherent. I dock from here because the synthesis resolution is not named and the suppression is not innocent: using cartography to disguise advocacy is a school-synthesis failure of integrity, not grammar. The map claims to be a neutral geographic fact and is not.

**Data/Story Integrity: 8/20**

This is the lowest Data/Story Integrity score I have assigned in this project, and it is not low enough to express what this map does. Let me work through the sub-steps:

Step 0c variable selection disclosure: Race is encoded as "risk" without disclosure that these are racial designations, not measured default rates. The framing effect is not incidental — it is the map's central operation. Dock −3.

Step A source quality: No default-rate data was collected before the grading. The HOLC appraisers did not examine mortgage performance records — they examined neighborhoods and reported their racial and social composition. The categories are based on the appraisers' racial attitudes, not financial measurement. Confidence suppression at maximum: the map presents a racial designation with the visual confidence of a financial measurement. Dock −3 to −4.

Step C causation: The map implies that D-grade neighborhoods are risky investments. The causal claim is circular: the designation causes the risk. Direct causal claim contradicted by the underlying data model. Dock −5.

These penalties compound. The result is a score in the 6–10 range under Steps A–D standards. I score 8/20.

**Legibility: 10/15**

Context: political presentation, banking and federal lending audiences. The map is exceptionally legible for its stated purpose: a lender can immediately see which neighborhoods to avoid. This is the horror of it — the map works perfectly as a lending exclusion instrument. For any viewer who does not know the category construction, the map reads as a neutral geographic risk assessment. That illegibility of the hidden category construction does not appear in the legibility score, but it should.

**Visual Economy: 12/15**

Cartographic economy is well-managed. The four-color system is minimal. No decorative excess. The appraisal sheets are not on the map — all the contaminating language is in a separate document that the map viewer does not see. From a pure mark-economy standpoint, the design is efficient.

**Resonance (Type III — Advocacy Efficacy): 13/15**

The map achieved its advocacy goal with terrible completeness. Lending institutions used it for decades. The FHA adopted HOLC-style redlining practices. The GI Bill was denied to Black veterans because of maps like this one. If advocacy efficacy is the criterion, this map is among the most effective visualizations I have reviewed. That it advocated for a system of racial exclusion does not change the efficacy score; it changes everything else.

**Craft: 13/15**

The cartographic execution is professional and consistent. Zone boundaries are clean. Color fills are consistent. The legend is legible. Nothing is imprecise in the execution.

## Cluster N Amendment Proposal

A v1.10 amendment is needed. The rubric currently has no mechanism for scoring works where the category construction itself is the integrity failure — where the wrong is not in encoding fidelity, data suppression, or causal overreach, but in the ontological constitution of the categories being encoded. I propose: **Step F — Ontological category integrity**, applied when a category system mixes incommensurable ontological types (empirical measurement + racial designation; economic fact + moral judgment) and presents the hybrid as a single objective classification. Dock −3 to −6 depending on severity and harm.

## Summary

Du Bois total: **70/100**

This map is technically accomplished and morally catastrophic. The rubric in its current form cannot fully express that. The encoding is valid; the argument is false; and the argument, believed and acted on for decades, denied homeownership to millions of Black Americans.
