# Historian Review — HOLC Residential Security Maps

## Context Specification

- **Primary audience:** Local real estate appraisers, commercial bank loan officers, FHA underwriters — professionals making mortgage lending decisions
- **Primary reading context:** Political presentation / professional decision-making — the map is used as a reference tool in lending decisions, not as a contemplative study
- **Contemporary context:** Museum display, academic research, journalism (Mapping Inequality project); context has shifted from professional tool to historical evidence of structural racism
- **Context decay:** Complete — the primary use context (professional lending decision) no longer exists; the contemporary use is the opposite of the primary use (the map is now evidence against the system it served)
- **version_reviewed:** Chicago 1940 HOLC map as digitized by the Mapping Inequality project (University of Richmond, 2016); closely matches the original archive materials
- **institutional_divergence:** Low — the Mapping Inequality digitization is faithful to the originals; no interpretive modification

## Historical Context

The HOLC maps were produced between 1935 and 1940 under the New Deal as part of the federal government's effort to stabilize the mortgage market during the Depression. The agency refinanced one million home loans. The residential security maps were the instrument by which the HOLC assessed the risk of neighborhoods for lending purposes.

The maps were not classified or secret. They were shared with the Federal Housing Administration and with private banks. The FHA's Underwriting Manual explicitly incorporated HOLC-style racial criteria for neighborhood assessment through the late 1940s. The Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (1975) and the Community Reinvestment Act (1977) were legislative responses to the persistence of redlining practices derived from these maps.

Research by economists and historians (Robert Caro on New York; Rothstein, *The Color of Law*, 2017; Aaronson, Faber, Hartley et al. on Chicago specifically) has demonstrated quantitatively that D-grade neighborhoods in 1940 have persistently lower homeownership rates, lower property values, and higher poverty rates in 2020 than otherwise comparable B-grade neighborhoods. The maps' effects are still measurable eighty years later.

The maps produced no comparable harm in the abstract. They produced it because they were believed, acted on, and enforced by institutions with the power to deny or grant credit. The visualization was instrumental: it converted a racial designation into an official-looking financial risk assessment that the lending industry could point to as objective justification for discriminatory decisions.

## Scoring

**School Fidelity: 13/20**

The advocacy-cartography synthesis is competent. The cartographic grammar is correctly applied for a 1940 professional cartography standard. I dock for: (1) the undeclared advocacy character — presenting a policy argument as a neutral geographic fact; (2) the failure to declare the racial basis of the D category, which a transparent advocacy work would have stated.

**Data/Story Integrity: 7/20**

Innovation #145 (period-analytical-framework decay, work 0017) applies here with full force: the analytical framework embedded in these maps — that racial composition of neighborhoods is a reliable proxy for mortgage risk — was already contested in 1940 (Robert Weaver published critiques of racially-based risk assessment in the late 1930s) and is now understood as empirically false and legally prohibited. The framework was not merely wrong in retrospect; it was wrong in its period when examined carefully.

I score 7/20 for: no default-rate data basis for any category; circular causation; racial designation presented as financial measurement; variable selection (race as operative variable) undeclared.

Innovation #179: **Period-framework falsity vs. period-framework decay** — distinct from Innovation #145 (analytical-framework decay: a framework that was valid in its period but is now understood as flawed). This map's framework — race as risk proxy — was not valid in its period by its own evidentiary standards (no measured default rate data was collected to support it). This is period-framework falsity: the framework was empirically unjustified at the time, not merely later revised.

**Legibility: 11/15**

Legible for the primary professional lending audience. The contemporary audience (researchers and advocates) can read it well — as evidence of the system it encoded.

**Visual Economy: 12/15**

Efficient cartographic encoding for its purpose.

**Resonance: 10/15**

Resonance Type VI (Delayed / politically constructed): the maps' significance as historical evidence of structural racism was constructed by later scholars and advocates. The delay was not epistemic — the harm was known and experienced in real time by the affected communities — but the elevation of the maps to documentary status occurred through scholarly and journalistic work from the 1960s onward. The Mapping Inequality digitization (2016) is the canonization event. Type VI, delayed / politically constructed: the delay was caused by institutional suppression of the affected communities' testimony.

**Craft: 12/15**

Consistent professional cartographic execution for the period.

These maps are the single most consequential visualization work reviewed in this project in terms of real-world harm. They are not the worst visualization — they are technically accomplished.
