# Nightingale Review — HOLC Residential Security Maps

## Opening

I spent my life arguing that visualization could change policy. I showed mortality figures to Parliament because I believed data could move decision-makers toward better outcomes for the people dying in their hospitals and slums. The HOLC maps are a reminder that visualization can also move decision-makers toward worse outcomes — and that the form I championed, advocacy visualization, is a tool that can be turned to any end.

I look at these maps and I recognize my own grammar. These are advocacy visualizations in the precise sense: categorical color applied to geographic space to produce a specific policy decision. The form is not wrong. The argument is unjust.

## Advocacy Form Assessment

The maps use advocacy visualization correctly. The four-category system produces a clear decision rule for lenders: green and blue get loans; yellow and red do not. The color sequence implies order and evaluative direction. The geographic display makes the argument spatially concrete: "this neighborhood, at this address, is hazardous." A lender can bring this map to a loan application and produce an immediate, defensible-looking decision. The visualization performs the advocacy function with precision.

What makes this a category-level failure — something beyond a normally wrong or misleading visualization — is that the advocacy is for harm, and the harm is systemic and large. A bar chart that truncates an axis may mislead a viewer about the magnitude of a difference. The HOLC maps misled an entire financial system about the creditworthiness of millions of people, sustained that misleading for decades, and produced structural consequences that persist in American wealth distribution to this day.

Innovation #176: **Advocacy-as-harm** — the advocacy visualization school assumes that the visualization's advocacy goal is legitimate. When advocacy visualization is used for an unjust conclusion, the school's strengths (effective in reaching decision-makers, clear decision-rule encoding, durable policy uptake) become factors that amplify harm. The form is complicit not through grammar failure but through application to an unjust argument. The rubric should have a mechanism for this.

## Advocacy Framing and School Fidelity

The maps' advocacy structure is also its school-grammar problem: the maps use cartographic form to disguise the advocacy character. My own work was explicit advocacy — the coxcomb diagrams were clearly designed to persuade Parliament to reform military hospitals. I did not pretend my charts were neutral geographic facts. The HOLC maps claim to be neutral geographic facts and are advocacy. The advocacy-cartography synthesis resolves the tension between the two schools by suppressing the advocacy character — which is a dishonest resolution.

The distinction matters for School Fidelity: the advocacy-visualization school's grammar requires declaring the argument. These maps do not declare the argument; they present it as objective data. This is not merely a school-synthesis failure; it is a school-faithfulness failure within the advocacy dimension.

**School Fidelity: 13/20**

The cartographic grammar is well-applied. The advocacy structure is effective. I dock primarily because the advocacy is disguised as neutral classification — which violates the school's honest-argument principle — and because the category construction does not declare its racial basis.

**Data/Story Integrity: 7/20**

The form is correct; the argument is unjust; the categories are contaminated. I apply Du Bois's analysis here: Step A (source quality: no measured default rate data), Step C (circular causation: the designation creates the risk it claims to measure), and the L amendment (variable selection: race is encoded as risk without disclosing that race is the operative variable). The combined dock brings the score to the 6–9 range. I score 7.

**Legibility: 11/15**

Highly legible for the lending audience in the primary context. Immediately legible for its decision purpose. A lender cannot misread this map.

**Visual Economy: 12/15**

Efficient for its purpose. Four categories, consistent color, geographic display. Nothing unnecessary.

**Resonance (Type III — Advocacy Efficacy): 12/15**

The map worked. This is not praise. Advocacy efficacy as resonance type asks: did the work move the audience toward the intended action? Yes. The intended action was lending discrimination. The resonance is achieved; the achievement is the harm.

**Craft: 13/15**

Professional cartographic execution throughout.

## What the Rubric Cannot Say

The rubric currently has no mechanism for assessing whether an advocacy visualization's argument is just. School Fidelity asks whether the grammar is correctly applied. Data/Story Integrity asks whether the encoding is accurate. Neither asks whether the thing being argued for is worth arguing for.

I do not think the rubric should impose an ethics test that disqualifies a work from assessment. But it should have a place to note when advocacy visualization is used for unjust ends — and it should allow that finding to affect the resonance score, because a work that achieves its advocacy goal by denying home loans to Black veterans is not achieving resonance in the sense the rubric celebrates.

## Summary

Nightingale total: **68/100**

The form is correct. The argument is unjust. The combination is the problem, and the rubric does not yet fully name it.
