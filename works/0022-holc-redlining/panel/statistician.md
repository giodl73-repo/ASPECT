# Statistician Review — HOLC Residential Security Maps

## Methodology Assessment

I begin with the fundamental question: what data was used to construct the D-grade designation?

Answer: No financial risk data. The HOLC did not collect mortgage default rates by neighborhood before grading neighborhoods. The appraisers did not examine lending performance data, foreclosure records, or payment history by geographic area. They walked the neighborhoods, interviewed local real estate agents and bank representatives, and recorded their impressions — including, consistently, the racial composition of the area.

This is not a measurement with high uncertainty. It is not a measurement at all. It is a racial designation presented in the visual form of a measurement. The entire quantitative apparatus (four-grade ordered system, geographic precision, professional institutional authority) is applied to what is essentially the appraiser's racial attitude.

Innovation #180: **The non-measurement presented as measurement** — distinct from confidence suppression (Step A: measurement was made, precision was overstated) and from data model undisclosure (Step D: measurement was made, analytical framework undeclared). The HOLC D-grade is not a measurement with suppressed uncertainty or an undeclared framework. It is a designation — a social act — presented as the output of a measurement process. The rubric has no category for this: Step A assumes there is a measurement and asks whether its quality is declared; the HOLC case begins before measurement.

## Circular Measurement Problem

The risk designation creates the risk it claims to measure. The mechanism:

1. Neighborhood designated D-grade based on racial composition
2. D-grade designation → lenders deny mortgage loans in D areas
3. Mortgage denial → reduced demand, reduced property values, reduced investment
4. Reduced property values → apparent validation of the D-grade designation
5. Repeat for decades

This is a textbook example of a self-fulfilling statistical prophecy. The methodology is not just imprecise; it is self-reinforcing. Any subsequent "data" that appeared to validate the D-grade designation (falling property values, community disinvestment) was generated by the designation itself. The prophecy fulfilled itself and then cited itself as evidence.

Innovation #181: **Self-fulfilling risk designation** — a measurement methodology in which the designation itself causes the measured property to manifest (causal arrow from designation to outcome, not from measurement to designation). Distinct from: correlation-as-causation (where correlation is real but causation is attributed wrongly); circular argument (where premises assume the conclusion); confirmation bias (where evidence is selected to support a prior belief). Self-fulfilling risk designation is a structural measurement failure: the designation intervenes in the system being measured, producing the outcome that confirms it.

## Scoring

**Data/Story Integrity: 7/20**

Step A: No measurement basis for the risk grades. The visual form implies measurement precision; there is none. Maximum confidence suppression dock: −4.

Step C: Causal claim (D neighborhoods have high investment risk) is circularly supported by the designation's own effects. Dock −3 to −4.

Step D: The analytical framework (race = risk proxy) is both undeclared and empirically unjustified in the period. Dock −3 (Level 4: undeclared and non-obvious).

These docks compound. I score 7/20.

**Legibility: 10/15**

The map is legible for its purpose. The decision rule is clear. The statistical methodology underlying the categories is invisible — which is part of the problem, not a neutral design feature.

**Other dimensions:** I review Data/Story Integrity and Legibility only.

## Concluding Note

From a statistical methodology standpoint, this is one of the most defective works in the project. It is defective not in the usual sense (imprecise measurement, undeclared uncertainty, correlation-causation confusion) but in a more fundamental sense: it presents a designation as a measurement, and the designation was designed to produce the outcome it claimed to measure. The rubric needs vocabulary for this. Du Bois's proposed Step F amendment is statistically warranted.
