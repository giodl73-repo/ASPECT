# Brief — Descartes's La Géométrie (1637)

## Work Description

*La Géométrie* is an appendix to Descartes's *Discours de la méthode* (1637). It is the founding text of analytic geometry — the discipline that unifies Euclidean geometric construction with algebraic manipulation. The diagrams in *La Géométrie* are the primary visual artifact assessed here: geometric figures showing the correspondence between algebraic operations and geometric constructions.

Descartes's central innovation: every geometric quantity can be assigned a numerical value through a unit segment, and algebraic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and the extraction of square roots) have exact geometric construction equivalents. The coordinate plane — the idea that every point in space can be identified by two numerical coordinates — is the most far-reaching consequence of this work, though Descartes himself does not fully develop the coordinate grid.

**The diagrams** show: unit segments, proportional constructions, the geometric equivalent of algebraic equations (e.g., how to construct the solution to a quadratic equation using ruler and compass), and the relationship between a curve and its algebraic equation.

## Historical Position in the Mathematical-Proof-Visualization School

Work 0018 (Euclid's Elements, ~300 BCE) established the school's founding grammar: diagrams that prove claims by construction, assessed under Step E (demonstrative encoding pathway). Euclid's grammar:
- Premises established by prior propositions or postulates (Step E1)
- Demonstrations complete — every step shown (Step E2)
- Constructions logically valid (Step E3)
- Diagrammatic conventions largely undeclared but traditional (Step E4)

Descartes is a Type A school member: he works explicitly within Euclid's demonstrative tradition, citing Euclidean constructions and building on them. But he introduces a new primitive — the **unit segment** — that Euclid's grammar does not have, and he uses it to bridge algebra and geometry in a way Euclid could not have done.

**The key question for the panel:** Does the introduction of algebraic elements change the epistemic mode of the diagrams? Or does the demonstrative encoding pathway (Step E) apply equally to analytic geometry as to classical Euclidean geometry?

## Key Questions

1. Does Step E apply equally to Descartes as to Euclid, or does the algebraic dimension require new sub-steps?
2. Is the unit segment — Descartes's new primitive — declared explicitly enough to satisfy Step E1 (logical foundation)?
3. Does the correspondence between algebraic and geometric operations constitute a new encoding claim (Step E3: does the diagram demonstrate what it claims to demonstrate)?
4. How does the school extend vs. remain stable across the 1,900 years between Euclid and Descartes?
