## Key Agreements

**Step E applies to Descartes with one important modification.** All five reviewers agree that the demonstrative encoding pathway is the correct assessment mode — Descartes's diagrams prove claims by construction, and the viewer verifies by following the argument. However, the panel identifies a new sub-type: **dual-mode demonstrative encoding** (Innovation #191) where the viewer must participate in two representational systems simultaneously (geometric and algebraic) to complete the verification.

**The unit segment is the school's most significant declared extension.** All reviewers note that the unit segment — Descartes's new primitive, declared in-work — satisfies Step E1 (logical foundation) and enables the algebraic-geometric correspondence. The declaration is the extension's strongest formal virtue.

**The algebraic assumption gap is systematic.** Tufte, Bertin, and Kandinsky all note that Descartes assumes algebraic rules without demonstrating them geometrically. For the primary 17th-century audience (trained mathematicians who know algebra), this is defensible. The Step E2 (demonstration completeness) dock is consistent across the panel: approximately −2 from a potential 18-20 score.

**Highest School Fidelity consensus in the project (17.0/20).** The extension is declared, consistent, and school-coherent. No reviewer finds a grammar violation.

## Key Questions Answered

**Does Step E apply equally to analytic geometry as to classical Euclidean geometry?**

Yes — with the addition of dual-mode verification as a new demand in Step E3. The assessment pathway is the same; the verification requirement is higher in Descartes. The rubric does not need a new step; it needs Step E3 to note that dual-mode demonstrative works require correspondence verification across both modes.

**Does the introduction of algebraic elements change the epistemic mode?**

Not in kind, but in degree. The epistemic mode remains demonstrative: diagram IS argument, viewer verifies by construction. The algebraic dimension adds a second verification domain. The mode is the same; the cognitive demand is higher.

**How does the school extend across 1,900 years?**

The Historian's Innovation #195 (upstream-enabling extension) answers this. Descartes extended the school at the one point where extension enabled the entire subsequent development of mathematical physics. The school's grammar is stable (demonstrative encoding); the extension is an addition of one new primitive (unit segment) that opened an enormous subsequent territory.

## What This Work Reveals

**The Step E pathway scales to school extensions.** Euclid (Type B founder) and Descartes (Type A extender) both correctly assessed under Step E. The pathway is robust across 1,900 years of school development. This is the strongest validation of the demonstrative encoding amendment in the project.

**The dual-mode claim requires dual-mode verification.** Innovation #191 proposes that Step E3 should note: when the demonstration's claim is a correspondence between two representational systems, the verification requires the viewer to follow the argument in both systems simultaneously. This is a natural refinement of Step E3, not a new step.

**The H amendment extends to demonstrative encoding.** Innovation #194 (argument-mandated density in demonstrative encoding) extends the H amendment's intrinsic complexity principle beyond scientific illustration to mathematical proof diagrams. In both contexts, complexity is intrinsic to the subject matter; the economy criterion is excess above the intrinsic necessity.
