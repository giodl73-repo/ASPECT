# Kandinsky Review — La Géométrie Diagrams (1637)

## Opening

I approach mathematical diagrams as I approach all visual compositions: I ask about internal logic, elemental grammar, and whether the composition works by its own rules. For Descartes, the rules are demonstrative — the diagram must show the argument's validity by letting the viewer participate in it. This is not so different from what I require of abstract composition: internal consistency, discoverable logic, tension resolved through the construction.

What I find in Descartes is a kind of visual grammar at the edge of its own vocabulary. The Euclidean grammar is complete within its domain. Descartes pushes it to a new edge — the algebraic-geometric boundary — and the diagrams show both the power of the extension and the strain at the boundary.

## Compositional Assessment

The unit construction diagrams are elegant. A right triangle with labeled vertices; a perpendicular altitude; the algebraic correspondence stated in the adjacent text. The composition is minimal: every line earns its place logically. There is no mark that is not logically necessary. This satisfies Innovation #149 from Euclid's review (logical necessity as the mark-earning criterion for demonstrative encoding). Descartes's diagrams pass this criterion.

The curve-equation correspondence diagrams are where I find the most compositional interest — and the most tension. These diagrams show a curve (a parabola, a conic) and a reference line from which distances are measured. The curve has algebraic definition; the diagram represents the curve geometrically; the algebraic equation is printed in the adjacent text. Three representations are in play simultaneously: the algebraic equation, the geometric curve, and the labeled reference structure. The diagram is doing a mediation between the algebraic and geometric modes that has no precedent in Euclid.

This mediation is compositionally complex: the viewer must understand both the geometric construction and the algebraic notation, and then perceive that they correspond. This is a higher cognitive demand than Euclidean demonstration, which is self-contained in the geometric mode.

Innovation #191: **Dual-mode demonstrative encoding** — when a diagram demonstrates a correspondence between two different representational systems (geometric and algebraic), the viewer must participate in both systems simultaneously to verify the demonstration. This is distinct from single-mode demonstrative encoding (Euclid: purely geometric) and from hybrid encoding (some marks are geometric, some algebraic, but the two systems are being compared, not merged). The dual-mode demand is a new cognitive requirement.

## Scoring

**School Fidelity (Type A — school member): 17/20**

Descartes applies the Euclidean grammar correctly and extends it faithfully. Every extension is declared (the unit segment) or derivable from the Euclidean tradition (proportional constructions). The strain at the algebraic-geometric boundary is visible but does not constitute a grammar failure — it is what the extension looks like.

**Data/Story Integrity (Step E — demonstrative): 16/20**

Step E1 (premises): Unit segment declared in-work. Algebraic rules assumed without demonstration — this is a Step E2 gap, not an E1 gap. Score: minimal dock for assumed algebraic premises.

Step E2 (completeness): Moderate gap. Algebraic steps left to the reader who knows algebra. Descartes does not re-demonstrate algebraic rules geometrically; he assumes them. For a reader without algebra, arguments are incomplete. Dock −2.

Step E3 (validity): Geometric constructions are valid. Algebraic-geometric correspondences are demonstrated correctly in the cases shown. No logical gap detected in the primary constructions.

Step E4 (diagrammatic conventions): Euclidean conventions mostly apply; Descartes declares the unit segment addition. Adequate.

Score: 16/20 — strong demonstrative encoding with one systematic gap (algebraic steps assumed).

**Legibility: 13/15**

Context: contemplative study by a trained mathematician of the 17th century. Within that audience, the diagrams are highly legible — the Euclidean figure conventions are familiar; the algebraic notation is increasingly standard in Descartes's period. The contemporary audience (non-mathematicians, museum visitors) finds these diagrams less accessible, but the primary audience scores well.

**Visual Economy: 14/15**

Minimal: every mark is logically necessary. No decoration. The diagrams are highly economical within the demonstrative encoding criterion (logical necessity as mark-earning criterion). Slight dock for construction-aid lines that could be distinguished from the proved figure with more care.

**Resonance: 13/15**

Type V (Domain-Gated): a mathematician reading the proof that algebraic multiplication corresponds to geometric proportion feels the specific aesthetic of mathematical necessity. The moment when the algebra and the geometry coincide — when you see that the construction produces the same result as the algebraic operation — is a moment of genuine insight. This is the resonance of demonstration: not beauty, but rightness.

**Craft: 13/15**

Clean 17th-century mathematical engraving. Consistent line weights. Clear letter labels. Not as precisely executed as a modern mathematical diagram, but professional within its historical context.

## Summary

Kandinsky total: **86/100**

Descartes extends the school's grammar to a new boundary — the algebraic-geometric correspondence — and the diagrams show both the power and the cognitive cost of that extension. The dual-mode demonstrative demand (Innovation #191) is the most significant finding.
