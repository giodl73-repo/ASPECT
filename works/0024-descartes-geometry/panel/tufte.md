# Tufte Review — La Géométrie Diagrams (1637)

## Opening

Mathematical diagrams are the ideal case of data-ink ratio: every mark must be logically necessary, or it is a proof error. I said as much in my Euclid review (Innovation #149). Descartes meets this standard consistently. I have few economy complaints.

What I find more interesting in this work is the information density question: Descartes's diagrams routinely encode two representations simultaneously — geometric and algebraic — and force the viewer to hold both in mind at once. This is high-density encoding. Whether it is appropriate density depends on whether the dual representation is the argument (it is) or decorative (it is not).

## Information Density in Demonstrative Diagrams

The curve-equation correspondence diagrams are the densest in the work. A single figure shows:
- A geometric curve (the shape)
- A reference line (the proto-coordinate axis)
- Labeled distances from specific curve points to the reference line (the algebraic variables)
- Adjacent text containing the algebraic equation

The viewer must parse four distinct representational elements and perceive their correspondence. This is denser than any Euclidean diagram in the Elements — Euclid's figures encode one geometric argument at a time.

The density is appropriate because the argument IS the correspondence. To show the correspondence between a geometric curve and an algebraic equation, you must show both simultaneously. The high density serves the argument; it is not chartjunk.

Innovation #194: **Argument-mandated density in demonstrative encoding** — when the demonstration's argument requires showing two representational systems simultaneously (to demonstrate their correspondence), high mark density is intrinsically necessary rather than additive. The H amendment's "intrinsic complexity" category applies: density is mandated by the argument's structure, not by decorative choice. Score on excess above the mandated density; there is none in Descartes.

## The Step E Pathway for Dual-Mode Arguments

I want to flag a rubric gap here. Step E (demonstrative encoding pathway) has four sub-steps:
- E1: Logical foundation
- E2: Demonstration completeness
- E3: Logical validity
- E4: Diagrammatic framework declaration

These steps assume a single-mode demonstration: the argument is purely geometric (Euclid) or purely compositional (Kandinsky). Descartes's dual-mode demonstrations — showing the correspondence between geometric and algebraic modes — require the viewer to verify in both modes simultaneously. Step E3 (logical validity) should include: does the correspondence claim hold? Can the viewer verify that the geometric construction produces the same result as the algebraic operation?

This is implied by Step E3 but should be made explicit for dual-mode works.
