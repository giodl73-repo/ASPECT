# Kandinsky Review — VDQI Diagrams (1983)

## Opening

I am asked to assess whether Tufte's meta-visualization diagrams have internal compositional logic. I approach this differently than Bertin or Nightingale: I ask whether the compositions produce an intended effect through the arrangement of their visual elements — not through data encoding, but through the grammar of visual attention.

## Compositional Analysis

**The data-ink removal sequence:** This is the most compositionally sophisticated diagram type in the book. A series of progressively simplified bar charts reads left-to-right (or top-to-bottom). The compositional logic is kinetic: the viewer's eye moves through the sequence and the composition "moves" — becomes lighter, more open, more breathable — as elements are removed. The effect is not symbolic; it is visceral. You feel the chart becoming easier to read before you analyze why.

This is compositional argument: the sequence IS the proof. There is no text that could produce the same effect as the visual removal. The argument is available only in the visual mode.

**The annotated comparisons:** These have a more static compositional logic. The "bad" example and the "improved" example sit side by side. The annotation apparatus (arrows, callouts) directs attention. The compositional rhythm is: look here → read critique → look there → see improvement. This is a directed reading path of four steps, consistently applied across examples.

The directed reading path is a compositional grammar: every annotated comparison creates the same four-step movement. The viewer learns the grammar after the first example and follows it automatically thereafter.

**The self-exemplification:** The book's own layout — precise typography, productive margins, high data-ink ratio in diagrams — produces a consistent compositional tone. The visual weight is light (minimal marks), the rhythm is even (consistent margins, consistent type sizes), and the overall effect is that of careful thought made visible. The book feels like what Tufte's principles describe.

## The Unusual Resonance Type

Tufte's meta-visualization diagrams do not produce aesthetic resonance (Type I) in the conventional sense — they are not beautiful in the way that a Haeckel illustration is beautiful. They produce a different effect: **the resonance of being shown one's own errors** — the shock of recognition when you see a chart you might have made, and then see it improved, and understand immediately why it is better.

This resonance type depends on the viewer's prior experience with visualization errors. A viewer who has never made a cluttered bar chart does not feel the shock of recognition. A viewer who has made them regularly does. It is audience-prior-state-dependent, like wonder (Innovation #171 from work 0021) but in the inverse direction: wonder corrects false beliefs about the world; this corrects false practice.

Innovation #200: **Error-recognition resonance** — resonance arising from the viewer recognizing their own previous errors in a visualized example. Requires: (1) the viewer has prior experience of the error; (2) the visualization shows the error clearly enough to produce recognition; (3) the improvement is visible and immediately comprehensible. Distinct from advocacy resonance (which requires the viewer to be moved to action) and from wonder (which corrects a false belief about the world). Error-recognition resonance corrects false practice.

## Scoring

**School Fidelity (Type B): 16/20**

The information-architecture school is coherently founded. The compositional grammar (removal sequence; directed reading path; self-exemplification) is consistent and learnable. Dock for: the grammar is not formally declared in terms of compositional principles; it emerges from consistent practice rather than explicit statement.

**Data/Story Integrity: 15/20**

Demonstrative elements valid. Reportive elements with minor Step A gaps. Score: 15.

**Legibility: 14/15**

The directed reading path (look here → read → look there → see improvement) is highly legible once the viewer learns it after the first example.

**Visual Economy: 14/15**

High economy. The annotation apparatus is minimal and functional.

**Resonance: 14/15**

Error-recognition resonance (Innovation #200) is the primary type. Strong for the target audience. Additionally, Type III (advocacy efficacy) is well-achieved.

**Craft: 15/15**

The typography and layout are the book's best argument. Exceptional craft.

## Summary

Kandinsky total: **88/100**

The error-recognition resonance type (Innovation #200) is the most important finding. It is a resonance type that the rubric should formalize: it depends on viewer prior-state (the viewer must have made the error to recognize it), it corrects practice rather than belief, and it is specific to meta-visualization and pedagogy contexts.
