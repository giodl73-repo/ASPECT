# Playfair Review — VDQI Diagrams (1983)

## Opening

I am here because Tufte cannot review himself, and because I apparently invented the forms he is theorizing. That is a peculiar position to be in — my bar charts and line graphs are Tufte's Exhibit A for why statistical graphics works. He builds his theory on my practice. Now I review his theory.

I have opinions. I generally agree with him. I also think he takes some of my principles further than I would and uses my name as authority for positions I might have argued differently if I had tried to write them down in 1786.

## Does the Work Practice What It Preaches?

This is the central School Fidelity question for a self-exemplifying information-architecture work. Tufte argues that:
- Non-data ink should be minimized
- Data density should be maximized
- Small multiples are superior to animation
- Legends should be replaced by direct labeling when possible
- Typography should be precise and consistent

His book satisfies all of these. The typography IS precise and consistent. The margins are used for data-dense annotations. The diagrams are stripped of decoration. The book's design is a demonstration of its own argument.

This is a genuine virtue — most books about design do not practice what they preach at this level. Tufte does. I credit this.

## Where I Would Have Done It Differently

**The lie factor claims are stronger than the evidence.** Tufte calculates lie factors for specific published graphics and presents them as demonstrations that the graphics distort reality. The lie factor is a ratio (visual change / data change). The calculation is correct. But: what counts as the "visual change"? Tufte measures by area, sometimes by height, sometimes by the most charitable or uncharitable reading. The lie factor is not a mechanical calculation; it depends on choices about what to measure. Those choices are not always disclosed.

This is a Step A variable-selection problem: the lie factor calculation embeds choices that are not declared. For some examples, a different measurement choice would produce a different lie factor. The principle (misleading graphics distort visual magnitude) is correct; the specific calculations are less certain than their presentation implies.

**The data-ink ratio removal sequences do prove their point** — removing chartjunk does improve the chart. I find this genuinely persuasive. The demonstrative sequence is well-constructed.

**The annotated Minard analysis** is the strongest passage in the book. Tufte shows what makes the Napoleon March map great. I agree with his analysis, and my agreement comes from someone who has worked with similar comparative data.

## Scoring

**School Fidelity (Type B — school founder): 17/20**

The information-architecture school is established with coherent, learnable principles. The grammar is declared in-work (each chapter states its principle). The work is self-exemplifying — the book demonstrates its own principles in its own design. This is a strong founding act. I dock slightly for: the grammar's scope (does it apply to all visualization, or only to statistical graphics?) is not fully resolved; some claims (lie factor) may be overstated.

**Data/Story Integrity: 15/20**

Mixed demonstrative/reportive encoding. Demonstrative sequences (data-ink removal, erasing) are well-constructed and valid. Reportive elements (the lie factor examples from published graphics) have a Step A gap: measurement choices for visual change are not always declared. Step C: causal claim that the specific lie factor values imply specific degrees of deception may be slightly overstated. Score: 15.

**Legibility: 14/15**

Context: contemplative study by practicing data visualizers and graphic designers. The book is designed for exactly this audience and context. Highly legible. The principles are stated before demonstrated; the demonstrations are clear; the annotated comparisons are parseable without the text. A professional coming to this book with graphic design knowledge will find it immediately accessible.

**Visual Economy: 14/15**

The book practices what it preaches. High data-ink ratio throughout. Margins used productively for annotations and secondary examples. Decorative marks: essentially zero. The economy is the argument.

**Resonance: 14/15**

Type III (Advocacy-Efficacy): the book changed statistical graphics practice. It sold over two million copies; it influenced a generation of data journalists, academic researchers, and information designers. The advocacy goal (reform how people make charts) was achieved at scale. This is the strongest Type III advocacy efficacy in the project for a non-political work.

**Craft: 15/15**

The book's craft is exceptional and historically documented: Tufte reportedly made significant financial investment in ensuring the printing quality matched his typographic standards. The Bembo typeface is precisely set. The diagram printing is clean. This is the highest craft investment of any non-illustrated work in the project.

## Summary

Playfair total: **89/100**

Tufte built a school on my foundations and did it more rigorously than I did. I find that irritating and appropriate. The book is correct in its principles, slightly overconfident in some of its calculations, and exceptionally well-made. A founding act.
