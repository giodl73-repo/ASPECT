# Kandinsky — 0026 The DEGAS Innovations Log as Visual Texture

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

The work is a genuine data art piece. It uses data as material and refuses to subordinate itself to the reading demands of statistical graphics. The hanging-column structure — columns suspended from a common top edge, varying in depth by innovation density — is a real compositional choice, not a chart convention dressed up. The six mark types (circle, diamond, dash, bar, asterisk, triangle) form a coherent vocabulary of small geometric forms at similar visual weight. They are not arbitrary.

What it lacks for the upper band: the compositional logic of the mark vocabulary is not internally declared. Bertin will say this, and I agree from a different angle: I can see that circle and diamond are different, but the system of choosing them — why these six shapes for these six dimensions — is not evident from the composition itself. The choice feels externally motivated (by a rubric table) rather than internally motivated (by what these marks want to be). A stronger work would have the mark shapes express something about their dimension: Resonance as asterisk (*) is good — it radiates. School Fidelity as circle is defensible. But Craft as triangle lacks internal necessity.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

The encoding is honest. Each mark is one innovation; the position encodes work number and ordinal position within that work; the color encodes cluster family; the mark type encodes dimension. Nothing is falsified. The aggregate pattern — dense warm center, cooler periphery — is a true representation of where the rubric's innovations concentrated.

Minor dock: the vertical spacing within columns (innovations stacked in a 3-per-row grid) is a layout choice, not a data encoding. The vertical position within a column encodes the ordinal number of the innovation within that work, but does not encode the date, the dimension, or the cluster strength. This is appropriate for data art, but a careful viewer might assume ordinal position carries more meaning than it does.

## Legibility /15 → 11

Primary context: gallery display, contemplative study. The viewer who spends three minutes with the legend panel can decode dimension and cluster family from individual marks. The viewer who does not will receive the gestalt — an irregular field of small colored marks, dense at center-left, sparse at right. Both readings are real. The separate legend is the right choice: it preserves the visual field from annotation clutter.

The separation of legend from image is slightly risky. A viewer who misses the legend entirely has no in-image key. For a data art work, this is acceptable; for a work that claims to represent real innovation data, it slightly undermines the full reading.

## Visual Economy /15 → 14

This is where the school's criterion inversion applies. In data art, the relevant question is not "how many marks per datum?" but "does every visual choice serve the aesthetic-data relationship?" By that criterion, this work is economical. The palette is muted; there is no decoration; the mark types are minimal geometric forms. Nothing in the field is superfluous.

I give a 14 rather than 15 because the 3-per-row grid layout inside columns creates regularity that feels slightly mechanical. The overall hanging-column structure has organic irregularity; the internal grid fights it. A more composed arrangement of marks within each column — closer to a natural scatter within the column's bounds — would have served the organic accumulation effect better.

## Resonance /15 → 13

Type I (Aesthetic-Intentional) is clearly the primary type. The work creates the intended effect: a viewer who knows the DEGAS project experiences recognition — "yes, this is what 202 innovations look like." The dense warm center corresponds to the period of most intense rubric development; the cooling and thinning toward the right corresponds to the rubric's maturation. This is a portrait of intellectual activity, and it reads that way.

The hanging-column cityscape is the strongest compositional choice in the work. It produces the "natural formation" feeling the designer intended. That is not nothing.

## Craft /15 → 12

The design specification calls for precise execution that I cannot fault in principle: flat fill, consistent mark sizes, controlled spacing, geometric type. The muted palette is well-chosen. My concern is execution fidelity — a 6-pointed asterisk at 6pt is difficult to render cleanly, and at that scale the mark distinction between asterisk and circle may collapse under poor reproduction. The specification is right; the execution risk is real.

## Innovation Flagged

**#203 — Data art economy criterion inversion**: the data art school requires a different economy test than statistical graphics. The relevant question is not data-ink ratio but "does every visual choice serve the aesthetic-data relationship?" This should be formalized in the rubric's Visual Economy step for data art works. The current rubric's Cluster H amendment (intrinsic complexity) partially addresses this, but data art's aesthetic-primary intention is a distinct case.
