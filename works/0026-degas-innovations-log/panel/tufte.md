# Tufte — 0026 The DEGAS Innovations Log as Visual Texture

## School Fidelity /20 → 15

I will not apply my statistical graphics standard here, because the claim is data art, not statistical graphics. The data art school has its own claim: aesthetic primacy, data as material, legibility negotiable. I assess whether the work is a genuine instance of that school.

It is. The design refuses the chart conventions I would prefer (labeled axes, numerical annotation, direct comparison) and does so with intention, not negligence. The mark vocabulary is constrained; the palette is controlled; the hanging-column structure is a genuine compositional choice. This is data art, not a failed bar chart.

Minor dock: the grid arrangement of marks within columns reveals a designer who has not fully committed to the aesthetic-primary position. A grid is a chart convention. If the goal is texture, the marks should accumulate with less regularity.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 14

Each mark represents one innovation. That is honest. The color encoding of cluster membership is honest. The position encoding of work number is honest. The vertical ordering within columns (ordinal position) is honest.

What I note, and would note more severely in any other school: the viewer cannot verify any of this. The 202 marks are not individually labeled. The cluster assignments are summarized into three color families, losing resolution. I accept this as appropriate to the school — data art does not promise individual readback. But I dock slightly for the loss of precision that makes the work unverifiable to a careful viewer who does not have access to the underlying innovation log.

## Legibility /15 → 9

In the primary reading context (gallery, contemplative study), a patient viewer who reads the wall label, locates the separate legend, and studies the image can achieve partial decoding: "these marks are innovations; the warm ones are in adopted clusters; the dense columns are the early high-innovation works." Full decoding to specific dimension and cluster is not achievable without the log itself.

This is the school's stated trade. I accept it. But I note it as a significant legibility limitation. The average gallery visitor will not have 10 minutes and will not locate the separate legend. They will experience the texture and depart.

## Visual Economy /15 → 9

Here I must make my objection explicit. The data-ink ratio of this work is extremely low. Each mark is one innovation, yes — but the mark is 6pt, and the white ground surrounding each mark is enormous by comparison. The total marks area is perhaps 5% of the image area. On my standard, 95% of the image surface is empty and therefore wasted.

I understand that the data art school inverts this logic. Kandinsky will say that the negative space is doing compositional work, and he is right. But I want to name what is being given up: a viewer who wanted to understand the DEGAS innovation log would find this work far less efficient than a table, a small multiple, or even a simple bar chart. The aesthetic choice has a real informational cost.

I apply the school's criterion (does every visual choice serve the aesthetic-data relationship?) and score accordingly — a 9, not the 5 I would assign on data-ink grounds alone.

## Resonance /15 → 11

The work produces its intended effect for the DEGAS-familiar viewer. The portrait of intellectual activity is recognizable to those who know the system. For a general viewer, the effect is more diffuse — a patterned field, interesting, somewhat abstract. The hanging-column structure suggests depth and accumulation without revealing it.

The stronger resonance effect would come from a work that could be read more completely. As it stands, the resonance is partial — the texture is felt, but the specific recognition (this cluster, this work, this wave of innovation) is available only to insiders.

## Craft /15 → 12

The specification is precise and the palette choices are defensible. The muted, slightly desaturated colors are right for the contemplative register. The separate legend panel is clean. My concern is the small-size rendering of complex mark shapes (asterisk, diamond, triangle at 6pt), which is a real execution risk.

## My Total: 70/100

I score this significantly lower than Kandinsky will, and I want to be transparent about why: I am applying the data art school's own criterion (aesthetic-data relationship) rather than my preferred criterion (data-ink ratio), and I still find the work leaves significant value on the table. The grid within columns is the easiest fix. The fundamental trade — aesthetic texture over legible chart — is the school's choice, and I respect it, but it has real costs that the rubric should name.

## Innovation Flagged

**#205 — Data art as rubric edge case: the economy criterion inversion problem**: the Visual Economy dimension as written does not fully address data art's aesthetic-primary logic. Cluster H (intrinsic complexity) provides a partial model, but data art's complexity is aesthetic-intentional, not subject-matter-intrinsic. The rubric needs a Step 0c for Visual Economy: "Is this a data art work claiming aesthetic-primary intention? If so, apply the aesthetic-data relationship criterion rather than the data-ink ratio criterion." Without this, data art works will consistently be penalized for choices that are correct within their school.
