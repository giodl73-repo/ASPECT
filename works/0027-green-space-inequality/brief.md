# Brief — 0027 Green Space Inequality in 10 US Cities

## The Story

Urban green space inequality is simultaneously a public health crisis, a climate justice issue, and a design failure. Low-income neighborhoods — disproportionately Black and Latino — have 34% less tree canopy coverage than high-income neighborhoods in the same city. The health consequences are measurable: heat-related mortality, respiratory disease, mental health outcomes all worsen with declining canopy. The cause is not nature; it is decades of policy decisions that actively stripped green space from certain neighborhoods while subsidizing it in others.

The design prescription: the 3-30-300 rule (every resident should see 3 trees from home, live in a neighborhood with 30% canopy, be within 300m of a park) is a specific, actionable, and evidence-based standard. No city fully meets it. The argument is that meeting it is achievable and necessary.

## Narrative Architecture

Five sections, progressive disclosure, scroll-triggered web format:

1. Opening (no chart) — satellite image of a block with heat overlay; a single resident's experience
2. City comparison (bar chart) — 10 cities, canopy % by income quartile
3. Neighborhood heat map — Chicago canopy + temperature, neighborhood level
4. Historical cause (timeline) — canopy growth/loss 1970-2023 for 3 cities
5. Design prescription — 3-30-300 rule as visual checklist
