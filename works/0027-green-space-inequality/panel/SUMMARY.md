## Key Agreements

**The narrative structure is complete and correct.** All five reviewers recognize the five-section sequence as a well-designed advocacy argument. The aha moment in Section 4 (redlining annotation on the timeline) is the most praised element. Nightingale, Du Bois, and Audience all note that the causal explanation arrives at the right moment in the narrative sequence.

**The prescription (Section 5) is the strongest element.** The 3-30-300 checklist is legible, actionable, and economical. The Audience lens notes it is the most immediately graspable section. Nightingale notes it satisfies her core criterion: "What should the viewer do after seeing this?"

**The satellite heat image (Section 1) is the right opening.** All reviewers accept that the emotional stakes must be established before statistics arrive. The infrared heat overlay stops the scroll.

## Key Tensions

**Rosling vs. Nightingale on the Section 4 timeline:**

Rosling wants more temporal granularity — annual or decadal data, an animation, more path-visibility than 6 points over 50 years. Nightingale accepts the 6-point line as sufficient advocacy evidence. These produce different recommendations: Rosling says "show the full change"; Nightingale says "show the change that is enough to establish the cause."

**Du Bois vs. Rosling on variable selection:**

Du Bois argues race should be encoded as a visual variable in Section 2, not just named in Section 4's text annotations. Rosling does not address this directly but would likely agree that race as an explicit variable would strengthen the trend argument. Du Bois's innovation (#209) identifies a rubric gap: the distinction between encoding race visually vs. naming it in text is not assessed by Data/Story Integrity Step A.
