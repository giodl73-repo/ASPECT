# Audience Lens — 0027 Green Space Inequality in 10 US Cities

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

I am the person who scrolls through a web essay on their phone during a commute, reads the opening headline, pauses at the heat image, and decides whether to keep scrolling. I kept scrolling.

The opening satellite image with heat overlay stopped me. I did not know what the colors meant precisely (I assumed hot = bad) but I could see the orange block and the cooler block side by side and the headline told me what I was looking at. I kept scrolling.

The bar chart in Section 2 took me about 20 seconds to understand — the sequential green bars growing from short (Q1) to tall (Q4) in each city cluster is intuitive once you read the legend. The 30% red dashed line made me count how many bars cleared it. Almost none. That landed.

The heat map in Section 3 is where I slowed down. Two layers plus three callout labels is a lot to hold at once. I read the callout labels first (Englewood, Hyde Park, Lincoln Park), then tried to match them to the map colors, then read the temperature numbers. This took maybe 2 minutes and I am a patient reader. A less patient reader stops here.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

I cannot verify the data sources but the information feels grounded and specific. City names, neighborhood names, actual percentages, real policy acts (Housing Community Development Act, 1973). The specificity creates trust. The causal argument (redlining caused the canopy gap) is stated confidently and I believe it because the evidence is sequential and logical.

## Legibility /15 → 13

For a general web audience in contemplative study context, this is well-calibrated. Each section has introductory text that tells me what I am about to see. The Section 5 checklist is the most immediately legible — I understand the 3-30-300 rule in about 10 seconds. The bar chart (Section 2) is legible in 20-30 seconds. The heat map (Section 3) takes 2 minutes. The timeline (Section 4) takes 1-2 minutes.

Scroll-through for a rushed reader: the headline from each section is visible above the chart before the chart is fully in view. A rushed reader who reads only the headlines gets: "canopy gap is structural" / "it follows the geography of historical disinvestment" / "trees that were lost were not replanted" / "the choice is political." That is a coherent argument delivered in 15 seconds. Good design.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

The five sections feel complete, not padded. I never felt the essay lingering when it should move. The transitions between sections are smooth — the text above each chart serves as a bridge between the previous section's conclusion and the current section's evidence.

The illustration icons in Section 5 (the stylized trees etc.) made me smile. They are not necessary but they make the prescription feel human rather than clinical. For an essay that is asking for political action, a little warmth at the end is good.

## Resonance /15 → 14

The closing text landed. "The choice is political" is not a complicated conclusion — it does not ask me to do cognitive work. It gives me a frame for what I have just seen. I arrived at the end of this essay feeling that I knew something I had not known when I started, and feeling that the thing I now know is important and actionable.

That is what I want from a scroll essay. Not just information — transformation of my picture of the situation, with a clear sense of what should be done next.

## Craft /15 → 13

The essay reads as professionally made. The typography is consistent, the chart styles are coherent, the color palette carries across sections. The satellite image is visually striking. The closing feels deliberate.

## My Total: 85/100

This is a well-made scroll essay. I understood the argument, I followed the evidence, I arrived at the prescription feeling that it was earned. My one note as an audience representative: the heat map (Section 3) is the hardest section for a general reader. Consider a guided annotation sequence that reveals the callout labels one at a time rather than all simultaneously — this would reduce the cognitive load of that section significantly.
