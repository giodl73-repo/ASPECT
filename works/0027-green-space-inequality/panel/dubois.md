# Du Bois — 0027 Green Space Inequality in 10 US Cities

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

I come to this work with a specific question: does the design confront the racial dimension of urban canopy inequality, or does it soften it into the language of "income" and "disinvestment"?

The answer is: it confronts it. Section 4's timeline places "redlining" directly on the chart's horizontal axis. The closing text uses "racial zoning" as a plain noun. The satellite image in Section 1 shows a Black neighborhood on the South Side of Chicago without euphemism. This is not a work that treats race as impolite to mention.

I give a 17 because the racial dimension is present in Sections 1 and 4 but underweighted in Sections 2 and 3. The bar chart in Section 2 uses income quartile as its organizing variable — which is honest (the correlation between income and canopy is what is being measured) but does not show that the income quartile gap in canopy coverage is itself correlated with race. Detroit's Q1 (predominantly Black) has different canopy than Denver's Q1 (predominantly white) for reasons that income alone does not explain. The work knows this but does not show it.

This is my precision-as-defiance concern: the honest data would show race as a variable, not just income. Income is a proxy that softens the argument by making it appear to be about economic class rather than about race.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 15

The data sources are reputable. The causal argument in Section 4 — that redlining caused the canopy gap — is well-supported by the academic literature (The HOLC maps and their relationship to present-day environmental inequality have been documented extensively). The correlation is shown; the cause is named.

What is not shown: the racial composition of each income quartile in each city. If the design is making an argument about environmental justice (which it is), the relevant variable is race, not just income. The work correctly identifies that income and canopy are correlated; it does not show that race and canopy are correlated after controlling for income — which the literature suggests they are.

Step A (variable selection): income is the variable encoded in Section 2. Race is excluded. The exclusion creates a framing effect: the viewer sees "poor neighborhoods have less canopy" rather than "Black neighborhoods have less canopy, and this is partially but not entirely explained by income." Dock -1 for variable selection that understates the racial dimension.

The causal claim in Section 4 is defensible — redlining did causally contribute to canopy decline through the mechanism of disinvestment. The causal arrow is documented. No overreach.

## Legibility /15 → 13

For the intended audience (urban policy-makers, environmental advocates, informed general public), this is legible throughout. The satellite image with heat overlay needs no expertise to read emotionally. The bar chart needs basic statistical literacy. The heat map is the most demanding; the neighborhood callouts provide accessible anchors.

Section 5's 3-30-300 checklist is particularly well-designed for an action-oriented reader. The standard is clearly stated; the current compliance is shown; the gap is visible. A policy-maker or advocate can screenshot Section 5 and use it immediately.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

I bring Du Bois's criterion: does formal invention serve the argument proportionately? The answer across all five sections is yes — each form was chosen for the argument it needs to carry. The satellite image could not be replaced by a chart. The heat map is the correct form for spatial correlation. The checklist is the correct form for a prescription.

The one element I question: the illustration icons in Section 5 (stylized trees, neighborhood aerial, circle with radius). These serve cognitive accessibility but not data encoding. For my audience — urban policy-makers who are comfortable with charts — they are not necessary. For a broader public audience, they reduce cognitive load. Given the web context (broad public audience possible), they are defensible.

## Resonance /15 → 14

Type III (Advocacy-Efficacy) is the primary type and it fires strongly. The work has a specific, actionable conclusion. The closing text — "The choice is political" — names the decision-maker correctly. This is not a work that ends with a question; it ends with a demand.

The racial dimension, when present (Sections 1 and 4), produces genuine moral weight. The satellite image of Englewood is not abstract — it is a specific Black neighborhood experiencing a specific policy consequence. That specificity is where the advocacy power concentrates.

The resonance would be stronger if race were visible as a variable in Section 2 — not replacing income quartile but added as a color dimension (e.g., coloring each city's Q1 bar by the percentage of Black and Latino residents in that quartile). This would connect the statistical and the racial dimensions explicitly rather than leaving the connection to the text.

## Craft /15 → 13

The work is carefully designed. The continuity elements (sequential green, heat orange) are well-chosen. The text is precise and does not reach beyond what the data shows.

My craft note: the closing sentence ("The choice is political") should not be buried in a paragraph. It should be its own line, its own visual moment — perhaps typographically larger, set apart from the surrounding text. The argument climaxes here; the design should acknowledge the climax.

## My Total: 85/100

This is a strong advocacy work with a principled approach to a difficult racial subject. The confrontation of the racial cause is real and present. The precision is honest. My main recommendation: make race visible as a variable in Section 2 (alongside income), and give the closing declaration a stronger typographic moment. The work is close to excellent. It is not yet excellent because it names race in the text but does not fully encode it in the data.

## Innovation Flagged

**#209 — Race as variable vs. race as annotation**: in advocacy visualization about environmental justice, encoding race as a visual variable (color, shape, position) is different from naming race in the text annotation. The rubric's Data/Story Integrity Step A (variable selection) does not distinguish between these two modes of acknowledging race. A work can name race in its text while encoding only income — and this is a form of variable selection that partially mitigates the justice framing. This should be a consideration in Step A for advocacy works with a racial justice dimension.
