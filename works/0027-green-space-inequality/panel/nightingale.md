# Nightingale — 0027 Green Space Inequality in 10 US Cities

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

This is narrative visualization done correctly. The five-section scroll structure is not decorative — each section has a specific argumentative function, and the sequence builds the case systematically. The opening satellite image is the right move: it establishes emotional stakes before the statistics arrive, so the viewer is already engaged when the data appears. This is the lesson I learned with the coxcomb: statistics must earn their place in an already-activated emotional field.

The aha moment is well-designed. Section 4's timeline, with the redlining annotations placed directly on the year axis, produces exactly the recognition I would want: "I can see when the canopy declined and I can read why." The historical cause and the current consequence are joined by the scroll sequence itself. That is narrative visualization working as it should.

Minor dock: the transition between Section 3 (Chicago heat map) and Section 4 (historical timeline) could be tighter. The heat map shows the current spatial pattern; the timeline shows the historical cause. The connection between them is made in the text, not in the visualization. A single transition visualization — perhaps the current neighborhood colors from Section 3 appearing as the starting point of the Section 4 timeline lines — would strengthen the causal link.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

The data sources (USDA FIA 2021, NOAA/NASA LST) are reputable. The income quartile comparison is correctly structured: the same metric (canopy %) across the same population breakdown (income quartile) across comparable cities. The 3-30-300 standard's 30% line in Section 2 is an honest reference — the viewer can immediately see how far short each city's lowest quartile falls.

Section 4's historical timeline uses estimated data from aerial photo analysis and USDA records, which is appropriate and declared. The limitation is that aerial photo analysis of canopy cover in 1970-1990 has meaningful uncertainty at the neighborhood level. This should be noted — even a brief footnote or annotation uncertainty note would satisfy Step A.

The framing of Section 4 as cause-and-effect (redlining caused the canopy decline) is stated strongly. The correlation is real and well-documented; the causal mechanism (disinvestment reducing maintenance and planting budgets) is plausible and stated. Step C applies: causation implied by structural framing, dock -1. Not a material problem for advocacy visualization, but honest reviewers should note it.

## Legibility /15 → 13

Context: standalone web, contemplative study. The five-section structure is clear and the scroll progression is intuitive. Each section has its own text introduction that tells the viewer what they are about to see. The 3-30-300 prescription in Section 5 is highly legible — the three-panel layout with large numbers and direct text makes the standard immediately graspable.

The Chicago heat map (Section 3) is the most demanding section. Two overlaid variables (canopy gradient + temperature dot density) with three callout annotations requires sustained attention. For a scroll-driven essay in the contemplative study context, this is appropriate — the viewer has the time. For a rushed reader, this section may lose them.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

Five heterogeneous visualization forms across five sections is ambitious. The economy question is whether each section's form is the most efficient form for its argumentative function. Section 1 (satellite image): yes — no chart would achieve the emotional impact of the heat overlay. Section 2 (bar chart): yes — canopy comparison across cities and quartiles is the bar chart's native task. Section 3 (heat map): yes — spatial patterns require spatial encoding. Section 4 (timeline): yes — historical change requires temporal encoding. Section 5 (checklist): yes — a prescription requires a format that implies action.

Minor dock: Section 5's checklist illustration icons add visual weight without encoding data. They serve a cognitive accessibility function (the icon reinforces the meaning of each number) but are not strictly necessary for an educated web audience.

## Resonance /15 → 14

Type III (Advocacy-Efficacy) is the primary type. Does this work move its audience toward support for urban green space investment? I believe it does — and the mechanism is well-constructed. The viewer is moved emotionally (Section 1), shown the scale of the problem (Section 2), shown its local specificity (Section 3), given a causal explanation (Section 4), and handed an actionable standard (Section 5). This is complete advocacy structure.

The closing text ("The choice is political") is exactly right. It does not let the reader retreat into "this is complicated" or "it's a budget problem." It names the political decision that is required.

## Craft /15 → 13

Five visualization types in five sections creates significant craft demands. The consistency requirements — typography, color palette, annotation style — must be maintained across heterogeneous forms. The specification is sound on this: the sequential green palette for income quartiles carries across Sections 2 and 5, creating visual continuity. The heat overlay colors (orange-red) connect Section 1 and the temperature layer in Section 3.

## My Total: 86/100

This is a strong advocacy narrative visualization. The structure is complete, the aha moment is well-timed, and the prescription is specific and actionable. The main revision need is strengthening the visual connection between Section 3 (spatial pattern) and Section 4 (historical cause).

## Innovation Flagged

**#207 — The multi-form narrative and cross-section visual continuity**: in a multi-section narrative visualization using different visualization forms per section, color palette and visual variable continuity across sections is a form of narrative coherence that functions like prose style — it tells the viewer they are still in the same argument. The rubric does not currently assess cross-section coherence in multi-form narratives. This is a School Fidelity consideration unique to narrative visualization.
