# Tufte — 0027 Green Space Inequality in 10 US Cities

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

Narrative visualization is a legitimate school and this work applies it correctly. The five-section scroll structure has a clear sequence logic; each section advances the argument; the text annotations are integral, not decorative. I apply the school's criteria, not mine.

My fidelity concern: the school's constitutive principle of "annotation as narration" is well-applied in Sections 2 and 4 (the bar chart and timeline have direct, minimal annotations that advance the argument). Section 3's callout annotations in the heat map are slightly over-annotated — three callouts is one too many for a single map. The viewer's eye should know where to go without being told three times.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

The data sources are reputable. The causal argument is defensible. I note one concern: the satellite heat overlay in Section 1 is a false-color infrared composite, and its relationship to the air temperature experienced at street level is indirect. The 8-degree F difference mentioned in the subtext refers to land surface temperature (LST), not ambient air temperature. At street level, the difference is real but smaller. This precision matters and should be declared in a footnote or annotation note.

Step A: data source declared (USDA FIA 2021, NOAA/NASA LST). Good. The LST-vs-ambient distinction should be noted.

## Legibility /15 → 13

The bar chart in Section 2 is well-designed by my standards: vertical bars, direct labeling on selected bars, a single reference line (30%), no background fill. This is clean work. The timeline in Section 4 is also clean — three directly labeled lines, six data points, two annotated events.

The heat map in Section 3 is the legibility risk in this essay. Two overlaid visual variables plus three callout labels is at the edge of what a casual web reader can process. For the contemplative study context, this is defensible.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

The bar chart (Section 2) and timeline (Section 4) are economical. The satellite image (Section 1) is justified by its rhetorical function — no chart would achieve the same impact. The heat map (Section 3) is complex but the complexity is intrinsic to the two-variable argument it must carry.

Section 5's illustration icons are the main chartjunk risk. They add visual weight without encoding data. For this audience and context, I accept them as cognitive accessibility marks — but they are on the borderline.

Overall: five sections, five forms, and only one element (Section 5 icons) that borders on unnecessary complexity. This is economical for its ambition.

## Resonance /15 → 12

The work makes an argument and the argument lands. The closing text is appropriately direct. My mild reservation: "The choice is political" is a strong conclusion but it floats slightly — political decision by whom? For what? The most effective advocacy conclusions specify the decision-maker ("City council members who vote on the urban forestry budget") rather than invoking politics abstractly.

Nightingale would agree with me on this point.

## Craft /15 → 13

The specification is precise and the design is consistent. The heat map execution requires careful attention to the two-layer overlay — the dot density and the value gradient must not fight each other. The annotation callout style must be consistent across sections.

## My Total: 83/100

A well-constructed scroll essay with strong execution across five heterogeneous sections. The satellite LST-vs-ambient distinction should be declared. The heat map callouts could be reduced to two. Section 5 icons are borderline but acceptable. The closing could be more specific about the decision-maker.

## Innovation Flagged

**#210 — Multi-form narrative coherence as a school fidelity criterion**: a narrative visualization using five different visualization forms must maintain visual and argumentative coherence across those forms. The rubric currently assesses School Fidelity for the primary encoding form; it does not assess whether the cross-form transitions serve the narrative logic. A multi-form narrative that switches forms without argumentative motivation is a school fidelity failure of a different kind than applying a single form incorrectly.
