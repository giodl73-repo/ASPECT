# Audience Lens — 0028 The DEGAS Rubric as a System Map

## School Fidelity /20 → 14

I am someone who is familiar with the DEGAS project — perhaps a regular reviewer or observer — but I am not an expert in information architecture diagrams. I see three horizontal bands with nodes and lines, and a reading direction arrow. I read the arrow: "works generate clusters; clusters amend dimensions." I look at the diagram and see that the bottom layer has many small circles (works), the middle layer has medium circles (clusters), and the top layer has rectangles (dimensions). This matches the arrow's description.

I can follow the logic. I understand that lines going up mean "generates" or "amends." I can see that some works have more lines than others — the ones on the left (early canonical imports) send more lines upward than the ones on the right (later works). I experience this as: "the early works were more important for defining the rubric." Whether that is the correct interpretation I am not entirely sure, but it feels right.

The feedback loop annotation on the right tells me that dimensions then shape how future works are scored, completing the loop. I believe this but I cannot see it in the main diagram — the loop does not close visually. For me, as a non-expert, the absence of a visible closing loop leaves the "system" feeling more like a hierarchy than a cycle.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 15

I trust the documented facts (cluster-to-dimension arrows, work-to-cluster lines). The correlation lines in Layer 1 are harder for me to evaluate — I do not know how "thick=strong correlation" was determined. I take it on faith.

## Legibility /15 → 10

For me, the diagram is legible in its large structure (three layers, reading direction) but opaque in its detail (which work is 0001? which cluster is K? where does the line from 0001 go?). The 6-7pt type labels are too small for me to read at normal viewing distance. I need to lean in very close to read specific work or cluster identities.

The color-coding is helpful: I can see that the thick line from work 0001 is in deep blue (School Fidelity) and follows it upward to Cluster A, B, C (early blue/green cluster nodes). This tracing works because the colors carry across layers. But I can only trace by color, not by label, because the labels are too small.

## Visual Economy /15 → 12

The diagram does not feel padded. Everything visible seems to be there for a reason. The feedback loop annotation is clearly separated from the main diagram and explains the reading logic I needed.

## Resonance /15 → 12

The recognition effect is real for me as a DEGAS participant: I see the rubric's development made visible as a structure. The early canonical imports (the dense left side of Layer 3) really were the most generative period. Seeing that visually is satisfying.

The feedback loop — that the diagram represents a self-improving system — does not land as strongly for me from the main diagram alone. The right-margin annotation helps, but the loop is described in text, not shown visually. For the resonance to fully land, I think the loop needs to close visually in the diagram.

## Craft /15 → 12

The work is clearly professionally conceived. The small-type labels are the main craft concern for a non-expert viewer.

## My Total: 75/100

As a DEGAS-familiar non-expert, I find this diagram accessible in its large structure and opaque in its detail. The reading direction mechanism works; the color-coding mechanism works; the label legibility at small type sizes does not work well for me. The most important revision for my reading: close the feedback loop visually in the main diagram, and increase the label size or provide a separate index of work numbers and cluster names.
