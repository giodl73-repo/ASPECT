# Kandinsky — 0028 The DEGAS Rubric as a System Map

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

I approach this diagram as a composition first. Three horizontal bands of different visual weight, reading bottom-to-top, with lines connecting all three. This is a compositional claim: the visual structure enacts the system structure. The bottom layer (works, small nodes, many lines) is visually dense and varied — appropriate for the most generative level of the system. The middle layer (clusters, medium circles, fewer but thicker arrows) is transitional. The top layer (dimensions, rectangular nodes, horizontal correlation lines) is ordered and structured — appropriate for the system's most formal level.

The compositional rhythm is correct. The bottom-to-top reading direction mirrors the system's bottom-up causality. The visual weight decreases from bottom to top (more marks at bottom, fewer at top), which is the correct representation of the relationship: many works generate fewer clusters which define even fewer dimensions. The diagram has a funnel shape that is compositionally coherent.

My fidelity concern: the correlation lines in Layer 1 (the bezier arcs connecting the six dimension nodes) are visually the warmest element in the composition — their curves interrupt the horizontal-and-vertical discipline of the rest of the diagram. In information architecture, this would be a grammar matter (curves vs. straight lines); for me, it is a compositional question: does the curve serve the layer's character, or does it introduce tension that does not resolve? I believe the curves are correct — they visually separate the correlation relationships from the causal relationships (straight-line arrows), and their warmth in the top layer provides a visual counterpoint to the dense bottom layer.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

The diagram represents a real system. I am not Bertin and will not conduct a full variable audit, but I note that the elements I can verify (cluster-to-dimension connections, work-to-cluster connections) correspond to documented facts in the DEGAS project record. The color palette faithfully carries the six dimension identities across all three layers.

The one element I cannot independently verify: the correlation line thicknesses in Layer 1. The specification claims these encode "observed cross-dimension interactions from the panel data" — but I cannot see the panel data from here. I trust that Tufte will flag this if it is a problem. From my angle: the correlation claims are compositionally appropriate (they add structure to the top layer without claiming more than they can support), but their empirical basis should be stated.

## Legibility /15 → 12

Context: contemplative study, academic practitioner audience. The three layers are visually distinct; the reading direction is declared; the color-continuity mechanism connects layers. A viewer who spends 5-10 minutes with this diagram can trace specific paths and understand the system's causal structure.

The density of the bottom layer (25 work nodes with multiple upward lines each) is the legibility risk. In the densest area (left side of Layer 3, where canonical imports triggered multiple clusters), the upward lines may overlap and be difficult to trace individually. The color-coding helps but does not fully resolve this in the densest areas.

Compositional suggestion: the transition zone between Layer 3 and Layer 2 (where the work lines enter the cluster layer) is where the density peaks. A slight expansion of the vertical gap between layers at this point would allow the lines to spread before reaching cluster nodes, reducing the convergence density.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

The diagram is economical for its complexity. The feedback loop annotation in the right margin is well-handled — it provides the meta-reading key without cluttering the main composition. The reading direction arrow is minimal and necessary.

My compositional economy concern: the small-type labels (6-7pt) require the viewer to lean in close to read them. This creates a two-distance reading: the composition reads at distance (three layers, color patterns, line density); the labels read close (work numbers, cluster names, dimension labels). Two-distance reading is appropriate for this work — it is designed for contemplative study — but the transitions between the two distances should feel natural, not jarring.

## Resonance /15 → 13

The diagram produces a genuine recognition for anyone who knows the DEGAS project. The rubric's development is not just a list of amendments — it is a system with shape. The three-layer structure gives that shape a visual form. This is the most important thing the diagram does, and it does it well.

The feedback loop annotation captures the system's self-referential quality more explicitly than the main diagram does. These two elements together — the main diagram showing the static structure, the annotation showing the dynamic process — give the composition two voices, which is compositionally interesting. The tension between the static structure of the main diagram and the dynamic loop annotation is productive.

## Craft /15 → 13

Precise execution at multiple scales (large dimension nodes, medium cluster nodes, small work nodes, very small labels) with bezier arcs, straight-line arrows, and color continuity across layers. This is achievable and demanding. The palette is well-chosen: the six colors are harmonious (all muted, professional) while remaining distinguishable.

The mixing of rectangle (dimensions), large circle (clusters), and small circle (works) as node shapes is a good three-tier vocabulary — the shapes are visually distinct without being arbitrary.

## My Total: 83/100

A well-composed system diagram with genuine resonance for its audience. The feedback loop tension (static map vs. dynamic process annotation) is the compositional discovery of this work. The main improvement would be expanding the Layer 2-3 transition zone to reduce convergence density in the densest areas.

## Innovation Flagged

**#213 — Two-voice composition in static system maps**: a system map that shows both the static structure (the three-layer diagram) and the dynamic process (the feedback loop annotation) uses two compositional voices simultaneously. The tension between structure and process, static and dynamic, is a compositional resource that the information architecture school has not named. This is analogous to the tension between Resonance and Craft in the rubric — two dimensions that interact but are scored separately. A system map that integrates both voices in a single visual field (rather than separating them into main diagram and annotation) would be a stronger compositional achievement.
