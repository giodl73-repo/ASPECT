## Key Agreements

**The gap-as-argument structure is a genuine innovation.** All five reviewers recognize the split bar with gap bracket as a design move that encodes a policy argument in visual structure. Nightingale identifies it as the advocacy school's logic in a new form; Du Bois notes it satisfies precision-as-defiance; the Audience representative notes it eliminates the mental arithmetic normally required of the viewer.

**Precision-as-defiance works.** Reporting to the nearest thousand (540,000 not "half a million") earns credit from Du Bois, Nightingale, and the Audience lens. No reviewer recommends rounding.

**The per-case cost annotation is the most contested element.** Tufte calls it an economy failure (seven repetitions of the same text); Du Bois and Nightingale defend it as cumulative rhetoric. The panel is divided but the weight (three of five reviewers) favors retaining it.

---

## Key Tensions

**Tufte vs. Du Bois on the per-case cost annotation:** Tufte sees seven redundant text elements; Du Bois sees seven reinforcements of the argument's key leverage point. The advocacy school's design logic (form chosen for audience impact, not statistical elegance) supports Du Bois. The panel retains the annotation with a note to consider a single callout box as an alternative.

**Rosling on the trend dimension:** Rosling is the consistent voice asking for more temporal context. The trend mini-table satisfies his concern at a minimum level. His innovation (#217, paired advocacy charts) suggests a stronger resolution: a companion chart showing the 2010 gap alongside the 2023 gap. This is a revision target for a future version.
