# Audience Lens — 0029 Child Mortality by Preventable Cause

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

I am a health policy analyst at a development organization. I read these kinds of charts regularly. The split bar format is familiar to me — I have seen similar charts in WHO and UNICEF annual reports. What makes this one different is the gap bracket with the orange annotation. Most charts of this type show two bars and leave the viewer to calculate the gap mentally. This one does the work for me and directs my attention to where the argument lives.

The title "The Gap Is the Argument" tells me immediately what this chart is about. I do not need to reconstruct the argument from the chart — the chart tells me its own logic. This is good design for a meeting context where I may have limited time.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 17

I recognize the sources (IHME, WHO, UNICEF) as authoritative. The 90% coverage scenario is a standard modeling framework I have encountered in UNICEF's coverage reports. The per-case cost annotations ($20/case) are in the right order of magnitude for the interventions listed — this passes my internal knowledge check.

The sort order (by gap) makes the priority ordering clear. I note that sorting by gap rather than by total deaths changes the apparent priority order slightly — malnutrition leads by gap, but neonatal sepsis and pneumonia compete when sorted by total deaths. For a policymaker allocating resources, this distinction matters. A brief footnote noting the sort logic would be helpful.

## Legibility /15 → 14

The chart is immediately legible for my audience. The split bar format reads naturally. The orange bracket is the eye's natural landing point. The exact numbers at bar ends are readable at standard report size. The trend mini-table is legible at close reading but would need to be larger for presentation.

The per-case cost annotation ($20/case) is perhaps the most legible single element — it appears on every row and requires no interpretation. I know immediately what it means for my budget: multiply gap deaths by $20 to get the rough intervention cost.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

The per-case cost annotation appears seven times, which is the element I notice as redundant on a first reading. By the third row, I understand the pattern and the repetition feels deliberate. I would have appreciated a single explanation of why the costs are similar across causes ($20/case) — is this a coincidence or a curated selection of causes that happen to be cheap? A brief annotation addressing this would strengthen the integrity.

## Resonance /15 → 14

This chart made me stop and count. The total gap (3.3 million preventable deaths at $20/case) implies a cost of roughly $66 billion at scale — significant, but within the bounds of what the global health funding system can achieve. That calculation happens in my head from this chart. The chart produced a thought I am ready to take to my next meeting.

That is the highest test of advocacy resonance from my perspective: does the viewer leave with a thought they are ready to act on? I do.

## Craft /15 → 14

The chart looks professional and could appear in a WHO/UNICEF report without modification. The palette is serious without being alarming. The typography is clear.

## My Total: 88/100

This is the strongest advocacy chart I have reviewed in the DEGAS project. It gives me what I need as a policymaker: the size of the problem, the tractability of the solution, and the cost reality check in one chart. The sort order note and the cost-similarity explanation are minor additions that would strengthen it further.
