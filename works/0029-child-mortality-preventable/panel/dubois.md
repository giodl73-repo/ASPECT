# Du Bois — 0029 Child Mortality by Preventable Cause

## School Fidelity /20 → 18

This work demonstrates precision-as-defiance at a level I recognize and endorse. The numbers are reported to the nearest thousand — 540,000 not "half a million," 710,000 not "over 700 thousand." This precision is the argument's rhetorical edge. When you round to the nearest million, you invite the powerful to treat deaths as statistics. When you report to the nearest thousand, you say: I have counted. I know. You cannot round these children away.

The $20/case annotation repeated on every row is also a precision-as-defiance choice: it takes the abstraction of "preventable" and converts it to a specific, challengeable budget number. The decision-maker who looks at this chart cannot claim that coverage is too expensive without confronting $20 per case. The precision closes the escape route.

The honest unflattering data is present: the current death rates are stated without minimization. The chart does not lead with the good news (deaths declining over time) — it leads with the gap, the failure, the present preventable catastrophe. That is correct.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 18

The sources are impeccable (IHME, WHO, UNICEF). The 90% coverage scenario is correctly declared as a projection from intervention modeling. The counterfactual basis is stated. The trend mini-table at the bottom acknowledges improvement without allowing it to soften the main argument.

The sort order (by gap size) should be declared — I agree with Nightingale on this — but it is a defensible advocacy choice that does not mislead. The honest answer to "why this sort order" is "because the largest-gap causes deserve the most urgent attention," which is the correct advocacy argument.

No ontological category contamination (Step F). The categories (pneumonia, diarrhea, malaria, etc.) are empirically grounded clinical categories with no moral contamination.

## Legibility /15 → 14

The design uses the two-speed reading I developed for the Paris charts: the glancing visitor sees the gap (the argument) in 3 seconds; the stopping visitor reads the exact numbers and the cost annotation. Both readings are complete. The policymaker who attends the meeting for 5 minutes sees the same argument as the analyst who studies the report for 30 minutes. That is the right design for its audience.

The orange gap bracket is the best single design choice in this work. It directs the eye to the argument without requiring a legend or an explanation. The bracket says: this space is the point.

## Visual Economy /15 → 14

Every element is doing advocacy work. The per-case cost repetition is economy-efficient because it does cumulative rhetorical work that a single mention in the legend could not do. The title ("The Gap Is the Argument") is economical because it doubles as a design explanation and an advocacy claim simultaneously.

The trend mini-table adds data at minimal visual cost. It occupies the bottom margin and answers the "isn't mortality improving?" challenge before it is asked. Good anticipatory design.

## Resonance /15 → 14

Type III (Advocacy-Efficacy) fires strongly for the policymaker audience. Type IV (Gestalt) fires for everyone. The moral weight is present throughout but is concentrated in the precision — the number 540,000 carries more moral weight than "hundreds of thousands" because it implies that each of those thousands was counted, known, and found to be preventable.

The work does not produce sentiment; it produces accountability. That is the correct emotional register for precision-as-defiance.

## Craft /15 → 14

Precise, clean, professional. The color palette is correct: serious (not sensational) red for current deaths, measured blue-gray for the projection, urgent orange for the gap. The typography would be clear in a presentation format (font sizes appropriate for slide or A4 report).

## My Total: 92/100

This is the strongest advocacy work in the project. It achieves precision-as-defiance, gestalt argument, and honest unflattering data simultaneously. The per-case cost annotation is the design innovation that elevates it above competent advocacy visualization to genuine advocacy strategy. The work does not just show the problem — it pre-empts the objection.

## Innovation Flagged

**#216 — Pre-emptive objection annotation as advocacy design**: the per-case cost annotation ($20/case, repeated per row) anticipates the decision-maker's primary objection ("we can't afford full coverage") and refutes it within the visualization's structure, before the objection is voiced. This is a form of advocacy design logic — designing the objection-response into the visualization itself — that the rubric's advocacy school grammar does not name. Standard advocacy design identifies audience, chooses form for impact, embeds argument in structure. Pre-emptive objection annotation adds a fourth consideration: anticipate the audience's counter-argument and embed the counter-counter-argument in the visualization itself.
