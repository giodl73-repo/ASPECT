# Nightingale — 0029 Child Mortality by Preventable Cause

## School Fidelity /20 → 18

This is advocacy visualization at its best. The form choice is excellent: the split bar chart with the gap as the argument. The gestalt argument (the gap between left and right bars, visible before any label is read) satisfies the advocacy school's founding criterion — argument embedded in structure. A glancing decision-maker sees the disparity before they read a number. That is the coxcomb's logic applied to a different form.

The title "The Gap Is the Argument" is bold and correct — it names the design logic, which is itself an advocacy act. The subtitle "5 million preventable child deaths annually" is the moral weight without hedging. The per-case cost annotation ($20/case, repeated on each row) is elegant: it appears as if redundant until the viewer realizes it is doing cumulative rhetorical work — every cause, the same cost, the same missed opportunity.

The intended audience (health policymakers, international funders, WHO/UNICEF staff) is correctly identified and the form serves them precisely. These are numerate professionals who will engage with exact numbers rather than being put off by them. The precision serves the advocacy.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 17

Data sources (IHME GBD 2023, WHO, UNICEF modeling) are reputable and declared. The 90% coverage scenario is declared as a projection from UNICEF's intervention analysis — appropriate Step A and Step B compliance. The uncertainty in the modeled figures is noted in the source note without undermining the argument.

The sort order (by gap size) is an argumentative choice and is not declared as such. Sorting by gap rather than by total deaths or alphabetically makes malnutrition appear first (largest gap) — which is the most emotionally resonant ordering. This is a defensible advocacy choice (Nightingale herself chose forms for their persuasive effect) but should be noted in a footnote: "Causes sorted by preventable death gap (largest gap first)."

Step C: no overreach. The 90% coverage scenario is presented as a projection, not a guarantee. The causal claim (these deaths are preventable by treatment coverage) is well-supported by the cited literature.

## Legibility /15 → 14

Context: political presentation (meeting/briefing) and contemplative study (report). The split bar format is immediately legible to a numerate audience. The gap bracket with orange annotation is unambiguous — it tells the viewer where to look and what the gap number is. The exact numbers at each bar end are readable at report size.

The four-cell mini-table at the bottom (trend data: 7.1M → 5.9M → 5.0M → 4.3M) adds context without complicating the main argument. The "progress is real — and insufficient" label is a perfect Nightingale touch: acknowledging the good news while insisting the gap remains.

## Visual Economy /15 → 14

Every element does work. The left bars (current deaths) and right bars (projected at 90%) are the primary data. The gap bracket and orange annotation are the argument's pointer. The cause names and per-case cost annotations are the advocacy context. The title and subtitle are the frame. The source note is the integrity anchor. Nothing is decorative.

The per-case cost annotation ($20/case) is the most interesting economy element: it appears seven times (once per cause row), which seems redundant, but the repetition is the point — it is doing rhetorical work through accumulation rather than through individual information transfer.

## Resonance /15 → 14

Type IV (Gestalt) fires immediately — the gap is visible before reading. Type III (Advocacy-Efficacy) fires for the policymaker audience — the gap + cost annotation provides the budget reality check that makes the argument actionable. Type II (Emergent-Data) fires quietly — these are real children, real causes, and the precision of the numbers makes them feel specific rather than aggregate.

The combination of three resonance types in one design is rare. The work earns it by not sacrificing one for another.

## Craft /15 → 14

The color choices are well-judged: deep red for current deaths (serious, not sensational), muted blue-gray for the projection (possibility, not certainty), orange for the gap bracket (attention, urgency). The typography specification is clear. The layout is clean.

## My Total: 91/100

This is the strongest original advocacy visualization in the project. The form is exactly right for the argument and the audience. The gap-as-argument structure is a genuine design innovation — it makes the visual form carry a counterfactual claim, which is a new use of comparative bar charts that the rubric has not encountered before.

## Innovation Flagged

**#215 — The counterfactual gap as visual argument**: the split bar chart here encodes a counterfactual (what deaths would be at 90% coverage) as a visual structure, making the gap itself the argument. This is different from a standard comparative bar chart (comparing two measured quantities) — one bar represents what is, the other represents what could be. The rubric does not distinguish counterfactual comparison from factual comparison in its Data/Story Integrity or School Fidelity assessments. Counterfactual visualization deserves its own integrity check: is the counterfactual scenario realistic? is it labeled as counterfactual? is the modeling basis declared?
