# Rosling — 0029 Child Mortality by Preventable Cause

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

The advocacy structure is correct and the gap-as-argument is a genuine design insight. I want to talk about the trend mini-table, because it is the most important thing in this work from my perspective — and it is treated as a footnote rather than as an argument.

The trend data (7.1M in 2010 → 4.3M in 2023) shows a 40% reduction in under-5 deaths in 13 years. That is remarkable progress. It is also insufficient. The work's label "progress is real — and insufficient" captures both truths in four words, which I admire. But the four-cell mini-table is too small to carry the weight of what it is saying. A 40% improvement in child mortality is one of the great achievements of global health in the 21st century. Putting it in a bottom-margin footnote minimizes a truth that should be part of the argument — not because it softens the gap claim, but because showing the progress makes the prescription ("we know how to do this") stronger.

The advocacy school fidelity is high: the form is chosen for the audience; the argument is embedded in structure; comparison selection is honest. My concern is a secondary one about what the temporal dimension could add.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 17

The IHME/WHO/UNICEF data are the gold standard for this topic. The 90% coverage scenario is a UNICEF modeling product that is widely used in the field and is defensible. The declaration of uncertainty in the source note is appropriate.

The sort by gap size is an argumentative choice. I accept it. The 90% figure (not 100%) as the target is also honest — achieving 100% coverage of any health intervention is unrealistic; 90% is the standard public health target (same as the vaccine coverage standard). This choice should be declared but is defensible.

I want to note what the temporal dimension adds here. The current chart shows the gap as of 2023. A second version of the same chart showing 2010 data would reveal that the gap in 2010 was larger — meaning progress has been made in closing it. This would strengthen the prescription: "We have been closing the gap for 13 years. We know how. The remaining gap is not harder to close — it requires the same interventions at larger scale."

## Legibility /15 → 13

The split bar format is legible to a numerate audience. The gap bracket with orange annotation is unambiguous. The per-case cost annotation reads clearly.

The trend mini-table's readability at report size is the legibility concern I raise. Four cells with year and death total — this is legible at 9pt if the viewer is reading at close distance. For a presentation context (slide at the front of a room), the trend table would need to be larger.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

The per-case cost annotation's repetition is the economy debate in this work. Tufte will call it redundant; Du Bois and Nightingale will defend it as cumulative rhetoric. I side with Du Bois and Nightingale on this — the repetition is doing real work — but I note that the economy cost is real: seven instances of the same text adds visual noise that a different annotation strategy might handle more elegantly (e.g., a single bold call-out box rather than repeated inline text).

## Resonance /15 → 13

The gestalt argument (the gap) fires strongly and immediately. What would make this work produce genuine wonder — my standard — is showing the trend. "In 2010, the gap was 4 million children. In 2023, it is 3.3 million. In 2033, at current rates, it will be 2.5 million. In 2040, it could be under 500 thousand if coverage reaches 90%." That is a wonder-producing argument. The current chart shows the present problem; showing the trajectory shows that the solution is working where it has been applied.

## Craft /15 → 14

Clean, professional, well-executed. The color palette is serious without being grim. The layout is clear. The gap bracket is visually distinct and correctly placed.

## My Total: 87/100

A strong advocacy chart that correctly identifies the gap as the argument. My consistent note: the temporal dimension is underused. The trend mini-table should be larger, and a second chart version showing the 2010 gap alongside the 2023 gap would be twice the argument at modest additional complexity. The work is excellent as a point-in-time advocacy chart; it would be exceptional with the temporal dimension fully engaged.

## Innovation Flagged

**#217 — The two-chart advocacy argument: present-state + trend as a paired pair**: in advocacy visualization where a counterfactual gap exists in the present (current vs. possible), and a historical trend shows the gap closing over time, the most complete advocacy argument requires both charts — the point-in-time gap and the trend. Neither alone tells the full story; together they argue both "the problem is urgent NOW" and "the solution is known and working." The rubric does not currently address the design of paired advocacy charts that carry different time dimensions of the same argument.
