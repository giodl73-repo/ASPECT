# Tufte — 0029 Child Mortality by Preventable Cause

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

The advocacy school is correctly applied. The split bar format with counterfactual right bars and gap brackets is a genuine design innovation — it encodes a policy argument in visual structure, which is exactly what the advocacy school demands. The title names the design logic. The precision is maintained throughout.

I accept the per-case cost annotation's repetition as advocacy rhetoric rather than chartjunk, though it stretches my tolerance. The argument for repetition (cumulative rhetorical effect) is a valid advocacy-school argument that the statistical graphics school would not accept.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 17

The sources are declared and reputable. The 90% coverage scenario is declared as a projection. The sort order should be declared. The counterfactual is labeled as counterfactual.

My concern: the per-case cost figures ($20/case, consistent across all causes) are presented without a cost breakdown. Are these direct treatment costs? Including health system delivery costs? Are they representative of Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, both? The cost claim is the chart's most actionable element; it is also the element most likely to be challenged by a skeptical policymaker. A more precise cost annotation — with a range or a geographic qualifier — would strengthen Step A compliance.

## Legibility /15 → 14

The split bar format is familiar to this audience. The orange bracket is the correct annotation device — it tells the viewer where to look without requiring a legend. The chart is legible to its intended audience without instruction.

## Visual Economy /15 → 12

Seven instances of "$20/case" is the economy failure I will name. I understand the advocacy argument for repetition. I still note it as an economy cost. A single prominent callout box stating "$20/case treatment cost across all causes" would achieve the same rhetorical function with one-seventh the text repetition.

The gap brackets add visual elements that do not encode additional data beyond what the bar positions already encode. The bracket is redundant with the bar-end labels. I accept it as a structural-attentional element (it directs the eye) rather than a data element, and I dock lightly (-1).

## Resonance /15 → 13

The gestalt is correct and fires immediately. The gap-as-argument is a genuine Type IV success. Type III fires for the policymaker audience through the cost annotation's budget reality check. The moral weight is present in the numbers without being performed in the visual design.

The trend mini-table at the bottom is correctly sized for its role (context note, not main argument). Its positioning as a footnote rather than a feature is the right priority choice.

## Craft /15 → 14

The design is precise and professional. The color palette is controlled. The layout is clean. I have no craft objections.

## My Total: 87/100

A strong advocacy chart with one economy issue (the repeated cost annotation) and one integrity enhancement needed (cost basis qualification). The gap-as-argument structure is a genuine contribution to the advocacy visualization school's design vocabulary.

## Innovation Flagged

**#218 — Counterfactual bar as a structural element in advocacy visualization**: the right-side bars in this split chart encode a counterfactual projection, not a measured quantity. The current rubric's Data/Story Integrity dimension does not distinguish between measured data bars and counterfactual projection bars. The distinction matters for Step B (accuracy tradeoff) and Step C (causation claim integrity): a counterfactual bar that is presented without declaration as a projection could create a false impression that both bars measure equal-status quantities. The counterfactual status of the right bars should be visually and textually marked as distinct from the empirical left bars.
