# Design Specification — 0030 History of Women's Suffrage

## Canvas

Portrait orientation, 60 x 80cm (or 1200 x 1600px). White ground. The design is a grid of country flag-pictograms arranged chronologically.

## The Pictogram System

Each country is represented by a flag-pictogram: a simplified, standardized rendering of the country's flag at a fixed size (20 x 14mm). Simplification follows ISOTYPE principles — flat color, no shading, no fine detail. Flag patterns are reduced to their essential color blocks. The silhouette is a consistent rectangle.

Flag-pictograms are the unit symbol. One pictogram = one country. The repetition grammar of ISOTYPE is preserved — but the dimension of repetition encodes time, not quantity. Position within the grid (reading left-to-right, top-to-bottom) encodes the year of suffrage adoption.

## Grid Layout

7 columns x 8 rows = 56 cells (sufficient for 50 countries plus 6 decade-divider cells). Cells are arranged in strict chronological order of suffrage adoption year.

The grid reads left-to-right, top-to-bottom like text. New Zealand (1893) occupies cell 1 (top-left). Australia (1902) occupies cell 2. Each pictogram is placed in the grid in order of adoption year.

**Decade dividers:** At the beginning of each new decade's cluster, a thin horizontal rule and a decade label (e.g., "1920s — 10 countries") separates the decade groups. The decades are:
- Pre-1910: 2 countries (New Zealand, Australia)
- 1910s: 3 countries (Finland, Norway, Denmark and others)
- 1920s: 10 countries (post-WWI wave)
- 1930s: 3 countries
- 1940s: 13 countries (post-WWII decolonization begins)
- 1950s: 6 countries
- 1960s: 6 countries
- 1970s: 3 countries
- 1980s: 2 countries
- 1990s-2015: 2 countries (Kuwait 2005, Saudi Arabia 2015)

The decade cluster sizes themselves are data: the visual density of pictograms per decade shows when suffrage adoption accelerated (1920s, 1940s) and when it slowed.

## Color Encoding (Region)

Each flag-pictogram has a colored region-border: a 2mm border frame in the region color. This separates flag colors (which are the flag's own colors, not encodings) from the additional regional encoding.

Regions and border colors:
- Europe: deep blue (#1A3D6B)
- Americas: forest green (#2A5C3F)
- Asia-Pacific: amber (#C9952A)
- Africa: terracotta (#B54A2A)
- Middle East: purple (#6B3A8C)

The regional border allows the viewer to trace regional clustering in the grid — e.g., the 1940s cluster shows a mix of European (blue-bordered), Americas (green-bordered), and Asia-Pacific (amber-bordered) countries adopting suffrage in the post-WWII period, which reflects the decolonization moment.

## Typography and Labels

- Each flag-pictogram has the country name in 7pt beneath it, centered
- The adoption year appears above each pictogram in 6pt
- Decade labels: 10pt, bold, at the left edge of each decade group
- Title: "When Women Won the Vote" — 18pt, top of image
- Subtitle: "50 countries, 1893-2023. Each flag is one country, arranged in the order suffrage was achieved." — 10pt
- Region key: a horizontal legend strip at bottom showing the five region border colors and names

## ISOTYPE Grammar Innovation (Temporal ISOTYPE)

Standard Neurath grammar: quantity is encoded by repetition within a row or column. A row of 10 worker symbols = 10 workers. The count is the argument.

This work's innovation: the dimension of repetition is time, not quantity. Reading the grid left-to-right, top-to-bottom, the viewer counts how many countries have suffrage at any point in the reading — but more importantly, they experience the temporal sequence of adoption as a reading experience. The grid "fills up" as history progresses. This is a departure from Neurath's grammar because:
- Standard ISOTYPE rows are interchangeable (the 5th worker = the 5th dollar = one unit)
- Temporal ISOTYPE grid cells are not interchangeable — each cell has a specific chronological identity that determines its reading position

The grammar question this raises: is position-encoding of time consistent with ISOTYPE's core principle (repetition encodes quantity)? Or does it violate it by using position (normally a neutral ordering device in ISOTYPE) as a data-encoding variable?

## Reading Experience

A viewer reading this grid from top-left to bottom-right experiences the progressive adoption of women's suffrage as a journey — country by country, flag by flag, decade by decade. The decade cluster sizes create visual rhythm: a dense block in the 1940s (the WWII decolonization wave), a thin line in the 1980s, the isolated pair at the bottom (Kuwait and Saudi Arabia) creating a visual sense of incompleteness — the story is not over.

The isolation of Saudi Arabia (2015) as the near-final cell is the work's most powerful compositional moment. It is almost alone in its decade, near the bottom-right corner, surrounded by empty space. The visual isolation IS the argument about how far behind some countries remain.
