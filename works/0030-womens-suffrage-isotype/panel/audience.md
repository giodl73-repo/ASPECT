# Audience Lens — 0030 History of Women's Suffrage

## School Fidelity /20 → 15

I see a grid of small flags, arranged in rows, with years above them and country names below. The decade labels tell me this is organized by time. I read left-to-right, top-to-bottom, starting with New Zealand (1893) and ending at the sparse bottom section with Saudi Arabia (2015). The reading experience is clear and intuitive — it reads like text, which is the correct intuition for a chronological sequence.

What I cannot decode without the legend: the border colors around each flag. Once I read the legend (Europe = blue border, Americas = green, etc.) I can trace the regional patterns in the grid. This is an additional layer of insight that requires some legend-reading effort.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 15

I trust the dates. I know that New Zealand was first and Saudi Arabia was very late. The specific dates for intermediate countries are not things I would know independently, but they feel historically plausible.

## Legibility /15 → 12

The flag grid is visually engaging — I was drawn to look at it before reading any text. The country names in 7pt type are at the edge of comfortable reading for me. The year labels above each flag in 6pt are very small. For a 60x80cm installation, I would need to walk up close to read individual labels. The decade dividers and labels help me orient without close reading.

The Saudi Arabia isolation at the bottom is immediately visible and immediately comprehensible — I see: the story is mostly done, but one country is still there, alone. No label needed for that argument.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

The flags themselves are carrying most of the visual load. The region border colors add a layer I appreciate but did not fully understand until I read the legend. The rest (year labels, country names, decade dividers) seems necessary. The composition does not feel cluttered.

## Resonance /15 → 13

Reading from New Zealand (1893) to Saudi Arabia (2015) across a journey of 50 countries and 122 years produced a genuine sense of historical scale for me. I also noticed the clusters — the 1940s was clearly a major wave, and the late additions are sparse. These patterns emerged from the reading experience without requiring any annotation.

The emotional high point: Saudi Arabia alone at the bottom, surrounded by empty space. I felt the argument without needing to read about it.

## Craft /15 → 13

The flags look simplified but recognizable. The largest flags (USA, UK, France, Germany) I recognized immediately. For smaller or less familiar countries, I relied on the name label.

## My Total: 81/100

An accessible and engaging historical visualization that produces the temporal argument through a reading experience rather than through a chart. The main legibility concern is the small type for individual labels. The regional border color requires the legend but adds real insight for the patient viewer.
