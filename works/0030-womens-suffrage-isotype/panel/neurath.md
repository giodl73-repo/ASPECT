# Neurath — 0030 History of Women's Suffrage

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

I must speak honestly: this work departs from my grammar in a significant way, and the departure is both interesting and dangerous.

My rules are: quantity by repetition, not by scaling. One symbol per unit. The array is the quantity. The viewer should be able to count the symbols to verify the quantity — the count IS the reading.

This work uses position (grid order, reading left-to-right, top-to-bottom) to encode time, rather than using symbol stacking to encode quantity accumulated over time. The count is still present — 50 flag-pictograms for 50 countries — and the viewer can count them. But the primary argument is about sequence and temporal clustering, not about the final count. The argument is "this is when each country adopted suffrage" rather than "this many countries have adopted suffrage."

I invented ISOTYPE to make quantity accessible to the working man who could not read a table. Position-as-time is more sophisticated: it encodes both the order and the clustering. It requires the viewer to understand that position in the grid = time order, which is not self-evident from the symbols themselves. The small year labels above each pictogram mitigate this — they make the temporal encoding explicit. But a viewer who does not read the year labels will see a grid of flags and not know they are in chronological order.

Despite this concern, I give 16 rather than a lower score because: (1) the flag-pictogram unit is a genuine ISOTYPE symbol (simplified, flat, standardized at a fixed size); (2) the repetition grammar is preserved (one symbol per country); (3) the decade dividers make the temporal structure explicit and make the cluster argument visible at a glance.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 17

The historical record of women's suffrage adoption years is well-documented (the sources available: Anna Howard Shaw papers, ICRW database, UN Women historical survey). The 50-country selection includes the relevant first-adopters, major waves, and late-adopters. The Saudi Arabia 2015 date is correct. The Kuwait 2005 date is correct.

One declaration needed: the definition of "suffrage" used — some countries granted partial suffrage before full suffrage (Australia 1902 granted full suffrage to white women only; Aboriginal women were not included until 1962). The dates here appear to reflect "full national suffrage for all women" — this should be stated.

## Legibility /15 → 13

Context: public display or gallery (general audience). The grid of flag-pictograms is visually engaging and invites close approach. The decade labels make the temporal structure legible. The region border colors require the legend to decode.

The 7pt and 6pt type labels (country names and years) require close approach. For a large-format print (60x80cm), these labels should be readable at arm's length.

The main legibility achievement: the isolation of Saudi Arabia near the bottom-right, surrounded by empty space, is immediately visible at any reading distance. The work makes the latecomer visible without requiring any label to be read.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

Fifty flag-pictograms plus five region border colors plus year labels plus decade dividers is a lot of visual information. The economy is appropriate — each element is doing a specific job. The region borders are the most economy-debatable element: they add a second color layer on top of the flags' own colors, creating potential visual confusion. But the information they encode (regional clustering patterns) is genuinely interesting and not available from the flags alone.

## Resonance /15 → 13

The reading experience — country by country, decade by decade, from New Zealand's first position to Saudi Arabia's near-isolation — produces a genuine sense of the suffrage story as a journey. The density changes (dense 1940s cluster, sparse 1980s, isolated 1990s-2015) create visual rhythm that reflects the political rhythms of the 20th century.

The Saudi Arabia isolation is the most powerful single compositional moment in the project's original designs. It is achieved through ISOTYPE grammar (position in a repetition grid) — the school produces the argument, not just the decoration.

## Craft /15 → 13

The simplified flag designs require careful execution. Flags with complex designs (Union Jack, Star of David patterns) are challenging to simplify to ISOTYPE flatness while remaining recognizable. The regional border color must be distinguishable from the flags' own colors — for blue-flag countries (France, Netherlands), a deep navy border may be confused with the flag's own blue. Consider using the border as a texture (hatched vs. solid) rather than a pure color for legibility.

## My Total: 85/100

This is a genuine ISOTYPE work with a significant grammar innovation (temporal position) that I accept as a forward development of the school. The isolation effect achieved with Saudi Arabia's position is the work's greatest achievement and could only be achieved through ISOTYPE's repetition grammar. The flags are the right symbol choice for this subject — they are instantly recognizable (at least for major countries) and cross language barriers, which is my democratic legibility standard.

## Innovation Flagged

**#219 — Temporal ISOTYPE: position-as-time as a departure from repetition-as-quantity**: standard ISOTYPE encodes quantity through repetition within a fixed reading direction. Temporal ISOTYPE encodes time through position in a chronological grid. This is a named departure from my grammar that the rubric's ISOTYPE school documentation should assess: when does using position to encode time violate the repetition principle, and when does it extend it? The departure is legitimate when: (1) the unit symbol is standard ISOTYPE (one symbol per unit, no scaling); (2) the temporal dimension is declared by labels; (3) the reading direction is made explicit. This work meets all three conditions.
