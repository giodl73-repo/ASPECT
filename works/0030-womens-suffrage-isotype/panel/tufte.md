# Tufte — 0030 History of Women's Suffrage

## School Fidelity /20 → 15

ISOTYPE applied correctly: one symbol per unit, no scaling, standardized at fixed size, flat color, no decoration. The temporal position innovation departs from strict Neurath grammar, and I treat this as a Type A fidelity assessment with a school-grammar extension: the work is ISOTYPE in its unit vocabulary and in its repetition discipline, but it extends the grammar's dimension axis from quantity-in-space to quantity-in-time. This is not a violation — it is a development. Neurath's grammar never explicitly forbade using position to encode time; he simply did not explore it.

Slight dock for the region border color potentially conflating with flag colors in blue-flag cases.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

The historical record is accurate. The Australia 1902 asterisk for Aboriginal women's exclusion is the main declaration needed. The 50-country selection is documented and representative.

The work's implicit claim — that countries appearing later in the grid have "worse" records than those appearing earlier — is a comparison that the design makes without declaring. Earlier adoption year does not necessarily equal stronger feminist governance (some early adopters later passed restrictive laws; some late adopters have strong contemporary feminist policy). The grid's chronological arrangement implies a progress narrative that is partially but not entirely accurate. A brief disclaimer note acknowledging this would satisfy Step C.

## Legibility /15 → 13

The flag-grid is legible as a reading experience. The decade dividers help. The small type (6-7pt) requires close approach. The region border colors require the legend. The Saudi Arabia isolation is legible at distance. For a museum or gallery installation at 60x80cm, these legibility demands are appropriate.

## Visual Economy /15 → 12

Fifty flag-pictograms with five region border colors, country names, year labels, decade dividers, and a legend — this is a complex composition. The economy question: is all of this earning its place? The flag-pictograms are the data. The region borders add a second encoding. The year labels are essential for the temporal claim. The country names are necessary for identification. The decade dividers provide structure. The legend decodes the regional colors.

The region border color is the most economy-debatable element. Removing it would simplify the image; keeping it adds regional clustering insight. I accept it as intrinsic-complexity data encoding (the regional patterns in adoption timing are real and interesting) rather than decoration.

## Resonance /15 → 13

The reading journey from New Zealand to Saudi Arabia produces the temporal argument correctly. The isolation effect at the bottom-right is visually powerful. The density changes across decades create rhythm.

The work does not produce "wonder" in my specific sense — it is more contemplative than astonishing. The chronological grid format is somewhat expected once you understand the design logic. The innovation (temporal ISOTYPE) is intellectually interesting rather than visually surprising.

## Craft /15 → 14

Simplified flag design at ISOTYPE standards is demanding craft work. The execution standard I set: each flag should be recognizable to a viewer who knows the flag, even at 20x14mm with flat color simplification. The Union Jack, the Australian flag, and flags with complex central emblems are the challenging cases.

## My Total: 83/100

A competent and innovative ISOTYPE work with good execution. The temporal ISOTYPE grammar extension is the design's genuine contribution. The economy is on the high side of acceptable. The Australia 1902 asterisk is the most important integrity addition.
