# Historian Lens — 0031 Soil Health Degradation

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

The ecological visualization school, as established by Humboldt's Tableau Physique (work 0010), uses the ecological gradient axis as the primary organizing dimension, shows multiple variables along this axis simultaneously, and contextualizes biological patterns against physical gradients. This work applies all three principles.

The precedents I reach for: Humboldt's altitude profiles show life zone transitions (altitude zones, species distributions) against physical gradients (temperature, humidity). Tansley's ecosystem concept diagrams show material flows through multiple compartments including soil. Odum's ecosystem energetics diagrams (1960s) show energy flows through above- and below-ground compartments. This work is closer to Odum than to Humboldt — the focus on below-ground ecology (soil compartments) is a 20th-century extension of Humboldt's above-ground mountain ecology.

The depth-axis inversion follows soil science's professional convention for borehole and soil profile diagrams. This convention predates Humboldt's altitude-axis convention and is established in pedological literature since at least the 1840s. The inversion is historically grounded, not novel.

One historical note: the 4-variable simultaneous encoding is a modern (post-1990) approach to soil health — before the development of microbial diversity measurement methods, the standard 2-variable profile (texture + organic matter) was the norm. The work's 4-variable approach is historically current.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

Historical accuracy of the depth zone boundaries (A/B/C horizon classification) is correct. The 8-region selection covers the historically most-studied agricultural systems. Data quality note (sparse in West Africa vs. dense in US Midwest) is historically accurate.

## Legibility /15 → 12

For a scientific/agricultural expert audience: legible with the expected effort of a technical scientific visualization. For a general audience: requires significant orientation time and explanatory support.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

The Cluster H criterion (intrinsic complexity) applies fully. The 4-variable depth profile structure is the minimum representation of soil health — fewer variables would lose important information. The 8-region comparison is the minimum for a global argument. The economy is intrinsic.

## Resonance /15 → 12

For a soil scientist or agricultural researcher (domain-gated, Type V): the profile format is immediately recognizable and the comparison is immediately interpretable. For a general viewer: the aesthetic impact (described by the Artist lens) is the primary resonance channel.

## Craft /15 → 13

The historical tradition of soil profile visualization is well-established and precise. The color encoding (especially the four sequential palettes) must follow the rigorous standards expected of scientific visualization in pedology journals.

## My Total: 82/100

A strong ecological visualization that correctly extends the Humboldt grammar to below-ground ecology. The depth-axis inversion is historically grounded in pedological convention. The work is the project's first below-ground ecological visualization and establishes this as a valid extension of the school.

## Innovation Flagged

**#224 — Below-ground ecological visualization as a Humboldt grammar extension**: Humboldt's ecological visualization established the mountain altitude profile as the canonical form for ecological gradient visualization. Below-ground ecology (soil science, marine depth ecology, cave ecology) requires the same grammar with axis inversion. This is not a new school — it is the same school applied to a different natural gradient direction. The rubric's ecological visualization school documentation should explicitly acknowledge below-ground ecology as within the school's scope, with axis inversion as the required grammar adaptation.
