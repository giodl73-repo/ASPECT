# Minard — 0031 Soil Health Degradation

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

This work achieves genuine dimensional layering: 4 variables × 5 depths × 8 regions = 160 data cells simultaneously present. This is the ecological visualization school's essential virtue — holding multiple dimensions of reality in a single visual field without losing any of them. The work does not simplify; it layers. The horizontal scan (comparison across regions at a fixed depth) and the vertical scan (depth profile for a single region) are both available simultaneously, which is the mark of a well-conceived multi-variable display.

The depth-axis downward convention is the grammar choice I most respect. The engineer in me approves: you draw what you see. Soil profiles go down. The axis must go down. This is not a stylistic choice; it is a professional necessity that the design correctly applies.

The right-margin environmental gradient panel is the element I find most congenial with my own approach — it is exactly the kind of contextual data layer I would add to a flow map. The physical gradients (light, temperature, moisture) explain the biological gradients (organic matter, microbial diversity) without requiring the viewer to have prior ecological knowledge. The panel is not decoration — it is the causal context.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

The 4-variable encoding per zone is honest: pH, organic matter, compaction, and microbial diversity are the four variables most widely agreed by soil scientists to characterize soil health. These are not cherry-picked for a predetermined conclusion.

The diverging pH encoding (acid to alkaline, neutral=optimal white) is the most complex variable to read. The viewer must understand that both orange and teal are "bad" (acid and alkaline extremes) while white is "good" (neutral). This requires a careful legend. The legend note "neutral (pH 6.5-7.5) = optimal for most crops" would clarify the encoding logic.

The 8-region selection is defensible and covers the major agricultural systems. A declaration of the selection criterion ("these 8 regions account for approximately 65% of global grain production") would strengthen Step A.

The depth profiles are modeled estimates based on published pedological surveys — this is declared in the subtitle's acknowledgment that the data is "measured" at the named depths. The source data quality varies by region (US Midwest has excellent historical soil survey data; West Africa has sparser data). A brief data quality note by region would satisfy Step A fully.

## Legibility /15 → 12

Context: contemplative study, scientific/agricultural audience. The 160-cell layout requires sustained engagement. The horizontal comparison scan (reading across all 8 profiles at the same depth level) is the most legible operation — the variable encoding at the same depth is directly comparable across columns. The vertical profile scan (reading one column top to bottom) reveals the depth gradient for that region. Both operations are intuitive once the encoding is understood.

The 6pt variable labels at the top of each column (pH / OM% / kPa / MDI) are the legibility risk — they are small and require close reading. For a scientific audience in contemplative study, this is acceptable.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

160 data cells is not 160 pieces of decoration — it is 160 data points. The Cluster H (intrinsic complexity) criterion applies: the subject matter (soil health at multiple depths across multiple regions) is genuinely multi-variable. The high mark density is intrinsic, not additive. The environmental gradient panel adds context at modest visual cost. No element is decorative.

The 6pt label trade-off: small labels reduce visual noise but risk legibility. For this audience and context, this trade is correct.

## Resonance /15 → 13

The gestalt — horizontal scan of the A-horizon (0-10cm) band across all 8 profiles — produces the key argument before any label is read: some topsoil zones are darker red and paler green than others. The pattern of degradation is visible as a pattern. The specific quantity of degradation requires reading the color scales, but the direction of degradation is immediately visible.

The work produces the specific resonance I value: quantitative weight felt before it is read. The degradation is visible; the numbers are available for those who want them.

## Craft /15 → 13

The most demanding craft challenge: 160 colored cells in 8 profiles, with consistent color calibration across all four encoding palettes. Color calibration errors (a slightly wrong green in one cell) would undermine the comparison. High-quality printing or high-DPI screen display required.

## My Total: 84/100

A strong ecological visualization with genuine dimensional layering. The depth-axis inversion is correct and should be documented as a grammar extension for the ecological visualization school. The right-margin environmental gradient panel is the work's most elegant design choice.

## Innovation Flagged

**#221 — Axis inversion as ecological grammar requirement**: the Humboldt ecological visualization convention assumes the altitude axis runs upward because the organism distributes along an ascending gradient from sea level to mountain peak. Soil science assumes depth runs downward because the profile distributes along a descending gradient from surface to bedrock. The axis direction is determined by the subject matter, not by the designer's preference. The rubric's ecological visualization school documentation should name this: "the ecological gradient axis runs in the direction of the natural gradient — upward for altitude, downward for depth, outward for radial gradients."
