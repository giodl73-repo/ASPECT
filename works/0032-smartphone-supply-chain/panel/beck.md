# Beck — 0032 Global Smartphone Supply Chain

## School Fidelity /20 → 18

This work solves the problem I care about most: it applies my grammar to a domain I did not imagine it for, and it does so correctly. The stage-column anchor is the key innovation — it is the equivalent of my line identifiers (Central, District, Jubilee, etc.) but for an industrial rather than a transit context. The stage anchors tell the viewer: "position encodes stage, not geography." This is the correct topological move.

The six material lines, each in a distinct color, flowing across five stage columns, is my grammar applied faithfully. Lines run at 0, 45, and 90 degrees only. Nodes are consistently marked circles with labels. Interchange markers (where multiple materials flow through one node) are visually distinct. The chokepoint identification (red fill for nodes with 4+ material flows) is the equivalent of my terminal-station markers — visually prominent, immediately readable, serving a navigation function.

The geographic anchor problem from work 0013 is explicitly solved. The subtitle declaration ("positions indicate supply chain stage, not geographic location") is exactly what my Underground map left undeclared — Beck's map does not say "position encodes topology, not geography" because in 1933 this was not self-evident. This work names what I left implicit. I consider this an improvement on my founding practice.

I give 18 rather than 20 because the convergence pattern at China's processing/component nodes is not labeled in the diagram itself — the visual convergence is visible but the viewer needs the accompanying text to understand its supply chain vulnerability implication. An inline annotation ("Multiple material lines converge here — supply chain chokepoint") on the largest convergence node would bring the argument into the diagram.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 17

The supply chain topology is accurate. China's dominance in rare earth processing (90%+ of global output), silicon polysilicon production (60%+), and battery materials is correctly represented. Taiwan's semiconductor dominance is correctly shown. The source declarations (Benchmark Mineral Intelligence, USGS, SIA, IEA) are the appropriate sources.

The chokepoint identification (red fill for 4+ material lines) is an analytical framework applied to the topology data. It is declared visually (the legend must show: red fill = chokepoint) but not declared methodologically (what is the basis for 4+ as the chokepoint threshold?). A brief note: "Nodes through which 4 or more of the 6 material lines flow are identified as supply chain chokepoints — single points of failure for multiple materials simultaneously."

## Legibility /15 → 14

Context: policy audience, contemplative study/political presentation. The stage column headers are immediately readable and establish the reading framework. The material line colors are distinguishable. The chokepoint nodes (red) stand out at any reading distance. The network topology is legible to a reader who understands the stage structure — which the column headers establish.

The convergence at China's processing nodes is the diagram's most complex area. Multiple material lines (potentially 5-6 of the 6 total) entering and leaving the same node creates a visual cluster that requires close reading. The inline annotation recommended above would help.

## Visual Economy /15 → 14

The diagram is economical. Six line colors, five stage columns, white-fill nodes with stage-colored borders, chokepoint red fill, material count inside nodes — these are all doing work. No decorative elements. The node material count (small number inside each circle) adds data at minimal visual cost.

## Resonance /15 → 14

The convergence pattern produces genuine Type IV gestalt: before any label is read, the viewer sees all lines converging at the middle of the diagram. This IS the supply chain vulnerability argument. The red chokepoint markers reinforce it. The argument is visible in the diagram's shape.

For a policy audience concerned with supply chain resilience, this is Type III (Advocacy-Efficacy): the diagram shows what a resilient supply chain would need to avoid (the red convergence nodes) and makes the current concentration visible as a risk pattern.

## Craft /15 → 14

The 0-45-90 degree angle discipline must be maintained precisely. Six material lines converging at processing nodes will create complex routing challenges — the lines must be routed without crossing where possible, and where crossing is unavoidable, a bridge (small gap in one line) must be used to indicate the lines do not connect. This is standard schematic cartography practice.

## My Total: 91/100

The best supply chain schematic in the DEGAS project. The stage-anchor solution to the geographic anchor problem is the design innovation I most endorse. The explicit declaration of the topology-over-geography principle is an improvement on my own founding practice.

## Innovation Flagged

**#225 — Stage anchors as the supply chain equivalent of geographic anchors in schematic cartography**: Beck grammar applied to supply chains requires an anchor system analogous to geographic anchors in transit maps. Stage anchors (supply chain stages: EXTRACTION, PROCESSING, etc.) serve this function by providing position reference without geographic encoding. This is the explicit solution to work 0013's geographic anchor problem (#121 in the innovations log). The confirmation of this solution closes a known rubric gap.
