# Audience Lens — 0033 Coral Reef Cross-Section

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

I am an educated museum visitor. I see an underwater scene showing a coral reef from the surface down to 60 meters. The depth axis on the left (0m at top, 60m at bottom) tells me immediately that I'm seeing a vertical cross-section. Three horizontal zone labels tell me the ecological names. The organisms are beautiful and I recognize several of them (sea turtle, parrotfish, ray) immediately.

What I experience: I begin at the surface (warm, busy, colorful) and descend with the image to the mesophotic zone (dim, sparse, quiet). By the time I reach the bottom, I feel the darkness and emptiness of the deep zone. I didn't need to be told that this is a more sparse environment — I experienced it visually.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

I trust the ecological accuracy. The species I recognize (turtle, parrotfish) are correctly shown near the surface. The organisms in the deep zone look genuinely unfamiliar and deep-water (wire corals, cup corals, crinoids) in a way that feels scientifically grounded.

The environmental variable panel on the right is harder for me — I understand "light intensity" declining with depth, but "carbonate saturation" requires more knowledge than I have. The panel is interesting without being fully comprehensible to me.

## Legibility /15 → 14

For a museum audience: excellent. The descent experience is intuitive. The zone boundaries and labels are clear. The depth scale provides concrete reference (60m is about the height of a 20-story building — this helps me understand the scale).

The species name labels in 6pt italic are at the edge of my comfortable reading distance for a museum installation. At gallery viewing distance, I'd need to walk up close to read them.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

The visual richness in Zone 1 and the visual sparseness in Zone 3 both feel appropriate — not one feels padded, not the other feels empty. The work has the right visual rhythm.

## Resonance /15 → 15

This is the most beautiful visualization I have encountered in the DEGAS project. The descent from Zone 1's warmth and richness to Zone 3's cool sparseness produced a genuinely emotional experience for me. I arrived at the mesophotic zone feeling the depth — the quiet, the cold, the distance from the surface world. This is exactly what I want from a museum visualization: an experience, not just information.

I also noticed that the surface zone's richness — which is what we would lose to bleaching — makes the conservation stakes visible without any text. I didn't need to be told that losing this would be a loss. I could see it.

## Craft /15 → 14

The illustration is beautiful. The color temperature progression from warm surface to cool depth is both accurate and emotionally evocative.

## My Total: 88/100

The most beautiful work in the DEGAS project. It produces a genuine emotional experience alongside scientific information. This is what the best scientific illustration does — it makes you feel what you are learning.
