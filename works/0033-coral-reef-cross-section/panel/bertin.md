# Bertin — 0033 Coral Reef Cross-Section

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

Variable audit of this scientific illustration:
- Position (vertical): depth — ordered/quantitative. Downward direction (same grammar as work 0031). Correctly matched to ecological gradient running downward.
- Position (horizontal within zone): organism placement — naturalistic (representing ecological co-occurrence, not encoding a data variable). Correct for scientific illustration — organisms are placed where they live, not where a data encoding dictates.
- Size of organisms: species-specific biological scale — not a data variable. Correct.
- Visual density of organisms per zone: ecological richness — quantitative (approximate). This is the work's most important encoding choice. Density is an intrinsically ordered/quantitative variable when used consistently across zones. Correct.
- Color of organisms: species identity — nominal. Correct — the parrotfish's orange-blue coloring, the grouper's spotted pattern, the wire coral's dark vertical form are all accurate to the organism and encode nothing abstractly.

The grammar is primarily representational (organisms shown as they look) rather than abstractly encoded (organisms shown by abstract marks). This is appropriate for scientific illustration — the encoding IS the representation. Bertin's framework applies at a meta-level: the visual variables encode species identity through morphological representation, not through abstract marks.

The density gradient (Zone 1 dense, Zone 3 sparse) is the closest thing to an abstract encoding in the work, and it is an honest one — the ecological richness genuinely declines with depth in the mesophotic transition.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 17

The species placements are ecologically accurate to published reef ecology literature. Acropora corals at 0-10m, plate corals at 10-30m, cup corals and wire corals in the mesophotic zone — this distribution is documented.

The environmental variable panel (light, temperature, carbonate saturation) provides the mechanism explanation for the distribution pattern. The correlation between declining light and declining density is not explicitly drawn as a causal arrow — it is available to the viewer who reads both the main image and the panel graphs together. Step C: causation implied by spatial proximity and structural framing (the two elements share the same depth axis). Dock -1 (mild causal implication, not overreach).

Source declaration needed: "Species distributions based on coral reef ecology literature (Bongaerts et al. 2019 mesophotic coral ecosystems review; Sheppard et al. Coral Reefs of the Indian Ocean)."

## Legibility /15 → 13

Context: educated general public (museum installation, National Geographic-quality publication), contemplative study. The main cross-section is immediately comprehensible as a "view into the ocean" — the depth axis is intuitive (deeper = lower in image), the species are recognizable, and the three zone boundaries are clearly marked.

The species name labels (6pt) are at close-reading scale. For a museum installation at 60x90cm, close approach is required to read individual species names. The ecological zone labels and depth scale are readable at normal viewing distance.

The environmental variable panel requires close reading and some scientific literacy (understanding what "carbonate saturation" means). It is correctly positioned as a secondary information layer for the scientifically literate viewer.

## Visual Economy /15 → 14

The visual density in each zone is intrinsic complexity (Cluster H) — it encodes ecological richness. The organisms are not decorative; they are the data. The environmental variable panel adds quantitative context efficiently (three small graphs in 10cm of margin). The depth scale and zone labels are minimal and necessary.

The title and subtitle are economical — the subtitle declares the encoding logic ("visual density reflects ecological richness") which is the most important schema declaration.

## Resonance /15 → 14

The main resonance type is Type II (Emergent-Data): the ecosystem's own ecological reality produces the aesthetic effect. The surface zone's richness and the mesophotic zone's sparseness are emotionally distinct visual experiences that accurately encode the ecological reality. The viewer who reaches the mesophotic zone at the bottom of the image feels the emptiness — and that feeling is scientifically accurate.

Type V (Domain-Gated) is also present: the reef ecologist will read this image as a scientific document, recognizing every species by name from their morphology.

## Craft /15 → 14

Scientific illustration at Haeckel's standard requires morphological accuracy for every rendered organism. This is demanding craft — each of the 15-20 distinct organism types must be rendered accurately and at the correct relative size. High-quality biological illustration expertise required.

## My Total: 89/100

A strong scientific illustration that correctly extends Haeckel's grammar to ecological scene composition. The ecology-as-composition innovation (visual density encodes ecological richness) is the work's most important rubric contribution. The source declaration and causal implication note are the primary integrity additions needed.

## Innovation Flagged

**#226 — Ecology-as-composition: density gradient as ecological data**: in a multi-taxon ecological scene (as opposed to a single-taxon morphological plate), the density of organisms per zone encodes the zone's ecological richness. This density IS data — it is not decoration. The rubric's scientific illustration school (Haeckel grammar) addresses intrinsic complexity in single-organism plates (Cluster H amendment) but does not address density-as-data in ecological scenes. A new step: "If the scientific subject is an ecosystem rather than a single taxon, assess whether organism placement density correctly encodes ecological richness — and whether the density gradient is explained in the title/subtitle."
