# Tufte — 0033 Coral Reef Cross-Section

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

Haeckel grammar applied to an ecological scene rather than a single-taxon plate. The grammar extension is justified by the subject matter: a coral reef is not one organism; it is a community of organisms. Haeckel's own plates occasionally depicted small ecological groupings. The extension to a 60m vertical cross-section with three ecological zones is a larger step but a coherent one.

The subtitle declaration ("visual density reflects ecological richness") is the key schema declaration — it names the encoding logic that allows the viewer to read density as data rather than as aesthetic choice. This declaration is correct practice.

The environmental variable panel is the element I most admire: it provides the mechanistic context (why do organisms distribute this way?) in a small, efficiently designed secondary panel. This is layered information — fine detail available at close reading, without cluttering the primary reading at distance.

## Data/Story Integrity /20 → 16

Species placements are ecologically accurate. The density gradient is honest — the mesophotic zone genuinely has fewer species than the surface zone. The environmental variable panel's graphs (light, temperature, carbonate saturation) are correctly modeled for a tropical reef.

The causal connection between declining light and declining density is implied by the shared depth axis — the two panels are visually aligned, suggesting a relationship. This is appropriate causal implication (the relationship is real and well-documented) but should be supported by a brief mechanism note: "Photosynthetic corals require light; as light declines with depth, coral diversity declines."

Source declaration needed (per Bertin's review).

## Legibility /15 → 13

The cross-section is immediately comprehensible as an underwater view. The zone boundaries and labels are clear. The organism rendering is recognizable (sea turtle, parrotfish, ray are familiar to a general audience). The depth scale provides quantitative reference.

The environmental variable panel requires scientific literacy to fully interpret (carbonate saturation, aragonite) — correctly positioned as a secondary layer.

## Visual Economy /15 → 13

This is intrinsic complexity (Cluster H) throughout. The organisms are the data — there are no decorative elements. The environmental variable panel is efficient (three graphs in 10cm). The zone labels and depth scale are minimal.

The organism count (15-20 distinct species) is appropriate to the subject's ecological richness. Fewer species would misrepresent the reef's biodiversity; more would exceed the visual field's capacity.

## Resonance /15 → 14

The density gradient produces genuine emotional impact. The surface zone's visual richness contrasts with the mesophotic zone's near-emptiness in a way that is both aesthetically compelling and scientifically accurate. Type II (Emergent-Data) resonance is the primary type — the ecosystem's own ecological reality produces the effect.

The implicit conservation argument (the surface zone's richness is under threat from bleaching) is available to the viewer who knows about coral bleaching without being stated in the image. This is correctly handled — the work shows, it does not argue.

## Craft /15 → 14

Haeckel-standard scientific illustration requires expert biological illustration skills. The accuracy of each organism's morphological rendering is the craft standard. The color rendering (warm surface tones transitioning to cool deep tones) is the compositional craft element.

## My Total: 87/100

A strong scientific illustration that correctly extends the Haeckel grammar to ecological scene composition and correctly applies intrinsic complexity. The source declaration and mechanism note are the primary additions needed. The density-as-data innovation is the work's most important rubric contribution.
