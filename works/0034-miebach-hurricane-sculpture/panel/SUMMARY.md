## Key Agreements

**The Economy of Means criterion does not apply to three-dimensional woven artifacts.** All five reviewers identify this gap. The rubric's Execution dimension derives from the data-ink ratio — a criterion for marks on flat surfaces. A woven basket has threads, not marks. The relevant economy question is: does every material unit earn its place by data encoding, structural necessity, or both? Miebach's basket is extraordinarily economical by this measure (the weave structure IS the data structure), but the rubric cannot say so. All reviewers dock Execution not because the work fails its own standard but because the rubric's standard is inapplicable. This is a rubric gap, not a design failure.

**The height encoding is the strongest.** All reviewers agree: wall height encoding wave height is the most grammatically correct, most retinally efficient, and most immediately legible of the four encodings. The storm's asymmetry is visible from across a room.

**Dual-mode reading is correct for the gallery context.** No reviewer penalizes the key-dependent data reading. The aesthetic layer is not noise; it is the first layer of a two-layer reading appropriate for the intended context.

**The color encoding (temperature as hue) is the weakest.** Bertin identifies a grammatical concern — hue is not an ordered variable, but temperature is ordered data. The blue-white-red convention partially repairs this via cultural association, but the encoding is less rigorous than the other three.

---

## Key Tensions

**Tufte vs. Nightingale on economy:** Tufte scores Execution at 11, docking for the rubric gap rather than any design failure. Nightingale scores 12, applying a communication-economy standard (does the form reach its audience efficiently?) rather than a marks-economy standard. Both are correct — they are measuring different things. The tension exposes the gap: the rubric needs both criteria named for works that operate in physical space.

**Gallery context vs. reproduction context:** The work's data encodings are substantially inaccessible in any reproduction (photograph, catalog, digital display). Bertin and Tufte note this; Kandinsky accepts it as appropriate for the medium. The tension resolves toward: legibility is correctly scored for the intended context (gallery + key), and reproduction legibility should be noted in brackets rather than penalized.

## CLUSTER Q — CONFIRMED ACROSS WORKS 0026 + 0034

**Cluster Q: Data-art economy criterion** is confirmed across two works from three reviewers in each work.

- **Work 0026** (#203 Kandinsky, #205 Tufte): the economy criterion gap was first identified for a two-dimensional data-art work (the innovations log as visual texture). Both innovations named the same rubric gap from different angles.
- **Work 0034** (#229 Kandinsky, #231 Tufte, #233 Nightingale): the same gap is confirmed with greater force for a three-dimensional woven artifact, where the inapplicability of the data-ink ratio criterion is undeniable.

**The confirmed criterion gap:** The v2.0 rubric's Execution dimension asks "does every mark earn its place?" This is a flat-surface, ink-based criterion. For data-art — particularly three-dimensional or tactile works — the equivalent criterion is **material-density economy**: does every material unit (thread, structural element, color application) earn its place by data encoding, structural necessity, or both? For Miebach's basket, the answer is yes — the material IS the data — but the rubric has no language for this conclusion.

**Amendment recommendation:** Add to Execution dimension, Economy lens:

> *For three-dimensional data-art and tactile/material encodings:* Apply material-density economy rather than data-ink economy. Ask: does every material unit earn its place by data encoding, structural necessity, or both? Works where structure and data are the same thing (wall height IS wave height; weave density IS barometric pressure) achieve maximum material economy. The rubric's "decorative marks" category maps to "materially superfluous elements" in this context.

**Status: CLUSTER Q CONFIRMED — two works, five reviewer instances, rubric amendment identified.**

## Design Revision Priorities

1. **Resolve the hue encoding**: replace temperature-as-hue with a value (lightness) gradient, which is an ordered variable and grammatically correct for ordered data.
2. **Add a handling invitation**: in gallery contexts where physical examination is permitted, a small card reading "Please handle with care — the weave texture encodes barometric pressure data" would activate the haptic encoding channel.
3. **Archive the key with the object**: the encoding key should be photographically archived and attached to the object's museum record to prevent archival key-separation risk.
