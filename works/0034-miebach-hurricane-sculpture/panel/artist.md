# Artist — 0034 Hurricane Noel Data Sculpture

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

Miebach is working in the tradition of data-driven sculpture — a tradition that includes artists like Liz West, Refik Anadol, and the earlier weaving-as-data experiments of Anni Albers. What distinguishes genuine data-art from data-illustrated art is whether the data generates the form or merely decorates it. Here, the data unambiguously generates the form. Wall height is wave height. There is no design decision that could separate the two without destroying both.

The circular plan encoding is the most compositionally sophisticated element — it preserves the storm's radial geometry without labeling it. A viewer who intuitively reads the circular form as hurricane-like is not projecting; they are reading correctly. This is the school's highest aspiration: the form teaches the viewer to read it through its own structure. Minor lapse: the color gradient feels applied rather than woven. Temperature as color is less materially integrated than pressure as weave density.

## Integrity /20 → 17

Reportive. The encoding is declared. The companion documentation provides the encoding key and declares the data source (NOAA meteorological records). I note one framing question: the encoding key is adjacent to the work, not embedded in it. A viewer who encounters the object without the key receives an honest aesthetic experience but cannot access the data reading. This is a choice, not a failure — but it creates a contingent integrity dependency. If the key becomes separated from the object (a real archival risk for sculptural works), the data reading is lost. This is not a dock; it is a note.

## Legibility /15 → 10

The gallery context is the right context. In a museum or gallery setting with the encoding key mounted on the adjacent wall, a patient visitor can read the object as data visualization. The four-variable encoding (temperature, pressure, wave height, wind speed) is complex but not illegible — the key provides the grammar and the object is physically accessible for close reading. Standalone legibility is genuinely low: [3/15]. But the work was not designed for standalone legibility, and I do not hold this against it.

What I do note: the weave-density encoding requires physical proximity that a gallery setting may constrain. If the object is behind glass or on a pedestal at distance, the pressure encoding becomes inaccessible. Miebach's best exhibition contexts allow handling or very close approach.

## Execution /15 → 12

The craft is excellent. The weaving is precise, the height variations proportional, the color gradients clean. The structural execution — a basket that holds its shape over the variable wall heights while maintaining weave integrity — is technically demanding and well achieved.

But the Economy of Means criterion — "does every mark earn its place?" — does not translate cleanly to a woven artifact. The rubric's criterion is ink-based; this work is thread-based. The relevant question is whether every thread is doing data work, structural work, or both. By that standard, the basket is extraordinarily economical — the weave structure IS the data structure, and nothing is purely decorative. But the rubric cannot score this correctly. I flag it as a rubric gap.

I score 12: the craft is high (13 would be correct by craft alone) but the Economy of Means criterion cannot be fully applied, creating scoring uncertainty.

## Resonance /15 → 14

The Experiential resonance is the strongest in this project for a sculptural work. The object demands presence — you must be in the room with it. The variation in wall height creates a physical topography that the viewer perceives intuitively as something wave-like before they read any label. This is pre-conceptual resonance: the form communicates the storm's character before the data reading begins.

With the encoding key, a second layer of resonance fires — the intellectual satisfaction of reading the data through the material, discovering that the looser weave corresponds to higher pressure and the tighter weave to the eye wall. This is a rare achievement: aesthetic resonance that survives the data revelation rather than being replaced by it.

## Purpose /15 → 13

Aesthetic purpose, fully realized. Miebach's stated goal is to give meteorological data material presence — to create an object that makes the storm touchable. This goal is achieved completely. The form serves the function. There are no human-stakes urgency considerations that the aesthetic register fails to address — the storm is past; the purpose is experiential and archival, not advocacy. Purpose score is high: the form is exactly right for this purpose.

## My Total: 72/100

A genuine achievement in data-art. The rubric's main limitation is in Execution, where the Economy of Means criterion was not designed for three-dimensional woven artifacts. This is Cluster Q territory — the same gap that appeared in work 0026 is now undeniable.

## Innovation Flagged

**#230 — Haptic encoding as a data channel**: Miebach's basket uses weave density as a data encoding, which operates haptically (through touch and close visual examination) rather than purely visually. The rubric has no criterion for haptic data channels — it assesses visual marks. A haptic channel creates a different kind of legibility, a different economy criterion, and a different resonance type. This is a genuine gap beyond the Economy of Means question that Cluster Q is tracking.
