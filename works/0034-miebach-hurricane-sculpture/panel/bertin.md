# Bertin — 0034 Hurricane Noel Data Sculpture

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

I approach this work as I approach any encoding problem: identify the visual variables in use, determine what data types they are encoding, and assess whether the pairing is grammatically correct. The complication here is that this is not a two-dimensional mark system. The visual variables of the basket are: height (quantitative), texture/density (ordered), hue (ordered), and pattern (selective). Let me audit each.

**Height encoding wave height**: height is an ordered and quantitative variable in three dimensions. Wave height is quantitative. The pairing is correct and maximally retinally efficient — height is read automatically and directly. No instruction needed. This is the strongest encoding in the work.

**Weave density encoding barometric pressure**: texture/density is an ordered variable with limited quantitative precision. Barometric pressure is quantitative. The pairing is appropriate for ordered discrimination but loses quantitative precision. A viewer can detect relative pressure but not estimate absolute values without the key. This is the most interesting encoding grammatically — density is used here near the edge of its perceptual capacity.

**Hue encoding temperature**: hue is selective and associative, not ordered or quantitative. Temperature is ordered and quantitative. This is technically a grammatical error — hue should not encode ordered data. However, the color gradient used (blue → white → red) leverages cultural and perceptual associations with temperature that partially repair the error. It is the weakest encoding.

**Pattern interruption encoding wind speed threshold**: selective, binary. Wind speed threshold is selective (above/below). Correct pairing.

## Integrity /20 → 17

Encoding key declared, data sources named, variable ranges documented. I find no integrity issues beyond the note about archival key-separation risk that other reviewers will name.

## Legibility /15 → 10

For three-dimensional encoding, I add a variable to the legibility assessment that the rubric does not explicitly address: physical accessibility. The weave-density encoding requires examination at approximately 15–30 cm distance. The height encoding is accessible from several meters. The color encoding is accessible from any distance. Pattern interruptions require close examination.

The multi-distance legibility structure is interesting: different encodings become accessible at different viewing distances, creating a layered reading that rewards approach. This is compositionally sophisticated. But it also means the full reading requires physical movement around and toward the object — a constraint that reduces legibility in any non-gallery context.

## Execution /15 → 11

I must note the rubric gap precisely. The Economy of Means criterion asks: does every mark earn its place? For a woven basket, the equivalent question is: does every thread earn its place by data encoding, structural necessity, or both? I count four encoding channels and one structural necessity (the frame). The frame is the only element not carrying data; it is structurally required. By material-density standards, the economy is very high.

But I cannot score this precisely because the rubric provides no anchor for material-density economy in three-dimensional data-art. I score the craft half of Execution at 13 (the technical execution is precise and well-controlled) and the Economy half at 9 (cannot fully assess by rubric criteria). Combined: 11.

## Resonance /15 → 12

Experiential resonance in physical encounter is strong. Domain resonance for data-art practitioners is high — they will recognize Miebach's grammar immediately and appreciate its formal rigor. For general gallery audiences without the key, resonance is aesthetic only.

## Purpose /15 → 13

Aesthetic purpose, correctly realized. The storm has been given material presence. Purpose is served.

## My Total: 69/100

The work is competent and grammatically sound in three of four encodings. The main rubric limitation is the Economy of Means criterion, which I confirm as a gap for this school. I note this as a Cluster Q confirmation from a third reviewer.

## Innovation Flagged

**#232 — Multi-distance legibility in three-dimensional data encoding**: a woven data sculpture creates a layered legibility structure where different encodings become accessible at different viewing distances. Height is legible from meters; weave density from 30 cm; pattern from 10 cm. This is a distinct legibility type not addressed by the v2.0 rubric, which assumes a primary viewing context and distance. Three-dimensional data-art may require a legibility modifier specifying viewing distance and accessibility constraints per encoding channel. This supplements the Cluster Q economy question with a Legibility question about spatial accessibility.
