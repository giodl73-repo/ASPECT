# Kandinsky — 0034 Hurricane Noel Data Sculpture

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

Data-art as a school asks whether the data and the aesthetic are genuinely fused — not illustrated by each other, but constitutive of each other. The standard is that removing the data would destroy the aesthetic, and removing the aesthetic would destroy the data's embodiment. Miebach's basket meets this standard completely. The wave height IS the wall height. The pressure IS the weave density. These are not metaphors; they are direct physical correspondences.

The circular plan form recovers the storm's radial geometry — a compositional decision that encodes information about the storm's structure without being named as such. This is economical in the way that abstract composition must be economical: every element does multiple work. I note one partial fidelity issue: the color gradient (temperature) is the most conventional encoding in the piece — it operates visually rather than materially, and feels slightly external to the object's dominant haptic logic. This is a minor lapse. The core grammar is otherwise complete.

## Integrity /20 → 17

The work is reportive: it represents external meteorological data. The encoding key is provided in adjacent documentation, which declares all variables encoded and their ranges. Barometric pressure range (945–1012 mbar) and the 50-knot wind threshold are named. The circular plan's orientation (north at 12 o'clock, clockwise reading) is declared. I find no suppression of confidence and no overreach — the object claims to represent the storm's measurements at specific bearings and moments, and the documentation confirms those claims. The ontological framing is straightforward: storm measurement data translated into material properties. No contamination.

## Legibility /15 → 10

Primary audience: gallery visitors, data-art researchers, museum-adjacent public. Reading context: contemplative study, accompanied by the encoding key. Standalone legibility is low — without the key, the basket is an aesthetically compelling object that reveals nothing specific. With the key, a patient viewer can extract quantitative information by reading the height, density, and color at each radial position.

The dual-mode reading is correct for a gallery context. I do not penalize it. But I note that even with the key, the simultaneous encoding of four variables in three dimensions requires sustained close reading. The weave-density encoding in particular demands physical examination — one must hold the object or be very close to it. Standalone legibility: [4/15].

## Execution /15 → 11

Here the rubric encounters its most serious limitation. The data-ink ratio — Tufte's Economy of Means — is a two-dimensional metric applied to marks on a flat surface. A woven basket has no ink. Its economy must be measured differently: how many variables are encoded per unit of material? how much material is load-bearing (structurally necessary) vs. data-encoding vs. aesthetically motivated?

By the material-density standard, the basket is highly economical. The weave structure is itself the data structure — there are no decorative additions. But the rubric does not provide this criterion explicitly. I score Execution at 11: strong craft (the weaving is precisely executed, the color gradients clean, the height variations proportional to the data), but the Economy of Means criterion is incompletely applicable. The gap is a rubric gap, not a design failure.

## Resonance /15 → 13

Experiential resonance is the primary type and it fires strongly. The object demands physical presence — one walks around it, examines it close and at distance, and experiences the storm through these two registers. The varying wall height creates a physical rhythm that corresponds to wave height variation; a viewer who has read the key and then looks at the object can feel the relationship between the visual form and the meteorological reality.

Domain resonance (meteorologists, data-art practitioners, weavers) is secondary and genuine — each domain brings its own reading. The multimodal nature of the object (visual, haptic, structural) amplifies rather than fragments the experiential effect.

## Purpose /15 → 13

This is an aesthetic work in the rubric's classification. It is designed to transform meteorological data into material experience — to give the storm a physical presence that numerical tables cannot achieve. The form serves this purpose fully. There is no human-stakes urgency requiring a different resonance register; Hurricane Noel's data is not deployed here as mortality argument but as material poetry. The Purpose dimension does not penalize this choice. A well-executed aesthetic purpose fully realized is a Purpose success.

## My Total: 71/100

The work is strong within data-art's grammar. The main limitation is rubric-external: the Economy of Means criterion is not defined for three-dimensional woven artifacts. Execution scores 11 because I am measuring what I can measure, not what the work deserves on its own terms.

## Innovation Flagged

**#229 — Material-density economy criterion for three-dimensional data-art**: the rubric's Economy of Means is derived from the data-ink ratio — a criterion defined for marks on flat surfaces. For woven artifacts, the equivalent criterion is material-density economy: how many data variables are encoded per unit of material, and does the material's structural necessity double as data encoding? Miebach's basket is highly economical by this measure (load-bearing structure = data structure), but the rubric cannot say so. This is a Cluster Q confirmation: the same rubric gap identified in work 0026 (innovations #203, #205) applies here with greater force because the material-physical dimension makes the gap undeniable.
