# Nightingale — 0034 Hurricane Noel Data Sculpture

## School Fidelity /20 → 16

Data-art is a school I respect but do not inhabit. My instinct with any visualization is to ask: who is this for, and what should they do after seeing it? For data-art, the answer to "what should they do" is: experience something they could not have experienced from the numbers. If the form achieves that, the school fidelity question is secondary to the experiential success.

Miebach's basket achieves this. The storm has become a touchable object. A meteorologist who has spent her career looking at pressure maps and wind speed tables encounters this object and understands the storm in a different register — not more accurately, but differently, bodily. That is what data-art is for, and this work does it.

The encoding key is the right structural decision. It keeps the object clean while providing the grammar for those who want the data reading. The wall height is the most immediately legible encoding — you see the storm's asymmetry before reading anything.

## Integrity /20 → 17

The data is NOAA-sourced and accurately encoded. The encoding key is present and complete. I have no integrity concerns.

One advocacy note: the storm data here is not deployed as advocacy — Miebach is not arguing that hurricanes should be prevented or that climate policy should change. The data is present as data, not as argument. From my perspective this is a missed opportunity — the intersection of hurricane intensity and warming sea surface temperatures is a powerful advocacy story. But data-art has its own Purpose, and I do not penalize a work for not being what it was not designed to be.

## Legibility /15 → 10

For a gallery audience with the encoding key: adequate. For a public audience without the key: low. The dual-mode reading is correct for Miebach's intended context, and I accept it.

My concern is that the work's most powerful legibility moment — the wall height variation visible from a distance — is also the most physically demanding to read precisely. A viewer who sees the basket's uneven perimeter from across a room intuits wave height variation without reading a single measurement. That is excellent legibility for the aesthetic mode. For the data mode, they must approach, consult the key, and examine the weave. Two very different legibility experiences in the same object.

## Execution /15 → 12

The craft is excellent. The weaving is precise. The color gradients are clean. The proportionality between data values and material properties is maintained. My concern with Economy of Means applies here as it does to Kandinsky and Tufte — the criterion was designed for flat visual marks, and it does not translate cleanly to a woven artifact. I note the gap.

I am less troubled than Tufte by the gap, because I think the relevant economy question for advocacy work is whether the form reaches its audience — and for data-art in a gallery context, the form reaches exactly the right audience in exactly the right way. Economy of communication, not economy of marks.

## Resonance /15 → 12

Experiential resonance is strong. Advocacy resonance is absent — this is not an advocacy work and I do not penalize it for that. Domain resonance (data-art practitioners, meteorologists, materials artists) is genuine.

The work will be remembered by people who have encountered it physically. That is the measure that matters most for gallery work.

## Purpose /15 → 13

Aesthetic purpose, fully realized. The work gives meteorological data material presence. That is its function, and the form serves it completely.

## My Total: 70/100

Excellent data-art with the rubric's main limitation being in Execution, where the Economy of Means criterion is not defined for three-dimensional material encoding. The panel will need to address this gap formally — I consider this a Cluster Q confirmation.

## Innovation Flagged

**#233 — Economy of communication vs. economy of marks in data-art**: Tufte's Economy of Means criterion optimizes for marks-per-datum on a flat surface. For data-art in gallery contexts, a different economy applies: does the form reach its intended audience with the intended effect? Miebach's basket is maximally economical by the communication economy criterion — every material choice serves both structure and data, and the object reaches its audience completely. The rubric needs both criteria, named and distinguished, for works that operate in physical rather than flat-surface space.
