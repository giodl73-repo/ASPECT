# Tufte — 0034 Hurricane Noel Data Sculpture

## School Fidelity /20 → 15

Data-art is a legitimate school. I have written about sculpture and dimensional work in *Envisioning Information* — the question of how three-dimensional data representation operates is real and serious. Miebach is working in this space correctly: the data is not illustrated by the form; the form IS the data. Wall height equals wave height. Weave density equals barometric pressure. This is not decoration; it is encoding.

The school fidelity question is whether the grammar is correctly applied. For data-art, the grammar demands that every material decision either encodes data or is structurally necessary. The circular plan form is correct — it encodes the storm's radial geometry. The color gradient encodes temperature. The pattern interruptions encode wind speed threshold events. I find the grammar largely sound, with the note that the color gradient is the weakest element — it operates visually like a conventional chart color, rather than materially like the other encodings.

## Integrity /20 → 17

The encoding key is the integrity anchor. It declares all four variables, their ranges, and the encoding conventions. The data source (NOAA) is named. The circular orientation (north at top, clockwise) is declared. I find no suppression, no overreach, and no framing dishonesty. The companion documentation does what good documentation must: it makes the encoding falsifiable. A viewer with the data and the key can verify that the wall height at the north position corresponds to the wave height at that bearing.

One note: the encoding key is adjacent to the work, not intrinsic to it. This creates a medium-term archival integrity risk (key separation). For this review I score it as declared — the key exists and is present in the intended reading context.

## Legibility /15 → 9

Here is where the work shows its limitation. The data-ink ratio framework exists because visual marks on a plane can be decoded by the eye without physical access. This basket requires physical proximity — the weave-density encoding is not legible from across a gallery; you must be within arm's reach. Pattern interruptions (wind speed events) require careful examination of the weave structure.

For its intended context (gallery + key), legibility is acceptable. For any reproduction context (photograph, catalog, digital display), three of the four encodings are substantially or completely inaccessible. I score 9 for the intended gallery context; standalone legibility in reproduction context would score 3.

## Execution /15 → 11

I want to be precise about what my Economy of Means criterion is doing and where it stops. The criterion — "does every mark earn its place by data, intrinsic necessity, structural function, or cognitive accessibility?" — was developed for visual marks on flat surfaces. A woven basket has threads, not marks. The data-ink ratio's denominator does not exist.

For three-dimensional data-art, the relevant economy question is: does every material unit (thread, structural element, color application) encode data, serve structural necessity, or both? By this criterion, the basket is extremely economical. The weave IS the data; the structure IS the data; very little material is purely decorative. But I cannot score this precisely because the rubric does not provide the criterion.

I score 11: good craft (the weaving is technically skilled, the proportionality between data and form is maintained) with a rubric-gap penalty on Economy that I cannot avoid. This is the same gap that appeared in work 0026. I name it again: Cluster Q. The data-art economy criterion is not in the rubric.

## Resonance /15 → 12

Experiential resonance fires for viewers who encounter the object physically with the key in hand. The experience of reading the storm through the material — finding the eye wall in the tightest weave, seeing the temperature gradient in the color, feeling the height variation correspond to wave action — is genuinely powerful. The pre-conceptual aesthetic layer (the object is arresting before its encoding is known) is also strong.

Domain resonance is moderate — meteorologists will find the encoding accurate but the form unfamiliar; data-art practitioners will recognize the grammar immediately. I score 12: resonance is genuine but somewhat contingent on physical presence and contextual preparation.

## Purpose /15 → 13

Aesthetic purpose, correctly pursued. The form gives meteorological data material presence, which is what aesthetic data-art is for. No human-stakes urgency requires a different resonance register. Purpose is well served.

## My Total: 67/100

Strong work in its school, with the primary limitation being the rubric's inability to assess Economy of Means for three-dimensional material encoding. I am scoring the gap rather than the work's failure.

## Innovation Flagged

**#231 — Economy of Means for three-dimensional data-art**: the v2.0 rubric's Execution dimension provides "Economy of means — does every mark earn its place?" for flat visual work. For three-dimensional data-art, the equivalent criterion is: does every material unit earn its place by data encoding, structural necessity, or both? Miebach's basket is maximally economical by this criterion — structure and data are the same thing. The rubric gap is confirmed for the second time across two works (0026 #203, #205; now 0034 #231). Cluster Q is confirmed.
