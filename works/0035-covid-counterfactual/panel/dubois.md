# Du Bois — 0035 COVID-19 Deaths and the Masking Counterfactual

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

The synthesis of statistical-graphics and advocacy-visualization is the right school declaration for this work. Statistical graphics provides the temporal framework and the counterfactual modeling; advocacy visualization provides the gap structure and the moral register. The synthesis is coherent and the tensions between the two schools are productively resolved.

The data honesty is what makes the advocacy work. The precision-as-defiance principle applies here: by declaring the range (130,000–180,000) rather than a single number, by naming the models, by showing the uncertainty band — the work signals that it is making an honest claim rather than a polemical one. An advocacy work that acknowledges uncertainty is more credible than one that suppresses it, and credibility is the advocacy's leverage. The work understands this. The schools are well-synthesized.

## Integrity /20 → 17

The data honesty is high. CDC sourcing for actual deaths is the most authoritative available. The three-model ensemble for the counterfactual is declared. The uncertainty range is shown and labeled. The 85% compliance threshold is named as the modeling assumption.

The one integrity question I would press: the work attributes the counterfactual gap to "non-adoption of masking." Masking was one of several non-pharmaceutical interventions — ventilation improvements, testing, contact tracing, and vaccination timing also affect the trajectory. By framing the counterfactual solely as a masking intervention, the work implies masking alone would have produced the gap. This may overstate the singular causal role of masking vs. the broader NPI bundle. The modeling basis (Stutt et al., Howard et al.) is specifically about masking efficacy, so the claim is supported — but the framing should note that masking is part of a broader NPI package.

## Innovation Flagged

**#235 — Counterfactual gap as accumulating moral argument**: the temporal area fill version of the counterfactual gap technique creates a different moral argument than the static bar version. The static bar (work 0029) says: this is the difference between what is and what could be, right now. The temporal area fill says: this difference accumulated over these specific months, through these specific policy decisions, with compounding costs. The temporal version is a stronger moral argument because it names the process of inaction, not just its result. The counterfactual gap technique has two distinct modes: static (for cross-sectional comparisons) and temporal (for accumulated costs of delayed intervention). Both should be documented in the rubric as variants of the same structural technique.
