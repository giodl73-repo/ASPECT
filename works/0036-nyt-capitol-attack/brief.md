# Brief — 0036 NYT "A 3-D Journey through the Capitol Attack"

## The Argument

On January 6, 2021, the United States Capitol was breached by a crowd assembled to prevent the certification of the 2020 presidential election results. The NYT Visual Investigations team spent months reconstructing the event from video footage, satellite imagery, architectural plans, and geolocation data to produce a scroll narrative that answers a single question: how did this happen, room by room, corridor by corridor, minute by minute?

The argument is carried by the sequence of visual forms. Each form — satellite imagery, floor plans, annotated video stills, 3D corridor reconstructions, timeline charts — reveals a different aspect of the event that the others cannot. The sequence creates a cumulative understanding: from aerial overview, down to the building, into the corridors, and finally onto the timeline of specific moments.

## Design Approach

Multi-form scroll narrative (web publication, NYT.com). Forms deployed in sequence:
1. **Satellite imagery** with crowd size annotation — establishes the scale of the assembly
2. **Annotated architectural floor plans** — shows the building's layout and the points of breach
3. **Video stills with movement paths overlaid** — places individuals in specific corridors at specific times
4. **3D corridor reconstructions** — gives spatial understanding that 2D plans cannot
5. **Timeline charts** — places the spatial events in temporal sequence

The coherence question: does the sequence of visual forms produce a single argument, or does the form-switching fragment the reader's attention? The design's test is whether each transition between forms feels necessary — does the new form answer a question that the previous form raised but could not resolve?
