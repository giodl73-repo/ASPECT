# Audience — 0036 NYT Capitol Attack

## School Fidelity /20 → 15

I don't come to this as a visualization expert. I come to this as someone who wants to understand what happened on January 6, and whether this work helps me do that. The answer is: mostly yes, with some friction from the form-switching.

The satellite imagery is immediately clear — I understand the scale of the crowd. The floor plan takes a moment — I need to orient myself (where is the Senate chamber? which door is the main entrance?) but the labels help. The video stills with paths are effective — I can see specific people in specific corridors, and the paths help me understand movement. The 3D reconstruction is the most visually striking form, and I need a moment to re-orient from the 2D floor plan, but it's worth it. The timeline is clear and important.

The school fidelity, from my perspective, is whether each form does something the others cannot. The answer is yes, for all five. Each form is answering a different question. The sequence works.

## Integrity /20 → 16

I trust the NYT's verification for this work. The level of sourcing documentation is publicly available and clearly extensive. I have no integrity concerns as a reader. The one thing I notice: some of the movement paths on video stills are drawn in a way that suggests certainty about paths I can't independently verify. A small "reconstructed" label on each path would reassure me.

## Legibility /15 → 13

The scroll is legible but demanding. I spent about 25 minutes with this work on my first read. The five form transitions are the main friction — each time the visual grammar changes, I need a moment to re-orient. The text bridges help, but there's a real cognitive cost.

My specific legibility concern: the timeline chart (the last form) is the most important for understanding sequence, but it arrives after the most demanding parts of the scroll. By the time I reached the timeline, I was slightly fatigued and had to make a real effort to read it carefully. An overview at the top — a thumbnail of all five forms with one-sentence captions — would help prepare me for what's coming.

## Execution /15 → 13

The execution is consistently high. Everything is clearly labeled. The color conventions are consistent. The video stills are well-chosen — they are evidentiary without being gratuitously disturbing. The 3D reconstruction includes good orientation aids.

My one execution note: the transition text between forms (the paragraphs that bridge from one visual grammar to the next) is doing very important work, but it's formatted as plain body text and can be easily skimmed past. Giving these transition paragraphs a slightly different visual treatment — a shaded background, a different font size — would signal their importance.

## Resonance /15 → 13

This work answered my questions about January 6. I now understand how it happened, in spatial and temporal detail that I couldn't get from text accounts. The 3D reconstruction in particular was striking — understanding the physical space of the corridors made the events more real and more serious than photographs alone had done.

The resonance for me as a general reader: I have a complete account. I know what happened. I can no longer accept "it wasn't that bad" or "the building wasn't really breached." The visualization constitutes knowledge that is difficult to argue with.

## Purpose /15 → 14

The work exists to give me, as a citizen and a reader, a complete visual record of what happened. It succeeds at this purpose. I came away from it knowing more, knowing it in a form that's more concrete and more memorable than text, and understanding the sequence of events in a way that is directly useful for forming political and civic judgments.

## My Total: 84/100

The five-form structure works for me as a reader, though with real cognitive cost. The Cluster R question (does multi-form narrative coherence work?) is answered: yes, it works, when each form is motivated by a question the previous form raised. The transitions feel necessary, not arbitrary.

## Innovation Flagged

**#245 — Reader fatigue in multi-form scroll narrative**: as a general reader who spent 25 minutes with a five-form scroll narrative, I experienced increasing cognitive fatigue with each form transition. By the fifth form (timeline), my reading was less careful than at the first form (satellite). This is a legibility concern that the rubric has no name for: the cumulative cognitive cost of multiple form transitions in a single reading session. A multi-form narrative should consider sequencing its forms in order of cognitive demand — lighter forms first, most demanding forms (requiring the most re-orientation) in the middle, and a synthesizing form last. The timeline, as the most abstract and sequential form, should perhaps appear earlier in the sequence, with 3D reconstruction as the emotionally demanding climax.
