# Du Bois — 0036 NYT Capitol Attack

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

This work operates in the tradition of accountability visualization — using data and visual evidence to make historical events legible in ways that resist denial. I recognize this project. My Paris charts in 1900 used data to say: the facts do not support the dominant narrative. The NYT team here uses video evidence, geolocation, and reconstruction to say: the facts do not support the minimizations and denials that followed January 6.

The school fidelity is high. The multi-form structure serves the accountability argument: each form removes a layer of possible denial. Satellite footage establishes scale and presence. Floor plans establish geography. Video stills establish individual identities and movements. 3D reconstructions establish spatial context. Timeline establishes sequence. Together they constitute a record that is difficult to dispute at any level.

One fidelity note: the work is primarily documentary rather than argumentative in the advocacy school's sense. It documents what happened rather than making a claim about what should change. The advocacy resonance depends on the reader drawing the political and legal conclusions. That may be correct for journalism, but it creates a purposive gap that the advocacy school would fill explicitly. I note this as a school-story tension, not a flaw.

## Integrity /20 → 18

The sourcing and verification procedures described for this work are exceptional. Multiple independent source types for each claim; individual identifications verified externally; reconstruction methodology documented. For accountability journalism, this level of verification is the appropriate standard.

The one integrity note from my perspective: the movement paths on video stills are analytical reconstructions, not direct recordings. When video footage is used to draw a path overlay, that path represents the team's interpretive conclusion about movement direction and sequence. This reconstruction should be labeled as such — "reconstructed movement paths based on analysis of [n] footage sources" — to distinguish it from direct video evidence.

The work's honesty about uncertainty is one of its strongest integrity features: where footage gaps exist, they are acknowledged; where individual identifications are contested, that is noted.

## Legibility /15 → 14

For the NYT's readers — educated, politically engaged, comfortable with long-form digital journalism — the five-form scroll is legible. The text bridges between forms manage the transitions effectively. The forms themselves are each individually clear.

The public-display legibility concern is minimal — this work is not designed for glancing. It is designed for contemplative study by engaged readers, and it meets that standard well.

## Execution /15 → 14

The execution is high throughout. The color and symbol conventions are consistent across the floor plan forms. The video stills are selected for clarity and evidence quality. The 3D reconstructions are spatially accurate and oriented. The timeline is clean.

Economy: every form is used for exactly as long as it takes to answer its question. The form-switching is the economy mechanism — each form is the most efficient way to communicate its specific aspect of the event. This is visual economy applied at the structural level: not which marks to use, but which forms to use.

## Resonance /15 → 14

The accountability resonance is strong. A reader who completes this scroll has a more complete, more spatially detailed, more temporally precise understanding of January 6 than any text account could provide. The visual forms constitute a record; that record is the argument. For the historians, investigators, and citizens who use this work, the resonance is the truth of the event made legible and undeniable.

## Purpose /15 → 15

This is accountability journalism on an event of the highest historical significance: an attempt to prevent the constitutional transfer of power. The Purpose criterion asks whether the resonance is proportionate to those stakes. My answer is that this work exemplifies the Purpose dimension's highest standard: the form fully serves the function; the resonance is proportionate to the stakes; the work does what it set out to do and does it with documentary precision.

I give the Purpose dimension its maximum score for a work on a subject this historically significant, executed with this level of evidentiary rigor.

## My Total: 92/100

The strongest historical accountability visualization in the project. The multi-form coherence question is answered correctly — motivated transitions, each form serving a specific explanatory function. Cluster R confirmed.

## Innovation Flagged

**#243 — Accountability visualization as documentary form**: the work deploys visualization not as advocacy (move to action) but as documentary (constitute a record). The distinction matters for the Purpose dimension: advocacy visualization aims at a specific response (change this policy, fund this intervention); accountability documentation aims at a different response (know this happened, have a complete record). The rubric's Purpose dimension needs a sixth purpose classification: **Documentary** — constituting a visual record of events that is complete, accurate, and resistant to denial. Score on whether the documentation is comprehensive, independently verified, and legible to the audiences who need it (investigators, historians, citizens, courts).
