# Historian — 0036 NYT Capitol Attack

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

Narrative visualization has a short but significant history. Its direct precedents include the NYT's own "512 Paths to the White House" (2012), the Guardian's data journalism narratives, and the earlier tradition of news graphics that combined maps, diagrams, and photographs in print. The multi-form scroll narrative is a specifically digital form that emerged approximately 2012–2015 with the first long-scroll web publications.

This work's most significant historical precedent is Minard's Napoleon map — not in form (there is no flow map here) but in ambition: the attempt to encode multiple dimensions of a complex event into a single coherent reading experience. The NYT team has done what Minard did for the Russian campaign: assembled disparate data types (satellite, architectural, video, geolocation, temporal) into a narrative that a reader can follow from beginning to end and arrive at a complete understanding.

The school fidelity is high. The form-switching is managed through a principle that narrative visualization has developed: each form should answer a question the previous form raised. The work meets this criterion in all five transitions.

## Integrity /20 → 17

From a historical perspective, the most important integrity criterion is: does this record represent what actually happened, and can future historians verify the evidentiary basis of each claim? The NYT team's methodology documentation (published separately) addresses this. The individual identifications are supported by multiple independent sources. The movement paths are labeled as reconstructed.

One historical integrity note: the 3D corridor reconstructions are the most interpretive element. A historian in 2050 consulting this work will need to know: are these 3D models derived from physical measurements of the actual spaces, or from photogrammetric reconstruction from video footage, or from architectural drawings scaled to known dimensions? Each has different accuracy implications. The reconstruction method should be explicitly declared in the figure notes, not only in the methodology documentation.

## Legibility /15 → 13

For the educated general reader in 2021, the five-form scroll is legible. For a historian in 2026 consulting this work, legibility depends on whether the interactive elements have been archived. Scroll narratives that depend on JavaScript-driven transitions are vulnerable to digital obsolescence. If the interactive elements fail, the text bridges between forms become orphaned prose attached to static images. The archival legibility of this work is a serious concern that the historical community has raised for all long-form interactive journalism.

Contemporary digital context: [legibility at time of publication: 13/15; archival legibility as static record: 7/15].

## Execution /15 → 13

The execution quality is high for each individual form. The consistency of conventions across the floor plan sequence is good — the same color for the same function, the same symbol for the same event type. The video still selection demonstrates careful editorial judgment (selecting frames that are maximally evidentiary without being gratuitous).

The one execution concern is the 3D reconstruction's frame of reference: north arrows and scale bars are present, but the orientation of the 3D views (is the viewer looking north? south?) is not always immediately obvious. A consistent orientation aid across all 3D views would improve navigability.

## Resonance /15 → 13

Historical resonance is high — this work will be consulted by historians, investigators, and the legal system for decades. It constitutes a primary source record of a historically unprecedented event. The domain resonance for historians and investigators fires at maximum intensity.

Public resonance at the time of publication was high. The work established the detailed factual record that became the basis for legal proceedings and congressional investigations. That is accountability journalism's highest resonance achievement.

## Purpose /15 → 14

Accountability journalism on the most significant domestic crisis in recent American history. Purpose is fully served. The work exists to constitute a record and make it publicly legible, which it does. I note Du Bois's innovation regarding "documentary" as a Purpose classification — I agree that this work exemplifies a purpose type the rubric does not currently name.

## My Total: 87/100

Excellent narrative visualization with historical significance. Multi-form coherence works. The archival legibility concern is the most important long-term issue.

## Innovation Flagged

**#244 — Archival legibility as a Legibility modifier for interactive work**: the v2.0 rubric notes that dynamic works should append [static: X/15] for their static reproduction score. Interactive scroll narratives that rely on JavaScript transitions face a more severe archival legibility issue: the interactive experience may become completely inaccessible as web technologies change, leaving only static images and orphaned text. A modifier should be added: for interactive web-based works, note [archival static: X/15] as a distinct score from [static: X/15], acknowledging the difference between a static printed reproduction (which preserves the visual information) and a broken interactive archive (which may not). This is the Cluster R adjacent question: multi-form coherence depends on the transitions working; if the transitions fail technically, the coherence fails with them.
