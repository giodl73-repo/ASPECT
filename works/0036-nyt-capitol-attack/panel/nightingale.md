# Nightingale — 0036 NYT Capitol Attack

## School Fidelity /20 → 18

Narrative visualization at its most demanding: five forms, a complex event, a historically significant subject, and the requirement that the reader arrive at a complete understanding without the disorientation that form-switching can produce. The NYT Visual Investigations team has largely succeeded.

The sequence logic is correct. Each form transition is motivated — the reader asks a question that the current form cannot answer, and the next form answers it. Satellite establishes scale; floor plan establishes interior geography; video stills establish individual presence; 3D reconstruction establishes spatial experience; timeline establishes temporal sequence. The transitions feel necessary rather than arbitrary. This is the school's most demanding criterion and this work meets it.

I note one fidelity lapse: the 3D corridor reconstructions are not labeled with their reconstruction methodology. Were these photogrammetric? Architectural drawings scaled to known dimensions? The visual authority of 3D imaging can suggest greater certainty than the reconstruction method supports. This is a school fidelity question — narrative visualization must be honest about its evidence basis for each form.

## Integrity /20 → 17

The sourcing is unusual for a news visualization: the NYT team processed thousands of hours of video footage, cross-referenced geolocation data, verified individual identifications through multiple source types, and had external review. This is accountability journalism with visualization as the primary medium. The integrity is high.

One disclosure gap: the movement paths on the video stills are analytical conclusions, not direct observations. A path drawn on a video still represents the NYT team's interpretation of where an individual moved — it is a reconstruction, not a recording. The distinction should be noted: "paths represent reconstructed movement from multiple footage sources; individual annotations independently verified."

## Legibility /15 → 13

For the intended audience (NYT digital readers, educated general public, investigators) in the intended context (web scroll, contemplative study at home), the legibility is good. The form transitions are managed with text headers that prepare the reader for each new visual grammar. The 3D reconstructions include orientation aids (north arrow, scale bar).

The main legibility cost is the form-switching itself. Each new form requires the reader to re-orient. A reader who has learned to read the floor plan's encoding must reset when the 3D reconstruction appears. The text bridges manage this transition, but there is irreducible cognitive cost in changing visual grammars five times. I score 13 rather than 14 for this friction.

## Execution /15 → 13

The execution is high quality throughout. The satellite imagery is correctly annotated (crowd density estimated, not asserted). The floor plans use consistent color and symbol conventions. The video stills are carefully selected. The 3D reconstructions are spatially accurate. The timeline chart is clean.

Economy: the form-switching IS the economy — each form is used exactly as long as needed to answer its question, and no longer. The scroll narrative's paragraph lengths are appropriate for the visualization density. No decorative elements.

## Resonance /15 → 14

Advocacy resonance is strong — this is accountability journalism, and accountability journalism's resonance criterion is whether the audience understands what happened clearly enough to hold the responsible parties accountable. The work achieves this. A reader who completes the scroll knows, in specific spatial and temporal detail, how the Capitol was breached, which corridors were used, which individuals were present, and in what sequence the events unfolded.

Domain resonance (investigators, journalists, historians) is high — the work is being used in legal proceedings and historical documentation. Experiential resonance is significant: the 3D reconstruction creates a sense of spatial presence that flat images cannot.

## Purpose /15 → 14

This is a demonstrative and accountability work on a historically significant event: an attempt to prevent the peaceful transfer of presidential power. The Purpose dimension asks whether the resonance is proportionate to those stakes. The answer is yes — the five-form reconstruction produces the most complete publicly available account of the event, and that completeness is the appropriate response to the historical significance.

## My Total: 89/100

Multi-form narrative coherence works. The transition logic (each form answers a question the previous form raised) generalizes from work 0027 (green space inequality) to this work. Cluster R confirmed.

## Innovation Flagged

**#241 — Motivated form transition as narrative coherence criterion**: the test for multi-form narrative coherence is whether each form transition is motivated by an unanswered question from the previous form. In work 0027, the transition from heat maps to timeline was motivated by the causal question the heat maps raised. In this work, each of the five form transitions is motivated by a spatial, individual, or temporal question that the preceding form raised. The generalization is confirmed: multi-form narrative coherence is a learnable, testable criterion. The rubric should name it: in narrative visualization using multiple visual grammars, each transition must be motivated by a question the previous form raised but could not resolve.
