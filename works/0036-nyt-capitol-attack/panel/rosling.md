# Rosling — 0036 NYT Capitol Attack

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

Narrative visualization takes a complex event and makes it comprehensible through a sequence of visual arguments. The five-form structure here is ambitious — most narrative visualizations I have encountered use two or three forms. Five is at the edge of what a scroll reader's attention can sustain. The NYT team manages it because the transitions are well-motivated: each form answers the question the previous form raised. This is the right structural principle.

The temporal dimension is where I have a consistent critique: the timeline chart (Form 5) arrives last, after the spatial forms have established the geography. This is correct — you need to understand where before you can understand when. But the timeline itself is underweight. It covers five hours (noon to 5 PM) with approximately eight annotated events. The granularity should be finer for the most important hours (1–4 PM), where the key breaches occurred within minutes of each other. The temporal sequence of the events matters for accountability — which breach enabled which subsequent movement — and the timeline as described does not resolve this at the necessary precision.

## Integrity /20 → 17

The work's sourcing is among the strongest in journalism. Thousands of hours of footage processed, cross-referenced, independently verified. The team's methodology is documented separately. The individual identifications are supported by multiple independent source types.

My integrity question is about the 3D reconstructions specifically: what is the accuracy tolerance of the photogrammetric reconstruction? Photogrammetry from open-source footage has known precision limits — typically ±5–15 cm in familiar commercial tools, worse for video footage vs. dedicated scan images. If the 3D reconstruction is used to argue specific positional claims (Person X was standing in front of Door Y at Time Z), the reconstruction's accuracy tolerance matters. This should be declared.

## Legibility /15 → 13

For the NYT's intended audience (informed general public, web readers), the five-form scroll is legible. The text bridges between forms prepare the reader for each new visual grammar. The forms themselves are each individually legible.

The form-switching friction is real: five different visual grammars in one scroll requires five separate cognitive re-orientations. Each re-orientation takes cognitive bandwidth. By the time the reader reaches the timeline (Form 5), they have already processed satellite, floor plan, video still, and 3D grammar. A percentage of readers — perhaps 30–40%, based on engagement data typical for long-scroll journalism — will not reach Form 5. The timeline is the most important form for establishing temporal accountability, and it arrives last for the readers who most need it.

One structural suggestion: a brief overview graphic at the top (a thumbnail showing all five forms in sequence with a one-sentence caption each) would prime the reader for the structural journey ahead, reducing cognitive re-orientation cost at each transition.

## Execution /15 → 13

Each form is executed at a high standard. The satellite imagery is correctly annotated. The floor plan is clean and spatially accurate. The video stills are carefully selected for maximum evidentiary clarity. The 3D reconstruction is spatially oriented with aids. The timeline is clean.

The one economy note: the form sequence itself should be treated as an economy decision. Five forms are justified — but could the same argument be made with four? I suspect the video stills and the 3D reconstruction could be merged into a single form (annotated 3D rendering) that achieves both individual presence and spatial experience. The current separation into two forms adds a transition cost. A future version should test the merger.

## Resonance /15 → 14

The temporal revelation is the strongest resonance moment: the timeline (Form 5) shows that the critical corridor breaches occurred within a 20-minute window, in a sequence that was not random but cascading — each breach enabled the next. This is the argument that turns spatial understanding into historical understanding. It arrives at the right moment, after the reader has the geographic context to interpret it.

The 3D reconstruction creates spatial resonance that is unusual in journalism — the reader understands not just where events occurred but what it looked like, how close the defenders were, how the physical geometry of the corridors shaped the event. This is domain resonance for investigators and historians.

## Purpose /15 → 14

Accountability journalism on a historically unprecedented event. The Purpose criterion is fully met: the form is proportionate to the stakes, the evidence is documented at the highest available standard, and the resonance serves the public accountability function. Purpose is not merely served — it is the reason this work exists.

## My Total: 88/100

Strong multi-form narrative with temporal structure as the improvement target. The Cluster R confirmation is solid — motivated form transitions are the coherence criterion that generalizes.

## Innovation Flagged

**#242 — Form-switching fatigue in long-scroll multi-form narrative**: a five-form scroll narrative requires five cognitive re-orientations. Engagement data from long-scroll journalism suggests reader attrition accelerates after the third form-switch. The rubric has no criterion for cognitive re-orientation cost in multi-form narrative. A modifier should be added: for works using more than three distinct visual grammars in sequence, the legibility assessment should note the cumulative re-orientation cost and assess whether the text bridges between forms are sufficient to manage it.
