# Audience — 0037 ISS Assembly Map

## School Fidelity /20 → 14

I know the ISS exists. I've seen pictures of it. I didn't know it was assembled from 42 separate missions over 26 years, and that five countries contributed different modules. This diagram teaches me those things immediately — the country colors tell me the political structure before I read a label, and the left-to-right sequence tells me the assembly happened over time.

The node-connection grammar is familiar to me from other contexts (org charts, network diagrams, subway maps). I apply that existing familiarity to this diagram: nodes are things, connections are relationships. I understand the basic reading before I read the legend. That's good design.

The one thing I don't understand immediately: what do the connections represent? Are the modules physically connected in this order? Or is this showing something about sequence? The distinction (docking sequence vs. physical adjacency) is not obvious from the diagram alone. I need a figure note for this.

## Integrity /20 → 16

I trust the historical data — this feels like a carefully researched diagram. The mass encoding (bigger node = heavier module) seems plausible based on what I know about the ISS's structure. The country attributions are consistent with what I've read elsewhere.

My one integrity question as a general reader: Zarya was Russian-built but US-funded. The diagram shows it as Russian. Is that accurate? I don't know the convention, and the diagram doesn't explain it. A figure note would help.

## Legibility /15 → 11

For a general reader like me, legibility is moderate. The main legibility success: country colors are immediately readable; chronological reading direction is intuitive; the overall "this is an assembly sequence" story is clear.

The main legibility challenge: 42 missions, 16+ modules, and five encoded variables create a dense diagram. At a typical screen or print size, the module names on smaller nodes will be very small. I find myself zooming in to read the smaller module names.

The meta-level (the self-referential grammar) is completely invisible to me. I read this as a node-connection diagram because it is a node-connection diagram. I don't know that ISS mission planners use the same grammar. The meta-level doesn't confuse me — it just doesn't exist for me as a reader.

## Execution /15 → 12

The execution looks professional and clean. The color choices are distinct. The node sizes vary clearly. The connection arrows are legible. My one practical note: at the density of 42 missions, connection lines will cross each other. The design needs to solve the line-crossing problem or I'll have trouble tracing specific chains of dependency.

## Resonance /15 → 12

Learning that the ISS took 42 missions and 26 years to build created genuine wonder for me as a reader. I didn't know this. The diagram makes that complexity visible in a way that a list of missions would not. The country-color pattern is particularly resonant: I can see that the early ISS was mostly Russian, that the middle period saw American expansion, and that the final modules return to a Russian-heavy phase. That's a political history I didn't know the ISS had.

## Purpose /15 → 13

The diagram teaches me things about the ISS that I couldn't have learned as easily any other way. That's what a good informational diagram is for. Purpose served.

## My Total: 78/100

A good informational diagram with real legibility challenges at high density. The meta-level is completely invisible to me, which means it neither helps nor hurts my reading. Cluster S question answered from the general reader perspective: self-referential grammar creates no legibility cost for the general reader.

## Innovation Flagged

**#252 — Meta-level invisibility as the criterion for self-referential grammar success**: from the general reader's perspective, the self-referential grammar in this diagram is completely invisible. I read the diagram as a node-connection diagram because it is one; I have no way of knowing it uses the same grammar as ISS mission planning tools. The invisibility is the criterion for success: a self-referential grammar that does not create legibility friction for the general reader achieves the meta-level for domain experts without penalizing general readers. The rubric should score self-referential grammar as: Resonance bonus for domain viewers (confirmed by Bertin and Tufte in this work); Legibility neutral for general viewers (confirmed by the Audience lens here). The self-referential layer should be invisible to those who do not recognize it and rewarding to those who do.
