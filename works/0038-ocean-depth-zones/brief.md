# Brief — 0038 Ocean Depth Zones

## The Argument

The ocean organizes life into vertical communities as reliably as Humboldt showed it does on mountain slopes. From the photic zone (0-200m, where photosynthesis occurs) through the mesopelagic twilight zone (200-1000m), the bathypelagic midnight zone (1000-4000m), the abyssal zone (4000-6000m), to the hadal zone (6000-11,000m), each depth band has its own characteristic light, temperature, pressure, oxygen, and species assemblage. The ecological insight is Humboldtian: depth creates community boundaries as reliably as altitude does.

The design applies the Humboldt ecological grammar — ecological zone as primary organizational axis — to a vertical ocean cross-section. The axis runs downward (0m at top, 11,000m at bottom), following gravity rather than fighting it.

## Design Approach

Vertical axis ecological profile. Primary axis: depth (0m to 11,000m), running top to bottom. Five environmental variables encoded alongside the depth axis:
1. **Light intensity**: horizontal bar graph, left side. Logarithmic scale (surface: 100%; 200m: 1%; 1000m: 0.001%). Color: warm white to black.
2. **Temperature**: horizontal line graph, right side. Range: 30C at surface to 1-2C at depth. Color: red to blue gradient.
3. **Pressure**: numeric annotation at zone boundaries. Range: 1 atm to 1,100 atm.
4. **Oxygen concentration**: horizontal bar graph, far right. Shows the oxygen minimum zone (OMZ) at 200-1000m depth — a counterintuitive dip in oxygen below the photic zone.
5. **Species density**: illustrated species silhouettes distributed within zones. Density of silhouettes encodes relative species diversity.

The depth axis runs downward — 0m at top, 11,000m at bottom. This is the axis inversion from innovation #221 (work 0031: soil health degradation), applied in a new ecological context. The question: does the downward depth axis feel as natural in an ocean context as in a soil/below-ground context?
