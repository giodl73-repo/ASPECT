# Artist — 0038 Ocean Depth Zones

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

The ecological visualization school's grammar is present and correctly applied. The depth axis running downward feels as natural as a body of water — the eye follows the descent. This is what the axis inversion delivers that a conventional bottom-up axis cannot: the visualization is oriented the way the subject is experienced. You don't swim upward to go deeper; you go down. The diagram goes down with you.

The species silhouettes in the central column are the design's most distinctive element from an artist's perspective. They are not illustrations in the decorative sense — they are data marks encoding species presence and relative diversity. But they are drawn in a style that goes beyond pure function: silhouettes of specific organisms (lanternfish in the mesopelagic, viperfish at the mesopelagic-bathypelagic boundary, anglerfish in the midnight zone, sea cucumbers and amphipods in the hadal zone) are individually recognizable to anyone who has spent time looking at deep-sea life. This specificity is the ecology-as-composition principle from Cluster W: the formal aesthetic quality of the diagram emerges from the correct and truthful distribution of actual organisms in actual zones.

The one element that could be stronger: the color system for the background of the central column. Currently the zone background appears to be a simple gradient from light blue (photic) to black (hadal). The actual color of the ocean at depth is more specific: the deep sea is not simply dark blue — it is bioluminescent, with specific color ranges depending on the organisms present. A more specific color system (pale blue-green photic zone → twilight blue mesopelagic → near-black with bioluminescent blue-green flickers in the bathypelagic → absolute black in the abyssal → geological gray-black in the hadal) would create stronger experiential resonance while still being ecologically accurate.

## Integrity /20 → 17

The species placements are accurate — the organisms shown in each zone are correctly assigned. Lanternfish (Myctophidae) are the dominant mesopelagic visual-signal species; viperfish (Chauliodus) inhabit the mesopelagic-bathypelagic transition; anglerfish (Melanocetus) are representative bathypelagic predators; sea cucumbers (Holothuria) and amphipods (Hirondellea gigas) are characteristic hadal fauna. The accuracy of species placement is important for domain resonance — ecologists will notice wrong species in wrong zones immediately.

The OMZ species response should be visible in the species density encoding: the oxygen minimum zone at 200-1000m has lower species density than either the photic zone above or the bathypelagic below, because many species cannot tolerate the low oxygen conditions. This species density dip should correspond to the oxygen bar dip.

## Legibility /15 → 13

For a general reader, the species silhouettes are the most engaging element — they provide an entry point before the numerical variables are engaged. A reader who sees a lanternfish and an anglerfish at different depths has already understood something true about how the ocean organizes life vertically. The illustrations do legibility work that bar graphs and line graphs cannot do.

The OMZ region (200-1000m) deserves special legibility attention — the dip in oxygen, the dip in species density, and the presence of the thermocline all coincide in this zone. This is the diagram's most information-dense region. The annotation burden in this zone may overwhelm the visual. Consider a small callout panel that expands this zone.

## Resonance /15 → 14

Experiential resonance fires strongly. The diagram creates a genuine sense of the ocean's vertical ecology — a place most viewers will never experience physically, made accessible through the combination of scale, species, and environmental data. The descent from the sunlit photic zone through the twilight mesopelagic to the absolute dark of the bathypelagic and abyssal zones is an experiential journey that the diagram structure enables.

The species silhouettes are the resonance mechanism: a reader who encounters the anglerfish in the midnight zone, floating in near-absolute darkness with its bioluminescent lure, has received an experience that the environmental variable graphs alone could not provide.
