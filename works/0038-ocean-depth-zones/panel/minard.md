# Minard — 0038 Ocean Depth Zones

## School Fidelity /20 → 18

The Humboldt grammar — ecological zone as primary axis, multiple environmental variables arrayed alongside it — is correctly applied. Humboldt's Tableau Physique (1807) used altitude as the primary axis with botanical, zoological, and atmospheric variables arrayed alongside it in columns. This design uses depth as the primary axis with five environmental variables in the same column arrangement. The grammar is directly descended from Humboldt.

The axis inversion is the critical school fidelity decision. Humboldt's axis ran upward (sea level at bottom, Chimborazo at top). This design's axis runs downward (surface at top, hadal zone at bottom). The inversion is not a deviation from the school's grammar — it is the grammar applied correctly to a different gravitational hierarchy. In Humboldt's mountain ecology, surface is the low point and altitude is the organizing gradient (up = toward conditions of reduced atmospheric pressure and temperature). In ocean ecology, surface is the high point and depth is the organizing gradient (down = toward conditions of increased pressure and reduced light). The axis direction follows the physical reality of the gradient. This is exactly what innovation #221 (axis inversion as ecological grammar requirement) identified in work 0031. The ocean context confirms it with even greater force.

The five-variable encoding is appropriate. Light intensity, temperature, pressure, oxygen, and species density are the primary ecological variables that organize ocean life vertically. I find no significant variables omitted and no irrelevant variables included.

## Integrity /20 → 18

All five variable encodings are empirically grounded:

**Light intensity**: the 1% depth (roughly 200m, the photic zone boundary) and 0.001% depth (roughly 1000m) are well-established oceanographic measurements. The logarithmic scale is the honest scale for light attenuation — linear scale would severely compress the meaningful variation.

**Temperature**: the thermocline (sharp temperature gradient, roughly 200-1000m) and the near-constant 1-2C below 2000m are accurate. The surface temperature (30C) is appropriate for tropical-temperate ocean; a geographic qualifier (tropical/temperate vs. polar) should appear in the figure note.

**Pressure**: 1 atm at surface, approximately 1 atm per 10m, so 1100 atm at 11,000m (Mariana Trench). Correct.

**Oxygen minimum zone**: the OMZ at 200-1000m is a genuine and counterintuitive ecological feature, accurately placed. The dip in oxygen below the photic zone (caused by bacterial decomposition of sinking organic matter exceeding oxygen supply) is the most important data feature in this diagram and it is correctly shown.

**Species density**: relative density shown through silhouette density is appropriate for a qualitative-to-semi-quantitative variable. The high density in the photic zone, the sharp decrease at the photic/mesopelagic boundary, and the sparse but real populations in the abyssal and hadal zones are all ecologically accurate.

## Legibility /15 → 13

The five-variable panel array flanking the central depth axis is a legible arrangement for a reader willing to spend time with the diagram. Each flanking panel has a single variable, labeled; the central column provides the depth anchor. A reader can follow any variable from surface to depth by reading across a horizontal row at any point.

The one legibility concern: the panel array (five columns flanking a central axis) may create visual fragmentation — the reader must look across from the left panel to the right panel to compare two variables at the same depth. A reader who wants to compare temperature and oxygen at 500m depth must look across the full width of the diagram. This is an inherent cost of the panel array format; it is the same cost in Humboldt's original Tableau Physique.

## Execution /15 → 14

Five variables, all intrinsically necessary for the ecological argument, plus species silhouettes that are intrinsically necessary for the species density encoding. No decorative marks. The Cluster H (intrinsic complexity) amendment applies correctly — the 11,000m depth range with five variables and illustrated species is intrinsic to the subject, not excess.

The logarithmic scale for light intensity is the correct craft decision — it is the honest scale for light attenuation in the ocean. The scale must be clearly labeled (log scale) to prevent the reader from interpreting the visual compression linearly.

## Resonance /15 → 14

Experiential resonance: the diagram creates a genuine sense of the ocean's vertical scale. The transition from 200m (photic zone boundary, the limit of recreational diving) through 1000m (the deepest submarines operate at the outer limits of this zone) to 11,000m (the Mariana Trench, reached by three humans in history) creates a scale awareness that numbers alone cannot produce.

Domain resonance for oceanographers and ecologists is very high — they will recognize the OMZ immediately and appreciate its accurate placement. The Humboldt grammar signals the ecological tradition, and ecologists will recognize it.

## Purpose /15 → 14

Informational purpose: making the ocean's vertical ecological structure legible. The primary insight — that depth creates community boundaries as reliably as altitude — is communicated in a form that directly references the Humboldt tradition. Purpose is well served.

The strongest ecological visualization in the project after Humboldt's own Tableau Physique. The axis inversion is correct and natural. Cluster U confirmed.

## Innovation Flagged

**#253 — Gravitational hierarchy as universal ecological axis principle**: the downward depth axis in this design is the same ecological grammar requirement as the downward soil axis in work 0031 (#221, #224). Both follow the principle: the primary axis of an ecological visualization should follow the gravitational hierarchy that organizes the ecological community. For mountain ecology (Humboldt): axis runs upward, following altitude above sea level. For soil ecology (work 0031): axis runs downward, following depth below surface. For ocean ecology (this work): axis runs downward, following depth below surface. The same gravitational logic governs all three cases. The ecological grammar requirement generalizes across soil, ocean, and (implicitly) atmospheric profiles. The rubric should name this as the gravitational hierarchy principle: ecological visualizations with a depth or altitude axis should orient the axis to follow the gravitational gradient, not to fit conventional chart orientation.
