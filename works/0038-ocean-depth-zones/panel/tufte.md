# Tufte — 0038 Ocean Depth Zones

## School Fidelity /20 → 17

The Humboldt grammar is correctly applied. Altitude-as-primary-axis becomes depth-as-primary-axis; the flanking variable panels are the same structural device as Humboldt's columns in the Tableau Physique. The school fidelity is direct and unambiguous.

The axis inversion — depth increasing downward — is the correct ecological grammar decision. I argued for it in the abstract (any ecological visualization with a gravitational gradient should orient the axis to follow gravity) and work 0031 (#221, #224) confirmed it for soil ecology. This work confirms it for ocean ecology. The same principle applies: the reader's intuitive sense of depth ("going deeper" = going down) should be honored by the axis direction. Fighting this intuition by running the depth axis upward would be a legibility failure in the service of conventional chart orientation.

The logarithmic scale for light intensity is the right decision — not a distortion, but the honest scale for a variable that attenuates exponentially. The scale must be declared.

## Integrity /20 → 17

The ecological data is accurate. The OMZ placement (200-1000m) is well-documented oceanographic fact. The thermocline depth range (200-1000m, varying by location and season) is correctly represented. Pressure values are calculable from depth (1 atm per 10m) and exact.

The geographic scope declaration is the primary integrity gap: the temperature profile is tropical-temperate. Polar ocean profiles differ significantly (weaker thermocline, near-0C surface temperatures). This should be declared.

The zone boundary fuzziness (shown as gradients) is the right integrity decision. Crisp zone boundaries would imply precision that oceanographic conditions don't support.

## Legibility /15 → 13

For a scientific audience — oceanographers, marine biologists, ecologists — this diagram is immediately legible. The five-panel format is standardized in the oceanographic literature. The zone names and boundary depths are familiar.

For a general reader, the OMZ dip is the key legibility challenge. It must be annotated with an explanation — the counterintuitive dip in oxygen between the photosynthetically active surface and the cold, deep water is the most important ecological feature in the diagram and the most likely to be misread as a data error.

The 11,000m depth range creates a display challenge: the hadal zone (6000-11,000m) occupies only 45% of the depth range but contains extremely sparse but real fauna. The zone proportions should be shown as proportional to actual depth, not stretched for visual emphasis.

## Execution /15 → 14

Five variables, all intrinsically necessary for the ecological argument (Cluster H applies). Species silhouettes are intrinsically necessary — they encode species density and diversity, not decorative illustration. No marks are decorative. Economy is high.

The one economy question: should pressure be encoded as a flanking bar graph rather than numeric annotation? A pressure bar graph would give the variable visual weight proportional to its ecological importance. Pressure (1100 atm at 11,000m) is not merely an annotation — it is the physical force that defines every organism's adaptation at every depth. I would give it a flanking bar encoding.

The logarithmic scale for light must be labeled clearly — without the log scale label, a reader will misinterpret the visual compression.

## Resonance /15 → 13

The depth scale creates experiential resonance when anchored to human reference points. The diagram should place: recreational diving limit (40m), deepest human breath-hold dive (214m), edge of photic zone (200m, about where human visual range ends in clear water), hadal snailfish discovery depth (8,336m). These reference points transform depth numbers into experiential understanding.

Domain resonance for oceanographers is high. The Humboldt grammar immediately signals the ecological visualization tradition; the OMZ placement signals accurate data.

## Purpose /15 → 14

Informational purpose fully served. The ocean's vertical ecological structure is legible in a form directly descended from Humboldt's mountain ecology grammar. The gravitational axis orientation makes the diagram physically intuitive.

## My Total: 88/100

Strong ecological visualization. The axis inversion is natural and confirmed for ocean ecology. Cluster U confirmed.

## Innovation Flagged

**#255 — Depth reference anchors as legibility requirement in deep-ocean visualization**: the 11,000m depth range spans a scale that most readers cannot intuitively grasp. Reference points anchored to known human experiences (recreational diving limit at 40m, commercial diving at 300m, submarine operational range at 1000m, Mariana Trench at 11,000m) transform numerical depth into experiential understanding. These reference anchors are not decorative — they are cognitive-accessibility marks, serving the same function as Humboldt's annotations of familiar altitudes (snowline, tree line) in his mountain profiles. For any ecological visualization using a depth or altitude axis at scales beyond ordinary human experience, cognitive-accessibility depth anchors should be considered required elements, not optional annotations.
