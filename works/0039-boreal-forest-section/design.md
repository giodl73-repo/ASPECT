# Design Specification — 0039 Boreal Forest Vertical Cross-Section

## Canvas

Portrait orientation, 70cm × 100cm. The primary cross-section occupies the central 50cm. Left margin (8cm): two environmental variable columns. Right margin (8cm): two environmental variable columns. Depth axis runs vertically on the far left edge of the central image, labeled in meters: +30m (canopy peak) to −2m (root zone bottom). Total ecological column height: 32m.

## Gravitational Axis

The primary axis reads from top (canopy peak, +30m) to bottom (root zone, −2m). This follows the gravitational hierarchy principle (Cluster U): the visual reading direction mirrors the gravitational reality of the ecosystem. Canopy is above — as it is in nature. Roots are below — as they are in nature. The viewer's proprioceptive understanding of "up" and "down" aligns with the ecological data.

## Four Ecological Zones and Visual Character

### Zone 1: Canopy Zone (15–30m)
**Visual character**: dense, interlocking crowns. The crown layer is visually the most complex zone — overlapping canopy tops, competing crown shapes, occasional gaps where a large tree has fallen. Light quality: bright above the canopy; dappled yellow-green filtering through the canopy layer itself.

Species and placement:
- *Picea mariana* (black spruce): narrow conical crowns, densely branched, occupying 20–30m; 4–5 individuals visible in cross-section, crowns interlocking
- *Picea glauca* (white spruce): broader crown, 18–30m; 2–3 individuals, occupying background position
- *Pinus banksiana* (jack pine): irregular, scraggly crown at 18–25m; 1–2 individuals, crown silhouette distinctive
- *Abies balsamea* (balsam fir): narrow spire form, 15–25m; 3 individuals, providing vertical rhythm
- Canopy gaps: 2 locations where fallen trunks have opened the canopy; shafts of direct light penetrating to understory level

**Compositional character**: highest visual density in the project to date. Crowns create an almost continuous visual mass with small irregular openings — the gaps are ecologically significant (regeneration niche) and visually significant (the eye seeks and finds them).

### Zone 2: Understory Zone (5–15m)
**Visual character**: sparse, irregular spacing. This zone has filtered, attenuated light — the canopy has taken most of it. The visual field opens compared to the dense crown layer. Organisms present:

- *Abies balsamea* (balsam fir) regeneration: young trees 3–8m, growing in canopy gaps; clustered near the two light shafts, sparse between gaps
- *Alnus incana* (speckled alder): multi-stemmed shrubby form, 4–8m; 2–3 clusters near moisture-rich areas
- *Sorbus americana* (mountain ash): small understory tree, 5–10m; 1–2 individuals, distinctive compound leaves visible
- Epiphytes on main trunks: lichen patches (Cladonia species), bark texture of spruce trunks visible through the understory

**Compositional character**: irregular, open spacing. This is the sparsest zone compositionally — and that sparsity is ecological data. The understory is not sparse because we are being restrained with marks; it is sparse because filtered boreal light does not support dense understory communities. The large trunks of the canopy trees dominate the visual field as vertical structural elements.

### Zone 3: Shrub and Ground Layer (0–5m)
**Visual character**: dense, low, mat-forming. A visually packed layer despite the short height range. The transition from sparse understory to dense ground layer is compositionally striking — the visual density inverts again.

Species and placement:
- *Rhododendron groenlandicum* (Labrador tea): dense waxy-leaved shrub, 0.5–1.5m; forms a nearly continuous mat across much of the ground layer
- *Vaccinium vitis-idaea* (lingonberry): low mat-forming shrub, 10–30cm; fills gaps between Labrador tea
- Sphagnum moss (*Sphagnum* spp.): dense, luminous pale-green/white mat at ground surface; highly textured rendering — the most visually distinctive ground cover
- Bracken fern (*Pteridium aquilinum*): bold frond forms rising 60–100cm from the sphagnum mat; 4–5 visible fronds adding vertical punctuation
- Cloudberry (*Rubus chamaemorus*): low, solitary flowers/fruits visible at several locations

**Compositional character**: dense, horizontal, richly textured. The sphagnum mat creates a strong visual baseline that clearly separates the above-ground and below-ground visual worlds. The bracken fern fronds are bold enough to register at reading distance and provide the only significant upward visual gesture from this zone.

### Zone 4: Root Zone (0 to −2m)
**Visual character**: knotted, dense, tangled — a visual complexity that mirrors the canopy but underground. The inversion of the canopy above creates a compositional symmetry that is ecological truth, not aesthetic device.

Root species and structures:
- Black spruce tap roots: deep, narrow primary roots penetrating 1.5–2m, approaching the permafrost boundary
- Balsam fir lateral root mats: wide, shallow, spreading horizontally 2–4m from the trunk base; interlocking with neighboring trees' root systems
- *Picea mariana* shallow root system: most boreal trees cannot penetrate permafrost; roots spread laterally above the permafrost layer, creating a dense mat at 0–60cm depth
- Mycorrhizal networks: ectomycorrhizal associations on spruce and fir roots rendered as fine white thread-like structures connecting multiple root tips and surrounding soil fungi (*Suillus* and *Russula* species indicated by fruiting body shapes at soil surface)
- Soil fauna: nematode forms visible at high-detail zones (magnified inset optional); Collembola (springtails) rendered as small dotted forms in upper soil; note that earthworms are rare in boreal soils due to acidity
- Permafrost boundary: shown as a horizontal blue-grey shading beginning at −1.5m — a physical limit that determines the entire root architecture above it

**Compositional character**: dense, tangled, directionally complex. Unlike the canopy's upward-reaching forms, the root zone's forms spread laterally and downward simultaneously. The permafrost line provides a strong compositional terminus — a hard visual boundary at the base of the image, as final as the sky above the canopy.

## Environmental Variable Columns

Four flanking columns, each 4cm wide × 85cm tall, calibrated against the same vertical axis as the main cross-section:

### Left margin (2 columns):

**Column L1 — Light Intensity (lux)**
- Scale: logarithmic, 0 to 50,000 lux (log scale necessary to show the 3-order-of-magnitude gradient)
- Above canopy: ~50,000 lux (full boreal summer sun)
- Within canopy layer: 5,000–15,000 lux (dappled through crown)
- Below canopy, understory: 200–800 lux (heavily filtered)
- Near ground: 20–100 lux (deep shade)
- Root zone: 0 lux
- Rendered as: horizontal bars extending rightward from vertical axis; warm yellow color fading to black

**Column L2 — Temperature Range (°C)**
- Scale: annual diurnal range; not mean temperature but the width of the range
- Above canopy: ±25°C daily in summer; annual range −45° to +32°C
- Canopy interior: range narrowed by 30–40% through buffering
- Understory: narrowed further; 15–20°C daily range in summer
- Ground level: 5–10°C daily range; strong buffering by sphagnum insulation
- Root zone: 0–2°C annual range; near-constant near-freezing adjacent to permafrost
- Rendered as: paired line graph showing max and min envelope; the narrowing width encodes the buffering effect

### Right margin (2 columns):

**Column R1 — Moisture Content (%)**
- Scale: 0–100%, volumetric
- Above canopy: 40–80% relative humidity (variable)
- Understory: 70–90% (less wind desiccation)
- Ground/sphagnum layer: 85–95% (sphagnum holds 15–20× its dry weight in water)
- Root zone upper: 80–95%
- Root zone at permafrost: 90–100% (saturated, permafrost impermeable)
- Rendered as: filled horizontal bars; blue color intensity encoding saturation level

**Column R2 — Soil Organic Matter (%)**
- Scale: 0–80% by weight
- Not applicable above ground (N/A marked in canopy and understory positions)
- Litter layer (just above 0m): accumulating needle fall, 10–20% OM
- Organic soil horizon (0–30cm): 50–80% OM (deep peat accumulation)
- Mineral soil horizon (30–150cm): 5–20% OM declining with depth
- Permafrost zone: 2–5% OM (frozen peat preserved)
- Rendered as: filled horizontal bars; brown color intensity encoding organic matter concentration

## Visual Density Strategy — Ecology-as-Composition

The key compositional rhythm is:

| Zone | Visual density | Ecological fact |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| Canopy | Very high — interlocking crown masses | High species density, intense competition for light |
| Understory | Low — scattered individuals, dominant trunks | Light limitation prevents dense understory in boreal |
| Ground layer | High — packed mat communities | Light reaches ground; mat communities saturate the available zone |
| Root zone | High, knotted, tangled | Roots compete aggressively for nutrients and water above permafrost; mycorrhizal networks dense |

This creates a visual rhythm: DENSE → SPARSE → DENSE → DENSE. This rhythm is not an aesthetic choice — it is the accurate rendering of how a boreal forest is actually organized. The visual surprise (the return of high density in the ground and root zones after the sparse understory) is ecologically correct and compositionally striking.

The subtitle declares this: *"Visual density reflects ecological reality — dense where communities are rich, sparse where they are not."*

## Haeckel Grammar Application

**Haeckel's grammar as applied to ecological scene (following work 0033 precedent):**

1. Morphological accuracy: each species rendered at accurate proportions and growth form
2. Ecological placement: each organism placed at the depth/height where it actually occurs
3. Ecological truth as composition: the density of organisms encodes ecological richness directly
4. Intrinsic complexity: the boreal column is genuinely complex — the illustration reflects real complexity, not decorative excess

**Extension beyond single-taxon Haeckel:** This work, like 0033 (coral reef), extends Haeckel's grammar from single-taxon plate to multi-taxon ecological scene. The grammar extension is justified: a cross-section of an ecological system reveals structural information that no single-species illustration can convey.

**Humboldt grammar import:** The flanking environmental variable columns are drawn from the Humboldt grammar of ecological-visualization. The scientific-illustration school is the primary attribution; the Humboldt flanking-column structure is imported from the adjacent ecological-visualization school. This is a declared cross-school element, not an accidental mix.
