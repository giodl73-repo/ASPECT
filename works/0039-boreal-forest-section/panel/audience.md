# Audience Review — 0039 Boreal Forest Vertical Cross-Section

## School Fidelity (20)

I'm not the historian or the semiotician, so I'll say what I see: this looks like a natural history plate — the kind you'd find in a field guide or mounted on a natural history museum wall. That's the right impression. The organisms are recognizable (spruce trees look like spruce trees; moss looks like moss; roots look like roots). The depth axis is labeled. The zones are named. If this is claiming to be scientific illustration in the Haeckel tradition, it succeeds at that claim from a general viewer's perspective.

Score: **16/20** (I trust the specialists on the finer grammar points; it reads as the claimed school)

## Integrity (20)

I can't verify the species distributions against the published sources cited, but I appreciate that the sources are named. As a general viewer, a specific citation ("Burns & Honkala 1990") is more reassuring than a vague "ecology literature" reference. The subtitle tells me that the density reflects ecology, not design preference. I believe it because the statement is there.

The permafrost line is clear and labeled. I understand it as a physical boundary. The fact that it determines the architecture of everything above it is communicated by the visual structure — the roots spreading sideways above it make the constraint obvious.

Score: **16/20**

## Legibility (15)

This is where I have the strongest opinion. The density rhythm is the most legible thing in the work. I understood — before reading any label — that there are four distinct zones, that the top is dense, the middle is open, the near-ground is packed again, and the underground is tangled. That understanding came from looking, not from reading. That's exceptional.

The environmental variable columns are too small for me. Four 4cm-wide columns flanking a central image will be legible at reading distance (the target context) but they feel like extra credit, not core content. A first-time viewer will likely ignore them until drawn in by the main image. That's probably fine — the design does not require the columns to be read first.

The species labels in 6pt type are at the very edge of readable for most general viewers. If this is going on a museum wall, those labels will need to be at least 8pt.

Score: **13/15** (outstanding primary legibility through compositional rhythm; small-print species labels are a minor friction)

## Execution (15)

The sphagnum mat is my favorite element. It has the right visual texture — pale, dense, different from everything above and below it. I've walked across sphagnum bogs in Canada and the texture in this rendering is accurate. That sounds small but it's not: when an organism that most viewers have not consciously identified before is rendered accurately, they often recognize it anyway from accumulated environmental exposure. "That's what that is" — a moment of named recognition. That's good craft.

The root zone is complex but not confused. I can distinguish trunk bases, lateral roots, and the thread-like mycorrhizal structures. The permafrost line grounds it all.

Score: **13/15**

## Resonance (15)

I had an experience looking at this that I want to describe. The density rhythm creates what I'd call a discovery structure: you find density at the top (expected — that's where the trees are), then you find sparseness in the middle (also expected — the understory should be open), then you're surprised by the density at the ground level (unexpected — I would have expected thinning all the way down), and then the root zone is even more complex (genuinely surprising — I had not thought about what a root system looks like when constrained by permafrost).

Each zone is a small discovery. The composition structures those discoveries in sequence. That's resonance — not just "this is informative" but "this reveals something I did not expect."

The ecology-as-composition idea is doing this work. The density gradient is communicating ecological structure before the labels are read. For me as a general viewer, that's the most impressive achievement.

Score: **14/15**

## Purpose (15)

The work achieves its informational purpose for me. I understand the boreal forest's vertical structure better after looking at this than before. The form is the right form — there is no other way to show all four zones simultaneously.

I want to flag one missed opportunity: the permafrost boundary could be connected to climate change in a brief subtitle or note. I now know that the permafrost is there, and that it determines the architecture above it, but I don't know from this image alone that permafrost is melting, and that melting permafrost releases stored carbon, and that boreal forests contain a significant fraction of Earth's terrestrial carbon. A single sentence connecting the ecology to the stakes would make this work more purposeful for a general audience.

Score: **13/15**

## Innovations Flagged

**Innovation #263 — Discovery sequence as legibility mechanism in cross-section illustration (W adjacent):** When the density rhythm of a cross-section creates a sequence of confirmations (expected) and surprises (unexpected), the compositional structure functions as a discovery engine for general viewers. The surprise at the ground-layer density return and root-zone complexity in the boreal cross-section created genuine viewer engagement — the composition pulled me forward rather than requiring me to work at it. This is a legibility mechanism that operates below the level of conscious reading: the viewer follows density changes the way a reader follows narrative tension. The W principle (ecology-as-composition) is not just a Resonance phenomenon; it is a Legibility mechanism for general audiences.
