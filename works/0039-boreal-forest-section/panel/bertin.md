# Bertin Review — 0039 Boreal Forest Vertical Cross-Section

## School Fidelity (20)

Type A, scientific-illustration school. The Haeckel grammar is correctly applied: morphological accuracy, ecological placement, intrinsic complexity as the subject. The extension of Haeckel's grammar from single-taxon plates to multi-taxon ecological scene has now been confirmed across works 0033 and 0039 — we have enough precedent to call this a named extension of the school, not a violation of it.

The Humboldt flanking-column import is declared in the design as a cross-school element. This is handled correctly. It does not constitute a synthesis (Type C) because the primary grammar remains Haeckel scientific illustration; the Humboldt columns are subordinate to the illustrated cross-section.

One concern: the logarithmic scale on the light intensity column must be declared explicitly on the column itself, not just in the figure note. A log scale that is not labeled on the axis is a grammar error — the visual impression of the gradient will be interpreted as linear by the viewer. This is a minor Legibility issue, not a School Fidelity violation, since it concerns execution of the data column rather than the illustration grammar.

Score: **17/20** (strong Type A application; declared cross-school element appropriately handled)

## Integrity (20)

Reportive mode. Three questions:

**Question 1 — Disclosure**: Source declaration is present and specific (Burns & Honkala 1990; Bonan & Shugart 1989; Vitt et al. 2010). This directly addresses the gap identified in work 0033. The environmental variable data (lux values, temperature ranges, moisture percentages, soil organic matter percentages) are drawn from published ecological literature. The figure note declares the sources. No significant undisclosed uncertainty.

One gap: the permafrost boundary at −1.5m is drawn as a clean horizontal line, but permafrost depth is variable across the boreal biome. The figure note should acknowledge that this represents a representative Canadian boreal profile, not a universal boreal condition.

**Question 2 — Argument integrity**: The density-gradient argument (visual density encodes ecological richness) is declared in the subtitle. The causal mechanism (light drives the canopy-to-understory gradient; permafrost constrains root architecture) is implicit in the design. A mechanism note would strengthen this — not required, but would elevate Integrity from 17 to 18.

**Question 3 — Framing integrity**: The analytical framework (vertical cross-section as ecological argument) is standard within the school. No novel framework requiring declaration.

Score: **17/20** (strong; minor permafrost boundary caveat)

## Legibility (15)

Context specification:
- **Primary audience**: ecologists, biology educators, natural history museum visitors with secondary-school biology background
- **Reading context**: contemplative study — natural history plate read in book or museum context
- **Narration mode**: standalone

The vertical depth axis with explicit zone labels and depth markers at each level makes orientation immediate. A viewer at any biology-literate level can identify the four zones within 5 seconds of first encounter.

The environmental variable columns require closer reading. The logarithmic light scale is the one potential confusion point for general readers — the steep early drop (50,000 to 800 lux) that appears gradual on the log scale may be misread as a gradual ecological transition. Label the log scale explicitly: "Log scale (×10 per division)."

The density rhythm — DENSE → SPARSE → DENSE → DENSE — is legible without instruction. This is the work's primary legibility achievement.

Score: **13/15** (strong primary-context legibility; log-scale labeling gap)

## Execution (15)

**Economy lens**: All visual elements in the main cross-section are either data marks (species placed at their documented positions) or intrinsically necessary structural marks (zone boundary lines, depth scale, trunk forms). The sphagnum texture is intrinsically complex — the subject is a mat-forming organism whose visual texture is morphologically significant. The mycorrhizal network rendering is intrinsically complex — it encodes the spatial distribution of a key ecological relationship.

The four flanking variable columns are all ecologically justified. No decorative excess is identified.

One economy concern: the figure note reference list (four citations) could be condensed. A footnote number system would reduce visual noise at the bottom of the image. This is a minor craft issue.

**Craft lens**: Typography follows the Futura system established in prior works. Scientific nomenclature in italic, zone labels in Light weight. The color transitions in the variable columns (yellow to black for light; blue-white gradient for moisture) are controlled and consistent with the illustrated spectrum of the central image.

Score: **13/15** (high economy; good craft; minor figure-note density)

## Resonance (15)

**Type: Experiential** — the work creates a direct experience of the boreal forest's vertical structure. A viewer who has stood in a boreal forest and looked up through the dense spruce crowns will feel recognition. A viewer who has not will acquire the felt sense of it.

The density rhythm is the resonance mechanism: the eye traces the descent from dense crown through sparse understory and then finds the unexpected density of the ground and root layers. The compositional surprise — that the root zone is as visually complex as the canopy — is ecologically correct and emotionally resonant. The forest continues underground, as richly organized as it is above.

The mycorrhizal network rendering provides the one genuinely novel visual element: the fine white threads connecting root systems across multiple trees visualize a relationship that has only been understood scientifically in the last three decades. This is not decoration — it is new ecology made visible.

Score: **13/15** (strong Experiential resonance; mycorrhizal network adds Domain resonance for ecology-trained viewers)

## Purpose (15)

**Purpose type: Informational** — the work exists to make the vertical structure of a boreal forest comprehensible to an educated general audience. This is a subject with genuine ecological stakes (boreal forests contain approximately one-third of Earth's terrestrial carbon; permafrost thaw threatens carbon release at a scale that matters for global climate).

The form serves the informational purpose well: the vertical organization, zone labels, and density gradient make the structural argument immediately accessible. The environmental variable columns add the quantitative layer for viewers who want mechanism, not just pattern.

Minor purpose concern: the title does not declare the stakes. "Boreal Forest: A Vertical Column, Surface to Permafrost" is accurate but quiet. A subtitle line connecting the ecology to the carbon storage or permafrost-thaw context would align the form's purpose with the subject's actual stakes. This is not required for the current informational framing — but the work would gain from a sentence that explains why the boreal column matters.

Score: **13/15** (form serves purpose well; stakes declaration is present only implicitly)
