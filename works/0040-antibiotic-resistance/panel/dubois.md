# Du Bois Review — 0040 Antibiotic Resistance (Cycle 1)

## School Fidelity (20)

Type A, statistical-graphics. The form is correct. The encoding is correct. What is missing is the argument that the data supports but the design does not yet make.

I work in a tradition where the choice of comparison is the political act. Who you put in the same matrix with whom, and in what order, is an editorial decision that shapes what the viewer sees and concludes. The current design puts India in the same alphabetical neighborhood as Italy — which makes the comparison appear natural. But these two countries have resistance rates that differ by 50 percentage points for some pathogens, for structural reasons that are documented and analyzable. The alphabetical proximity conceals that structural chasm.

Sort by income tier and the chasm becomes the argument. The viewer sees it before they read it.

Score: **15/20**

## Integrity (20)

The data is sound. The sources are authoritative. Missing data is marked.

I want to raise a different Integrity concern: the work is designed for policymakers, and policymakers have power over funding and regulation. The design must be honest about what the data does and does not support. It supports the correlation between income/stewardship quality and resistance rates. It does not, by itself, prove that investing in stewardship infrastructure in lower-income countries will achieve Nordic-level resistance rates in five or ten years. The causal arrow is plausible but not established by this data alone.

The design should include a note in the figure legend: "Correlation between stewardship quality and resistance rates does not establish the rate of achievable improvement through stewardship investment. See [WHO stewardship intervention studies] for intervention timescale estimates." This is the precision-as-defiance standard applied to advocacy data: show the honest case, including its limits.

Score: **15/20**

## Legibility (15)

High-literacy audience, contemplative study. The matrix is legible. The argument is not. See Bertin and Playfair's sorting critiques — I agree with them completely.

I add one additional legibility concern: the income tier dots are small and require close reading. The income tier should appear as a visible structural element of the matrix, not as a small notation beside country names. Color-code the country name background by income tier (light blue for high income, light yellow for upper-middle, light orange for lower-middle, light red for low income). Then the income tier is immediately visible without requiring any close reading of the dot notation.

Score: **11/15**

## Resonance (15)

Advocacy resonance is the target and it is only partially achieved. The data is there. The scale of the problem is visible. But the structural argument — that stewardship, not income alone, is the critical variable — is not visible in the current form.

I have built my advocacy career on the insight that precision is the persuasive tool when the data is on your side. The data here is unambiguous: the resistance rates for Nordic countries (even with high antibiotic use) are dramatically lower than for high-use low-stewardship countries. This comparison is the argument. Make it structurally visible.

Score: **11/15**

## Purpose (15)

Advocacy purpose requires that the form serve the argument's stakes. The stakes are clear: antimicrobial resistance is projected to kill 10 million people annually by 2050 if current trends continue. A work designed to reach WHO health ministers on this subject has moral weight proportionate to those stakes. The current design does not take its subject seriously enough. A standard heat matrix with alphabetical ordering treats a public health emergency as a data display exercise.

Score: **11/15**
