# Tufte Review — 0040 Antibiotic Resistance (Cycle 1)

## School Fidelity (20)

Type A, statistical-graphics school. The heat matrix is legitimate within the tradition Bertin formalized and that I have applied extensively in VDQI. The encoding is correct: color value (lightness) for ordered quantitative data. The grammar is applied without violation.

The sorting failure is a grammar violation of the first order. Bertin's reordering matrix insight — that sorting rows and columns by similarity reveals structure that random order hides — is not an optional refinement. It is the core argument for why matrices are useful. An unsorted matrix is like a bar chart with bars arranged randomly: technically legible but argumentatively inert.

Score: **15/20**

## Integrity (20)

Data integrity is sound. WHO GLASS and ECDC are the correct authoritative sources. Missing data is properly marked. The income tier indicators in the margin are an honest disclosure of the structural variable.

I want to flag the numeric labels inside cells. Resistance rate percentages printed inside the cells are a strong data-ink commitment — the viewer gets the precise number alongside the visual encoding. This is correct practice for a high-literacy audience in a contemplative reading context. Do not remove them in revision. They add significant data density without confusion.

Score: **16/20**

## Legibility (15)

The matrix is legible to a high-literacy policy audience. The color scale is clearly labeled. The income tier dots are legible at reading distance. But the pattern — the argument — is not legible because the sorting hides it.

There is also a small multiples opportunity that this design misses. A second panel showing the same matrix sorted by income tier, or a small scatter plot of income vs. average resistance rate by country, would give the viewer two simultaneous views: the matrix for country-by-country comparison and the structural plot for the systemic argument. This is the Envisioning Information layering approach.

Score: **11/15**

## Execution (15)

Economy: clean. Every element earns its place. The cross-hatch for missing data is economical and honest. The color scale bar is appropriately positioned.

Craft: the sequential red palette is technically correct. But the palette choice is conservative — it shows magnitude (how much resistance) without showing the structural argument (how much deviation from achievable good-stewardship rates). A diverging palette anchored at WHO-target rates would make the argument visible in the color encoding itself. This is the most significant craft upgrade available.

Score: **12/15**

## Resonance (15)

Domain resonance for infectious disease specialists: they will recognize the magnitude of resistance in high-burden countries immediately. The dark cells for India and Pakistan in the E. coli and K. pneumoniae columns are striking to any clinician.

Advocacy resonance is partial: the data is presented, but the structural argument is not made through form. A policymaker who does not already know that stewardship matters will not learn it from this matrix.

Score: **11/15**

## Purpose (15)

The purpose is advocacy: persuade WHO health ministers to invest in stewardship infrastructure, not just antibiotic volume reduction. The form is appropriate for the audience. But the form is not making the argument — it is displaying the data and asking the viewer to make the argument themselves. The revision needs to make the form do the argument's work.

Score: **11/15**
