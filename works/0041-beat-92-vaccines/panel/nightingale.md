# Nightingale Review — 0041 Beat-92 Vaccines

I have spent my working life trying to get policymakers to see what the data shows. The mortality tables from the Crimea were clear to any statistician — the hospital deaths were preventable. But tables did not move Parliament. A diagram moved Parliament.

This work will move policymakers. I am certain of it.

The argument is immediate: the right bars are shorter than the left bars, and the countries where they are most dramatically shorter are the countries where the gradient fills are most saturated. You do not need a statistician's training to read this. You need ten seconds and the willingness to look. The title confirms what the eye has already seen: children are surviving because vaccines are working. The decision-maker who sees this understands two things simultaneously — the scale of improvement, and that the improvement came from something specific (vaccines) and actionable.

What I particularly appreciate is the design's honesty about what remains undone. The countries at the top of the list — where the 2023 bars are still long — tend to be the countries where the gradient fills are palest. That is an honest display. It does not hide the failure cases. It incorporates them as part of the argument: we know what works, we need to do more of it where coverage is still low. Precision-as-defiance, Du Bois would say. I say it differently: honesty is the most powerful advocacy tool, because it cannot be dismantled by showing the unflattering numbers. You have already shown them.

The one weakness I would name: the chart is designed for contemplative study by sophisticated readers. A policymaker who encounters it on a screen in a six-second scroll will see the bars but may not engage with the gradient encoding. I would recommend a companion version simplified for public-display contexts — not to replace this one, but to extend its reach. This chart as presented earns its full marks for its intended context.
