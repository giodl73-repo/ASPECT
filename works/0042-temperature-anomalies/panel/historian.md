# Historian Review — 0042 Temperature Anomalies

The precedent for this form is Rosling's animated bubble chart (2006), which demonstrated that animation could be a primary visualization variable. The temperature anomaly bar animation draws directly from that tradition: one variable animated through time, with a presenter narrating inflection points. School attribution — dynamic-statistical-graphics (Type A) — is correct.

Two historical comparisons are instructive. First, Ed Hawkins' "warming stripes" (2018) — same data, color bands rather than animation. The stripes are static and achieve resonance through pattern recognition; the animation achieves resonance through temporal experience. They answer different questions: the stripes show the full pattern at once; the animation forces sequential experience. Second, the NOAA annual temperature anomaly bar chart, updated each year. The static form is technically adequate; the animation transforms the argument from "look at the end state" to "watch it happen."

The key innovation question raised by this work: should animated visualizations be scored frame-by-frame or as a whole? The panel consensus is: as a whole. Each frame is nearly empty — a single bar with minimal annotation. Scoring each frame individually would penalize the work for the very parsimony that makes the animation work. The animation is the unit of evaluation; the frames are its marks. This is analogous to scoring a sparkline by the sequence of ink strokes rather than by the whole.

This is original design, correctly attributed. It advances the school by demonstrating that the Rosling grammar (animate, narrate, highlight) works for single-variable time series as well as multi-variable scatter.
