# Rosling Review — 0042 Temperature Anomalies

This is what I have been saying for years. Time is data. You cannot show 144 years in a static chart and expect the viewer to feel what happened. They will see the endpoint. They will not feel the acceleration.

Watch the first 50 frames — 1880 to 1930. The bar jiggles up and down near zero. Sometimes slightly above, sometimes slightly below. No trend is obvious. The viewer relaxes. They might think: "this is noise." Then in the 1980s the bar starts going up. Not dramatically at first — a steady creep. Then 1998 jumps. The viewer feels that jump; they have been watching zero for a century and suddenly the bar leaps. Then it comes back, slightly, and they exhale. Then 2016 leaps again, higher. And 2023, higher still. By 2024 the bar is deep red and the viewer understands, in a way no static chart can produce, that this is not noise. This is a trend. The experience of watching it happen in real time is irreplaceable.

The design choices support this experience at every point. The single bar — not 144 bars side by side — forces the viewer to watch the change rather than see it. The large year label keeps the temporal location visible without cluttering the frame. The trailing 10-year line gives memory without adding complexity. The color gradient — blue through gray to red — does something that a monochrome bar cannot: it makes the thermal meaning visible in the bar itself. You do not need to look at the axis to know whether the year was warm or cold. The bar tells you.

My one disagreement with Tufte: the red is not manipulative. Temperature anomaly data — warm, cold, hot — has an intrinsic thermal quality. The color encodes that quality honestly. The deep red of 2024 is earned by the data, not imposed by the designer's alarm.

I ask only this: add a running average line (10-year smoothed) that appears alongside the bar as the animation progresses. Not to replace the bar, but to help the viewer distinguish signal from noise. A viewer watching 1880–1920 would see the bar jiggling around a flat running average. A viewer watching 2000–2024 would see the bar consistently above a rising running average. The contrast between noisy-but-flat and noisy-but-rising would be unmistakable.
