# Tufte Review — 0042 Temperature Anomalies

I will say what needs to be said about the color. Blue-to-red is a connotative choice. The data does not require this encoding — the anomaly value is fully communicated by bar height, which is the correct primary variable. The color is a redundant encoding at best; at worst it imposes an emotional valence on the data. Red = danger is a cultural association, not a perceptual fact. I have always maintained that emotional color is chartjunk's cousin — not useless, but suspect.

That said, I will score this chart on its total design economy, not on one debatable choice. Per-frame data density: excellent. One bar. One zero-line. One trailing context line. Year label. Bar-tip annotation. Y-axis with labeled ticks. That is the entirety of the frame. No background color. No gridlines. No decoration. The data-ink ratio per frame is among the highest I have seen in dynamic visualization. The designer has my attention.

The animation variable itself: I said in VDQI that small multiples are usually better than animation. Let me qualify that position for this specific case. Small multiples of 144 annual bars would be legible — the trend would be visible — but the experience of watching the red grow would be absent. The animation produces something that small multiples cannot: temporal weight. The viewer who watches 1880–2024 in real time has spent 144 seconds feeling each year pass. That is a different experience from seeing 144 years simultaneously. For this specific argument — climate change as a lived temporal process — the animation variable is justified. I will not dock for it.

My primary concern is the "Watch the red grow" narration. This is an emotional cue from the presenter, not from the data. The data shows the anomaly growing; the narration tells the viewer how to feel about it. If this is accompanied work, the narration is part of the encoding and I score it together with the visualization. The narration is advocacy-by-voice. This is Nightingale's method applied to animation: the presentation does advocacy work that the chart alone does not do.

The execution question: animation is scored as a whole, not frame by frame. Each frame is nearly perfect in economy. The animation as a whole is temporally honest — no frames are dropped, no years are skipped, the pace is uniform. The execution grade is high.
