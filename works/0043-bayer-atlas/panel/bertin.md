# Bertin Review — 0043 Bayer World Geo-Graphic Atlas

The Atlas demonstrates unusual semiotic literacy for 1953. Let me audit the visual variables systematically.

The dot density map uses position (geographic location) to encode geographic position — the most natural mapping — and dot density to encode population density. Dot density is a texture variable in my taxonomy, which is selective and associative but not naturally quantitative. The viewer can judge "more" vs. "fewer" but cannot estimate ratios accurately. Bayer declares the unit (1 dot = 100,000 persons) which converts the texture variable into a count variable through declaration. This is a legitimate technique — it works because the dots are discretely countable in low-density areas and give a reliable gestalt impression in high-density areas.

The bar charts in the right margin are standard and correctly executed. Horizontal orientation appropriate for category labels; zero baseline maintained; proportional lengths verified.

The age pyramid is a well-established butterfly chart form. Both halves use a shared zero axis; the male/female split is categorical (hue) × quantitative (length). Hue for categorical is correct; length for quantitative is correct. No grammar errors.

The urban/rural stacked percentage bars: percent encoding in stacked bars is a known precision loss (viewers cannot accurately read interior segments), but the design uses only two categories (urban/rural), which reduces the precision loss to near-zero. Correct choice.

My primary concern: the regional color washes on the dot density map encode geography redundantly (the region is already identified by the dots' positions). The color washes function as visual grouping aids, not data encodings. They are structural-attentional marks — not chartjunk, but not data. A small penalty.

The six-element composition hangs together through the consistent typographic and color system — a genuine information-architecture achievement.
