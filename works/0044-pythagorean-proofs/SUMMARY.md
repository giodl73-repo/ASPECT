## The Central Innovation: Proof Elegance as Rubric Score

The comparative scoring reveals a genuine DEGAS innovation: proof elegance is not an aesthetic preference — it is a real scoring variable that operates through the E-pathway.

**Why the Chu-pei proof scores 6.9 points higher:**

1. **E1 (Foundation):** Euclid requires prior propositions I.35-I.38 plus SAS congruence from earlier in Elements. The Chu-pei proof requires only area conservation (visually verifiable) and labeled dimensions (given). Lower foundation requirement = higher E1 score = higher Integrity.

2. **E2 (Completeness):** Both proofs are complete. But completeness at 2 steps vs. 10 steps affects the Legibility score: a 2-step complete proof is legible to a broader audience than a 10-step complete proof.

3. **Execution (Economy lens):** The Chu-pei proof achieves the intrinsic minimum — it cannot be simpler without losing validity. Euclid's proof is at the intrinsic minimum for its proof strategy, but its proof strategy requires more complexity. The Economy lens penalizes the excess above intrinsic necessity, not the intrinsic necessity itself. But "intrinsic necessity" for a theorem can vary depending on which proof approach is used. The Chu-pei approach has a lower intrinsic minimum.

4. **Resonance:** The Chinese proof produces a moment of immediate visual conviction — the transformation is experienced as revelation. The Euclid proof produces satisfaction on completion of a multi-step argument. The Chinese proof's resonance is more immediate and experiential.

**The key finding: Proof elegance maps to rubric score through the E-pathway.** A simpler proof that achieves the same validity is not just aesthetically preferable — it genuinely scores higher because it satisfies E1 with less foundation, makes E2 verifiable more broadly, and achieves intrinsic minimum complexity in Execution. Elegance is not a separate criterion; it emerges from the existing criteria applied correctly.

## Does This Mean the Euclid Proof Is Worse?

No. Euclid's proof does something the Chinese proof does not: it proves the theorem within a formal axiomatic system, establishing it as a consequence of prior-stated postulates. This is important for mathematics as a discipline — the formal deductive structure matters beyond any individual proof. The Chinese proof is more elegant as an isolated visual argument; the Euclid proof is more important as a contribution to the deductive tradition. The rubric scores the visual proof as a visualization. Euclid's broader importance to mathematics is not scored — it is a context note.
