# Brief: Visual Proofs of the Pythagorean Theorem

## Subject

Two proofs of the Pythagorean theorem (a squared + b squared = c squared) reviewed as a comparative set:

1. Euclid's proof (Elements I.47, "Bride's Chair"): constructs squares on all three sides; uses area-equality arguments through triangle congruence and rectangle-equivalence; 10+ construction steps.

2. Chinese "Chu-pei" rearrangement proof (3rd century BCE, preserved in Chou-pei Suan-ching): four right triangles rearranged within a square; the remaining central square has area c squared; the same four triangles arranged differently enclose two squares of area a squared + b squared; therefore a squared + b squared = c squared. One visual move.

## Design Intent

Score both proofs using the demonstrative encoding pathway (E1-E4). The key finding: proof elegance matters to the score. Simpler proofs score differently on E2 (completeness) and require different foundation levels for E1.

## School

Mathematical-proof-visualization (Type A). Both proofs are members of the school; neither founded it (the school predates formalization).

## Score Target

85-90. The innovation: proof elegance as scoring variable. Does the Chinese rearrangement proof (one move, immediately convincing) score higher than the Euclid proof (many steps, more complex foundation) on the E-pathway questions?
