# Artist Review — 0044 Pythagorean Proofs

I am asked to review two geometric proofs. I will be honest: I prefer the Chinese one, and for reasons that are not entirely separable from my aesthetic sensibilities, but let me try to articulate them precisely.

Euclid's proof is a cathedral. It is elaborate, internally organized, built on deep foundations, and impressive in its ambition. You see the whole structure — three squares, the right triangle at the center, the altitude bisecting the hypotenuse square — and you feel the weight of the construction before you begin following the argument. The proof has visual presence. It demands respect. But it does not surprise. You know you are embarking on a formal proof; the visual structure announces this from the first mark. There is no moment of revelation, only the satisfaction of arriving where you knew you were going.

The Chinese rearrangement proof is a conjurer's trick. You see four triangles in a square. Fine. Then the designer moves the triangles. And you understand, in the instant of the move, that the whole theorem is right there in the gap between before and after. The moment of understanding is a visual experience — not arrived at through steps, but delivered in a single transformation. That instant of revelation is what I mean by resonance. The Chinese proof has it. The Euclid proof earns it through patience.

Is the Chinese proof original? No — it has been known for centuries. But it is more original as a visual experience: it achieves, in two diagrams, what no verbal argument can produce — the theorem's truth felt as a direct perceptual event. That is the specific achievement of proof-visualization as a school: not to explain but to make visible.

Both proofs are remarkable. The Euclid is more comprehensive; the Chinese is more memorable. The rubric correctly gives higher scores to minimum necessary complexity when validity is preserved. The Chinese proof achieves validity at minimum complexity. It wins on Economy. It also wins on something harder to name: the experience of sudden understanding.
