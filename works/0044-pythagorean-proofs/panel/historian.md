# Historian Review — 0044 Pythagorean Proofs

Historical grounding for both proofs.

**Euclid (c.300 BCE):** The proof in Elements I.47 is Euclid's own formulation — not a discovery of the theorem (which was known centuries earlier across multiple cultures) but a proof in the formal deductive style that Elements established. The "Bride's Chair" nickname comes from the shape of the construction: the square on the hypotenuse, bisected by the altitude line, with the two leg squares arranged to either side, resembles a chair. The proof was the canonical version in Western mathematics for over 2,000 years. Proclus (5th century CE) suggested alternative proofs; Euclid's was taught in schools through the 20th century. The visual diagram has remained essentially unchanged from Euclid's original.

**Chinese Chu-pei rearrangement:** The proof appears in the Chou-pei Suan-ching (Zhou Bi Suan Jing), an ancient Chinese mathematical text. The dating is contested: the text was compiled around 100 BCE but may contain material from the 3rd century BCE or earlier. The rearrangement form of the proof was independently developed in multiple cultures — it appears in Indian mathematics (Sulbasutras, c.800-200 BCE) and Chinese sources. It is typically called the "dissection proof" or "rearrangement proof" in modern histories of mathematics.

Historical comparison question: do the two proofs score the same? They should not. The Euclid proof establishes a formal deductive system — it is a proof within a larger axiomatic framework. The Chinese proof demonstrates a specific theorem visually — it is a proof by visual argument. These are different proof traditions with different epistemological foundations. The Chinese proof is not inferior; it operates by different rules. Scoring both under the same E-pathway is appropriate: both are demonstrative (the diagram proves the claim), both must satisfy E1-E4. The difference emerges in E1 (foundation requirements) and E2 (complexity of the argument).

The innovation this work identifies: proof elegance is a real property that affects rubric scores through the E-pathway. Simpler proofs with fewer construction steps score higher on E1 (less foundation) and achieve the same E2-E4 scores. This is not arbitrary preference — it reflects the genuine rubric criterion that intrinsic complexity should be scored at the intrinsic minimum.
