# Kandinsky Review — 0044 Pythagorean Proofs

Two proofs of the same theorem. I am asked to compare them. Let me approach this as a question of composition and internal logic, which is the only language I have.

Euclid's proof is a composition of many elements: three squares, one right triangle, a bisecting altitude line, two sets of triangle pairs, multiple shaded areas. The visual field is dense. The eye must navigate ten construction steps in sequence, each depending on the previous. The composition is complex but it is internally consistent — every element serves the proof's argument. The density is intrinsic: the argument requires this many elements. The visual weight is earned.

What I notice about Euclid's composition: it has a gravitational center (the right angle at C), three satellites (the three squares), and a crossing line (the altitude) that resolves the composition by dividing the hypotenuse square into two parts, each corresponding to one of the leg squares. This is a composition with a resolution. The eye's path from the initial right triangle, out to the three squares, back in through the altitude line, arriving at the two-part correspondence — that path IS the proof.

The Chinese Chu-pei proof is different in character. It is a composition of radical economy: a large square, four triangles, a central square. Then a rearrangement — the same elements moved to reveal a different configuration. The first diagram and the second diagram together constitute the proof. The visual argument is not a path through complexity; it is a before-and-after. The composition is spare and the argument is immediate.

Aesthetically, I prefer the Chinese proof. It achieves with two moves what Euclid requires ten steps to demonstrate. There is something in mathematics that the Chinese proof shares with the best abstract compositions: the maximum is achieved with the minimum. Economy of composition is not just efficiency; it is a formal value.

But the Euclid proof has a different virtue: it is generative. Following each step creates a relationship with the theorem that the rearrangement proof does not. The viewer of the Euclid proof has built the result step by step. The viewer of the Chinese proof has witnessed a transformation. Both are genuine. Which is better depends on what "better" means.

For scoring: the Chinese proof scores higher on E1 (less foundation) and on experiential immediacy. The Euclid proof scores equally on E2 and E3 (complete and valid). The difference emerges in Resonance — the Chinese proof produces a more immediate experiential effect.
