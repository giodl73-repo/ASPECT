# Tufte Review — 0044 Pythagorean Proofs

These are demonstrative works, not reportive ones. I apply E1-E4, not questions 1-3.

For Euclid: the diagram achieves something remarkable — high information density in a small space. The "Bride's Chair" construction fits three squares plus the bisecting altitude plus all the triangle relationships into a single diagram without clutter. Every line in the diagram carries argumentative weight. This is close to my ideal of data density — here, proof density. No mark in Euclid's construction is decorative.

My concern with Euclid is different: it is a proof with high barrier to entry. You need to have read and internalized Elements I.1-I.46 before this diagram is legible as a proof rather than a picture. That is a significant E1 cost. The proof is internally complete but externally demanding.

The Chinese rearrangement proof achieves something I rarely see: a proof where the diagram alone IS the argument, without any written text. You see the first configuration, you see the second configuration, and if you can see that area is preserved under rearrangement, you have the theorem. The proof requires essentially no prior knowledge. The diagram requires only two marks: a labeled first configuration and a labeled second configuration.

In VDQI terms: the Chinese proof has a higher proof-density-to-complexity ratio than Euclid. Euclid achieves maximum rigor at high complexity; the Chinese proof achieves equivalent validity at minimum complexity.

My verdict: both proofs score extremely high, but differently. Euclid's is a more complete formal system; the Chinese is a more elegant individual proof. On the Economy lens of Execution, the Chinese proof wins — it achieves the same conclusion with fewer marks. This is the purest expression of Economy of Means I have encountered in the mathematical-proof-visualization school.
