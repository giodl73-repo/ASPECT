# Audience Review — 0045 Language Endangerment as Visual Fade

My first response standing in front of this: it is beautiful and I find it slightly difficult to understand.

I see a field of dots. Some are bright, some are dim. The title says 7,168 languages and 3,000 will not survive this century. I look for the legend. I find it in small text at the bottom: bright = safe, dim = endangered. Now I understand: the dim dots are dying languages. The barely-visible dots are nearly extinct. Some of the blank spaces might be where languages have already gone silent.

The experience once I understand it: genuinely affecting. I start looking for the bright clusters (large language families — Mandarin, Spanish, Arabic) and then I look at the dim areas and the near-invisible traces. I find myself trying to count the barely-visible marks in the Pacific Northwest. I can't. That is the point.

What I wish I had: a clearer legend, positioned at eye level, not at the bottom of a large display. And a way to zoom in. If this is a web display, I want to be able to hover over a mark and see: this language is Tlingit, 500 speakers, Southeast Alaska. The data is in the UNESCO Atlas; the interaction would make the field personal.

Three things I take away: (1) most of the world's languages are in Asia and the Pacific, (2) a lot of them are barely visible, (3) some places that look empty have only ghost traces. That is a richer understanding than I had before. The work works.
