# Nightingale Review — 0045 Language Endangerment as Visual Fade

I designed my coxcomb to reach people who would not read statistical tables. This work is designed to reach people who may not know that language death is happening at this rate or scale. They are different problems with different solutions.

My question: who will see this work and what will they do differently because of it? If this is a museum installation or a public gallery display, the audience is self-selecting — visitors who are already interested in cultural heritage, language, or information design. For that audience, the work succeeds: it creates a felt experience of language loss that a table or bar chart cannot produce. The viewer leaves with something they did not have before: a sense of the scale and distribution of the crisis.

If this is meant to reach decision-makers — policy officials, funders, governments — then it needs an action frame. What does "3,000 languages will not survive this century" mean for policy? What can a government do? What should a funder prioritize? The work creates the emotional precondition for action but does not provide the action pathway. For public-display advocacy, this is often enough. For policy advocacy, it is a starting point, not a finish.

The density-endangerment confound that Bertin and Kandinsky identified is real, and I will add a practical dimension: in a public-display context, viewers will not read the legend carefully. The confound will create the impression that all sparse regions are regions of language loss, when some are simply regions of low linguistic diversity. This is an integrity problem in the public-display context even if it is not in the contemplative-study context.

The solution is a more prominent legend and a brief explanatory text: "Each dot is one language. Brightness shows survival: bright = safe, dim = endangered, nearly invisible = critically endangered or extinct." Five lines of text, positioned at arm's-length reading distance from the primary display.
