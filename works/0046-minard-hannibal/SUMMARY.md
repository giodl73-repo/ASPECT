## The Three-Way Minard Comparison: Hannibal (85.1) vs. Napoleon (85.5) vs. Wine (see 0048)

The Napoleon march scored 85.5 under v1.6 (revised). The Hannibal map scores 85.1 under v2.2. The gap is 0.4 points — negligible, within the margin of reviewer variation.

**Is the Napoleon map's score inflated by emotional weight?**

The panel verdict: No. The difference (0.4 points) is explained by:

1. **Data quality difference (Integrity):** Napoleon's figures come from contemporary military records; Hannibal's come from ancient historians with documented biases and contested figures. The Integrity penalty for the Hannibal map is real and justified — it is not an emotional inflation of the Napoleon map's score.

2. **Dimensional information difference (Execution):** The Napoleon map encodes six variables across two registers; the Hannibal map encodes four variables in one register. The Napoleon map includes a temperature second register that encodes a causal variable for the attrition. The Hannibal map shows attrition without quantitatively encoding cause. This is a genuine execution difference, not emotional weight.

3. **What does NOT explain the gap:** The Napoleon map's cultural resonance for French audiences, Minard's personal connection to the French national memory, or any emotional inflation from the subject matter. If we score only the technical elements — School Fidelity, Legibility — both maps are tied (18/20 and 13/15 respectively).

**Conclusion:** The Napoleon march's 85.5 score is technically earned. The 0.4-point gap over the Hannibal map is attributable to (a) higher data quality in Napoleon's figures and (b) greater dimensional information through the second register. Emotional weight does not appear to have inflated the Napoleon score. The rubric handles the comparison correctly.

## Dimension Analysis

**School Fidelity (18.0/20):** Both maps apply the same flow-map grammar identically. School Fidelity scores are tied between the Napoleon and Hannibal maps. The grammar (band width = quantity; path = geographic route; Type C synthesis) is correctly applied in both.

**Integrity (14.85/20):** The most significant gap from the Napoleon map (which scored approximately 17-18/20 on Integrity). Ancient sources from Polybius and Livy carry uncertainty that is not declared in the Hannibal map. Minard used the high estimates for the starting figure (~90,000); modern historians put the number lower. The proportion of loss displayed may be distorted if the starting number is overstated.

**Legibility (13.0/15):** Comparable to the Napoleon map. The single register is actually simpler to read — there is no need to understand the linked x-axis that connects the two registers in the Napoleon map.

**Execution (13.35/15):** Lower than Napoleon primarily because the Napoleon map's second register adds dimensional information about cause (temperature → attrition). The Hannibal map shows the effect (attrition) without encoding cause. This is a legitimate design limitation: terrain and temperature data for 218 BCE would be speculative.

**Resonance (12.85/15):** Lower than Napoleon. The historical distance of 2,000+ years from Hannibal's campaign is real; Minard made the Napoleon map within living memory of the campaign. The resonance of watching a familiar historical event compressed into a flow band is different from watching an ancient campaign. This is an honest difference in achievable resonance, not an artifact of scoring.

**Purpose (13.0/15):** Historical documentation with moral weight. Form serves purpose. Comparable to Napoleon.

## Top 3 Design Recommendations

1. Declare uncertainty in data sources — a footnote stating "Troop figures from Polybius (c.150 BCE) and Livy (c.10 BCE); modern estimates range from 26,000 to 50,000 survivors in Italy" would raise Integrity by 2-3 points.
2. Add an elevation profile as a second register — a schematic elevation cross-section below the march band, showing the Alpine crossing, would encode cause (terrain severity) alongside effect (attrition). This would bring the Hannibal map closer to the Napoleon map's dimensional completeness.
3. Show historical uncertainty as band width range — a thin confidence band around the main flow band, encoding the range of ancient source estimates, would make the data uncertainty visible rather than hidden.
