# Brief: Minard's Hannibal Crossing the Alps (1866)

## Historical Work

Carte figurative et approximative representant les pertes successives en hommes de l'armee carthaginoise dans sa campagne contre les romains, depuis la jonction du Rhone, jusqu'en fin de la campagne. Published 1866 by Minard; three years before the Napoleon march map (1869).

## Subject

Hannibal's campaign of 218 BCE, from Cartagena through Gaul to Italy. Band width encodes troop count at each stage. Start: approximately 90,000-100,000 troops at the Rhone crossing. Arrival in Italy (Po valley): approximately 26,000. The Carthaginian force lost roughly 70% of its strength crossing the Alps and through early attrition.

Geographic path: from the Rhone junction (southern Gaul) eastward through the Alps, down into northern Italy (the Po valley, where Hannibal's campaign continued for another 15 years).

## Score Target

84-88. The Hannibal map is technically comparable to the Napoleon map. The key differences: (1) historical distance (Hannibal is ancient history; Napoleon is living memory for Minard); (2) data quality (Hannibal's troop counts are from Polybius and Livy, with significant historical uncertainty; Napoleon's figures are from contemporary military records); (3) geographic scope (the Alps crossing covers less geographic complexity than Russia); (4) the Napoleon map includes a second register (temperature chart) that the Hannibal map lacks.
