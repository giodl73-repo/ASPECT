# Historian Review — 0046 Hannibal Crossing the Alps

The Hannibal map (1866) is one of Minard's 51 figurative maps produced between 1844 and 1869. It predates the Napoleon march map by three years and represents Minard's application of his flow-map grammar to ancient military history — a different methodological challenge than his work on contemporary trade and military data.

Historical context of the data: Polybius (c.200-118 BCE) is the primary source. He claims Hannibal had 38,000 infantry and 8,000 cavalry at the Rhone, reduced to 12,000 African and 8,000 Iberian infantry plus cavalry on arrival in Italy. Livy's figures are higher (c.59 BCE-17 CE): 38,000 foot and 8,000 horse crossing the Pyrenees, substantially reduced by the Alps. Modern military historians (e.g., de Beer, Lazenby) range from 26,000 to 50,000 arriving in Italy. Minard's figure of ~26,000 survivors is consistent with Polybius on arrivals, but his starting figure of ~90,000 is from the more generous ancient counts including Gallic allies acquired along the route.

The key historical distinction from the Napoleon map: Napoleon's campaign was documented contemporaneously in French military records that Minard could access directly. The figures in the Napoleon map have a specific provenance — they come from the Grande Armée's own records and Ségur's history. Hannibal's figures are secondary reconstructions from ancient sources. This is not a criticism of Minard's method — it is the best he could do — but it means the Hannibal map's quantitative claims carry more uncertainty than the Napoleon map's.

The Hannibal map's historical importance: it demonstrates that Minard's flow-map grammar was not specific to 19th-century data. The grammar is time-agnostic — it can represent any military campaign for which troop counts and routes are known at some level. This makes the Hannibal map a methodological statement as much as a historical one.

Score comparison with Napoleon (85.5): the Hannibal map should score lower primarily on Integrity (data quality and uncertainty disclosure) and secondarily on Execution (no second register). The School Fidelity scores should be comparable — both maps apply the same grammar correctly.
