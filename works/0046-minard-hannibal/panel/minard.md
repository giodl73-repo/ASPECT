# Minard Review — 0046 Hannibal Crossing the Alps

I made this map three years before the Russian campaign map. I have been asked whether my voice changes when I review my own other work. It should not, and I will try to ensure that it does not.

The Hannibal map is technically sound. The band width encodes troop count at the same linear scale I use in all my figurative maps: a declared unit (one millimeter = some thousands of men) that converts the visual width into a readable number. The geographic base is honest — I have simplified the Alps, as I simplify all complex terrain, to preserve legibility of the band. The route is accurate to the best historical knowledge of 1866.

Let me be precise about the differences between this map and the Russian campaign map, because the differences explain any scoring gap.

First: the data quality. For Napoleon's campaign, I had access to the records of the Grande Armée — contemporary military reports, supply records, accounts from officers who survived. For Hannibal's campaign, I relied on Polybius, who wrote approximately 50 years after the events, and Livy, who wrote 200 years after. The numbers are different in the two sources. I used the figures that seemed most consistent across the sources, but I cannot certify their accuracy as I can certify Napoleon's numbers. This is a genuine integrity difference.

Second: the second register. The Russian campaign map includes a temperature chart below the march map, linked by a shared horizontal axis. This second register adds a variable (temperature) that explains a cause of the attrition that the march band shows. The Hannibal map has no second register. It shows the loss — the band narrows dramatically at the Alpine crossing — but it does not show the cause directly. The cause is implied by the geography (the Alps are there) but not encoded as a variable.

Third: the emotional weight. I am asked whether the Napoleon map's emotional weight inflates its score. I will say this carefully: the stakes of the Hannibal campaign (74 BC BCE) are no less than the stakes of Napoleon's campaign to any historian. The loss of 70% of an army in the Alps is a moral weight. But for me, personally, in 1866, the Russian campaign is the living memory of my nation. I made that map because the weight of those numbers required a body. I made the Hannibal map because the method was right for the data.

Does emotional proximity affect my score? Let me score this carefully and compare to the Russian campaign map.
