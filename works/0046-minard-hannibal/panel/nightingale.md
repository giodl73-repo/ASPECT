# Nightingale Review — 0046 Hannibal Crossing the Alps

Three years before the Russian campaign map, Minard made this. He was already thinking about army losses — the moral weight of military attrition made visible through band width. The Hannibal map is where he found the form; the Napoleon map is where he perfected it.

This gives the Hannibal map an interesting historical status. It is not as accomplished as the Napoleon map, but it precedes it and in some ways anticipates it. From a historical perspective, the Hannibal map is the proof of concept; the Napoleon map is the mature statement.

My primary concern is the same as my colleagues': the data uncertainty. I built my career on using data to reach decision-makers, which means I believe in being precise about what data can and cannot say. Minard's troop counts for Hannibal's campaign are from ancient historians with known biases (Polybius was favorable to Rome; Livy was writing for a Roman audience). The starting figure of 90,000+ is the high estimate; modern military historians suggest numbers closer to 50,000-60,000. If the starting number is overstated, the proportional loss displayed in the band is distorted. The viewer who sees the band narrowing from "90,000" to "26,000" may think they are seeing a precise 71% loss when the actual loss rate is uncertain.

The Hannibal map lacks the second register that would explain causes. It is a record of attrition without a cause analysis. This is still valuable — knowing the scale of loss matters — but it is less complete than the Napoleon map.

The moral weight of 70,000 lives lost in the Alps is real regardless of whether the number is 50,000 or 90,000. The map captures that weight. It is a lesser achievement than the Napoleon map, but it is still Minard.
