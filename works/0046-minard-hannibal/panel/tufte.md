# Tufte Review — 0046 Hannibal Crossing the Alps

I compared this map to the Napoleon march map in my review of 0001. Let me revisit that comparison systematically.

The Napoleon map encodes six variables in a single figure: army size (band width), direction of march (color — tan vs. black), geographic position (map), route and sequence (band path), temperature (lower register line chart), and time (linked x-axis). Six variables, two registers, one reading.

The Hannibal map encodes four variables: army size (band width), direction of march (single color — no retreat band, Hannibal's army did not return), geographic position (map), and route/sequence (band path). Four variables, one register.

This is a real difference in information content. The Napoleon map says more. Whether "more" is "better" depends on whether the additional variables earn their place. In the Napoleon map, temperature does earn its place — it explains the rate of attrition during the retreat in a way that geography alone cannot. The temperature variable is the second-register argument: the army was destroyed partly by the cold. Without it, you see the attrition; with it, you understand a cause.

The Hannibal map shows only the attrition. It shows the narrowing band at the Alpine crossing, but it does not encode what caused the narrowing — terrain? combat? disease? starvation? The band narrows and the geography of the Alps is present as a label and symbol, but the terrain variable is not quantitatively encoded. This is a legitimate design choice (terrain data for 218 BCE would be speculative) but it is a lower information density than the Napoleon map.

Data quality: I noted in my Napoleon review that Minard's data for the Russian campaign is from contemporary records. The Hannibal campaign data is from ancient sources. This is an integrity difference, not a design difference. But integrity is part of what we score.

The Hannibal map is very good. It is less dimensional than the Napoleon map, and its data is less certain. These are real differences that justify a slightly lower score.
