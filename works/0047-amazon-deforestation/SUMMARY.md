## Top 3 Design Recommendations

1. Normalize environmental variable bars to a common percentage-of-1980 scale — all four variables expressed as % of 1980 reference value makes the rate-of-change comparison immediately visual. Carbon loss (96%), biodiversity loss (92%), rainfall reduction (82%), erosion increase (4,700%) become directly comparable.
2. Declare slope assumption for soil erosion figures — soil erosion varies 10x by slope; stating the assumed terrain slope (e.g., "15-degree slope, typical for cleared hillside Amazon terrain") makes the 24 t/yr/ha figure verifiable.
3. Add a small fourth panel showing secondary succession — a 2023 secondary forest panel (10-15 years of recovery on previously cleared land) would honestly represent the full spectrum of Amazon land use while keeping the primary argument intact. It would answer Du Bois's objection about selective framing.

## Innovations Logged

**#278 — Ecological absence as advocacy argument:** When a vertical ecological cross-section's 2023 panel is nearly empty (grass layer only), the vast white space from 1m to 45m represents the lost ecological structure. This is a convergence of absence-as-encoding (#276) and ecology-as-composition (Cluster W): the visual absence of the ecological layers IS the advocacy argument. The white space is not a design failure; it is the visual representation of what clearance removes.

**#279 — Environmental variable sidebar as advocacy quantification panel:** Pairing an ecological cross-section with a variable sidebar (carbon, biodiversity, water cycle, erosion) that quantifies each ecological dimension creates a two-level advocacy structure: the visual argument (the collapse) and the quantitative argument (the numbers). The pairing is a Type C synthesis reinforcement — the ecological school provides the structure; the advocacy school provides the quantification.

## Cluster Implications

#278 extends Cluster W (ecology-as-composition) into advocacy contexts. Previously, W was established for scientific illustration (boreal forest, coral reef, ocean depth). #278 shows that the principle also operates when the ecological subject is degraded — the visual density of a depleted ecosystem is as much an ecological argument as the density of an intact one. The cluster principle should be generalized: ecology-as-composition applies to the full spectrum of ecological states, including degraded and destroyed states.
