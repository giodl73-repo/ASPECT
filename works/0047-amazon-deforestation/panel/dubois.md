# Du Bois Review — 0047 Amazon Deforestation Cross-Section

I want to examine the advocacy framing carefully.

The work shows 1980, 2000, and 2023. The argument is: deforestation has removed forest layers and reduced environmental services. The temporal sequence implies a trend that will continue unless intervention occurs. This is a legitimate advocacy structure — show the change, quantify the consequence, imply the stakes. It is not a counterfactual (it does not show what would have happened under different policies); it is a factual sequence. The rubric does not require a counterfactual. The factual sequence is the argument.

The data sources: INPE (Brazil's National Institute for Space Research) provides satellite deforestation data — this is the gold standard for Amazon deforestation measurement. AmazonFACE provides carbon data from field experiments. IUCN biodiversity estimates are modeled, not directly measured, and carry more uncertainty than the satellite data. The soil erosion figures (0.5 to 24 tonnes/year/hectare) are within the range reported in the scientific literature for these land-use transitions.

The advocacy selection question: what is the counterfactual against which the 2023 state is measured? The design implicitly uses 1980 as the reference state — a primary forest baseline. Is 1980 the right baseline? It is the most favorable baseline for the advocacy argument, but it is also the most honest — 1980 is the pre-industrial-scale-deforestation baseline for the Brazilian Amazon. The selection is honest.

One concern: the work shows deforestation as a one-way sequence (primary → logged → cleared). This is accurate for the specific land parcels where clearance has occurred. But it does not address regenerating secondary forest, where cleared land is partially recovering. The advocacy works against including regeneration because it complicates the message. But an honest account of Amazon land use would note that some cleared areas are in secondary succession. I dock one point for this selective presentation.

The environmental variable bars quantify the stakes correctly. Carbon loss (300 → 12 tonnes/hectare), biodiversity collapse (100 → 8 index units), rainfall recycling reduction (78% → 14%) are all within published research ranges. Soil erosion (0.5 → 24 tonnes/year/hectare) is accurate for the transition from primary forest to compacted cattle pasture. The data is honest.
