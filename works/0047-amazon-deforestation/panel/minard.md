# Minard Review — 0047 Amazon Deforestation Cross-Section

I made maps of losses. I understand the logic of this design: show the state at three moments, so the viewer sees the diminishment without being told about it. The band that narrows is the argument. The forest layer that collapses is the argument. Show the number in the width of the band; show the ecological structure in the density of the cross-section. The work tells you what was there before and what is there now.

I want to examine the structural decision to use three equal panels rather than a graduated sequence. In my flow maps, the loss is continuous — the band narrows gradually as you follow the route, so the viewer sees every stage of attrition. Here, the work uses three discrete snapshots: 1980, 2000, 2023. The viewer sees before, middle, and after. They do not see the continuous process of loss.

This is a legitimate design choice. For forest cross-sections, a continuous animation would require data at every year, which may not exist at the level of specificity needed to draw accurate cross-section profiles. The three snapshots are the honest representation of what the data allows. But the discrete structure misses something the continuous structure has: the accumulation. In my Napoleon map, the viewer follows the band through every labeled point and feels each narrowing. In this work, the viewer sees before and after with one intermediate point, which is persuasive but not accumulative.

The environmental variable bars work correctly as a supplement to the visual argument. Carbon stored, biodiversity index, rainfall recycling, and soil erosion are the right variables — they capture the four most consequential dimensions of forest loss for human welfare and climate. The numbers are large enough to be immediately legible as catastrophic loss even without the cross-section diagrams.

The gravitational hierarchy — the vertical organization of the forest layers by their natural ecological position — is exactly correct. Emergent canopy at the top, root zone at the bottom, layers ordered by their actual position in the living forest. The collapse of this hierarchy in the 2023 panel (all layers gone except the 1m grass) is the work's most direct visual argument.
