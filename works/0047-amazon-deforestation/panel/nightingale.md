# Nightingale Review — 0047 Amazon Deforestation Cross-Section

This is the right form for this argument. And that is the most important thing I can say.

The vertical cross-section shows three things simultaneously: the physical structure of the forest at each time point, the change in that structure over time, and the quantified environmental consequences of the change. A viewer who looks at the three panels in sequence sees a forest collapse. Not described as collapsing — seen as collapsing. The 1980 panel is dense, layered, full of the visual weight of a living ecosystem. The 2023 panel is nearly empty white space with a thin strip of grass at the bottom. The visual argument is immediate and devastating.

The advocacy work is honest about the data. The environmental variable bars — carbon, biodiversity, rainfall recycling, soil erosion — do not merely state that deforestation is bad. They quantify the specific costs at each stage. A policymaker looking at these bars sees that moving from primary forest to selective logging loses 60 tonnes/hectare of carbon and cuts the biodiversity index in half. Moving from logged to cleared pasture loses another 168 tonnes/hectare and reduces biodiversity to 8% of reference. These are real numbers from real research programs (INPE, AmazonFACE). The advocacy is data-based.

The advocacy design choice that matters most: the three panels are equally sized. If the 2023 panel were smaller (showing only what remains), the visual argument would be less powerful. By giving 2023 the same spatial allocation as 1980, the work makes the void visible — the 2023 panel is full of absence. The white space from 1m to 45m is not a design failure; it is the visual representation of what has been lost.

One concern: the environmental variable bars are small relative to the cross-section panels. For a policymaker audience, these numbers are crucial — the carbon, biodiversity, and rainfall values quantify the stakes that make climate and biodiversity policy relevant. I would make the variable bars larger and more prominent, perhaps as a fourth panel with their own scale rather than as sidebars.
