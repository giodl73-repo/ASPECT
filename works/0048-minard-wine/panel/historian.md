# Historian Review — 0048 Wine Export Flow Map

The wine export map (1864) is one of Minard's most representative commercial works. He produced many figurative maps of trade flows: cotton, coal, immigrants, grain, wine. The wine map is among the clearest and best-preserved.

Historical context: 1864 French wine exports were at a historical high point before the phylloxera crisis (1863-1880) devastated French vineyards. The phylloxera aphid, introduced from North America, destroyed 40% of French vineyards by 1880 and essentially eliminated French wine exports for a generation. A map of 1864 wine exports therefore shows the last peak before catastrophe — though Minard could not have known this when he made it.

This historical context is not encoded in the map but is relevant for contemporary interpretation. The 1864 wine map inadvertently documents a moment of abundance that would not return for decades.

Technical comparison to the Napoleon map: I have examined both maps closely. The print quality, lettering style, band encoding, and geographic base handling are essentially identical. The wine map is executed to the same technical standard as the Napoleon map. The difference is entirely in the subject matter.

School attribution: Type C synthesis (cartography + statistical-graphics) is correct for both maps. The founding grammar — flow band width = quantity, geographic routing = real geography — is the same. Both maps correctly declare the Type C synthesis through their dual encoding (spatial position + quantitative variable).

The historical question the panel poses: does the Napoleon map deserve its score over the wine map? My assessment agrees with the emerging consensus: the technical scores should be nearly identical (perhaps 0-2 points in any direction); the difference emerges in Resonance and Purpose, where human tragedy vs. commercial data is a genuine distinction.
