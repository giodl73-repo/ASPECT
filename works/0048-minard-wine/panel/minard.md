# Minard Review — 0048 Wine Export Flow Map

I made this map in 1864. It was commercial work — the École des Ponts et Chaussées, where I spent my career as a civil engineer, had an interest in economic geography. Wine was one of the most important exports of France. Knowing where it went, and in what quantities, was commercially and administratively relevant.

I will be precise about what I was trying to do with this map and whether the form serves it.

The question the wine map poses: where does French wine go, and in what proportions? The flow bands answer this directly. The band to the UK is thicker than the band to Switzerland; the viewer can compare them without looking up the numbers. The bands radiate outward from France; the viewer immediately understands France as the source and the other countries as destinations. The geographic path of each band is approximately correct — the band to England crosses the Channel; the band to Algeria crosses the Mediterranean. The form serves the question it poses.

Does this map deserve to score as high as the Napoleon map? Let me think about this carefully, because the panel has asked me to compare my own works.

The technical execution is equivalent. The band width grammar is the same. The geographic base is handled the same way. The scale declaration is the same. If School Fidelity, Legibility, and Execution are scored on technical grounds alone, the wine map should score identically to the Napoleon map on those dimensions.

The difference is in Purpose and Resonance. Wine exports in 1864 are commercially interesting. They are not morally urgent. The band that represents 100,000 hectoliters of Bordeaux going to England is not a moral claim; it is a commercial measurement. The band that represents 100,000 French soldiers dying in Russia is a moral claim. The Purpose dimension exists to capture this difference. I find it appropriate that it does.

I would say one more thing: making a map of wine exports served France's commercial understanding. Making a map of the Russian campaign served France's moral understanding of what had happened to it. Both are legitimate purposes. The first is informational; the second is advocacy. The rubric correctly distinguishes them in Purpose.
