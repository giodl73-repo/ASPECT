# Playfair Review — 0048 Wine Export Flow Map

Wine exports. This is my subject — commercial data, trade flows, economic comparison. I invented charts for exactly this kind of data. Let me be direct about what I think of Minard's approach compared to mine.

Minard's flow map approach encodes direction in a way that my bar charts and line graphs cannot. The fact that wine flows from France to England, and not from England to France, is visible in the band's direction and path on the map. I could show wine export volumes as a bar chart — England gets the tallest bar, Switzerland gets a shorter bar, and so on. The volume comparison would be as clear or clearer in a bar chart. But the geographic dimension — where these countries are, that England requires crossing the Channel, that Algeria requires crossing the Mediterranean — is lost in a bar chart.

Minard's flow map gives you the commercial geography. My bar chart gives you the commercial comparison. These are different arguments.

For commercial and trade data, I would usually prefer the bar chart: it is easier to read precise quantities, easier to rank destinations, and easier to compare without geographic knowledge. But the flow map has a genuine advantage for audiences that need to see the commercial relationships in their geographic context — shipping routes, trade dependencies, geographic patterns of French economic influence.

The wine map is technically well executed. The band widths are proportional; the geographic routing is correct; the labels are clear. It answers the question it poses. My slight preference for bar charts in commercial contexts is a preference, not a ruling.

The scoring question: does this commercial work score differently from the military works? Yes, because the stakes are different. But the technique is strong.
