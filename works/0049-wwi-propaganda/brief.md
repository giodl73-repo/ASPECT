# Brief: WWI British Recruitment Propaganda (1915)

## Historical Work

"Daddy, What Did YOU Do in the Great War?" — British Parliamentary Recruiting Committee poster, designed by Savile Lumley, 1915. One of the most famous recruitment posters of WWI. The image: a domestic scene; a father sits in an armchair, looking guilty and distant; a young girl sits on his lap asking the title question; a boy plays on the floor with toy soldiers.

## Subject

A propaganda poster whose argument is: if you do not enlist, your children will judge you. The implied statistical claim: "most fit men are enlisting; you are an exception." No data is displayed. The "statistical claim" is embedded in the cultural context of the poster, not in any visual encoding.

## School

Advocacy-visualization (Type A). But advocacy serving manipulative ends — the poster is designed to coerce through guilt rather than persuade through evidence.

## The Core Scoring Questions

1. Can the rubric score a work that is technically accomplished (clear legibility, clear craft, clear purpose) but serves an argument built on:
   - Ontological contamination: "fit man" as a moral/physical category presented as neutral obligation
   - Implied statistical claim without data
   - Purpose serving manipulation rather than honest advocacy

2. Can Purpose score below 5 for a work whose stated purpose is manipulation?

## Score Target

65-72. This is the project's lowest score. The score should reflect a genuine integrity and purpose failure, not score the propaganda work high because it "works."
