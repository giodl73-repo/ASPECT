# Design Description: WWI Recruitment Poster (1915)

## Visual Structure

**Composition:** A domestic interior (sitting room). A father in civilian clothes sits in a cushioned armchair. His posture is slightly slumped; his gaze is inward. On his lap: a small girl (approximately 5-6 years old) looks up at him, holding a book of toy soldiers. On the floor: a small boy plays with toy soldiers in formation.

**The girl's question (title text):** "Daddy, what did YOU do in the Great War?" — large text at the bottom of the image. "YOU" is emphasized.

**The structural argument:** The viewer (a male civilian of military age) is placed in the position of "the father." The father's guilty posture and averted gaze communicates the anticipated answer: "I stayed home." The children's innocent question is designed to project that future guilt backward into the present, creating a guilt loop: "If you don't enlist now, this will be you later."

## Integrity Assessment

This is the critical dimension. The poster makes several claims:

**Claim 1: "Most fit men are enlisting."** The implied claim (not stated) that positions the viewer as an "exception" who must justify his absence. In 1915, this was factually uncertain — the volunteer army was large but had not reached saturation. The implied claim has no data basis in the poster.

**Claim 2: "Your children will judge you."** This is a psychological claim about future guilt. It may or may not be true; it is not a statistical claim. But it deploys a prediction about family relationships as if it were inevitable.

**Ontological contamination:** The poster embeds the category "fit man who has not enlisted" = "coward / bad father" without stating it. The category is not defined; it is shown through the father's posture and gaze. The viewer is invited to apply this categorization to himself. This is ontological contamination — a moral judgment presented through visual encoding as if it were a neutral classification.

**Purpose — manipulation vs. persuasion:** The distinction matters. Honest advocacy presents evidence and lets the audience decide. Manipulative advocacy targets psychological vulnerabilities (guilt, fear of social judgment, parental identity) to bypass the audience's rational assessment. This poster is designed to make rational assessment impossible: the guilt loop is triggered before the viewer can ask "is this actually true?"
