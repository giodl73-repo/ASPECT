# Du Bois Review — 0049 WWI British Recruitment Propaganda

I will begin with what this work correctly does, because I believe in honest review.

The legibility is high. The composition is immediately understood: a father in a chair, a girl on his lap, a question. The typography is legible; the scene is clear; the visual encoding of the father's guilt (slumped posture, averted gaze) is conventionally readable. If this were an advocacy work serving an honest argument, the craft would be commendable.

Now let me examine the argument, because that is where this work fails.

I spent my life working with data to counter false narratives. The dominant narrative about Black Americans in 1900 was that they were incapable of civilization, education, or family. I brought data to Paris because I knew that precise, unflattering, honest data was the strongest possible counter-argument. The precision was the weapon.

This poster is the inverse of my method. It makes no claim that can be checked. It implies a statistical reality ("most men are enlisting") that was not verified in 1915 and was in some contexts false. It invokes a category ("fit man who has not enlisted") that it does not define, filled with a moral charge that it does not argue for. A viewer who asks "is it true that most men are enlisting?" or "is it actually true that my children will judge me?" or "who is this 'fit' man, exactly?" cannot find answers in the poster.

The ontological contamination is structural. The category "man who has not enlisted" is equated through visual encoding with "bad father, coward, man who will be judged by his children." This equation is embedded in the image — in the father's posture, in the girl's question, in the juxtaposition of toy soldiers with civilian comfort — and it is presented as if it were the natural consequence of the situation, not as an argument to be accepted or rejected. The viewer cannot opt out of the guilt loop without first recognizing it as a loop, which the design is intended to prevent.

This is advocacy-as-coercion. The distinction from advocacy-as-persuasion is: persuasion presents evidence and argument and lets the audience decide. Coercion engineers a psychological state that makes refusal feel impossible. This poster engineers guilt. The guilt is real; the argument is false.

The Purpose score: I will not give this work a high Purpose score because its purpose is manipulation. A work that takes its subject (military service, civic obligation, family loyalty) seriously would present those things honestly. This work presents them as weapons. The Purpose dimension asks: "does the form serve the function? Does the work take its subject seriously?" No. It takes its subject as a mechanism. The men who were coerced by this poster, many of whom died in the trenches, deserved honesty. They received manipulation.
