# Statistician Review — 0049 WWI British Recruitment Propaganda

No data. Let me be very clear about what that means for this review.

The poster makes no explicit quantitative claims. But it implies statistical claims:

1. "Most fit men are enlisting" — implied by positioning the non-enlisting viewer as a guilty exception. In 1915, British voluntary enlistment had produced approximately 2.5 million men by mid-year. Total male population aged 18-40 in Britain: approximately 5-6 million. Enlistment rate was approximately 40-50%, not a majority. The implied claim ("most men") was factually uncertain and probably false.

2. "Your children will judge you" — this is a prediction about human behavior, not a statistical claim. It may be correct or incorrect; it has no evidential basis in the poster.

3. "Civilian status = cowardice/bad fatherhood" — this is a normative categorization presented as if it were an empirical description. In statistical language, this is an undeclared categorical variable with an embedded ordinal judgment. The category is constructed, not measured.

As a statistician, I note that the poster's "argument" has no data entry point. A viewer who asks "where do you get your numbers?" receives no answer because there are no numbers. The coercive mechanism is specifically designed to bypass the question-asking step. Effective manipulation must prevent interrogation; this poster is designed to trigger emotional response before rational assessment can occur.

Can Purpose score below 5 for a work whose stated purpose is manipulation? The rubric says Purpose scores 0-1 when "the form and purpose are incompatible." I would argue the opposite: the form (guilt-triggering illustration) and the purpose (guilt-based recruitment) are perfectly compatible. The form serves the purpose excellently. The problem is not form-purpose incompatibility; it is that the purpose itself is manipulation. The Purpose anchor at 0-1 ("the work has failed at the level of intention") does not apply here — the work has succeeded at its manipulative intention. The low Purpose score comes from the "Does the work take its subject seriously?" question, where the answer is clearly no: the work treats soldiers, family bonds, and civic obligation as tools for engineering guilt.
