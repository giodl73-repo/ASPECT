# Nightingale Review — 0051 Tobacco Physician Endorsement Campaigns

I know something about using statistics to reach an audience that would otherwise not listen. My coxcomb diagrams were designed to make Army administrators see preventable deaths they had been trained not to see. I chose the visual form that would work on that specific audience. The form served the honest argument.

This campaign does something structurally similar and entirely opposite. The designers chose statistics-adjacent form — large integer, authority figure, declarative conclusion — because they knew that form would work on magazine readers trained to trust survey data. They made the same audience calculation I made. They applied it to a false argument.

The physician figure is the telling element. I used data to reach decision-makers because data, properly presented, reaches people who otherwise distrust abstractions. The tobacco designers used a physician because physicians, properly placed in a layout, reach people who trust professional authority. The physician in the advertisement is not a specific person with a verified opinion; the physician is a visual variable encoding "expert consensus" without any expert having actually reached consensus. The authority is visual, not evidential.

What distinguishes this from legitimate advocacy is not the form — statistics-adjacent advocacy is a legitimate school with legitimate practitioners. What distinguishes it is that the evidence apparatus was manufactured. I could not have run these ads honestly because the data would have shown the opposite of what they claim. The form required the falsification.

**Rubric gap:** The rubric handles suppressed uncertainty and undeclared limitations but does not have scoring language for evidence fabrication — specifically, the case where the "data" was produced by the advocacy apparatus and the methodology was withheld to prevent verification. This is more severe than suppressed uncertainty; it requires specific naming.
