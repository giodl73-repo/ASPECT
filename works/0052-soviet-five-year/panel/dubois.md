# Du Bois Review — 0052 Soviet Five-Year Plan Progress Charts

I spent my life arguing that data is not neutral — that the choice of what to count, how to frame it, and whom to present it to are all political acts. But there is a distinction I want to draw precisely: arguing with data that has been framed is not the same as visualizing data that cannot be reported honestly.

The Soviet charts represent a condition that is different from any other case in this project. The tobacco industry manufactured false data through a deliberate process. WWI propaganda had no data. These charts had data — data that was generated under conditions where accurate reporting was physically dangerous to the reporter. The charts are not lying. The data pipeline is lying. That is a different mechanism, and it requires a different name.

I call this the captive source. The reporters — local party officials across the USSR — were not free to report what they observed. They reported what survival required them to report. The charts are an honest compilation of coerced numbers.

The Holodomor context is the sharpest illustration. In 1932–33, millions died of famine in Ukraine while official grain production charts showed record achievement. Those charts were not made by sadists who enjoyed lying; they were made by bureaucrats who received numbers that showed achievement because reporting failure was a capital offense. The grain numbers flowed upward through the falsification chain; the charts at the top of the chain were technically accurate.

This is the most severe Purpose failure in the cluster: not a work that fails to take its audience seriously, but a system in which the visualization actively conceals a famine from the people experiencing it. The audience could not evaluate the charts because all alternative information was suppressed.

**Rubric gap:** The current rubric has no mechanism for captive source — data generated under conditions where accurate reporting is impossible without personal danger to the reporter. This is distinct from suppressed uncertainty (the reporter knows and chooses not to say) and from manufactured consensus (the advocate produces the evidence). Here, the reporters knew, wanted to say, and could not.
