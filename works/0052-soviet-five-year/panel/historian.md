# Historian Review — 0052 Soviet Five-Year Plan Progress Charts

The Soviet Five-Year Plan charts must be understood in their full epistemological context: a state that controlled all media, all data publication, all statistical reporting, and all channels of public communication. When we ask "did the audience have access to alternative information?" the answer is structurally no.

This is the defining historical feature of the captive-source mechanism. The tobacco industry operated in a society with independent journalism, academic medicine, and competing commercial interests. American readers of the Lucky Strike ads could, in principle, have found contrary evidence. Soviet citizens viewing the Five-Year Plan charts in 1933 could not. The press was state-controlled; foreign correspondents were restricted and often collaborating with the official narrative; independent statistical analysis was a category that did not exist; the very concept of "alternative data" was politically inadmissible.

The falsification chain is well-documented by historians. Robert Conquest's work on the Holodomor, Andrea Graziosi's studies of Soviet agriculture, and the declassified Soviet archives all confirm: local officials were required to report meeting targets and were punished for shortfalls. The incentive structure produced systematic upward falsification. Gosplan compiled these numbers; the charts visualized Gosplan's numbers; the charts were technically correct. The falsification is not at the chart-making step.

This creates an unusual scoring problem. The School Fidelity and Execution dimensions score what the chart-makers did, which was largely correct. The Integrity score reflects the data environment, which was comprehensively falsified. The Purpose score reflects the state-level decision to use this visualization system to prevent citizens from understanding a famine.

**Rubric gap:** The current rubric's Integrity pathway asks about the work's disclosure of its own limitations. It does not have a pathway for the case where the chart-maker cannot know the data is false — because the falsification is produced by the coercive structure above and below them in the reporting chain. The rubric's subject is the visualization author; here, the author may be a victim. This complexity requires v2.3 treatment: how do we score an honestly-executed chart of false data when the data's falsity was structurally produced and the chartmaker had no reliable alternative?
