# Nightingale Review — 0052 Soviet Five-Year Plan Progress Charts

My approach to data visualization was built on a specific conviction: that decision-makers could be reached and changed by evidence, if the evidence were presented in the right form. The entire premise of my coxcomb was that the people with power to prevent deaths would act on honest data if they could see it. The implicit contract was that the data was honest and the form was designed to communicate it.

The Soviet Five-Year Plan charts break this contract at the source level, and the break has a specific character I want to name carefully. These are not simply "wrong charts." The designers of these charts — Gosplan's statisticians — may in many cases have believed the numbers they were given. The falsification entered the data stream before the visualization stage. The chart-makers were, in a sense, victims of the same coercive system as the public who viewed the charts.

This is different from the tobacco case (0051). The American Tobacco Company's researchers knew they were manufacturing a survey. Gosplan's chart-makers received numbers from a reporting chain that could not report honestly. The tobacco case is deliberate at the top; the Soviet case may have been deliberate only in the structure of the incentive system that produced the falsification, not necessarily in the final visualization step.

This distinction matters for how we score and how we name the mechanism. I call this institutional falsification through captive reporting — the system is designed so that accurate information cannot reach the visualization stage, regardless of whether any individual visualizer intends to deceive.

The audience's position is the most important analytical fact here. A British reader of my coxcomb could ask questions: write to a Member of Parliament, consult a physician, read competing accounts. A Soviet citizen viewing these charts in 1933 had none of these options. The suppression of alternative information is part of the coercive apparatus, and it is as important as the falsification itself.
