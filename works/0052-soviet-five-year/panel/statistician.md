# Statistician Review — 0052 Soviet Five-Year Plan Progress Charts

I want to distinguish two levels of statistical failure in this work, because they require different analytical treatment.

**Level 1: Visual encoding.** The bar charts, target lines, and percentage figures are correctly encoded. Bar length represents quantity; the target line represents the plan threshold; the percentage represents achievement ratio. The visual grammar is sound. A statistician viewing the encoding alone would find competent work.

**Level 2: Source data.** The numbers encoded are systematically false. The question is: by how much, and with what structure?

The falsification has a characteristic shape: it is asymmetric upward. Local officials had strong incentives to report at or above target and strong disincentives to report below. This means the falsification is not random noise; it is directional bias with a specific causal mechanism. The charts do not show random error; they show results from a system that structurally produced above-target results regardless of actual production.

For agricultural data during the 1932–33 famine: actual grain production fell by an estimated 25–40% from pre-collectivization levels. Official charts showed increasing production. The gap between chart and reality was not a rounding error — it was a catastrophic directional falsification maintained across multiple reporting levels simultaneously.

**The captive-source problem for statistical assessment:** Standard statistical audit asks: is the methodology sound? Are the numbers accurately encoded? Here, the methodology is sound and the numbers are accurately encoded, but the input to the methodology was generated under conditions where accurate input was impossible. This is a failure mode that the statistical audit framework does not naturally handle — the chart is statistically valid and empirically false simultaneously.

The rubric needs a mechanism for assessing source integrity independent of encoding integrity. The current Question 1 (source quality disclosure) focuses on what the chart-maker declares. Here the chart-maker may not have known the full extent of falsification. The failure is systemic, not individual.
