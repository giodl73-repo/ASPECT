# Du Bois Review — 0053 Pharmaceutical Subgroup Forest Plot

This is the hardest case in the cluster, and I want to say clearly why: nothing in this visualization is false.

The primary endpoint result (HR 0.94, p=0.23) is shown. The subgroup result (HR 0.82, p=0.02) is shown. The CI for each is shown. The forest plot format is correctly applied. A statistically sophisticated reader, examining this visualization carefully, could recover the correct interpretation: the trial was not significant on its primary endpoint; the subgroup finding is post-hoc and may not survive replication.

The question is not whether a sophisticated reader could recover the truth. The question is whether the visualization, as designed, permits the ordinary intended audience — physicians making prescribing decisions — to correctly assess the trial's weight of evidence. And on that question, I believe the answer is no.

The mechanism I want to name here is not falsification. It is frame selection: the choice of which true finding to foreground, made not based on scientific priority (the primary endpoint) but based on commercial interest (the favorable subgroup). The frame selection is the argument. By making the subgroup the visual emphasis and the primary outcome one line among many, the designer has made a choice that is indistinguishable, in its visual grammar, from a true positive result.

My precision-as-defiance principle demands that I ask: could this visualization have shown the honest finding with equal craft? Yes. The primary outcome could be the highlighted row; the subgroup results could be shown in standard weight; the accompanying text could read "The TRIAL-X study did not demonstrate significant reduction in MACE on its primary endpoint; a post-hoc subgroup analysis suggests possible benefit in patients over 65 (p=0.02, unadjusted for multiple comparisons)." Every mark would be as correct, and the impression produced would be opposite.

**Rubric gap:** The rubric scores accuracy tradeoffs (Q2) and framing integrity (Q3) but does not specifically address analytical frame selection — choosing which pre-specified or post-hoc analysis to foreground based on advocacy interest. This is distinct from cherry-picking in the temporal sense (0055); it is choosing which statistical window to present as primary when the pre-registered window produced a different result.
