# Historian Review — 0053 Pharmaceutical Subgroup Forest Plot

The history of clinical trial reporting is directly relevant here. The CONSORT statement (2001, updated 2010) was developed precisely to address selective reporting: the tendency for published trials to emphasize positive findings over negative ones, and subgroup findings over primary outcomes. The CONSORT requirement — that primary and secondary endpoints be pre-specified and all results reported — was a response to a documented pattern in the medical literature.

This type case represents the most modern and sophisticated variant of that pattern. Unlike earlier periods, where negative trials were simply not published, the contemporary mechanism works differently: the trial IS published (often required for regulatory purposes), the primary endpoint IS reported, and the subgroup analysis IS statistically real. The coercion operates entirely within the disclosure framework — it shows everything required, while foregrounding what serves the commercial interest.

This is an important historical development. Earlier forms of selective reporting (publication bias, outcome switching) are increasingly detectable through trial registries. The analytical frame selection mechanism is harder to detect because everything disclosed is true. The audience — physicians — has in principle access to the trial registration (ClinicalTrials.gov) where the primary endpoint is specified. In practice, the physician seeing the conference presentation or journal supplement does not cross-reference the registry.

**Power context:** Physicians making prescribing decisions are the audience. They have formal training in statistical interpretation but are subject to cognitive shortcuts and time constraints. The forest plot is designed to reach them in a mode where the visual grammar does the work — where the highlighted subgroup reads as "the result." The manufacturer controls which visualization physicians encounter; alternative presentations (showing the primary endpoint prominently) are not produced.

**Rubric gap:** The rubric's Integrity questions address framing (Q3) but not the specific case of analytical priority inversion — where the pre-registered primary analysis is de-emphasized in favor of a post-hoc analysis. The mechanism needs naming: not "novel framework undeclared" but "primary endpoint hierarchy inverted by post-hoc selection."
