# Nightingale Review — 0053 Pharmaceutical Subgroup Forest Plot

I want to address the forest plot as a form before I address this particular instance of it.

Forest plots were developed precisely to make clinical evidence accessible to decision-makers who would otherwise struggle with tables of hazard ratios. They are a genuine contribution to the legibility of evidence — the visual grammar of the CI bar crossing or not crossing the null line is one of the most useful binary encodings in medical statistics. I recognize the form as a legitimate and important tool.

Which is exactly why this case is so serious.

The forest plot format carries epistemic authority precisely because it is associated with rigorous, registered clinical trial reporting. When a physician sees a forest plot, the implicit communication is: "this is the systematic presentation of clinical evidence as it was collected and pre-analyzed." The form implies the integrity of the evidence.

This forest plot deploys that implicit authority while violating the integrity condition that earns it. The primary endpoint is pre-registered; the subgroup analysis is post-hoc; the distinction is not disclosed in the visualization or accompanying text. The physician reading this chart receives a forest plot that says "subgroup in patients >65" — a plausible and common clinical question — without knowing that this subgroup was identified after the primary analysis failed.

The question I always ask is: who is this for, and does the design serve their actual needs? This forest plot is for prescribing physicians. Their actual need is to know: "does this drug work?" The honest answer, from TRIAL-X, is: "we don't know — the pre-registered trial was not significant." The forest plot answers: "yes, in patients over 65." The design serves the manufacturer's commercial interest, not the physician's clinical need.

I score this higher than the tobacco case (0051) or Soviet case (0052) because the primary endpoint IS shown and the data IS real. But the coercion is real: the analytical frame is being selected to produce a false clinical impression.
