# Brief — 0054 North Carolina Gerrymandering District Map

## Historical Context

The North Carolina 12th Congressional District is one of the most studied examples of electoral district manipulation in American legal history. It appears in two notable configurations:

**1993 Version (Shaw v. Reno):** The district was drawn as a long, thin corridor following Interstate 85 through the Piedmont region, connecting Black communities in Charlotte, Greensboro, Winston-Salem, and Durham. The district was designed to create a majority-Black district in compliance with the Voting Rights Act's majority-minority district requirements. Justice Sandra Day O'Connor, writing for the majority in *Shaw v. Reno* (1993), described it as "so bizarre on its face that it appears to constitute an unconstitutional racial gerrymander." The district was challenged, redrawn, challenged again, and ultimately upheld in modified form after multiple Supreme Court cases.

**2012 Version (post-Citizens United partisan gerrymander):** After the 2010 Republican wave in North Carolina, the Republican-controlled legislature redrew all congressional districts. The 12th district was modified but remained an instrument of partisan engineering — along with other districts, it was designed to pack Democratic voters (disproportionately Black and urban) into a small number of districts while spreading Republican voters across many competitive districts. North Carolina, a genuine swing state, elected a 10–3 Republican congressional delegation in 2012 while Obama won the presidential race. The district map was challenged in *Common Cause v. Rucho* and ultimately upheld by the Supreme Court in 2019 (*Rucho v. Common Cause*) — the Court held that partisan gerrymandering is a political question beyond federal judicial reach.

## The Process Coercion Mechanism

This is the most analytically unusual case in Cluster X. The map is honest. The district boundaries shown are the real district boundaries. The colors are the real district assignments. The cartographic grammar is correctly applied. Nothing in the visualization is false.

The coercion is in what the visualization is showing: the output of a district-drawing process designed to produce specific electoral outcomes. The map presents as geographic fact what is actually an artifact of political engineering. The district shapes are not the result of geographic, administrative, or community logic; they are the result of computational optimization for partisan advantage.

The audience — voters, journalists, civic organizations — sees a district map that appears to represent natural or administrative geographic units. There is no visual signal in the map that the district boundaries were drawn to produce a specific electoral outcome. The map truthfully represents manipulated geography.
