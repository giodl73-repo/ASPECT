# Du Bois Review — 0054 North Carolina Congressional District Map

This work requires that I separate two questions that the other Cluster X cases allowed me to treat together: is the visualization honest, and is the thing being visualized honest?

The visualization is honest. The district boundaries shown are the real district boundaries. The map is an accurate representation of North Carolina's congressional district structure. A voter trying to learn which congressional district they live in could use this map reliably. In this sense, the work is entirely truthful.

The thing being visualized is the product of a process designed to produce specific political outcomes — to translate North Carolina's genuine political division (a swing state) into a near-permanent 10-3 Republican congressional delegation. The map presents that outcome as if it were geography.

I want to say precisely what the coercion is: it is the naturalization of a political artifact. District maps carry the visual authority of geographic fact — they look like the map of North Carolina in the same way a topographic map or county map does. The viewer receives the message "this is how North Carolina is divided" when the accurate message would be "this is how North Carolina was divided by a partisan process in 2012." The map cannot carry both messages in its standard cartographic grammar.

The race dimension of the 1993 version intensifies this. The majority-minority district was drawn to give Black communities representation — a legitimate goal under the Voting Rights Act — but was challenged as racial gerrymandering that isolated rather than empowered. The map of the 1993 district presents this contested design as geographic fact. Communities that may have had alternative interests in district configuration see a map that naturalizes the decision made about them.

**Rubric gap:** The rubric has no mechanism for process coercion with truthful encoding — the case where the visualization is honest but what it represents was produced by a coercive process. The current Integrity questions ask about the work's honesty; they do not ask whether the subject of the work was produced honestly. This is a genuine gap and a philosophically interesting one.
