# Historian Review — 0054 North Carolina Congressional District Map

Gerrymandering is as old as American democracy — Elbridge Gerry's salamander-shaped district in Massachusetts in 1812 gave the practice its name. What changed in the post-2010 period was the computational precision of the manipulation. Where earlier gerrymanders relied on hand-drawn maps, the modern partisan gerrymander uses census-block-level data, sophisticated mapping software, and explicit optimization algorithms to maximize partisan seat advantage.

The 2012 North Carolina district maps were drawn using Maptitude for Redistricting software, with explicit partisan modeling of each district's likely voting behavior. This is documented in the *Common Cause v. Rucho* litigation, where internal emails and maps showed that the legislature's mapmaker was given explicit partisan targets. The district map that a voter sees in 2012 is the output of a computationally optimized partisan process — but the standard district map gives no indication of this.

The Supreme Court's 2019 decision in *Rucho v. Common Cause* is directly relevant to the power context: the Court held that federal courts cannot adjudicate partisan gerrymandering claims, finding them non-justiciable political questions. This means voters have no federal judicial recourse against the manipulation represented by these maps. The district map presents the output of a process that has been explicitly held to be unchallengeable in federal court.

**Power context:** The audience — voters — sees a map that represents their electoral environment as a fait accompli. The alternative information available includes: the *Rucho* decision itself, statistical analyses of the efficiency gap, academic work on the detected manipulation. But this alternative information is not in the map; it requires active research to find. The standard district map is the authoritative representation most voters encounter.

**Rubric gap:** The rubric does not distinguish between visualizations of naturally-occurring facts and visualizations of politically-produced facts. For civic cartography, this distinction is essential: a topographic map of NC shows natural geography; a district map shows the output of a political process. The rubric should have a modifier for civic cartography that asks: is the political process that produced the subject disclosed?
