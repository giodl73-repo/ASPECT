# Nightingale Review — 0054 North Carolina Congressional District Map

This is the case where I most strongly feel the gap between the rubric's current framing and what the work actually requires.

My approach to advocacy visualization was always: show the evidence, let the decision-maker see what the situation actually is, and trust that honest information will produce better outcomes. The premise is that the viewer receives honest evidence. The district map gives the viewer honest evidence about district structure. But the district structure was itself produced by a process that bypassed the civic process that would normally constrain it.

I want to put this in terms the rubric can work with: what is the advocacy purpose of a district map? It is to inform voters, journalists, and civic organizations about how representation is structured — so they can participate in, challenge, or accept that structure. For this purpose, a standard district map is partly adequate and partly inadequate. It tells you the structure; it does not tell you how the structure was produced or whether the structure was produced fairly. For democratic deliberation, the second question is essential.

The coercion is not in the map. The coercion is in the district-drawing process that the map represents. But this creates a subtle problem: if we hold the district map responsible for the process that produced its subject, we open a general question about all visualization — are we responsible for the honesty of whatever we depict? I think the answer is: not fully, but we are responsible for framing. A district map that shows boundaries without any annotation about the process that produced them does less civic work than it could.

**For Cluster X purposes:** The mechanism here — process coercion with truthful encoding — is genuinely different from all other mechanisms. The visualization does not manipulate; the process manipulated, and the visualization honestly shows the result. This is the edge case of the cluster.
