# Statistician Review — 0054 North Carolina Congressional District Map

From a statistical standpoint, the district map presents a number — the distribution of congressional representation — as a visual fact, when that number is the output of an optimization process with specific parameters.

**The efficiency gap.** Political scientists Nicholas Stephanopoulos and Eric McGhee developed the efficiency gap metric to quantify partisan gerrymandering. For North Carolina's 2012 districts, the efficiency gap was approximately 20% — one of the largest documented in American history. This means Democratic voters' votes were, on average, about 20% more "wasted" than Republican voters' votes, by design. The standard district map shows the geographic result; it does not show the efficiency gap that quantifies the manipulation.

**The seat-share / vote-share gap.** In 2012, North Carolina voted approximately 50.6% Democratic and 49.4% Republican in congressional races (across all districts). The seat result was 9 Republicans, 4 Democrats — a 69-31% seat split from a near-50-50 vote split. The district map does not show this relationship. A voter looking at the map cannot see that the seat allocation is disproportionate.

**Statistical audit of the map:** The standard district map encodes one variable (district identity) in color, one variable (geographic extent) in shape and position, and no variable related to: partisan composition, expected seat allocation, vote-share efficiency, degree of compactness, or deviation from alternative plans. A complete statistical representation of "this is how representation is structured" would require additional panels.

**The statistical audit finds:** The map is correct — a correct representation of the district configuration. The configuration is the output of a process designed to produce a specific statistical outcome (partisan seat allocation). The map presents the output without the process or the statistical consequence.
